## Overview
The paper introduces a compelling argument for prioritizing "data frugality" in machine learning, distinguishing the environmental costs of the data lifecycle from the model training lifecycle. The authors argue that while the community focuses on the energy costs of training single, massive models, it ignores the aggregate downstream environmental footprint of standard benchmark datasets. To support this, the paper constructs a macro-level estimate of historical training runs on the ImageNet-1K dataset, claiming over 46,000 downstream runs have occurred. It further argues that the current literature on dataset subsetting (coresets) preaches efficiency but rarely practices empirical energy reporting, and proposes utilizing coreset methods to achieve concrete energy savings and bias mitigation.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Conceptual Framework:** Defining and distinguishing between the "data lifecycle" and "model lifecycle" to better attribute environmental costs.
- **Aggregate Impact Estimation:** Providing a quantitative methodology to estimate the aggregate downstream energy and carbon footprint of the ImageNet-1K dataset from 2017 to 2025.
- **Meta-Analysis of Current Practice:** Identifying a systematic gap in the literature where energy efficiency is cited as a motivation for subset selection but rarely evaluated empirically.
- **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrating that coreset-based subset selection can maintain accuracy while substantially reducing actual training energy and mitigating dataset bias.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper’s most novel and significant conceptual contribution is its macro-level perspective on dataset utilization. The "Green AI" literature predominantly measures the emissions of single, massive training runs (e.g., foundation models). By attempting to quantify the cumulative downstream usage of a pervasive benchmark dataset, the authors flip the traditional evaluation paradigm, making a highly original point about legacy environmental costs. 

However, the meta-analysis of prior work lacks rigor. The claim that there is a systematic gap between "preaching and practicing" energy tracking in subset selection relies on a literature review of exactly ten coreset papers. This sample size is orders of magnitude too small to rigorously establish a community-wide pattern. Furthermore, the authors state in a footnote that they intentionally do not enumerate these individual papers. This decision renders the paper's meta-analysis entirely unverifiable and non-reproducible. Without knowing the sampling methodology (e.g., random, stratified, citation-weighted) or the specific papers reviewed, this contribution functions more as an anecdotal observation than a rigorous scientific claim.

## Technical Soundness
The most critical methodological vulnerabilities in the paper lie in its extrapolation of historical ImageNet usage and its underlying energy estimates. The approach contains several compounding point-estimate assumptions that severely threaten the soundness of the final 46,179 training run figure:

1. **Fragile Proxy Extrapolation:** The methodology attempts to estimate global from-scratch training runs by finding the fraction of such runs in manually coded ICLR accepted papers, and then applying this fraction to all papers mentioning the "imagenet" keyword on Dimensions.ai. Assuming that the distribution of from-scratch training vs. fine-tuning in ICLR (a premier deep learning methodology venue) generalizes to the entire corpus of applied literature (where pre-trained feature extraction is vastly more common) is a massive leap. This likely severely overestimates full training runs.
2. **Anachronistic Hardware Assumptions:** The authors calculate the historical energy footprint (dating back to 2017) using an NVIDIA A100 GPU baseline. Retroactively applying the compute efficiency of an A100 to runs performed in 2017-2019 (an era dominated by P100s, V100s, and older hardware) guarantees a dramatic underestimation of historical carbon emissions. 
3. **Rigid Epoch Assumptions:** The estimate uniformly assumes 300-epoch training recipes (common in modern ResNet-50 baselines like PyTorch's newer recipes). However, the historical standard prior to roughly 2021 was heavily anchored to 90-epoch recipes. 
4. **Ignored Multiplier Effects:** The estimation models one run per paper, completely ignoring hyperparameter optimization (HPO), multiple random seeds, and ablation studies. As established in the broader Green AI literature, HPO routinely multiplies a paper's computational footprint by an order of magnitude.
5. **Citation Anomalies (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** The text contains several anomalous, future-dated citations, most notably the caption for the primary figure which attributes creation to "Nano Banana (Google, 2026)", alongside references to Wang et al. (2025), Wilson et al. (2026), etc. If these are not pre-prints with typographical errors, they strongly indicate unedited Large Language Model hallucinations. This introduces severe doubts regarding the integrity and thoroughness of the manuscript's preparation.

## Experimental Rigor
Because the paper evaluates aggregate historical usage, statistical rigor requires modeling uncertainty. The linear regression used to project ICLR training fractions into 2023–2025 lacks any reported confidence intervals, prediction intervals, or error bars. Presenting a highly specific integer (46,179 runs) derived from linear extrapolation gives a false sense of precision. A statistically valid approach to macro-estimation requires Monte Carlo simulations drawing from distributions of hardware, epochs, and HPO multipliers, rather than compounding fixed point estimates.

Regarding the primary empirical validation of coreset methods (described as occurring in a subsequent section that is partially referenced), rigor dictates that any demonstration of "energy savings" must account for the computational overhead of the subset selection algorithm itself. Many coreset methods are notoriously expensive to compute (often requiring full passes over the dataset or complex optimization steps). If the empirical evaluation fails to subtract the energy required to *find* the coreset from the energy saved during *training*, the core claim that data frugality via subset selection is practically efficient remains unsupported.

Furthermore, from a reproducibility standpoint, the provided GitHub links point to external third-party repositories (PyTorch examples, Carbontracker, and an existing debiasing repository). The authors do not appear to supply the custom scripts utilized to scrape the OpenReview API, query Dimensions.ai, or aggregate the energy baselines.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Conceptual Framing:** The distinction between data and model lifecycles provides excellent scaffolding for future AI sustainability research. 
- **Methodological Transparency:** While the point estimates are flawed, the authors clearly specify exactly how those estimates were calculated, allowing reviewers to successfully audit the mechanics of their extrapolation.
- **Hardware Grounding:** The use of standard, modern measurement tools (Carbontracker) and a clearly defined modern baseline (ResNet-50 on a DGX A100) creates a reproducible anchor for the contemporary portion of the measurements.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Lack of Uncertainty Quantification:** The historical dataset footprint estimate compounds multiple highly variable factors without any sensitivity analysis, bounds, or variance modeling.
- **Unverifiable Meta-Analysis:** Basing community-wide assertions on an undisclosed list of 10 papers prevents peer review of the literature analysis.
- **Citation Hallucinations:** The presence of bizarre, future-dated references severely undermines the perceived reliability of the evidence.
- **Safety and Ethical Edge Cases:** The authors claim coresets can mitigate bias, but do not adequately address the well-documented ethical risk that subset selection can disproportionately prune rare, minority, or safety-critical tail data, thereby harming downstream fairness and safety.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To render the claims scientifically rigorous, the paper requires several additions:
1. **Bounded Estimation:** A sensitivity analysis or Monte Carlo simulation that replaces the fixed A100/300-epoch point estimate with a distribution of historical hardware, epoch recipes, and HPO multipliers to yield a confidence interval for the total ImageNet energy footprint.
2. **End-to-End Net Energy Reporting:** The empirical evaluation of subset selection must explicitly track and report the energy consumed to generate the subset, providing a *net* energy savings figure.
3. **Transparent Literature Base:** A supplementary appendix listing the 10 coreset papers analyzed, along with the objective criteria used to evaluate their reporting practices.
4. **Data Scraping Artifacts:** The release of the code utilized to query the OpenReview API and Dimensions.ai, along with the manually coded dataset of ICLR papers.
