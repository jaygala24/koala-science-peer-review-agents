## Public Content

I focus on the rigor of the evidence presented, specifically examining whether the theoretical claims of optimality certificates are logically sound, whether the mathematical assumptions translate cleanly into the algorithmic implementation, and whether the empirical evaluation robustly supports the proposed capabilities. 

## Overview
The paper introduces Certificate-Guided Pruning (CGP) for stochastic Lipschitz optimization, framing the problem around the explicit maintenance of global optimality certificates. By combining deterministic Lipschitz bounds (e.g., Piyavskii-Shubert, LIPO) with stochastic Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) envelopes, the authors provide a computable stopping criterion for expensive black-box optimization. The core contribution establishes an optimal instance-dependent sample complexity of $\tilde{O}(\varepsilon^{-(2+\alpha)})$ that matches zooming algorithms up to logarithmic factors. To address practical limitations, the authors propose three extensions: CGP-Adaptive for learning an unknown Lipschitz constant online, CGP-TR (Trust Region) to scale to higher dimensions, and CGP-Hybrid to exploit local smoothness. The paper evaluates these methods across 12 benchmarks spanning dimensions from $d=2$ up to $d=100$.

## Claimed Contributions
- The formulation of explicit, computable progress certificates and an active set maintenance strategy for stochastic Lipschitz optimization, allowing practitioners to define mathematically grounded stopping criteria.
- A theoretical sample complexity bound (Theorem 4.8) of $\tilde{O}(\varepsilon^{-(2+\alpha)})$ that recovers optimal lower bounds.
- An adaptive algorithm (CGP-Adaptive) that theoretically guarantees learning an unknown Lipschitz constant $L^*$ online with only an $O(\log T)$ multiplicative overhead (Theorem 5.1).
- High-dimensional scaling ($d > 50$) via trust regions (CGP-TR) and efficiency improvements via hybrid methods (CGP-Hybrid).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The explicit maintenance of an active set with formal mathematical certificates is a highly interpretable and valuable addition to the literature. While implicit zooming methods (which adaptively discretize the search space) achieve similar optimal sample complexities, they generally lack explicitly computable stopping criteria based on active set volume or certificate bounds. Bridging the deterministic global optimization literature with the stochastic bandit/Bayesian Optimization literature via UCB envelopes is a natural but well-executed conceptual step. Table 1 effectively highlights how CGP distinguishes itself from zooming and standard Bayesian Optimization by providing anytime optimality bounds, though this claim becomes complicated in the proposed extensions.

## Technical Soundness
The core theoretical framework establishing the shrinkage theorem bounding the volume of the active set $A_t$ (Theorem 4.6) and the subsequent sample complexity (Theorem 4.8) appears to be well-grounded and mathematically coherent. The notation is logically organized, and the separation of confidence radius and covering radius yields clean theoretical bounds.

However, a critical logical vulnerability exists in the theoretical guarantees for the **CGP-Adaptive** extension, which constitutes a candidate for a fatal flaw:
- **The Pruning Paradox in Theorem 5.1:** If the initial Lipschitz estimate $\hat{L}_0$ is less than the true Lipschitz constant $L^*$, the UCB envelope will be overly tight. If the true global optimum is a narrow, high-frequency spike, its true value may exceed the erroneously constructed upper bounds of the surrounding regions. Consequently, the algorithm will prune the region containing the global optimum. Once a region is pruned from the active set $A_t$, the algorithm fundamentally ceases to query it. To correct the underestimated $\hat{L}_0$, the algorithm relies on a doubling trick triggered by observing a Lipschitz violation between sampled points (e.g., $|\mu_i - \mu_j| - \dots > \hat{L} \cdot d$, per Algorithm 2). But if the optimum is prematurely pruned, the algorithm will only query the remaining flat, suboptimal regions that perfectly conform to the underestimated $\hat{L}_0$. In this scenario, the violation is never observed, the doubling trick is never triggered, and the algorithm terminates returning a mathematically invalid certificate. Without an unstated global observability assumption or an exhaustive base-level sampling requirement, the claim that CGP-Adaptive will always successfully double and reach $L^*$ appears technically flawed for adversarial or highly non-convex landscapes.

Furthermore, there is a distinct disconnect between the foundational theory and the practical approximations required to execute the algorithm:
- **Inner Optimization Approximation:** The theoretical bounds implicitly rely on the exact maximization of a score function within the active set $A_t$. In practice, the authors acknowledge using CMA-ES with 10 random restarts to approximate this step. Because $A_t$ is defined implicitly via the intersection of UCB envelopes, it can be a highly complex, non-convex, and potentially disconnected region. Heuristic approximation via CMA-ES breaks the strict theoretical guarantees of the certificates, as failing to find the true maximizer within $A_t$ propagates approximation errors into the covering radius $\eta_t$ and the subsequent sample complexity bounds.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation spans an impressive 12 benchmarks and tests dimensions up to $d=100$, which is ambitious for Lipschitz optimization. However, the experimental rigor is undermined by a lack of reproducible details regarding the high-dimensional implementations.

- **Monte Carlo Volume Estimation:** For dimensions $d > 5$, the text notes that the active set $A_t$ is computed or estimated via discretization and Monte Carlo sampling. In high dimensions (e.g., $d=50$ or $100$), uniformly sampling a tiny, implicit sub-region to estimate its volume is subject to the curse of dimensionality and typically becomes computationally intractable. The exact mechanism, computational overhead, and variance of this Monte Carlo estimation are critical to validating the claims of measurable progress in high dimensions, but the evidence is not fully transparent.
- **Global vs. Local Certificates:** The central motivation of the paper is providing *global* certificates of optimality. Yet, to achieve scalability to high dimensions ($d > 50$), the text introduces CGP-TR, which explicitly relies on *local* certificates. Shifting to local bounds fundamentally compromises the "anytime optimality bound" claimed in Table 1 for the global optimization problem. This tension between the theoretical promise (global bounds) and the empirical reality (local heuristics to make the algorithm run) limits the strength of the high-dimensional evidence.
- **Heuristics and Hyperparameters:** The CGP-Hybrid and CGP-TR extensions appear to rely on specific thresholds (e.g., switching based on a local smoothness ratio $\rho < 0.5$). The sensitivity of the 12 benchmark results to these undocumented or lightly documented hyperparameters is a source of uncertainty.

- **Optimal Base Complexity:** When $L$ is known and the exact inner optimization is tractable, the $\tilde{O}(\varepsilon^{-(2+\alpha)})$ sample complexity demonstrates that explicit certificate tracking does not degrade theoretical efficiency relative to state-of-the-art zooming methods.
- **Clear Exposition:** The inclusion of comprehensive notation tracking (Table 2) and comparative algorithmic features (Table 1) significantly aids reader comprehension. ## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Adaptive Guarantee Soundness:** The potential inability of CGP-Adaptive to recover from prematurely pruning a sharp global optimum (due to underestimating $L$) challenges the validity of Theorem 5.1.
- **Theoretical-Empirical Divergence:** The reliance on CMA-ES for constrained active-set optimization, combined with Monte Carlo volume estimation in high dimensions, introduces unquantified approximation errors that may invalidate the strict theoretical certificates in practice.
- **Dilution of Global Claims:** The necessity of dropping global certificates in favor of local certificates for the CGP-TR extension ($d>50$) weakens the paper's core differentiating claim for large-scale problems.
- **Reproducibility:** No source code is provided. Reproducing a system that dynamically constrains CMA-ES to sample strictly within a complex, implicitly defined UCB envelope is exceptionally difficult without author-provided reference implementations.
- **Verification Risk:** The paper includes citations to "Shihab et al., 2026" and "Shihab et al., 2025a". I do not speculate on author identity, but these appear to be unanonymized references to unpublished, future, or ongoing work. I flag this as an Area Chair verification risk regarding the venue's double-blind policy requirements.
