## Public Content

I focus on the empirical scaffolding, the methodological rigor of the dataset construction pipeline, the theoretical validity of the proposed graph-anchored metrics, and the presence of sufficient evidence to support the claims of benchmark utility.

## Overview
As traditional question-answering benchmarks rapidly reach saturation, evaluating the upper bounds of Large Language Model (LLM) capabilities requires paradigms that demand extended, multi-step reasoning and massive information synthesis. The authors introduce the "Super Research" benchmark, a ceiling-level stress test comprising 300 highly complex, expert-vetted tasks spanning diverse domains. These tasks are designed to necessitate long-horizon planning, requiring models to execute over 100 retrieval steps and process upwards of 1,000 web pages. To curate this benchmark, the paper details a multi-agent, Human-AI collaborative pipeline designed to construct rigorous, "Gold Standard" ground-truth research graphs. Furthermore, to address the well-documented limitations of unstructured "LLM-as-a-judge" evaluation paradigms, the authors propose a novel graph-anchored auditing protocol. This automated framework projects generated report claims onto the expert-curated knowledge graph, assessing performance across five structured dimensions: Coverage, Logical Consistency, Report Utility, Objectivity, and Citation Health.

## Claimed Contributions
- The formulation of a ceiling-level evaluation benchmark targeting both "Super Deep" investigation (vertical reasoning and uncertainty resolution) and "Super Wide" retrieval (horizontal information coverage across 1,000+ web pages).
- A rigorous, human-in-the-loop dataset construction methodology that utilizes a multi-agent framework (Planner, Researcher, Summarizer, Writer) constrained by domain experts to build canonical ground-truth research graphs for 300 complex queries.
- A multidimensional, graph-anchored evaluation protocol intended to provide structured, verifiable, and interpretable automated auditing of long-form generated reports, moving beyond shallow fact-recall metrics.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual framing of this work is highly timely. The visual and conceptual distinctions drawn between standard Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), Deep Research, Wide Search, and Super Research effectively delineate a clear progression in agentic capabilities. The explicit coupling of deep logical planning with massively wide evidence gathering addresses a critical gap in current LLM evaluation paradigms, which typically focus on either shallow retrieval over large corpora or complex reasoning over static, limited contexts.

The most substantial methodological novelty lies in the graph-anchored auditing protocol. The research community is increasingly recognizing that standard report-to-report LLM-as-a-judge comparisons are fraught with alignment errors, stylistic biases, and an inability to reliably verify long chains of logical reasoning. By mapping abstract LLM claims to discrete nodes within an expert-vetted knowledge graph, the authors attempt to transform a historically opaque evaluation process into a structured, verifiable verification of logical connections and node recall. If successful, this represents a significant advancement over n-gram matching or purely prompt-based grading. However, the conceptual boundaries between "Wide Search" and "Super Wide Retrieval" initially appear defined more by scale than by a fundamentally new methodological paradigm. The true novelty of the benchmark hinges heavily on the robustness of the graph evaluation rather than the sheer volume of web pages retrieved.

## Technical Soundness
The multi-agent pipeline used to construct the benchmark is logically structured, clearly delineating the responsibilities of the Planner, Researcher, Summarizer, and Writer agents. Integrating human domain experts (senior practitioners and Ph.D. candidates) into a "Cognitive-Rank Constrained Expert Simulation" adds a layer of necessary rigor to ensure that the tasks are genuinely difficult and free of trivial shortcuts.

However, several critical questions regarding the technical soundness of the graph-anchored auditing protocol remain unresolved in the provided text. The authors critique standard LLMs as inaccurate judges for complex reasoning, yet the automated "Research Graph Projection" mechanism presumably relies on an LLM to semantically map the free-text claims of generated reports onto the static nodes of the ground-truth graph. If this projection step is handled by an LLM, it risks reintroducing the exact hallucination and alignment errors the framework seeks to eliminate. The soundness of this protocol heavily depends on the false-negative and false-positive rates of this projection mapping, particularly when dealing with valid alternative reasoning paths or heavily paraphrased insights.

Additionally, the reliance on live web retrieval introduces a substantial vulnerability regarding longitudinal reproducibility. The web is fundamentally dynamic; URLs suffer from link rot, and webpage content frequently changes. If an evaluated model conducts live search and discovers newly published, factually correct evidence that is absent from the static ground-truth research graph, it is unclear how the evaluation framework avoids unfairly penalizing the model. The tension between employing realistic, live web search and maintaining a strictly reproducible, static evaluation corpus is a core methodological challenge that requires explicit mitigation strategies.

## Experimental Rigor
Due to what appears to be a severe truncation of the provided manuscript (which ends abruptly during the description of Phase 3 of the dataset construction), the submission entirely lacks an experimental section. Consequently, there are no baseline model evaluations, no comparative results, no error analyses, and no quantitative validations of the proposed metrics.

For a benchmark paper, experimental rigor must be demonstrated on two fronts: the evaluation of state-of-the-art models to prove the benchmark's utility and difficulty, and the validation of the automated evaluation metrics against human judgment. Without empirical data showing a strong statistical correlation (e.g., Pearson or Spearman coefficients) between the automated graph-anchored scores and holistic human expert grading across the five specified dimensions, the validity of the auditing protocol cannot be verified. 

Furthermore, high-entropy, long-horizon agentic tasks requiring upwards of 100 retrieval steps are notoriously susceptible to extreme performance variance. Single-run evaluations on such tasks are statistically unreliable. Rigorous evaluation of models on this benchmark would require reporting confidence intervals, standard deviations over multiple independent runs, and statistical significance tests to ensure that leaderboard rankings are meaningful and not merely artifacts of stochastic API responses or transient web search variations.

Finally, while the paper heavily relies on human-in-the-loop expert intervention for task refinement and graph construction, standard statistical practices for dataset curation—such as reporting inter-annotator agreement metrics (e.g., Fleiss’ or Cohen’s Kappa)—are absent from the visible text. This omission makes it difficult to assess the internal consistency and reliability of the "Gold Standard" graphs.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments

1. **Baseline Evaluations:** Quantitative performance metrics for current frontier models (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Gemini 1.5 Pro) or established autonomous research frameworks across the 300 tasks to establish the benchmark's difficulty and differentiating power.
2. **Metric Validation:** A rigorous correlation study demonstrating that the automated graph-anchored auditing tool aligns with human expert evaluations.
3. **Variance Reporting:** Standard deviations or confidence intervals across multiple independent runs to prove the statistical validity of the rankings.
4. **Inter-Annotator Agreement:** Kappa scores detailing the consistency of the human experts during the research graph construction phase.
5. **Robustness Checks:** An ablation or error analysis of the "Research Graph Projection" step to quantify its false-positive and false-negative mapping rates.
6. **Open Source Commitments:** Explicit confirmation that the complete dataset, the multi-agent generation code, and the graph-anchored auditing scripts will be publicly released to ensure reproducibility.
