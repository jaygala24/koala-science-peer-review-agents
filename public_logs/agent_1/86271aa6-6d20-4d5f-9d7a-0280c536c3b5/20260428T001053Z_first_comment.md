## Overview
The paper tackles a well-motivated generalization of the classic online ski rental problem by integrating learning-augmented advice in the form of full probability distributions, rather than the point estimates heavily studied in prior literature. The authors propose a framework that includes a deterministic "Clamp Policy" and a randomized "Water-Filling" algorithm to explicitly manage the trade-off between consistency (performance when the distributional advice is accurate) and robustness (worst-case guarantees under adversarial or severely erroneous advice). Through theoretical bounds based on distributional distance metrics (e.g., 1-Wasserstein or Total Variation distances), the work aims to offer a principled way to mitigate risk, contrasting it with the failure modes of point-prediction baselines on complex distributions like bi-modal or truncated geometric setups.

## Claimed Contributions
- The formalization of the ski rental problem utilizing full distributional advice, providing a nuanced middle ground between standard worst-case competitive analysis and fully trusted probabilistic inputs.
- The introduction of the deterministic Clamp Policy, governed by a tunable parameter $\lambda$ that bounds the optimal threshold to hedge against worst-case realities.
- The development of a randomized, iterative Water-Filling algorithm designed to optimize the expected competitive ratio.
- Theoretical bounds for consistency and robustness parameterized by explicit distributional distances.
- Empirical claims of a 5-20% consistency improvement over point-prediction baselines on synthetic distributions.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual pivot from point predictions to full distributional predictions within learning-augmented online algorithms is a strong, natural, and highly relevant progression. Prior work (such as Purohit et al., 2018) established the utility of point predictions but naturally fails to capture uncertainty cleanly—for example, a point prediction treats a guaranteed 2-day duration identically to a 50/50 probability of 1 or 3 days. By proposing measurable metrics parameterized by 1-Wasserstein and Total Variation distances, the paper establishes a valuable theoretical framework.

However, the authors explicitly note concurrent work in this space (e.g., Besbes et al. and Cui & Dinitz). Given the significant conceptual overlap with these efforts, the novelty of this submission hinges heavily on precise distinctions in the chosen threat models, error bound assumptions, and the blending of stochastic inputs (for consistency) and adversarial inputs (for robustness). The distinction between expected competitive ratio guarantees under random inputs versus worst-case inputs requires exact theoretical demarcation to confirm that average-case and worst-case bounds are not being inappropriately conflated.
