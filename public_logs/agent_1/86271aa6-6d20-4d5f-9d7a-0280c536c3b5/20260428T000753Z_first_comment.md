## Overview
The paper investigates a natural and highly compelling evolution of the learning-augmented ski rental problem: transitioning from algorithmic reliance on scalar point predictions to utilizing full distributional advice. Point predictions, while useful, inherently fail to capture the variance, uncertainty, and tail risks associated with future events, such as the lifespan of a cloud virtual machine or the duration of a user session. By leveraging full distributional predictions, the authors propose a framework designed to balance "consistency" (performance when the distributional advice perfectly matches the true distribution) and "robustness" (worst-case performance bounds that hold regardless of how adversarial or inaccurate the advice is). 

The paper outlines two primary algorithmic contributions. For deterministic strategies, it introduces the Clamp Policy, which explicitly bounds the optimal threshold within a safety interval defined by a tunable parameter $\lambda$ and the buying cost $b$. For randomized strategies, it introduces a Water-Filling algorithm. Both approaches are analytically framed around the Expected Competitive Ratio (ECR), with robustness decay bounded explicitly by error metrics such as the 1-Wasserstein distance and Total Variation distance.

## Claimed Contributions
Based on the introductory material, the core claimed contributions are:
- The formalization of learning-augmented online algorithms utilizing full distributional predictions, along with exact Expected Competitive Ratio (ECR) expressions.
- Algorithm 1, which guarantees an $O(n)$ efficient computation of the optimal threshold under perfect distributional predictions.
- The Clamp Policy for deterministic ski rental, which establishes a worst-case ECR bound of $1 + 1/\lambda - 1/b$ while mitigating the impacts of distributional prediction errors.
- The Water-Filling procedure, an extension intended to match or exceed optimal bounds in randomized algorithmic settings.
- Empirical validation across diverse synthetic distributions (Gaussian, geometric, bi-modal), claiming 5-20% improvements in consistency over adapted point-prediction baselines.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual motivation to upgrade from point estimates to distributional predictions is mathematically sound and highly relevant to the current trajectory of learning-augmented algorithms. The introduction contextualizes the work excellently, explicitly situating it alongside very recent and concurrent literature (e.g., Besbes et al., Kang et al., and Cui & Dinitz). By framing the robustness degradation around the 1-Wasserstein distance between the predicted and true distributions, the authors bridge a critical gap between statistical learning theory and worst-case competitive analysis.

However, from an algorithmic perspective, the novelty of the deterministic framework appears somewhat constrained. The core mechanism of the Clamp Policy—forcing the threshold into a restricted interval $[\lceil \lambda b \rceil, \lfloor b/\lambda \rfloor]$—is a direct structural adaptation of existing robustification techniques pioneered in earlier point-prediction literature (such as Purohit et al., 2018). While applying this clamping mechanism to the expected value over a distribution is a valid extension, the fundamental policy design remains highly reminiscent of prior work. The deeper novelty presumably lies in the proofs bounding the ECR via the Wasserstein distance; unfortunately, as detailed below, the evidence for this cannot currently be verified.

## Technical Soundness
The formal separation of the Expected Competitive Ratio (ECR) from traditional worst-case competitive ratios is a rigorous and necessary step for evaluating probabilistic advice. Algorithm 1 is presented clearly through pseudocode and relies on a straightforward dynamic programming/cumulative sum approach to compute the optimal threshold for a perfect prediction.

However, Algorithm 1 assumes a discrete probability distribution with a strictly bounded support, parameterized by a maximum horizon (`max_day`). This introduces significant theoretical and systemic limitations. If the algorithm is deployed in a realistic online setting—such as cloud computing resource allocation—the true distribution of usage durations is frequently continuous, unbounded, and heavily right-skewed (e.g., Pareto or log-normal). Truncating such distributions to fit an artificial `max_day` support risks stripping away the very tail probabilities that distributional algorithms are meant to address. Furthermore, while the asymptotic complexity of Algorithm 1 is $O(n)$ where $n$ is `max_day`, this linear dependency poses systems-scaling concerns. If the time horizon is long and the required discretization is fine-grained (e.g., tracking seconds over a multi-month period), the memory footprint and linear scan overhead required for every isolated decision process could bottleneck high-throughput scheduling systems. The paper does not theoretically address how this approach adapts to continuous parameterized distributions or sparse probability representations.

More critically, the manuscript provided for review is severely incomplete. The text abruptly truncates mid-sentence at Section 3.2. Consequently, the core theoretical sections—including the proofs for the Clamp Policy's robustness guarantees, the definition and analysis of the Water-Filling algorithm for randomized settings, and all theorems bridging the ECR to the 1-Wasserstein distance—are entirely missing. This is a fatal flaw. Without the mathematical proofs, it is impossible to verify the soundness of the theoretical claims or assess whether the algorithms correctly satisfy the strict adversarial constraints required in competitive analysis.

## Experimental Rigor
The abstract and introduction make specific empirical claims, noting that the proposed framework "improves consistency by significantly over existing point-prediction baselines" and yields "roughly 5-20%" performance gains. However, there is zero available evidence to support these claims because the experimental section (Section 6) is missing from the truncated manuscript.

Even evaluating the experimental design purely from the introductory descriptions reveals substantial evidence gaps. First, the evaluation relies entirely on synthetic data (Gaussian, geometric, and bi-modal distributions). While synthetic distributions are useful for checking boundary conditions and validating exact analytical bounds, they do not sufficiently prove the practical utility of the framework. Real-world usage durations rarely follow clean synthetic curves. To prove that distributional advice is practically superior to point advice, the algorithms must be benchmarked on real-world datasets, such as public cloud VM lifetime traces (e.g., Azure or Google Cloud traces) or job duration logs. 

Second, the manuscript asserts that the method performs "significantly over" the baselines but provides no indication of statistical rigor. The term "significantly" implies statistical significance, yet there is no mention of independent sampling trajectories, empirical variance, confidence intervals, or formal hypothesis tests (e.g., paired t-tests) used to validate the 5-20% gains.

Finally, the systems-level cost of distributional advice is ignored in the experimental framing. In a deployed system, generating a full distributional forecast via a modern machine learning model requires substantially more compute and memory overhead than outputting a single point prediction. An empirically rigorous evaluation must account for this end-to-end latency and resource overhead to ensure that the theoretical competitive ratio gains are not entirely offset by the cost of inference.
