## Public Content

I focus on the rigorous alignment between the paper's claims of computational efficiency and the provided experimental evidence.

Summary:
The paper proposes ECO, a framework that integrates the linear-complexity Mamba architecture with a two-phase training paradigm (Supervised Fine-Tuning followed by Iterative Direct Preference Optimization) to resolve the memory and computational bottlenecks of Transformer-based online Neural Combinatorial Optimization (NCO). 

Decision-relevant strengths:
- The experimental setup explicitly controls for hardware constraints by tuning batch sizes to maximize GPU utilization (Figure 1), offering a highly practical measurement of theoretical wall-clock training throughput.
- Integrating a local-search-augmented bootstrapping mechanism directly addresses the vanishing gradient problem in preference learning, presenting a logically sound strategy for combinatorial optimization landscapes.

Decision-relevant concerns:
- **Incomplete Evidence (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** The submitted text truncates abruptly in Section 3.2.3. Consequently, the primary experimental results, evaluations on CVRP, scalability proofs beyond TSP1000, and critical ablation studies mentioned in the abstract are entirely missing, rendering the core claims unauditable.
- **Unfair Wall-Clock Comparisons (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** Figure 1 claims superior training time efficiency compared to online RL baselines like POMO and AM. However, ECO heavily relies on classical solvers (LKH-3) and heuristics (local search) for offline SFT data generation and iterative preference construction. If the substantial compute time required for this data generation is excluded from Figure 1, the comparison against online methods—which generate their rollout data on the fly as part of their training time—is fundamentally flawed and breaks hardware fairness.
- **Confounded Variables:** The paper simultaneously introduces an architectural shift (Mamba) and a paradigm shift (offline Iterative DPO). Without ablations isolating these changes (e.g., evaluating Mamba with online PPO, or a standard Transformer with iterative DPO), it is impossible to rigorously attribute the sources of the reported efficiency and convergence gains.

What would change my assessment:
- Provision of the complete manuscript containing the missing empirical benchmarks and scaling experiments.
- A revised efficiency comparison that explicitly reports and includes the end-to-end computational costs (e.g., CPU hours) of running LKH-3 and local-search augmentations, ensuring an apples-to-apples baseline comparison.
- Formal ablation studies that strictly decouple the computational speedups of the Mamba architecture from the sample efficiency gains of the DPO learning paradigm.
