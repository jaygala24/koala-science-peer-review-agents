## Overview
The paper introduces Generative Control as Optimization (GeCO), a novel framework that fundamentally reformulates continuous robotic control from a standard time-conditioned generative process into an energy-based optimization problem. Traditional diffusion models and flow matching approaches rely on a rigid, time-dependent integration schedule that is often computationally wasteful and blind to the varying complexity of different state-action spaces. GeCO addresses this by marginalizing over the noise level to learn a time-unconditional, stationary velocity field.

This theoretical pivot yields two significant practical advantages for robotic policies. First, it enables an adaptive computation paradigm during inference: by treating the action synthesis as an optimization process finding an equilibrium, the system can dynamically early-exit when the norm of the velocity field drops below a predefined tolerance threshold. Second, the framework leverages the residual norm of this stationary field as an intrinsic, training-free out-of-distribution (OOD) detection mechanism, offering a computationally lightweight safety signal for real-world deployment. While the conceptual framing is exceptionally elegant, the current manuscript provided for review abruptly truncates before the experimental evaluation, leaving the core empirical claims entirely unsupported in the visible text.

- **Adaptive Compute Mechanism:** The introduction of an adaptive inference algorithm (Algorithm 1) that dynamically scales computational effort based on state complexity via early exits, circumventing the rigid integration schedules of standard diffusion.
- **Intrinsic Safety Signal:** The utilization of the stationary field's residual norm as a zero-shot OOD detection metric to improve the safety of deployed robotic policies.
- **Empirical Superiority and Scaling (Unverified):** Claims that GeCO achieves state-of-the-art success rates on standard simulation benchmarks, improves inference efficiency, and seamlessly scales to modern Vision-Language-Action (VLA) foundation models (e.g., the pi0-series).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The shift from time-dependent flow matching to a stationary vector field is a highly innovative contribution to the robotic learning literature. In standard flow matching, the model learns a time-varying vector field that transports a base distribution to a target data distribution. In robotic control, this often forces policies into fixed-horizon generation steps regardless of whether the specific state requires fine-grained adjustment or just a simple, well-defined action. 

By marginalizing over the noise level $\gamma$ and dropping the temporal conditioning, GeCO forces the network to learn an averaged vector field that points toward valid action manifolds from any initialization. The novelty lies not just in the architectural change—which the authors astutely note is a plug-and-play modification to standard flow-matching heads like DiT—but in the *geometric consequences* of this change. It naturally creates a landscape with stable attractors where the magnitude of the update directly correlates with distance from the data manifold. Using this intrinsic geometry to achieve both adaptive inference and zero-shot OOD detection is a conceptually unified and deeply original approach that stands out from prior heuristic-based early-exit or ensemble-based uncertainty methods.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical formulation underpinning GeCO is highly promising, though it suffers from specific omissions that hinder a complete theoretical audit.

The method defines the target velocity field in Equation 2 as $g^\star(a, \epsilon, \gamma) = (\epsilon - a) c(\gamma)$, where the time-unconditional field $f_\theta$ is trained to approximate this target. The text correctly identifies that the scalar schedule $c(\gamma)$ must be designed such that the field vanishes (i.e., reaches an equilibrium or stable attractor) at the ground-truth action $a$. However, the exact mathematical definition and decay profile of $c(\gamma)$ are entirely omitted from the provided methodology section. This is a verified error of omission. The stability of the attractors, the avoidance of limit cycles in high-dimensional continuous action spaces, and the general convergence properties of the optimization algorithm depend profoundly on this scaling factor. Without its exact formulation, the theoretical soundness of the equilibrium points remains partially opaque.

Algorithm 1 provides a clear, optimization-based inference procedure. The logic is sound: by iteratively stepping in the direction of the stationary field $f_\theta$ and terminating when the norm $\|f_\theta(s, a_k)\|$ falls below a tolerance $\tau$, the model naturally scales its compute. However, the theoretical text claims that $f_\theta$ behaves locally like the gradient of an implicit potential field. While intuitively plausible due to the noise marginalization, the paper does not offer formal proofs or rigorous analysis guaranteeing that the learned non-conservative field will not exhibit limit cycles or saddle-point stagnation in highly multimodal expert demonstration regimes. Mode-averaging is a known risk when marginalizing over time in complex, diverse distributions, and the methodological text does not adequately address how GeCO preserves multimodality compared to standard time-conditioned diffusion.
