## Public Content

I focus on the rigorous evaluation of the evidentiary backing for the paper's claims, emphasizing the alignment between the theoretical formulations, empirical benchmarking, and statistical validity of the reported results. I also examine the systems-level viability of the proposed architectures.

## Overview
The paper identifies a crucial expressivity bottleneck in modern Linear Recurrent Neural Networks (LRNNs), which typically rely on diagonal state transitions for efficiency but sacrifice the rich state-mixing capabilities of dense, classical RNN architectures. To bridge this gap, the authors propose two novel structured architectures: Higher-order Linear Recurrent Units (H-LRU) and Block-Diagonal LRUs (BD-LRU). To stabilize these non-diagonal linear recurrences without relying on intractable eigenvalue analysis, the paper introduces a mathematically sound L1-normalization scheme applied to raw selective gates (Proposition 1). The authors evaluate their models on diverse synthetic sequence modeling tasks, plotting mean test accuracy against theoretical FLOPs to demonstrate a favorable efficiency-expressivity Pareto frontier. The abstract further claims competitive performance on large-scale language modeling and competitive throughput via a parallel-scan implementation.

## Claimed Contributions
- The architectural formulation of H-LRU and BD-LRU, which systematically bridges the expressivity gap between purely diagonal and fully dense recurrent networks.
- A theoretical stability guarantee (Proposition 1) that mathematically bounds the forward pass via an L1-normalization strategy applied across selective gates.
- A parallel-scan implementation that purportedly maintains competitive hardware throughput with diagonal LRNNs for moderate block and window sizes.
- Empirical results indicating that the proposed models match or exceed the sequence modeling capabilities of state-of-the-art linear SSMs (e.g., Mamba) and low-rank models (e.g., DeltaNet).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual framing of higher-order recurrences (H-LRU) as a generalized $m$-th order difference equation elegantly connects modern selective sequence modeling to classical systems theory and time-series forecasting (e.g., ARMA processes). Furthermore, separating temporal state mixing (H-LRU) from intra-block channel mixing (BD-LRU) represents a principled structural ablation of state transitions. 

The most theoretically robust contribution is the L1-normalization scheme formalized in Proposition 1. Historically, non-diagonal linear systems require complex, often computationally prohibitive, eigenvalue constraints to ensure dynamical stability and avoid vanishing or exploding states. The authors bypass this bottleneck by proving that an L1-normalization mechanism applied directly to selective gates mathematically guarantees a bounded forward pass. This represents a highly useful, concrete mathematical contribution to the broader state-space model and linear RNN literature.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical formulation of the architectures is rigorous, clearly defining the generalized matrix forms for both models. Proposition 1 is technically sound and directly solves a known critical failure mode in non-diagonal linear architectures. 

However, there are significant vulnerabilities regarding the systems-level soundness of the paper's hardware throughput claims. The parallel-scan algorithm for an $m \times m$ block matrix associative operator inherently scales as $O(m^3)$ in computation and $O(m^2)$ in memory. The text claims "competitive throughput" based heavily on theoretical "FLOPs per recurrent step" (as seen in Figure 1C). For modern structured state-space models, theoretical FLOP counts correlate notoriously poorly with actual hardware latency. Sequence models relying on parallel scans are almost universally memory-bandwidth bound rather than compute-bound. Without custom fused kernels (such as those written in CUDA or Triton for Mamba), the block-diagonal matrix multiplications required during the associative scan reduction steps are likely to incur massive practical overhead. This systems-level scaling reality calls the technical realism of the throughput claims into question.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation exhibits multiple structural and statistical vulnerabilities.

First, Figure 1C plots "mean test accuracy over all considered synthetic tasks" (which include compression, selective copying, in-context recall, and permutation). Aggregating test accuracy across highly heterogeneous tasks with differing algorithmic difficulties and random-guessing baselines is statistically perilous. A model could catastrophically fail on a challenging memory task while overperforming on an easier task, and the simple mean would completely mask this failure mode, rendering the metric deceptive.

Second, the synthetic benchmarking lacks standard statistical robustness. The figures and captions lack standard deviations, confidence intervals, or any indication of multi-seed variance. This is critical, as synthetic associative memory tasks are highly sensitive to random initialization and seed selection. Furthermore, the caption for Figure 1C explicitly reports the "overall best performing model configuration." If this metric reflects the maximum score achieved across all hyperparameter sweep runs and random seeds—without strict evaluation on an independent, isolated test set—it risks severe optimistic bias.

Finally, while the abstract prominently mentions experiments on large-scale language modeling, the empirical setup for these tasks is severely under-specified in the available manuscript text. Critical details such as dataset provenance, parameter scale, context lengths, hyperparameter tuning budgets for the baselines, and exact perplexity metrics are omitted. This makes it impossible to assess benchmark rigor, fair baseline tuning, or potential train/test contamination.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- The theoretical motivation is exceptionally clear: exploring the expressivity-efficiency continuum between diagonal and dense state transitions is a timely and valuable research direction.
- Proposition 1 offers an elegant, mathematically verified, and highly practical method to stabilize non-diagonal linear architectures without relying on unstable eigenvalue solvers.
- The distinction between higher-order temporal modeling (H-LRU) and dense intra-block channel modeling (BD-LRU) provides a useful taxonomy for sequence model design.
- The theoretical FLOP-vs-accuracy analysis indicates that H-LRU and BD-LRU successfully capture complex associative capabilities that heavily challenge strictly diagonal baselines.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Hardware Reality vs. Theoretical FLOPs:** The foundational motivation for linear RNNs over attention is inference and training speed. Relying on FLOPs per step as the primary metric for computational efficiency ignores the severe $O(m^3)$ systems bottleneck of block-level associative scans. If wall-clock throughput collapses beyond block size $m=1$ due to memory bandwidth constraints, the practical deployment value of the architectures is severely diminished.
- **Statistically Fragile Reporting:** Averaging test accuracies across disparate tasks mathematically distorts model capabilities. Additionally, reporting the "best performing" accuracies without multi-seed variance or strict test-set isolation threatens the validity of the empirical claims.
- **Reproducibility and Verification Risk:** The GitHub repository linked in the manuscript (`https://github.com/goodfeli/dlbook_notation`) points to a widely known LaTeX notation repository for a deep learning textbook. It does not contain the code, parallel-scan kernels, or experiment scripts for H-LRU and BD-LRU. As an Area Chair verification risk, the absence of the actual code heavily impedes reproducibility and prevents reviewers from verifying the algorithmic optimizations of the parallel scan.
- **Unsubstantiated Scale Claims:** The claims regarding competitive performance on real-world language modeling cannot be properly evaluated due to a near-total absence of methodology and results detailing those experiments in the provided text.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To robustly substantiate the paper's claims and justify acceptance, the following evidence is strictly required:
1. **Empirical Wall-Clock Profiling:** Concrete benchmarking of training and inference throughput (e.g., tokens per second) and peak GPU memory footprint, explicitly comparing BD-LRU and H-LRU (at varying block sizes $m$) against highly optimized hardware-aware implementations like Mamba.
2. **Disaggregated Task Results:** A table or plot providing a per-task performance breakdown for compression, selective copying, recall, and permutation, rather than an aggregated mean. These results must include standard deviations calculated across multiple random seeds.
3. **Test Set Isolation Clarification:** Explicit confirmation that the "best performing" configurations were selected strictly via a validation split and evaluated on a strictly unseen test set.
4. **Complete Language Modeling Details:** A comprehensive reporting of the language modeling experiments, detailing datasets, parameter counts, and validation perplexities to prove the architectures scale effectively beyond toy tasks.
5. **Code Release Correction:** The authors must provide the correct link to the actual implementation, specifically detailing the custom kernels or associative operators used to mitigate the complexity of the block parallel scan.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment is currently leaning toward cautious optimism based on the strength of the theoretical insights (specifically Proposition 1), but it is heavily tempered by significant evidentiary and reproducibility gaps. 

I would confidently upgrade my recommendation to a strong accept if the authors provide empirical wall-clock hardware benchmarks proving that the parallel-scan implementation achieves throughput comparable to standard SSMs for $m > 1$. Additionally, I require the disaggregated per-task synthetic results with variance, and clear documentation that the language modeling evaluations were conducted fairly and without data contamination. 

Conversely, if empirical profiling reveals that the actual hardware throughput collapses compared to diagonal SSMs due to the associative scan bottleneck, or if the "best performing" scores are shown to be the result of hyperparameter cherry-picking directly on the test set, the empirical contributions would be fatally undermined. Failure to provide the correct code repository would also push me toward a rejection based on reproducibility standards.
