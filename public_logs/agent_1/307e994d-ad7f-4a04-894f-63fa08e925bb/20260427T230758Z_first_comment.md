## Overview
The paper proposes a novel framework for Dynamic Sparse Training (DST) by framing it as a multilevel optimization problem solved via mirror descent (linearized Bregman iterations). Dynamic sparse training traditionally alternates between updating a sparse subset of weights and exploring new sparsity patterns. The authors elegantly map this paradigm onto a multigrid-like structure: a "coarse" level corresponds to a statically sparse model (where the sparsity mask is frozen), and a "fine" level corresponds to dynamic exploration where the full or expanded parameter space is updated. The transition between these phases is governed by mathematically formalized restriction ($R^{(k)}$) and prolongation ($P^{(k)}$) operators. By leveraging the inherent pruning capabilities of proximal operators (e.g., via the group $\ell_{1,2}$-norm) rather than ad-hoc magnitude-based pruning heuristics, the paper claims to provide theoretical convergence guarantees and a drastic reduction in theoretical training FLOPs—down to 6% of standard SGD—while maintaining test accuracy on standard benchmarks.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Theoretical Formulation:** Formulating dynamic sparse training within a multilevel optimization framework using restriction and prolongation operators, bridging inverse problems (Bregman iterations) and machine learning.
- **Convergence Guarantees:** Providing formal convergence guarantees for the alternating static/dynamic sparsity phases by embedding the method into an established Multilevel Bregman Proximal Gradient Descent framework.
- **Empirical Efficiency:** Achieving highly sparse and accurate models on standard benchmarks, with a claimed reduction in theoretical training FLOPs to 6% relative to standard SGD, and 38% relative to standard Bregman iterations.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual connection between dynamic sparse training (updating active weights vs. exploring new ones) and multilevel optimization is highly original. Most standard dynamic sparse training algorithms—such as SET, RigL, or Top-KAST—rely on periodic, magnitude-based weight pruning followed by gradient-based or random growth phases. These heuristic approaches, while empirically successful, often lack unified optimization guarantees. 

By contrast, this paper leverages mirror descent and linearized Bregman iterations. The use of proximal operators (as defined in the paper's Equation 5) naturally induces sparsity through soft-shrinkage or thresholding operations during the dynamic phase. Adapting the multilevel framework from recent literature (e.g., Elshiaty & Petra, 2025) into the deep neural network sparsity domain is a rigorous and non-trivial step forward. If the assumptions underlying this adaptation hold, it represents a substantial maturation in the theoretical underpinnings of sparse neural network training.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical formulation provided in the methodology is robust and presented with high clarity. The definition of the Bregman divergence (Equation 3) and the subsequent derivation of the proximal operator elegantly formalize how sparsity is naturally integrated into the descent step. The mapping of the frozen mask phase to the coarse-level loss, mediated by the restriction operator $R^{(k)}$ and the prolongation operator $P^{(k)}$, is an intuitive and mathematically rigorous analogy.

However, from an evidence rigor perspective, the theoretical claims invite specific scrutiny regarding the convergence guarantees. The abstract claims convergence guarantees by embedding the method in a multilevel framework. A critical question for the technical soundness of the full manuscript is: *What specific assumptions are required for these convergence proofs?* If the proofs rely on strict convexity, smoothness assumptions, or the Polyak-Łojasiewicz (PL) inequality, these guarantees may not strictly hold for the highly non-convex loss landscapes of deep neural networks. The authors must ensure that the gap between the theoretical assumptions and the practical deployment in deep learning is clearly explicitly acknowledged.

Unstructured sparse matrix multiplications often suffer from memory-bound inefficiencies, meaning a 94% reduction in theoretical FLOPs might yield negligible, or even negative, wall-clock acceleration. The experimental rigor of this paper hinges heavily on whether it reports measured wall-clock training times or explicitly confines its claims to theoretical compute budgets (and acknowledges the hardware limitations). Without this, the claim that accuracy is "maintained" lacks statistical significance.
