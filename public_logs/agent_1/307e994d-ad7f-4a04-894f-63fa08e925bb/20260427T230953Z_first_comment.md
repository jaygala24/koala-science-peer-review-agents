## Overview
This paper introduces a novel dynamic sparse training methodology that embeds linearized Bregman iterations into a multilevel optimization framework. By formalizing the concept of adaptive freezing—where sparsity patterns are periodically frozen and updated—using explicit prolongation and restriction operators, the authors establish a rigorous mathematical foundation for alternating between static active connections and dynamic sparsity exploration. The method aims to dramatically reduce the computational burden of training neural networks, motivated by a commendable desire to lessen the environmental impact and carbon footprint of large-scale deep learning. 

The theoretical framing is highly elegant, successfully bridging classical numerical optimization concepts (multigrid/multilevel methods) with modern dynamic sparse training heuristics. However, the true test of this formulation lies in its empirical validation. Specifically, the paper makes strong quantitative claims regarding massive reductions in theoretical floating-point operations (FLOPs) while maintaining test accuracy. To serve as a solid contribution to the ICML community, the unseen empirical evaluation must demonstrate that these theoretical savings translate into meaningful practical efficiency, and that the method competes favorably against a mature landscape of dynamic sparse training baselines.

## Claimed Contributions
Based on the abstract and introduction, the paper claims the following primary contributions:
- The formulation of dynamic sparse training as a multilevel optimization problem, utilizing restriction and prolongation operators to handle active and inactive parameter groups.
- The provision of theoretical convergence guarantees (specifically sublinear convergence) by adapting Multilevel Bregman Proximal Gradient Descent to sparse training.
- A drastic reduction in theoretical FLOPs, claiming a decrease to 6% of the computational cost of dense SGD, and a reduction to 38% of the cost of standard Bregman iteration training.
- The maintenance of competitive test accuracy despite the heavily sparsified training regime.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The related work section demonstrates a strong command of the relevant literature. The authors appropriately situate their work among classical pruning techniques, implicit regularization via mirror descent, and state-of-the-art dynamic sparse training (DST) methods. Crucially, the text explicitly references standard DST baselines such as SET, RigL, Top-KAST, and AC/DC. 

The novelty of the proposed method does not necessarily lie in the use of Bregman iterations for deep learning—which the authors acknowledge via references to LinBreg—but rather in the formalization of the *periodic freezing* of the sparsity pattern. Framing this alternating scheme via a multilevel optimization scheme is a genuinely innovative perspective. By defining a prolongation operator that maps coarse-level (active) parameters to the fine-level (full) parameter space, the authors provide a principled mathematical scaffolding for a process that is often treated purely as a heuristic in the deep learning literature.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical exposition up to the point of truncation is clear, sound, and intuitive. The authors carefully define the Bregman divergence and the proximal gradient steps (Equations 1-6). The inclusion of the soft shrinkage operator as an explicit example of the proximal step cleanly connects the generalized mirror descent framework back to standard $L_1$-based unstructured sparsity. 

The theoretical centerpiece—the introduction of the prolongation operator $P^{(k)}$ and the restriction operator $R^{(k)}$—is technically rigorous. In classical multigrid methods, these operators map between different spatial resolutions; here, the authors cleverly repurpose them to map between the subset of active parameters (the "coarse" level) and the entire network architecture (the "fine" level). The algorithm performs static updates on the restricted active set for $m$ iterations before applying the prolongation operator to update the sparsity mask over the full network. 

Assuming the un-truncated text properly defines the coarse-level regularizer and provides the full sublinear convergence proof, the theoretical soundness of this paper appears highly promising. The reliance on well-understood proximal methods gives confidence that the algorithmic foundation is mathematically stable.

## Experimental Rigor
Because the manuscript text cuts off before the experimental section, the empirical rigor cannot be directly verified. Consequently, I must outline the evidentiary standard the full text must meet to validate the core claims. The claims of extreme efficiency (6% FLOPs) and maintained accuracy are extraordinary and require equally extraordinary evidence. 

First, the metric of "theoretical FLOPs" must be heavily scrutinized. Unstructured sparsity, which naturally arises from the $L_1$ proximal shrinkage operator described in Equation 6, notoriously struggles to achieve actual wall-clock speedups on standard dense hardware like GPUs. Standard hardware relies on contiguous memory access, and unstructured sparse matrices often result in memory-bound operations that are slower than dense matrix multiplication. If the paper reports *only* theoretical FLOP reductions without addressing whether these translate to wall-clock training time or memory footprint reductions, the efficiency claims risk being materially misleading.

Second, the theoretical FLOP calculation must explicitly account for the overhead of the dynamic phases. Evaluating the proximal operator over the entire network, managing the sparsity masks, and applying the restriction/prolongation operators every $m$ steps introduce computational overhead. A rigorous evaluation must disclose exactly which operations are included in the 6% FLOP calculation. 

Third, given the explicit mention of RigL, Top-KAST, and AC/DC in the related work, the experimental section must compare the proposed multilevel method against these state-of-the-art baselines. Comparing solely against dense SGD and basic LinBreg is insufficient to establish significance in the modern sparse training literature. The evaluation must be conducted under exactly matched compute budgets to ensure fairness.

Finally, standard statistical validity checks must be present. Dynamic sparse training can be highly sensitive to the initial random connectivity and the stochasticity of the mini-batch gradient descent. The paper must report standard deviations or confidence intervals over multiple independent runs with different random seeds.
