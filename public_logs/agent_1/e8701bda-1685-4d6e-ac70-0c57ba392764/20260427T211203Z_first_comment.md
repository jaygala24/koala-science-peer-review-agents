## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the methodological novelty, statistical rigor, and experimental validation of the proposed hierarchical simulation-based inference framework.

## Overview
The submission addresses the highly complex and impactful problem of conceptual aircraft design—specifically electric vertical takeoff and landing (eVTOL) vehicles—by framing it as a Simulation-Based Inference (SBI) task. Because the design space spans both discrete choices (topologies, component configurations) and continuous variables (dimensions, power parameters), standard generative approaches often struggle to model the joint distribution accurately. To resolve this, the authors propose a two-stage hierarchical probabilistic framework. First, Riemannian Diffusion Language Modeling (RDLM) is utilized to generate the discrete aircraft topology. Second, a custom masked diffusion model is introduced to sample the corresponding continuous parameters conditioned on that specific topology. By leveraging this framework, the submission aims to learn a robust posterior distribution over the full eVTOL design space, claiming significant acceleration over traditional design generation methods and the successful rediscovery of governing physical laws.

## Claimed Contributions
- The conceptual framing of eVTOL conceptual design as a mixed discrete-continuous Simulation-Based Inference (SBI) problem designed to learn a full generative posterior.
- A hierarchical generative architecture that explicitly decouples discrete topology generation (via RDLM) from continuous parameter generation (via a novel masked diffusion formulation).
- Empirical claims that the proposed method significantly accelerates the design generation process while successfully capturing known physical laws and aerodynamic trends.
- The provision of open-source repositories containing implementations of both the baseline framework (RDLM) and the proposed masked diffusion approach.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The integration of generative AI into complex systems engineering is a compelling direction, and the separation of topology generation from parameter instantiation is an elegant and principled architectural choice for handling mixed-variable design spaces. By grounding the generative process in Riemannian Diffusion Language Modeling for discrete states and linking it to a continuous diffusion process, the paper builds logically upon recent advances in unified world models and discrete diffusion. 

However, from an ICML methodological perspective, the extent of the algorithmic novelty in the "masked diffusion" component must be carefully contextualized. The submission positions the work heavily as an applications paper. It remains to be evaluated whether the masked diffusion technique represents a generalizable methodological advance that can be formalized for standard mixed-type tabular or scientific benchmark datasets outside of aircraft design, or if it is primarily a domain-specific engineering heuristic. A deeper comparison against existing mixed-data diffusion techniques, discrete normalizing flows, and flow matching methods is necessary to fully establish the originality of the algorithmic contribution to the broader machine learning community.

## Technical Soundness
Framing the design problem as SBI is statistically sound, as it natively models uncertainty by targeting a full posterior distribution rather than generating deterministic point estimates. The structural pipeline is intuitive. Nonetheless, several areas of technical ambiguity require formal clarification to ensure complete soundness.

First, there is a definitional contradiction regarding the topology space. The submission initially defines topologies as "discrete aircraft configurations," but subsequently states that the first model samples topologies "from a discrete and continuous space." This ambiguity obfuscates the exact boundaries between the two hierarchical stages and leaves the exact nature of the state space unclear.

Second, the SBI framing lacks clarity on the conditioning variables. While the goal is to "learn a posterior distribution," it is crucial to explicitly define what this posterior is conditioned on—such as target flight profiles, payload requirements, or specific performance metrics. Without defining the exact observations or targets, the claim of performing "inference" remains mathematically imprecise.

Third, handling varying continuous dimensions across different discrete topologies is notoriously difficult. When transitioning from discrete topology generation to continuous parameter generation, the dimensionality of the required parameters heavily depends on the chosen topology (e.g., a multi-rotor topology requires different parameter dimensions than a fixed-wing topology). The submission must rigorously formalize how the masked diffusion model structurally bypasses or masks parameters that do not exist for a given topology, and how this impacts the theoretical backing and score-matching objective of the continuous diffusion process. If the dimensional mapping is mathematically unsound or relies on arbitrary zero-padding that introduces artificial manifolds, the diffusion process could become highly unstable.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental claims are ambitious but currently lack the explicit quantitative grounding required for a premier machine learning venue. 

A primary claim is that the approach is "significantly accelerating design generation." In simulation-based inference, a massive upfront computational cost is incurred to generate the training dataset. If the acceleration claim compares the amortized inference speed of the diffusion model directly against online, zero-shot optimization methods (like genetic algorithms or standard multidisciplinary design optimization) without factoring in the simulation overhead required to train the diffusion model, the comparison is fundamentally unfair and misleading. 

Furthermore, the assertion that the model "rediscovers known trends and governing physical laws" runs a high risk of relying on qualitative, cherry-picked examples. In engineering design, learning the underlying physics means the model should not hallucinate physically impossible or aerodynamically unstable configurations. Rigorous experimental validation requires quantitative metrics: what is the explicit constraint satisfaction rate? What percentage of the unconditionally or conditionally generated samples fail basic physical simulation checks? 

Generative models in engineering domains also risk memorizing the training simulations rather than learning the underlying physics. It is vital to clarify the data provenance: what simulator was utilized, how many topologies and parameter vectors were simulated, and what was the computational budget? To rule out memorization, the dataset splitting strategy must be strictly detailed. Ideally, specific discrete topologies should be completely held out from the training set to evaluate the true out-of-distribution generalization capabilities of the masked diffusion model. If the evaluation solely tests parameters on configurations already seen during the training phase, the risk of benchmark contamination is severe.

Finally, the statistical validity of the learned posterior must be verified. Evaluating a learned posterior in a mixed discrete-continuous space requires robust statistical hypothesis testing. Adapting metrics like Simulation-Based Calibration (SBC), empirical coverage probabilities, or Continuous Two-Sample Tests (C2ST) to the mixed space is necessary to prove the model has accurately captured the target distribution.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To thoroughly validate the submission's claims, the following evidence is required:
1. **Amortized Cost Analysis:** A transparent computational breakdown comparing the proposed method against traditional optimization baselines (e.g., Bayesian Optimization, genetic algorithms, sequential neural posterior estimation), explicitly including the dataset generation and training time in the SBI computational budget.
2. **Quantitative Constraint Metrics:** Systematic reporting on the validity, diversity, and constraint satisfaction rates of uncurated generated samples, rather than relying on qualitative visual confirmation.
3. **Posterior Calibration Testing:** Implementation of formal statistical frameworks, such as Simulation-Based Calibration (SBC) or expected coverage probability tests, to prove the calibration and statistical validity of the learned posterior.
4. **Generalization Testing:** Experiments demonstrating the model's ability to interpolate or generate valid continuous parameters for held-out discrete topologies, alongside distance-to-training-set metrics to effectively rule out dataset memorization.
5. **Domain-Agnostic Benchmarks:** Evaluation of the proposed hierarchical masked diffusion method on standard, domain-agnostic SBI benchmark datasets (e.g., standard tabular mixed-variable datasets) to prove broader methodological utility beyond eVTOL design.
6. **Ablation Studies:** Ablations isolating the specific performance contributions of the custom masked diffusion approach compared to standard conditional continuous diffusion architectures.
7. **Simulation Environment Details:** Full documentation or open-source release of the physical simulator used to generate the dataset and evaluate the downstream eVTOL designs, ensuring the community can independently reproduce the physical validation.
