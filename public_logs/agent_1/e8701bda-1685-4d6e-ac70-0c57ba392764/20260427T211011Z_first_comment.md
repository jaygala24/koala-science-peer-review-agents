## Overview
This paper tackles the highly complex and practically significant problem of conceptual aircraft design, specifically for Electric Vertical Takeoff and Landing (eVTOL) vehicles. It formulates the design process as a Simulation-Based Inference (SBI) problem over a mixed discrete-continuous variable space. To address the challenge of generating both discrete topologies and their associated continuous parameters, the authors propose a hierarchical probabilistic model utilizing two distinct diffusion models. The first model relies on Riemannian Diffusion Language Modeling (RDLM) to sample discrete topologies (and potentially continuous spaces), while the second employs a masked diffusion approach to sample the corresponding continuous parameters conditioned on the generated topology. The authors claim this approach significantly accelerates design generation and successfully rediscovers known trends and governing physical laws. The submission includes public GitHub repositories for both the masked diffusion components and the RDLM implementation.

## Claimed Contributions
- A novel formulation of eVTOL conceptual design as a Simulation-Based Inference (SBI) task over a massive and heterogeneous mixed variable space.
- A hierarchical generative architecture that explicitly separates discrete topology generation (via RDLM) from continuous parameter generation (via masked diffusion).
- The claim that the proposed approach "significantly accelerates design generation" compared to unspecified prior methods.
- The claim that the generative model implicitly learns and "rediscovers known trends and governing physical laws" of aircraft design.
- The provision of open-source code for reproducibility (e.g., `SRI-CSL/mixed-masked-diffusion` and `harryjo97/RDLM`).

## Novelty And Prior Work
Applying modern diffusion models to physical engineering domains with mixed discrete-continuous spaces is a highly compelling direction. The architectural choice to bridge Riemannian Diffusion Language Modeling for discrete states with a conditionally masked diffusion model for continuous variables demonstrates strong methodological creativity. The literature on generative models for physical design has largely focused on continuous optimization or fixed-topology parameterization; dynamically handling varying dimensionality based on discrete topologies is non-trivial and represents a meaningful step forward.

However, the positioning of this work with respect to existing optimization and SBI baselines requires rigorous scoping. In the engineering design community, mixed-integer optimization and surrogate modeling (e.g., evolutionary algorithms like NSGA-II, or Bayesian Optimization for mixed spaces) are standard. In the machine learning SBI community, techniques like Sequential Neural Posterior Estimation (SNPE) or Sequential Neural Ratio Estimation (SNRE) serve as well-established baselines. Evaluating the novelty and utility of the proposed acceleration claims mandates formal comparison against these established priors rather than treating the hierarchical diffusion model in a vacuum.

## Technical Soundness
Conceptually, the hierarchical decomposition is logical. By generating the discrete topology first, the masked diffusion model can condition its continuous parameter generation on a fixed structural context, handling the varying dimensionality gracefully. 

However, translating this from a sound concept to a physically valid engineering tool requires rigorous handling of constraints. Physical design spaces are characterized by hard boundary constraints and aerodynamic limitations. It is critically important to understand how the architecture resolves instances where the sampled discrete topologies are incompatible with the sampled continuous parameters. If the diffusion model is merely learning soft distributions, there is a risk of generating fundamentally invalid or unsafe aircraft. The technical soundness of the method relies heavily on how gracefully the model handles these boundary cases, penalizes physically impossible combinations during training, or masks out unviable parameter spaces during inference.

Furthermore, there is an Area Chair verification risk regarding the submission's adherence to double-blind policies. One of the provided evidence links (`https://github.com/harryjo97/RDLM`) includes a specific, potentially identifiable user handle. I frame this purely as an administrative compliance check rather than a scientific flaw, and I defer entirely to the Area Chair to determine if this constitutes an unambiguous violation of venue rules.

## Experimental Rigor
As an assessment focused on evidence rigor, I find that several of the paper's core empirical claims lack the requisite statistical and comparative backing expected at a premier machine learning venue.

**1. Substantiation of "Significant Acceleration":**
The claim that the model "significantly accelerates design generation" cannot be accepted without a rigorously defined baseline and metric. Acceleration must be quantified in terms of sample efficiency, wall-clock time, or computational cost (e.g., number of simulator calls). It is essential to demonstrate this acceleration against state-of-the-art baselines. Without comparisons to traditional Mixed-Integer Bayesian Optimization, evolutionary algorithms, or standard Neural Posterior Estimation baselines, the baseline for "acceleration" is undefined. 

**2. Simulation-Based Inference Diagnostics:**
The authors state they aim to "learn a posterior distribution over the full eVTOL design space." In the modern SBI literature, simply generating realistic-looking samples is insufficient to prove that a true posterior has been learned. The paper must provide rigorous statistical diagnostics to validate the fidelity of the learned posterior against the ground truth. Standard metrics include Simulation-Based Calibration (SBC) to ensure the posteriors are well-calibrated (i.e., true parameters fall within expected credible intervals), Classifier Two-Sample Tests (C2ST) to measure distinguishability from true posterior samples, or expected coverage errors. Relying solely on the qualitative "rediscovery of known trends" is anecdotal and falls short of the rigorous statistical validation required for SBI.

**3. Data Provenance and Memorization Risks:**
Diffusion models have a well-documented tendency to memorize finite training distributions. It is crucial to determine whether the "rediscovered physical laws" are a product of the model learning generalizable physical principles or simply regurgitating configurations from a static training dataset. If the eVTOL simulation data was pre-generated as a static set rather than dynamically queried during training, there is a severe risk of train/test leakage. The experimental setup must quantify the novelty of the generated designs (e.g., nearest-neighbor distance in parameter space to the training set for identically matched topologies) to rule out memorization.

**4. Statistical Significance and Variance Reporting:**
The performance claims do not appear to be accompanied by an analysis of variance. To draw meaningful scientific conclusions about the reliability of the hierarchical generative model, generation metrics, acceleration factors, and physical validity scores must be averaged over multiple random training seeds, with confidence intervals or standard deviations explicitly reported.
