## Overview
The paper tackles a highly practical and ubiquitous challenge: class imbalance in Relational Deep Learning (RDL) applied directly to Relational Databases (RDBs). The authors argue that traditional graph imbalanced learning methods, which primarily assume homogeneous graph structures, are ill-suited for the complex, heterogeneous entity graphs derived from RDBs. To address this, the paper proposes Rel-MOSS, an architecture featuring two core modules. First, a relation-wise gating controller (Rel-Gate) is designed to modulate message passing, preventing the structural signals of minority entities from being overwhelmed by majority relations. Second, a relation-guided minority synthesizer (Rel-Syn) is introduced to perform structure-aware synthetic oversampling by generating minority entities based on structural context constraints called "relational signatures." The authors claim to evaluate Rel-MOSS across 12 entity classification datasets, reporting notable improvements in Balanced Accuracy (BAcc) and G-Mean.

## Claimed Contributions
- The identification and formalization of the class imbalance problem specifically within the context of heterogeneous entity graphs derived from Relational Databases (RDL framework).
- The introduction of Rel-Gate, a relation-wise message modulation mechanism intended to selectively filter and balance relational structural signals for minority entities.
- The development of Rel-Syn, a synthetic oversampling technique that utilizes relational signatures to preserve relational consistency when generating synthetic minority entities.
- An extensive empirical evaluation across 12 RDB entity classification benchmarks, claiming average improvements of up to 2.46% and 4.00% in Balanced Accuracy and G-Mean.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The framing of class imbalance explicitly within Relational Deep Learning applied to RDBs is a timely, highly relevant, and relatively under-explored direction. Identifying how heterogeneous multi-modal features (e.g., timestamps, text, numeric columns) interact with diverse relation types in an imbalanced setting is a strong conceptual starting point.

However, the claim that existing graph imbalanced learning methods primarily focus on homogeneous graphs slightly overstates the novelty of the broader problem space. There is existing literature addressing imbalanced node classification in heterogeneous graphs (such as FincGAN, and adaptations of GraphSMOTE or GraphMixup to heterogeneous settings). For the novelty claims to be fully substantiated, the paper must explicitly differentiate how an RDB-derived heterogeneous graph fundamentally differs from general heterogeneous information networks, and it must benchmark against these existing heterogeneous imbalanced graph learning algorithms. If the evaluation strictly compares the proposed method against naive GNNs or homogeneous baseline adaptations without incorporating state-of-the-art heterogeneous imbalance techniques, the claimed novelty of the performance gains would be at risk of being overstated.

## Technical Soundness
The conceptual motivation for the architecture is logical. The preliminary sections establish a clear mapping from standard RDB constructs (primary keys, foreign keys, tables) to heterogeneous entity graphs. Furthermore, Figure 1 offers an intuitive visual explanation of why naive aggregation fails for minority classes in RDB schemas. 

However, several methodological details require precise formalization to ensure technical soundness:
1.  **Mathematical Formalization of Relational Signatures**: The introductory methodology discusses "relational signatures" that dictate how the Rel-Syn module generates synthetic minority features. To be technically sound, the paper must rigorously define how these signatures are formulated, parameterized, and enforced mathematically. Generating synthetic multi-modal RDB features (mixing categorical, textual, and timestamp modalities) while enforcing rigid schema consistency is notoriously difficult. Without explicit mathematical formulations proving how schema constraints (like mandatory foreign key references or cardinality constraints) are preserved during synthesis, the method risks generating structurally invalid out-of-distribution nodes.
2.  **Scalability overhead**: Modulating messages on a per-relation basis (Rel-Gate) and generating synthetic structural subgraphs (Rel-Syn) often introduces severe computational and memory bottlenecks. The theoretical time and space complexity of these operations on massive RDB schemas with numerous tables must be analyzed to justify the method’s applicability to real-world databases.
3.  **Caption Errata**: The caption for the primary architectural diagram (Figure 3) is currently cut off mid-sentence ("the relation-guided minority synthe..."), which should be corrected.

## Experimental Rigor
Given my role, the empirical and statistical validation of this paper is my primary focus. While evaluating on 12 datasets suggests an exceptionally robust sample size, several factors currently jeopardize the empirical rigor of the claims:

1.  **Ambiguous Statistical Claims**: The abstract highlights an "average improvement of up to 2.46%". This phrasing is a statistical oxymoron that risks misleading readers. "Average" implies a mean expectation over the 12 datasets (or over multiple runs), while "up to" implies a maximum bound. The authors must clarify the exact mean improvement over the strongest valid baseline across all datasets. Furthermore, the significance of a ~2% improvement cannot be assessed without reporting the variance (standard deviations) across multiple random seeds and conducting rigorous statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests).
2.  **Metric Selection for Severe Imbalance**: The authors emphasize Balanced Accuracy (BAcc) and G-Mean. While these are standard for moderate imbalance, RDB classification tasks (such as the fake account detection example cited in the introduction) often feature severe imbalance ratios (e.g., <1% minority class). In such scenarios where false positives are highly costly, BAcc and G-Mean can yield overly optimistic assessments. The inclusion of Precision-Recall Area Under the Curve (PR-AUC) or Average Precision (AP) is critical to prove the method's superiority in practical RDB applications.
3.  **Data Split Integrity (Target Leakage)**: Evaluating predictive tasks on relational databases introduces a severe risk of future information leakage if standard random node-level splits are used. In real-world RDBs, predictions must respect strict temporal cutoffs (i.e., you cannot use a user's future interactions to predict their historical account status). It is imperative that the authors explicitly confirm whether the 12 datasets employ rigorous, timestamp-based temporal splits. If random splitting was used, the benchmark results may be invalid due to target leakage. 
4.  **Dataset Provenance**: It is unclear if the 12 datasets are derived from standardized RDL benchmarks (such as RelBench) or if they are custom-curated. Standardized benchmarks are heavily preferred to prevent dataset selection bias and ensure reproducibility.
