## Public Content

I focus on the rigorous evaluation of the theoretical formulation, the validity of the empirical claims, and the structural integrity of the experimental design. 

## Overview
This paper proposes a method for depth compression in large language models by framing transformer block removal as a Constrained Binary Optimization (CBO) problem. Instead of relying on purely local or heuristic metrics to determine block importance, the authors introduce auxiliary binary scaling parameters for each block. By taking a second-order Taylor expansion of the loss with respect to these auxiliary parameters and approximating the Hessian matrix using empirical Fisher Information on a small calibration set, the method explicitly models second-order interactions between transformer blocks. The resulting combinatorial problem is mapped to an Ising model (or Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization, QUBO) and solved to identify optimal and near-optimal ("excited states") block pruning configurations. The pruned networks are then subjected to a short phase of knowledge distillation (KD) retraining to recover performance.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Principled Combinatorial Formulation:** Extending second-order Taylor expansion pruning techniques to the macroscopic level of transformer blocks, reducing the problem to a computationally tractable CBO over a small number of variables (equal to the number of blocks, $N$).
- **Discovery of High-Performing Non-Contiguous Configurations:** Demonstrating empirically that standard heuristics favoring contiguous block removal are suboptimal, and that non-contiguous "low-lying excited states" (near-optimal proxy solutions) often outperform both contiguous baselines and the strict global minimum ("ground state") of the proxy objective.
- **Computational Efficiency:** A highly scalable pipeline where the $N \times N$ Hessian only needs to be computed once using per-sample gradients over a small calibration dataset (e.g., 2048 samples), after which various compression constraints can be rapidly evaluated on standard CPU hardware.
- **Empirical Outperformance:** Claimed state-of-the-art performance retention at high compression rates, citing absolute improvements of up to 6 points on benchmarks like MMLU compared to existing structured pruning methods.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The core conceptual contribution—adapting second-order interaction pruning (akin to Optimal Brain Damage or Combinatorial Brain Surgeon) to the block level—is a mathematically grounded, elegant extension of existing literature. Standard block-dropping methods often rely on greedy layer-by-layer sensitivity analyses or enforce artificial contiguous-block constraints. By mathematically capturing the interactions between distant layers via the Hessian, this work provides a rigorous framework for exploring a broader, structurally diverse pruning space. The method's ability to cleanly generalize to heterogeneous architectures, such as Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models, is a distinct advantage over simpler similarity-based metrics. 

However, the manuscript over-indexes on statistical physics jargon. By framing standard computer science and operations research concepts (QUBO, global/local optima, suboptimal feasible solutions) exclusively through the lens of physics ("Ising model", "fixed magnetization", "ground state", "excited states"), the text risks obfuscating the mathematical simplicity of the approach. Furthermore, the suggestion to utilize quantum annealers is highly overstated; for modern LLMs with $N \le 100$ blocks, a dense QUBO problem is trivially and optimally solved in fractions of a second by standard classical mixed-integer programming solvers (like Gurobi) or simulated annealing. 

## Technical Soundness
The reduction of block pruning to a tractable CBO is theoretically sound, but it relies on two strong approximations that warrant deeper critical analysis:

First, the authors drop the first-order Taylor term (the gradient), assuming $\nabla L(\alpha) \approx 0$ because the base model is well-trained. The text honestly acknowledges that this contrasts with recent prior work (e.g., Rosenberg et al., 2025), which found that including the first-order term improves results. This assumption may break down if the calibration dataset distribution diverges from the original pre-training distribution, or if the model is not perfectly converged. 

Second, the true Hessian is approximated using the uncentered empirical Fisher Information Matrix ($H \approx \frac{1}{m} A^T A$). While computationally convenient, the empirical Fisher is a known theoretically flawed proxy for the true curvature of the loss landscape, especially when the first-order gradients evaluated on the calibration data are non-zero. The authors do not explicitly define the dimensions and aggregation mechanisms of matrix $A$ in the text (e.g., how multi-token sequences are handled—averaged versus summed gradients), which hinders a complete theoretical audit.

Most notably, the observation that the "17th excited state" (a structurally diverse, suboptimal solution under the proxy energy) outperforms the "ground state" (the exact optimum of the proxy energy) on downstream benchmarks is fascinating, but logically it highlights a misalignment in the proxy objective. While the authors frame this as a positive flexibility of the method, it actually indicates that the empirical Fisher Hessian approximation and the assumption of negligible first-order gradients are failing to perfectly rank the true loss landscape. This proxy misalignment should be analyzed as a limitation rather than exclusively as an advantage.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental design evaluates standard models (e.g., Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct) on widely adopted benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K, BBH) using `lm-eval-harness`, which is commendable for standardizing measurements. The computational budget for calibration (2048 samples) and recovery (0.5 to 1 epochs of KD) is clearly reported.

However, the experimental pipeline contains severe methodological risks that threaten the validity of the empirical claims:

1.  **Test-Set Selection Bias:** In the discussion of low-lying excited states, the authors highlight the "17th excited state" for possessing unique structural properties (removing an early block) and outperforming the ground state on all tested benchmarks. It is entirely unclear whether this 17th state was selected *a priori* based purely on its structural novelty, or if multiple states (e.g., states 1 through 20) were systematically evaluated on the test benchmarks, and the 17th was reported post-hoc because it achieved the highest MMLU/GSM8K scores. If the latter is true, this constitutes explicit test-set leakage, artificially inflating the method's performance and invalidating comparisons to zero-shot baselines.
2.  **Benchmark Contamination via OpenHermes-2.5:** The pruned networks undergo Knowledge Distillation using the OpenHermes-2.5 dataset. OpenHermes-2.5 is a massive compilation of synthetic and scraped instructions known to have significant n-gram overlaps with evaluation sets like MMLU, GSM8K, and BBH. If benchmark questions are present in the KD dataset, the teacher model will output correct probability distributions for those specific questions, allowing the pruned student to memorize benchmark solutions during retraining. There is no decontamination analysis provided to rule this out.
3.  **Baseline Fairness:** It is not stated whether the baseline block-removal methods (e.g., BI, SWM) were subjected to the *exact same* Knowledge Distillation retraining pipeline on OpenHermes-2.5. If the baselines were evaluated zero-shot, or retrained under different parameters, the claimed absolute improvements (e.g., "up to 6 points on MMLU") cannot be confidently attributed to the CBO block mask—they may largely be an artifact of the KD recovery phase. 
4.  **Lack of Uncertainty Quantification:** The manuscript lacks error bars, standard deviations, or statistical significance tests. The sensitivity of the chosen block configuration to the random seed used to sample the 2048 calibration examples is unknown.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To validate the methodology, the following evidence is required:
1.  **Selection Protocol:** Explicit clarification of how the 17th excited state was chosen. If multiple states were evaluated, a scatter plot showing their proxy CBO energy versus their downstream benchmark performance on a held-out validation set is necessary.
2.  **Baseline Retraining Parity:** Empirical proof that all baseline block-mask methods were retrained using the identical KD setup (same teacher, same OpenHermes-2.5 subset, same epochs).
3.  **Decontamination Analysis:** An assessment of n-gram overlap between the 2048 calibration samples, the KD subset of OpenHermes-2.5, and the specific test sets (MMLU, GSM8K, BBH) to rule out memorization via the teacher logits.
4.  **Sensitivity Analysis:** Reporting the variance in benchmark performance and block mask overlap across multiple random seeds for the 2048 calibration samples.
5.  **Ablation on First-Order Term:** Given prior work, a small-scale ablation evaluating whether incorporating the first-order Taylor term alters the "ground state" configuration and aligns it better with actual performance.
