## Overview
This paper addresses a persistent and highly relevant challenge in text-driven image manipulation: enabling significant texture modifications while strictly preserving the original geometric structure of the scene. Diffusion-based editing models frequently suffer from structural leakage, where aggressive texture prompts inadvertently alter the underlying object topology or background geometry. To mitigate this, the authors propose a comprehensive pipeline spanning dataset curation, algorithmic fine-tuning, and evaluation. 

The core of the proposed method, TexEditor, is built on a two-stage approach. First, the authors construct a synthetic supervised fine-tuning (SFT) dataset using Blender (TexBlender), which inherently provides perfect structural priors (e.g., depth maps, normals, and masks) for cold-starting the model. Second, to bridge the domain gap between synthetic renders and real-world scenes, the authors introduce StructureNFT, a Reinforcement Learning (RL) based training framework optimized with structure-preserving losses. To validate their approach, the authors evaluate their model (built on a base architecture denoted as "Qwen-Image-Edit-2509") on specialized domain datasets, a general-purpose benchmark (ImgEdit), and a newly introduced real-world texture editing benchmark (TexBench). 

While the dual data-centric and algorithmic approach is conceptually sound and directly targets a known limitation in the field, severe anomalies in the reported baseline comparisons, a lack of transparency regarding RL training stability, and a prominent double-blind policy violation significantly obscure the validity of the scientific claims.

## Claimed Contributions
- **TexBlender:** A high-quality synthetic SFT dataset generated using Blender, designed to provide exact structural ground truth for texture editing tasks.
- **StructureNFT:** An RL-based training methodology incorporating structure-preserving losses to adapt synthetic structural priors to real-world editing scenarios.
- **TexBench:** A new general-purpose, real-world evaluation benchmark aimed at assessing structure-preserving texture modifications.
- **TexEditor:** The final resulting model, which is claimed to consistently outperform strong baselines on existing benchmarks, ImgEdit, and TexBench.
- **Open Science:** A commitment to releasing the code and data to facilitate community reproducibility.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual novelty of this work is strong. Utilizing 3D rendering engines (Blender) to extract perfect geometric proxies for an initial SFT phase is a highly logical step for this subfield. Furthermore, identifying the synthetic-to-real domain gap and explicitly targeting it with an RL-based optimization phase (StructureNFT) represents a mathematically compelling trajectory for diffusion model alignment.

However, a rigorous evaluation of novelty requires contextualization against the current state-of-the-art. The standard trajectory for preserving structure in text-to-image diffusion involves conditioning mechanisms such as ControlNet (using Canny edges, depth, or normals) or instruction-tuned models like InstructPix2Pix and MagicBrush. The manuscript must explicitly differentiate the mathematical formulation of StructureNFT from standard reward-weighted fine-tuning (e.g., DPOk, DDPO) and clarify why standard structural conditioning is insufficient without the proposed RL phase.

## Technical Soundness
The technical foundation relies heavily on the specific formulation of the "structure-preserving losses" integrated into the StructureNFT RL framework. Reinforcement learning applied to image generation is notoriously difficult to constrain; without carefully balanced reward functions, models are prone to reward hacking (e.g., perfectly preserving structure by ignoring the text prompt entirely and outputting the original image, or conversely, maximizing CLIP score by destroying the geometry). 

To be considered technically sound, the paper must provide precise mathematical definitions of these RL reward signals. Are the authors utilizing pixel-space metrics (e.g., L2/L1 on masked regions), perceptual metrics (LPIPS), structural metrics (SSIM), or deep feature matching (e.g., DINOv2 self-similarity)? The abstract omits these crucial details. Furthermore, the decoupling of texture and structure in the TexBench evaluation metrics requires rigorous definition. Standard metrics often struggle to penalize geometric shifts without simultaneously penalizing valid, requested texture changes. The validity of TexBench hinges entirely on how these disentangled metrics are calculated.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental rigor of this submission currently presents several critical red flags that preclude a confident assessment of the method's superiority:

1. **Unconventional Baselines:** The authors claim the method consistently outperforms "strong baselines such as Nano Banana Pro." This is a highly anomalous and non-standard model name within the academic literature for text-guided image editing. It appears to either be an internal project codename, a translation artifact, or a niche placeholder. Claiming state-of-the-art performance by outperforming an unknown or unverified baseline provides zero empirical evidence of the model's actual utility. Rigorous comparison mandates evaluation against established, peer-reviewed models such as InstructPix2Pix, ControlNet-augmented stable diffusion baselines, or recent state-of-the-art editing models (e.g., Emu Edit, MagicBrush).
2. **Opaque Base Model:** The proposed TexEditor is based on "Qwen-Image-Edit-2509." It is unclear if this is a publicly available, open-weight foundational model or an internal, proprietary checkpoint (the "2509" suffix suggests an internal date stamp). If the base model is vastly superior to the base models of the chosen baselines, any observed performance delta cannot be reliably attributed to TexBlender or StructureNFT.
3. **RL Variance and Statistical Significance:** RL-based training (StructureNFT) introduces significant variance. Different random seeds can yield drastically different final checkpoint capabilities. The empirical results must report performance averaged over multiple independent training runs (seeds) with corresponding standard deviations or confidence intervals. A single favorable checkpoint is insufficient to prove algorithmic superiority in an RL context.
4. **Lack of Ablation Detail:** The dual approach requires ablation. To prove the necessity of StructureNFT, the authors must report the performance of the model trained *only* on the TexBlender SFT dataset, compared against the model trained with both TexBlender and StructureNFT.

- **Data-Centric Methodology:** Generating perfect structural priors via Blender (TexBlender) is an excellent, verifiable approach to solving the geometry-leakage problem in editing.
- **Comprehensive Evaluation Scope:** Evaluating the method across specialized datasets, a newly proposed targeted benchmark (TexBench), and a broad-coverage benchmark (ImgEdit) demonstrates a commendable commitment to thorough testing.
- **Reproducibility Commitment:** The explicit promise to release both the code and the newly curated datasets (TexBlender and TexBench) is highly valuable. If executed correctly, this will provide the community with essential tools for future research.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Anonymity and Venue Policy Violation:** The abstract and metadata explicitly provide a GitHub URL (`https://github.com/KlingAIResearch/TexEditor`) that contains the institutional/group name of the authors. Under the strict double-blind policies of venues like ICML, this is a severe de-anonymization risk. This issue must be flagged for Area Chair verification, as it often constitutes grounds for desk rejection independent of scientific merit.
- **Empirical Validity (The Baseline Problem):** As highlighted above, evaluating against "Nano Banana Pro" undermines the credibility of the empirical claims. The scientific community cannot contextualize the performance of TexEditor without standard anchors.
- **Reproducibility of the RL Pipeline:** While code release is promised, RL pipelines are notoriously brittle. If the repository only contains inference scripts or omits the exact hyperparameters, reward formulations, and random seeds used for StructureNFT, the core algorithmic contribution will remain irreproducible. Similarly, if "Qwen-Image-Edit-2509" is a closed model, the community cannot verify the training pipeline.
- **Ethics and Data Provenance:** The new TexBench benchmark relies on "real-world scenes." The submission must clarify the source of these images. Have they been vetted for Personally Identifiable Information (PII), human faces, or copyrighted material? Establishing the ethical provenance of real-world evaluation datasets is a baseline requirement.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To cross the threshold for a confident acceptance, the following evidence is required:
1. **Standard Baseline Comparisons:** Quantitative and qualitative comparisons against recognized, contemporary text-guided editing models (e.g., InstructPix2Pix, ControlNet-based pipelines) using identical base-model capacities where possible.
2. **Ablation Studies:** A clear empirical decoupling of the performance gains attributed to the SFT phase (TexBlender) versus the RL phase (StructureNFT).
3. **Statistical Uncertainty Reporting:** Metrics reporting the variance across multiple random seeds for the StructureNFT RL training phase.
4. **Metric Definitions:** Explicit mathematical definitions of the "structure-preserving losses" used during training and the disentangled evaluation metrics used in TexBench.
5. **Asset Availability:** Confirmation that the repository includes not just the rendered 2D images of TexBlender, but the raw Blender scripts and 3D assets to allow the community to generate diverse structural variants.
