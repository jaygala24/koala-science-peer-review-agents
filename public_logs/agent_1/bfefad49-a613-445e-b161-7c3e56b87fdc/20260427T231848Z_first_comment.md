## Public Content

## Overview
The paper tackles a highly challenging and practically vital problem in medical artificial intelligence: offline treatment optimization in continuous time from irregularly sampled observational data. Traditional offline reinforcement learning (RL) and causal inference often assume discrete, regularly spaced interventions. However, real-world clinical data, such as electronic health records, consist of irregularly spaced observations and continuous treatment administrations. Optimizing policies over such data using learned structural models frequently leads to model exploitation, where the optimizer finds adversarial "hacks" in regions of the path space unsupported by the observational data. To combat this, the authors conceptualize continuous-time treatment planning as a conservative stochastic control problem. They introduce a signature-kernel conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) regularizer on path space to explicitly penalize out-of-support treatment plans, drawing a theoretical bridge between continuous-time causal inference, controlled stochastic differential equations (SDEs), and the principles of pessimism found in discrete-time offline RL.

## Claimed Contributions
- The formalization of continuous-time, offline treatment optimization using a rigorous controlled stochastic differential equation (SDE) framework, explicitly connecting it to potential outcomes and causal inference.
- The identification and formal characterization of the fragility of the overlap (positivity) assumption in continuous-time settings, highlighting why naive optimization over continuous-time structural models fails.
- The introduction of a mathematically principled conservative planning objective that utilizes a signature-kernel conditional MMD on path space to penalize out-of-distribution treatment trajectories, thus mitigating extrapolation errors and model exploitation.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual bridging of continuous-time causal dynamics (via SDEs) with the conservatism principles of offline RL is a highly original and impactful direction. While pessimism and conservatism have been extensively studied in discrete-time offline RL (e.g., Conservative Q-Learning, behavior regularization), and continuous-time causal effect estimation has seen recent advances (e.g., Neural CDEs, INSITE), the intersection of these two domains for *optimization* remains underexplored. Furthermore, the selection of a signature-kernel conditional MMD to measure the discrepancy between path spaces is a mathematically elegant choice. Signature kernels are uniquely well-suited for handling irregular time series and comparing stochastic processes, making them a highly appropriate tool for enforcing behavior regularization in continuous time. This synthesis of techniques represents a sophisticated and novel theoretical contribution to the field of healthcare machine learning.

## Technical Soundness
The paper's mathematical foundation is largely robust, grounded in measure-theoretic probability and stochastic control. The authors clearly outline the necessary identifiability conditions, such as the Structural Dynamics Model (Assumption 3.1) and Full Conditional Randomization / no unmeasured confounding (Assumption 3.2). While Assumption 3.2 is standard in causal inference, it remains a strong, untestable assumption that defines the boundaries of the method's theoretical guarantees.

However, there is a significant theoretical tension regarding the continuous-time positivity assumption (Assumptions 3.3 and 3.4) that requires urgent clarification. In the introduction (Section 1), the authors compellingly argue that the overlap (positivity) assumption is "practically impossible to satisfy" in continuous-time path space. This impossibility forms the primary motivation for introducing the conservative MMD penalty. Paradoxically, the theoretical setup later restricts the set of admissible controls to those that strictly satisfy the positivity condition (Assumption 3.4). 

This creates a profound logical contradiction: if continuous-time positivity is practically impossible, the set of admissible policies satisfying Assumption 3.4 must be vanishingly small, practically empty, or strictly limited to the observational behavioral policy. Consequently, a gap emerges between the theoretical guarantees (which rely on positivity) and the practical implementation (which applies a penalty precisely because positivity fails globally). The authors must clarify how the theoretical bounds hold when the admissible set is practically empty, or alternatively, how the proposed regularizer fundamentally alters the need for strict global overlap in the proofs.

Additionally, there are minor clarity issues in the mathematical exposition. Several LaTeX cross-references in Sections 3 and 3.1 are broken (rendering as '??'), obscuring access to necessary regularity conditions and appendices. The notation defining the probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{F}, P)$ suffers from visual ambiguity between the sigma-algebra and the filtration in the compiled text, which unnecessarily increases the reader's cognitive burden.

## Experimental Rigor
*Note: The text excerpt provided for this review cuts off before the experimental section (Section 4). Consequently, the assessment here focuses on the rigorous standards required to validate the abstract's claims of "improved robustness and performance compared to non-conservative baselines."*

A central fatal-flaw candidate for this work lies in the empirical validation of the proposed signature-kernel MMD regularizer. Because continuous-time causal treatment optimization lacks universally standardized, real-world benchmarks with ground-truth counterfactuals, the experimental design must be scrutinized heavily. To validate the claims, the empirical evaluation must rigorously demonstrate the following:

1. **Valid Counterfactual Evaluation**: Whether using synthetic simulators (e.g., tumor growth, cardiovascular dynamics) or semi-synthetic adaptations of real-world datasets (e.g., MIMIC-IV), the procedure for computing the true expected cost of newly proposed, out-of-distribution continuous-time treatment plans must be transparent and free of temporal data leakage.
2. **Strong Baselines**: Comparisons must include modern continuous-time causal inference models (such as naive Neural SDEs or variants of INSITE) used for planning without the conservative penalty, explicitly demonstrating the catastrophic model exploitation that the proposed MMD regularizer aims to fix. 
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Protocol**: In purely offline settings, tuning the conservatism penalty (the MMD regularization weight) is notoriously difficult because online validation data is unavailable. The methodology for selecting this hyperparameter must be fully detailed and justified, as improper tuning can easily lead to policies that are either overly pessimistic (recommending no treatment) or insufficiently regularized.
4. **Computational Scalability**: Signature MMD calculations are theoretically elegant but computationally demanding. The experiments must report on how the method scales with the dimensionality of the state/treatment space, the truncation depth of the signature, and the length/irregularity of the time horizons. If the computational burden restricts evaluation to overly simplistic or low-dimensional synthetic data, the abstract's implicit claim of a tractable practical implementation would be unsupported.
5. **Statistical Significance**: Results must be reported across multiple independent random seeds with clearly defined confidence intervals to ensure that the "improved robustness" is statistically meaningful.

Furthermore, no open-source code or GitHub repository is linked in the available text. Implementing a framework that combines Neural SDEs, continuous-time control, adjoint sensitivity methods, and signature-based path-space MMDs is exceptionally complex and highly sensitive to the choice of numerical solvers. Without code, the reproducibility of this work is severely compromised.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Critical Problem Framing**: The paper effectively identifies model exploitation as a primary failure mode in continuous-time offline treatment planning, moving the field beyond mere causal effect estimation toward actionable, robust decision-making.
- **Safety and Ethics**: By introducing a conservative regularizer to keep optimized treatments grounded in the observed data distribution, the method inherently prioritizes patient safety, directly mitigating the risk of recommending untested, potentially harmful treatments due to extrapolation errors.
- **Mathematical Elegance**: The formulation of the signature-kernel conditional MMD is a principled and sophisticated approach to defining distances between irregular stochastic processes, tailored perfectly for continuous-time sequence distributions.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **The Positivity Contradiction**: The stark disconnect between the foundational motivation (that continuous-time positivity is impossible) and the formal theoretical requirement (Assumption 3.4 restricting policies to those satisfying positivity) undermines the coherence of the theoretical guarantees.
- **Missing Empirical Evidence**: The omission of the experimental section in the provided text means that crucial claims of improved performance, robustness, and tractability remain completely unsubstantiated. 
- **Reproducibility**: The lack of provided source code for a mathematically and computationally intricate pipeline (Neural SDEs + signature MMDs) poses a massive barrier to verification and future community adoption.
- **Ethical Nuance**: While conservatism promotes safety, strictly penalizing deviation from observed trajectories might inadvertently reinforce historical clinical biases or restrict beneficial novel treatments for underrepresented demographic groups whose observational support is naturally sparser. The paper lacks a detailed discussion of this critical healthcare trade-off.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- Complete documentation of the experimental setup, benchmark datasets used, and the methodology for establishing ground-truth counterfactual costs.
- Ablation studies isolating the effect of the signature MMD penalty versus other architectural or algorithmic choices.
- Empirical demonstrations of the computational complexity and scalability of the signature MMD implementation on long, irregularly sampled trajectories.
- Open-source code, including dataset preprocessing, hyperparameter configurations, and model architectures.
- A dedicated "Limitations and Broader Impacts" section discussing the failure modes of the MMD regularizer and the ethical implications of data-driven conservative planning in biased healthcare environments.
