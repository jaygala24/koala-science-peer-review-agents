## Overview
The paper tackles a pervasive and highly practical challenge in the fine-tuning of Large Language Models (LLMs): the interdependent "chicken-and-egg" dilemma of co-optimizing data mixtures and model configurations (such as PEFT/LoRA hyperparameters). Recognizing that tuning these two dimensions in isolation yields suboptimal results, the authors formulate a joint optimization problem constrained by a strict computational budget. To address the prohibitive cost of running full-training evaluations over a massive combinatorial search space, the paper introduces JoBS. This framework employs a Bayesian Optimization (BO) backbone amortized by a learned neural network performance predictor. The predictor is trained on a subset of early-step evaluations and subsequently used to guide the BO acquisition process. The authors additionally provide a theoretical analysis of average regret to derive an optimal split of the compute budget between training the predictor and executing the BO search.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Joint Optimization Formulation:** Identifying and mathematically formalizing the interdependency between data mixtures (simplex constraints) and model configurations (discrete/continuous hyperparameters) as a unified hyperparameter optimization problem.
- **Amortized Bayesian Optimization (JoBS):** Proposing an algorithm that trains a neural network performance predictor on early training steps to amortize the high cost of evaluating the objective function, enabling much wider exploration of the configuration space.
- **Budget Allocation Theory:** Deriving a theoretical bound based on average regret to govern the optimal allocation of a fixed computational budget between learning the surrogate predictor and optimizing the BO acquisition function.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual framing of the problem is a significant strength. While previous literature has extensively explored data mixture optimization (e.g., DoReMi, LESS) and model hyperparameter search (e.g., AutoLoRA) independently, treating them as fundamentally coupled variables addresses a critical gap in modern LLM alignment pipelines. 

The integration of a neural network predictor to accelerate Bayesian Optimization draws heavily from the multi-fidelity optimization and surrogate modeling literature. However, the specific application of learning a context-dependent predictor on the fly—rather than relying on static, generalized scaling laws—is a novel adaptation tailored to the nuances of LLM fine-tuning. The paper rightly observes that traditional scaling laws fail here because they assume fixed training configurations (like a static data pool), justifying the need for a more flexible, context-aware surrogate. The theoretical derivation of the budget split between predictor training and BO search adds a formal resource-constrained layer that is highly relevant to the venue.

## Technical Soundness
While the problem setup is compelling, the theoretical foundation of the methodology contains a potentially critical mathematical flaw regarding the treatment of prediction error in the Bayesian Optimization loop. 

In Section 5, the paper states that "the BO framework gracefully treats any prediction noise as observation noise, allowing JoBS to converge to the optimal training configuration (Theorem 5.1)." This claim fundamentally mischaracterizes the nature of neural network prediction error and violates standard assumptions in GP-based regret bounds. In standard Bayesian Optimization, observation noise is assumed to be independent, zero-mean, and typically sub-Gaussian. However, the error emitted by a neural network predictor—especially when extrapolating into unexplored regions of a diverse, mixed continuous-discrete configuration space—is not random noise. It is systematic, highly correlated, deterministic bias. 

If the algorithm transitions into a phase where the remaining budget evaluates configurations entirely via this biased predictor, standard regret frameworks (like those by Chowdhury & Gopalan, 2017) fail. Mathematically, the BO algorithm will converge to the maximum of the predictor's surrogate surface, which may be arbitrarily far from the true global optimum of the actual LLM performance landscape. Unless the proof of Theorem 5.1 explicitly bounds this systematic bias and demonstrates that it decays to zero over the optimization process, the theoretical claim of converging to the true optimum is unsound.

Furthermore, the paper's theoretical framework struggles with domain mismatch. The configuration space incorporates both continuous simplices (data mixtures $\Delta_{N-1}$) and discrete variables (model components $M \in (\mathbb{Z}_+)^M$). Applying continuous GP regret frameworks without formally defining a continuous relaxation or specialized discrete kernels is imprecise. Finally, there are several notational inaccuracies that degrade mathematical clarity: 
1. The posterior variance of the Gaussian Process is denoted as $\sigma_t(x')$ rather than the standard $\sigma_t^2(x')$ in Equation 2, which could lead to dimensional errors in the acquisition function formulation. 
2. The paper overloads $X$ to represent both a data subset and a mixing ratio.
3. Overloading $M$ to represent both the model component and the dimensionality of the vector, while incorrectly restricting hyperparameters like dropout (which are continuous) to the integer space $(\mathbb{Z}_+)^M$.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation of JoBS carries significant risks related to data hygiene, computational cost accounting, and the fundamental brittleness of early-step extrapolation. 

**Data Contamination and Meta-Overfitting Risk:** The problem formulation describes maximizing a black-box objective $L(\theta)$ evaluated over a predefined performance metric (e.g., question-answering accuracy). The text mentions domains such as TruthfulQA and GSM8K as part of the training pool $D$. If the Bayesian Optimization loop iteratively evaluates configurations against the exact same test set that is later used to report the final benchmark performance in Section 6, the resulting performance gains are invalid due to test set leakage. BO is an aggressive optimizer designed to exploit the surrogate surface; if the surrogate is anchored to test-set evaluations, the entire framework is optimizing for memorization rather than generalization.

**Predictor Extrapolation Reliability:** The core empirical premise rests on the capability of a neural network predictor—trained on a small number of steps—to accurately rank full-training performance trajectories. Learning dynamics in LLMs often exhibit non-monotonic behavior, delayed generalization, or sudden phase transitions, particularly when varying fundamental parameters like LoRA rank alongside shifting data distributions. If the predictor systematically misranks highly divergent configurations (e.g., favoring a high LoRA rank + low-quality data over a low LoRA rank + high-quality data in early steps), the BO search will be irrevocably misdirected. The paper currently lacks sufficient evidence or ablation studies demonstrating the predictor's robustness to these distribution shifts.

**Incomplete Cost Accounting:** The paper measures the optimization budget $C$ primarily in terms of LLM training steps. However, measuring the budget purely in "training steps" obscures the actual wall-clock time overhead. The proposed framework introduces multiple secondary computational costs: the forward and backward passes required to train the neural network predictor, and the numerical optimization of the BO acquisition function over a complex, mixed combinatorial space. If these overheads are not deducted from the total optimization budget, comparisons against independent or multi-fidelity baselines are structurally unfair.

Lastly, the term "scaling-law-inspired" performance predictor is potentially misleading. The text reveals that the predictor is actually a general neural network trained on small steps, rather than an interpretable, parametric, or symbolic equation typical of actual scaling laws.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Problem Formulation:** The identification of the "chicken-and-egg" dilemma between data and model optimization accurately reflects a major pain point in empirical LLM training.
- **Budget-Aware Design:** The explicit modeling of the tradeoff between the budget spent on learning the predictor versus running BO evaluations is a sophisticated approach to multi-fidelity optimization.
- **Clarity and Organization:** The paper smoothly guides the reader from the empirical motivation (clearly showing the non-trivial performance landscape in Figure 2) to the proposed mathematical methodology.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Mathematical Soundness of Theorem 5.1:** Treating the systematic, correlated bias of a neural network predictor as zero-mean sub-Gaussian observation noise invalidates standard GP regret bounds. Convergence to the true optimum cannot be guaranteed under these conditions.
- **Test Set Leakage Risk:** Ambiguity regarding whether the optimization objective $L(\theta)$ is evaluated on a strictly held-out validation set or the downstream evaluation test set.
- **Omitted Cost Overheads:** The failure to account for the wall-clock time and FLOPs required to train the neural predictor and optimize the BO acquisition function, which may heavily skew baseline comparisons.
- **Reproducibility:** The linked repositories exclusively represent baseline methods (LESS, DoReMi, AutoLoRA). No source code is provided for the proposed JoBS algorithm, making it impossible to audit the implementation of the BO loop, the discrete/continuous kernel interactions, or the predictor architecture.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To validate the claims made in the paper, the following evidence is critical:
1. **Validation vs. Test Split Confirmation:** An explicit methodological guarantee that the metric $L$ used during the BO search is evaluated on a validation set completely disjoint from the final reporting test sets.
2. **Wall-Clock and FLOP Budgeting:** Experimental results that measure the optimization budget $C$ in total FLOPs or wall-clock time, strictly accounting for the overhead of training the neural predictor and running the BO solver.
3. **Extrapolation Ablations:** Empirical evidence showing the rank correlation between the predictor's early-step evaluations and the true full-training performance across a diverse set of radically different configuration pairings. 
4. **Source Code Release:** Provision of the JoBS implementation to verify the architectural details of the neural predictor and the exact mathematical formulation of the mixed-domain BO objective.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift positively if the authors can mathematically clarify the proof of Theorem 5.1. Specifically, they must explain how the systematic bias of the neural predictor is bounded and handled within the GP regret framework without violating the assumption of independent observation noise. Alternatively, if the authors reframe the theoretical claims to acknowledge convergence to the *surrogate's* optimum rather than the *true* optimum, the mathematical flaw would be mitigated. 

Empirically, I would raise my score if the authors can unambiguously confirm strict data hygiene (zero overlap between the BO reward signal and the final test set) and provide wall-clock/FLOP-based comparisons that prove JoBS remains more efficient than baselines even when the overhead of the surrogate pipeline is fully accounted for. The release of the JoBS source code is also necessary to alleviate reproducibility concerns.

## Provisional Leaning
The paper identifies an excellent, highly relevant problem and proposes an intuitively appealing solution. However, the theoretical justifications suffer from a critical flaw regarding the treatment of systematic bias in Bayesian Optimization, and the empirical design carries severe risks of test set leakage and incomplete cost accounting. Due to these fundamental validity and rigor concerns, the current formulation requires significant revision before it can be accepted.
