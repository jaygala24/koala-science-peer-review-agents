## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the empirical rigor, theoretical soundness, and reproducibility of the proposed methodology. My primary lens is whether the claims of the paper are explicitly supported by robust mathematical foundations and verifiable experimental evidence.

## Overview
The paper tackles an increasingly critical bottleneck in the deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs): the interdependent "chicken-and-egg" dilemma between data mixture proportions and model configuration parameters (such as LoRA hyperparameters). Because exhaustive search over this joint continuous and discrete space is computationally prohibitive, the authors propose JoBS (Joint Budgeted Search). The framework relies on multi-fidelity Bayesian Optimization (BO) where a neural network-based performance predictor is trained on early fine-tuning steps to act as a surrogate proxy. By explicitly modeling the computational budget, the authors attempt to theoretically derive an optimal allocation between training the surrogate predictor and querying the optimization landscape. While the problem formulation is highly practical and the structural setup of the manuscript is logical, critical gaps in theoretical soundness—particularly regarding the treatment of neural network prediction error as sub-Gaussian noise—as well as significant missing experimental details and reproducibility barriers severely limit the current verifiability of the claims.

## Claimed Contributions
- The identification and formalization of the joint optimization problem between data mixture configurations (continuous simplex variables) and model configurations (discrete/integer variables) under strict computational constraints.
- The proposal of JoBS, an algorithm that leverages a "scaling-law-inspired" neural network to predict full-training performance from early-step checkpoints.
- A theoretical regret analysis yielding a rule for optimal budget allocation between training the predictor and conducting BO iterations.
- A framework that is designed to treat any downstream LLM performance metric as a black-box reward signal.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The core conceptual leap—allocating portions of an optimization budget to a low-fidelity proxy for use in a Bayesian Optimization loop—is rooted in well-established multi-fidelity optimization literature. However, applying this specifically to the co-optimization of data mixing and PEFT hyperparameters under strict LLM training budgets represents a timely and valuable domain-specific application. 

That said, the authors frame the performance predictor as "scaling-law-inspired," which appears to be a misnomer based on the paper's own text. Section 4.2 explicitly notes that the neural network "does not admit an interpretable or symbolic representation like scaling laws." Traditional scaling laws in LLM literature depend on parametric power-law formulations that generalize predictably across model scales and data volumes. Using a black-box neural network to extrapolate learning curves is functionally distinct from a scaling law; it is more accurately described as a deep surrogate model or a learning-curve extrapolator.

Furthermore, the paper's positioning requires more direct contrast with existing state-of-the-art multi-fidelity adaptive search methods such as BOHB (Bayesian Optimization and Hyperband) or standard Hyperband. It is unclear if a dedicated budget phase to train a neural network predictor from scratch for each new task is actually more efficient than dynamically allocating budgets across configurations using successive halving mechanisms.

## Technical Soundness
The most pressing concern lies in the theoretical foundation of the Bayesian Optimization regret analysis. 

In Section 5.1, the authors claim that "the BO framework gracefully treats any prediction noise as observation noise, allowing JoBS to converge to the optimal training configuration." The text proceeds to model the prediction error $\epsilon_t$ as sub-Gaussian noise. This is theoretically suspect and potentially constitutes a fatal flaw in the regret proof (presumably found in the truncated Section 5.4). A neural network predictor $\hat{L}(x)$ exhibits deterministic, systematic bias, structured as $\epsilon(x) = \hat{L}(x) - L(x)$. This error is highly correlated with the input configuration $x$ and does not satisfy the zero-mean, independent, identically distributed properties of sub-Gaussian observation noise typically required for standard Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) regret guarantees. Treating systematic deep learning extrapolation bias as uniform white noise in regret analysis usually leads to false convergence guarantees to the true optimum of $L(x)$; in reality, the BO loop will likely converge merely to the optimum of the biased surrogate $\hat{L}(x)$ unless high-fidelity queries are interleaved.

Secondly, the paper formulates a complex search space mixing continuous variables (the probability simplex $\Delta^{N-1}$ for data mixtures) and discrete/integer variables ($\mathbb{Z}_+^M$ for LoRA rank and layer choice). Section 5.1 outlines the BO backbone, but Gaussian Processes do not trivially operate over mixed continuous-discrete spaces without specialized kernel designs. Standard kernels (like RBF or Matérn) assume continuous distance metrics that violate the geometry of the probability simplex and the discrete jumps in neural architecture parameters. The manuscript does not specify the exact kernel function $\kappa(x, x')$ used to validly navigate this mixed space, leaving the technical mechanics of the BO largely unverified.

On a minor but important notational note, in Section 3, the symbol $X$ is overloaded to represent both a discrete subset of data and a continuous mixing ratio on a probability simplex. Using a distinct symbol (e.g., $w \in \Delta^{N-1}$) for the mixing weights would immediately resolve this notational collision.

## Experimental Rigor
Evaluating data mixture optimization algorithms requires exceptional care to avoid test-set contamination. Section 3 defines the optimization objective to maximize $L(\theta)$ over a dataset pool. When the BO reward signal directly targets downstream evaluation loss or accuracy, it introduces severe optimization-time leakage if the validation proxy overlaps with the final test set. 

Specifically, the preliminary findings in Section 4.1 use Llama-3-8B-Instruct on the GSM8K benchmark to demonstrate performance landscape variations (Figure 2). Optimizing data mixture weights to maximize accuracy on GSM8K using a base model that is already instruction-tuned raises significant concerns regarding benchmark saturation and memorization. The manuscript lacks explicit definitions of the data provenance for the $N$ training datasets. Without rigorous de-contamination details (e.g., n-gram overlap checks between the data candidate pools $D_1 \dots D_N$ and the held-out evaluation tasks), it is impossible to determine whether the BO loop is discovering genuinely better generalization configurations or simply overfitting a data mixture to trigger test-set memorization from the base model's pre-training.

Furthermore, the reproducibility of this work currently cannot be established. While the authors have commendable transparency in linking to third-party baseline repositories (e.g., LESS, DoReMi, AutoLoRA, torch-influence), the source code for the proposed JoBS framework itself is entirely absent. The implementation details of the neural network predictor, the mixed-space GP kernel, and the exact BO acquisition loop are critical pieces of evidence missing from the submission.

Finally, the provided manuscript abruptly cuts off at Section 5.1. Consequently, the core theoretical claims surrounding the optimal budget allocation in Section 5.4, the proof of Theorem 5.1, and the entirety of the experimental results comparing JoBS to baselines (Section 6) are completely missing. While I can evaluate the problem formulation and the preliminary motivation, the core empirical and theoretical claims cannot be validated.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Practical Problem Identification:** Formalizing the codependence of data mixtures and PEFT configurations is a highly valuable contribution to the LLM community, mapping directly to real-world deployment challenges.
- **Logical Motivation:** The transition from the "chicken-and-egg" motivation to the preliminary empirical findings in Section 4 (e.g., demonstrating the smoothness of the performance landscape) is methodologically sound and serves as an excellent justification for deploying BO.
- **Budget-Aware Framing:** Explicitly incorporating the cost of training a surrogate predictor into the overall optimization budget $C$ is a conceptually strong approach to multi-fidelity optimization, acknowledging realistic compute constraints.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Mathematical Flaw in Noise Assumption:** Modeling deterministic neural network prediction bias as independent sub-Gaussian observation noise in the BO framework undermines the validity of any downstream convergence guarantees to the true objective function.
- **Test-Set Contamination Risk:** Optimizing data mixtures via a direct feedback loop on downstream capabilities (like GSM8K) poses a high risk of optimization-time meta-overfitting, and the paper lacks explicit details on train/validation/test isolation.
- **Incomplete Methodological Details:** The specific Gaussian Process kernel used for the mixed continuous-simplex and discrete-integer optimization space is not defined.
- **Misleading Terminology:** The "scaling-law-inspired" framing is at odds with the paper's own admission that the network uses a generic, non-symbolic deep representation.
- **Missing Verifiable Artifacts:** The manuscript is truncated, lacking the main theoretical proofs and the primary empirical evaluation section. Additionally, the source code for JoBS is not provided alongside the baselines.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To rigorously evaluate the claims made in the abstract and introduction, the following evidence is required:
1.  **The Full Manuscript:** Sections 5.4 (budget allocation theory), the proofs for Theorem 5.1, and Section 6 (empirical results).
2.  **Kernel Specification:** Explicit mathematical formulation of the GP kernel $\kappa(x, x')$ used to handle the mixed configuration space.
3.  **Data Isolation Protocols:** Clear documentation of how the evaluation splits used to guide the Bayesian Optimization proxy are strictly isolated from the final held-out test sets.
4.  **Baseline Comparisons:** Empirical results demonstrating that the upfront budget of training the neural predictor from scratch outperforms dynamic multi-fidelity methods like BOHB or Hyperband under an identical total compute budget $C$.
5.  **Code Release:** An anonymized repository containing the JoBS framework implementation, including the BO loop, the predictor architecture, and the exact data selection configurations.
