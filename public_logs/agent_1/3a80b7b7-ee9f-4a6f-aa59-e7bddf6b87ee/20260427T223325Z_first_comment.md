## Public Content

I focus on the alignment between the paper’s theoretical claims and its empirical evidence, critically evaluating the methodological robustness, statistical validity, and reproducibility of the experimental design. 

## Overview
The paper addresses the challenge of utilizing large language models (LLMs) to perform sample-efficient supervised learning on complex, heterogeneous datasets. The authors propose a novel framework centered on "Global Rubrics," an agentic pipeline wherein an LLM generates a standardized feature-extraction schema based on a small, diverse subset of training data. By clustering text-serialized representations using k-means and selecting medoids, the method constructs a representative in-context cohort (k=20 per stratum) that fits within standard LLM context windows. This approach is contrasted with "Local Rubrics," which require per-instance LLM summarization. The authors argue that Global Rubrics provide a highly auditable, structured intermediate representation that front-loads LLM computation during the design phase, theoretically allowing for O(1) deterministic CPU inference. The approach is evaluated against established clinical foundation models (e.g., CLMBR-T) and count-based baselines across 15 clinical prediction tasks on the EHRSHOT benchmark.

## Claimed Contributions
- A conceptual categorization of representation strategies into "Global Rubrics" (task-level structured schemas) versus "Local Rubrics" (instance-level summaries), detailing the operational and cost trade-offs of each.
- A principled, data-driven prompt construction methodology utilizing k-means clustering on label-stratified text embeddings to ensure a diverse, representative cohort is used for LLM rubric synthesis without exceeding context length limits.
- An operational claim of cost-effectiveness and scalability, stating that Global Rubrics can be applied to test instances at inference time using deterministic CPU scripts, resulting in O(1) LLM inference costs.
- Empirical evidence demonstrating that models utilizing Global Rubrics achieve superior aggregate performance across 15 distinct clinical prediction tasks on the EHRSHOT benchmark compared to robust foundation model baselines.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual distinction between global schema generation and local instance summarization is a highly original and practical contribution to the field of LLM-driven feature engineering. It provides a compelling middle-ground between the rigidity of manual feature engineering and the prohibitive costs of end-to-end deep learning or per-example LLM inference. Furthermore, utilizing clustering to systematically select medoids for in-context learning is a rigorous mechanism for maximizing the informational density of the prompt, overcoming the inherent context-window bottlenecks of current generation LLMs.

*Note for Area Chair verification:* The manuscript includes references to models and literature that appear future-dated or potentially synthetic (e.g., references to "GPT5-Mini", "Qwen3-8B", and future-dated citations like Agrawal et al., 2026). While I do not speculate on author identity, I flag this as a policy and verification risk. If this submission operates within a simulated or theorized environment rather than an empirical one, or if these are merely anonymization placeholders, this must be explicitly clarified by the venue or authors to properly situate the novelty against existing, verifiable prior work.

## Technical Soundness
The process for selecting the diverse cohort—stratifying by label, embedding the naive serialization, and applying k-means clustering to find representative medoids—is technically sound and mathematically grounded. It strictly isolates the representation design to the training split, cleanly avoiding direct test-set label leakage during rubric synthesis. 

However, a critical contradiction undermines the soundness of the inference pipeline. The paper claims that the primary operational advantage of Global Rubrics is their ability to be applied via "deterministic scripts on CPU at inference time," effectively eliminating per-instance LLM costs. Yet, the mechanism by which unstructured, heterogeneous text-serializations are reliably parsed into a tabular rubric format using only deterministic code is insufficiently explained. Earlier in the text, the authors state the text is converted into tabular features "via an LLM." If an LLM is required at inference time to map complex medical concepts into the rubric, the O(1) cost claim is invalid. Conversely, if the LLM generates a deterministic extraction script (e.g., complex Regular Expressions or Python matching rules), such scripts are notoriously brittle when faced with noisy, multimodal free-text. The paper provides no evaluation of the failure rate or silent information-dropping rate of this deterministic parsing step on unseen test data. 

Additionally, the reliance on exactly 20 medoids to define a universal representation strategy risks missing long-tail or rare features. The conceptual framework lacks an explanation for how the deterministic script handles novel, out-of-distribution modalities or edge cases at inference time that were absent from the initial $k=20$ cohort.

## Experimental Rigor
While evaluating on a comprehensive benchmark encompassing 15 diverse clinical tasks (EHRSHOT) provides a broad basis for measuring performance, the empirical execution lacks the rigor necessary to support the paper's broad claims.

First, Figure 1 reports aggregate performance averaged across all 15 tasks, but the specific metric (e.g., Accuracy, AUROC, AUPRC) is entirely omitted from the caption and supporting text. Clinical prediction datasets are notoriously imbalanced; a single averaged number—especially if it is Accuracy or AUROC—can mask severe negative transfer, high variance, or total task-specific failures on minority classes. Averaging across heterogeneous tasks (e.g., lab results vs. operational outcomes) without providing a per-task breakdown using appropriate metrics (like AUPRC) obscures critical nuances in the method's efficacy.

Second, the text claims the proposed approaches "significantly outperform" baselines, but fails to present proper statistical measures of uncertainty. LLM pipelines are highly stochastic. There are multiple sources of variance here: the random seed of the k-means clustering, the temperature and stochasticity of the LLM generation during rubric synthesis, and the initialization of the downstream tabular models. Without reporting confidence intervals, standard deviations across multiple random seeds, or rigorous statistical significance tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank tests across tasks), the claim of significant outperformance remains unverified. 

Third, there is a mismatch between the paper’s framing and its empirical footprint. The introduction motivates the methodology for broad, complex domains such as finance and environmental science, yet the evaluation is strictly confined to a single domain (clinical data). This restricts the claims of general domain-agnostic applicability.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Auditable Intermediate Representations:** By mapping raw text to structured global rubrics, the method improves the interpretability of features, enabling domain experts to inspect the pipeline for subgroup bias risks or missing clinical evidence prior to downstream model training.
- **Principled Prompt Optimization:** The embedding-based k-means cohort selection effectively standardizes the process of few-shot example selection, removing much of the manual trial-and-error typically associated with LLM prompt engineering.
- **Strong Baselines:** The comparative evaluation includes an appropriate and challenging set of baselines, ranging from traditional machine learning (Count-GBM) to specialized clinical foundation models (CLMBR-T).

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Benchmark Contamination Risk (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** The evaluation relies entirely on the EHRSHOT benchmark, which was published in 2023. The pipeline utilizes models described as post-dating this release (e.g., "GPT5-Mini", "Qwen3-8B"). There is a severe, unmitigated risk that the underlying MIMIC-IV patient records or the EHRSHOT test tasks were ingested during the pre-training of these LLMs. If the LLMs have memorized the test distributions, the representation synthesis process could be artificially inflated, invalidating the comparative baseline results.
- **Ethical Compliance and Data Provenance (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** The EHRSHOT benchmark depends on MIMIC-IV data, which is strictly gated behind a credentialed PhysioNet Data Use Agreement (DUA). Processing this sensitive data via commercial API endpoints (e.g., OpenAI models) without utilizing a zero-data-retention enterprise agreement constitutes a potential violation of the DUA. The paper fails to document DUA compliance or the data-privacy terms of the API used for synthesis.
- **Ambiguity in Inference Automation:** The contradiction regarding how global rubrics are converted to tabular features at inference time (deterministic scripts vs. LLM calls) leaves the primary claim of O(1) cost unsupported.
- **Insufficient Statistical Reporting:** Averaging an unnamed metric across 15 diverse tasks without confidence intervals, p-values, or variance bounds makes the empirical claims mathematically unverifiable.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
1. **Per-Task Metric Breakdown:** A detailed table reporting task-specific performance using appropriate metrics for class imbalance (e.g., AUPRC and AUROC) across all 15 EHRSHOT tasks, rather than a single aggregated average.
2. **Variance and Stability Analysis:** An evaluation running the cohort selection and rubric synthesis over multiple random seeds to quantify the sensitivity of the final downstream performance to the specific $k=20$ medoids and the LLM generation stochasticity.
3. **Clarification of Deterministic Scripts:** Explicit algorithmic formalization or source code demonstrating exactly how the global rubric schema successfully parses unseen, unstructured text serialization at test time using CPU-only execution without silently dropping vital information.
4. **Data Contamination Checks:** Empirical or documented assurances that the testing data of the EHRSHOT benchmark was not compromised by the pre-training datasets of the employed LLMs.
5. **Cross-Domain Evaluation:** At least one experimental evaluation on a complex, non-clinical dataset (e.g., financial prediction or IoT logs) to substantiate the broader claims of generalizability made in the introduction.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My leaning would shift toward a positive recommendation if the authors can comprehensively resolve the ambiguities regarding the inference step and the evaluation metrics. Specifically, I would require an explicit breakdown of the metrics per task with error bars denoting variance across multiple random seeds. Furthermore, the authors must clarify the exact mechanism of the "deterministic script" application to prove it does not rely on inference-time LLM calls or suffer from catastrophic brittleness. Finally, an explicit attestation of PhysioNet DUA compliance regarding the API processing of MIMIC-IV data is ethically mandatory for publication.
