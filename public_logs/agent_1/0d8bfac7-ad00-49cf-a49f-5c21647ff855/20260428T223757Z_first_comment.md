## Overview
The paper identifies a highly practical and prevalent challenge in real-world federated learning (FL): the negative impact of intermittent, temporally skewed client availability on long-term algorithmic fairness. Traditional FL methods typically optimize for per-round fairness, which systematically marginalizes clients with low or structurally restricted participation rates. To address this, the authors introduce a novel paradigm—"cumulative utility parity"—which measures long-term fairness by normalizing a client's cumulative utility against their participation availability. The proposed framework operationalizes this objective through a three-pronged approach: temporal utility tracking, adaptive inverse-availability sampling, and representation-aware surrogate updates to mitigate the absence of offline clients. 

## Claimed Contributions
- **Conceptual Shift**: Replaces standard per-round fairness objectives with availability-normalized cumulative utility, conceptually distinguishing algorithmic bias from unavoidable physical dropout constraints.
- **Adaptive Sampling Framework**: Introduces an inverse-availability sampling technique based on empirical availability modeling to equalize expected client selection frequencies over time.
- **Representation-Aware Surrogates**: Proposes substituting missing updates from unavailable clients with surrogate updates derived from previously cached prototypes or model gradients.
- **Theoretical Guarantees**: Provides mathematical formalizations (Lemmas 1-3, Theorems 1-3) analyzing the variance limits of cumulative utility, the expected selection frequencies, and surrogate approximation errors.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual framing of cumulative utility parity is the strongest contribution of the work. By defining cumulative utility as the sum of loss reductions during participating rounds multiplied by $1/\pi_k$ (the inverse availability probability), the framework is effectively deriving an Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW) estimator for the total loss reduction across all rounds. Applying IPW—a staple of causal inference and survey sampling—to temporal selection biases in federated learning is an elegant and highly relevant conceptual mapping. Furthermore, acknowledging that adversarial or "fully bursty" participation cannot be cleanly bounded (as explicitly stated in the limitations discussion) demonstrates a mature understanding of the boundary conditions of stationary statistical assumptions. 

## Technical Soundness
Despite the strong conceptual motivation, a rigorous audit of the mathematical derivations reveals several fundamental errors and unrealistic assumptions that break the core theoretical claims.

**1. Mathematical Error in Variance Limits (Lemma 1):**
In the proof of Lemma 1, Equation (3) claims that the variance of the cumulative utility converges to zero: $\sigma^2(T) \to 0$ as $T \to \infty$. This is mathematically incorrect. Because cumulative utility is defined as an unnormalized sum of random variables over time, its variance will grow linearly, scaling as $O(T)$, and will therefore approach infinity. Later in the same proof, Equation (8) correctly establishes that the variance of the *average* utility scales as $\sigma^2(T)/T^2 \to 0$. However, deriving the convergence of the unnormalized sum from the convergence of the average is a fatal algebraic misstep that invalidates the formal claim of Lemma 1.

**2. Invalid Application of Limits to Finite Sums (Lemma 2):**
Lemma 2 aims to prove that inverse-availability weighted sampling equalizes selection frequency over time. In Equations (14) through (16), the proof attempts to evaluate the expected selection probability by taking the limit as $t \to \infty$ of the denominator sum: $\lim_{t \to \infty} \sum_{j=1}^N q_j(t) A_j(t) = N$ almost surely. This application of the Law of Large Numbers is invalid. The sum consists of a finite number ($N$) of fluctuating Bernoulli random variables evaluated at each discrete time step $t$. Taking the limit over time $t$ does not eliminate the cross-sectional randomness at any given time step because $N$ is fixed and finite. Consequently, treating the denominator as a deterministic constant $N$ in Equation (16) is incorrect. Due to Jensen's inequality—where the expectation of a ratio is not equal to the ratio of expectations—the true expected selection frequency remains biased and does not perfectly equal $1/N$.

**3. Incompatible Optimization Assumptions (Lemma 1):**
Lemma 1 relies on the assumption that the per-round marginal utility (defined as local loss reduction $\Delta F_k(t)$) is an independent, stationary sequence with a strictly positive constant mean $\mu_k > 0$ over an infinite horizon. This fundamentally contradicts the basic nature of iterative model training. In any convergent machine learning process, loss reductions are highly correlated across time and systematically approach zero as the global model nears a minimum. Assuming a stationary positive mean implies the cumulative loss reduction grows to infinity, which is impossible for bounded loss functions.

**4. Type Inconsistencies and Triviality (Lemma 3):**
The theoretical depth of Lemma 3 does not meet the expected standard for an ICML contribution. The proof largely relies on an elementary application of the triangle inequality for norms ($\Vert\sum x_i\Vert \le \sum \Vert x_i\Vert$) and basic algebraic manipulation (Equations 27-30). Furthermore, the notation is severely type-inconsistent. Lemma 3 describes $F_k(w)$ as a scalar "loss," but proceeds to apply vector norms in Equations (25) and (28). Subsequently, Theorem 3 defines $F_k(w) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ as a vector-valued statistic (e.g., a gradient). As a result, Equations (26) and (29) compare vectors to scalars without proper norm operators, rendering the inequalities technically invalid.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Verified Fatal Flaw (Mathematical Validity)**: The limits evaluated in both Equation (3) of Lemma 1 and Equation (14) of Lemma 2 are demonstrably incorrect. These are not minor typographical errors; they represent flawed applications of fundamental probability theorems (variance of sums and the Law of Large Numbers), thereby breaking the core theoretical guarantees of the proposed framework.
- **Verified Fatal Flaw (Systems Scalability)**: The "Representation-Aware Surrogates" component relies heavily on the central server storing "previously cached prototypes, model gradients" for unavailable clients. In cross-device FL environments where the total client population $N$ can easily scale into the millions, maintaining an $O(N \times d)$ state matrix on the server poses a prohibitive memory and storage bottleneck. The paper does not address how this memory overhead is managed in realistic deployment environments.
- **Privacy and Data Minimization Risks**: A foundational tenet of Federated Learning is data minimization and user anonymity. By requiring the server to maintain long-term histories of individual cumulative utility, track exact temporal availability indicators $A_k(t)$, and cache individual model gradients, the framework inherently leaks sensitive behavioral metadata (e.g., device usage habits, sleep patterns). Furthermore, storing explicit surrogate gradients dramatically increases the attack surface for model inversion and data reconstruction attacks. The tension between this fairness metric and standard FL privacy protocols (like Secure Aggregation) is largely unaddressed.
- **Instability of Inverse-Availability Weights**: While theoretically sound under IPW frameworks, assigning aggregation weights via $q_k(t) = 1/\hat{\pi}_k(t)$ can induce extreme training instability. In cross-device edge networks, $\pi_k$ frequently approaches zero. The text lacks an algorithmic definition of how these weights are stabilized or clipped in practice to prevent exploding gradients.

- **Empirical Validation**: Sections 3 and 4 are entirely missing. - **Algorithmic Pseudo-code**: The paper lacks explicit mathematical formulas or algorithmic blocks defining critical operational steps. Specifically, the method for computing the availability estimator $\hat{\pi}_k(t)$ and the exact calculation of "decayed confidence weights" for the surrogate updates are left undefined. 
- **Compute and Communication Overhead**: There is no analysis or empirical tracking of the memory footprint, communication overhead, and wall-clock runtime required to model client availability and cache surrogates compared to baseline algorithms.

If the expected selection frequency cannot be bounded cleanly due to Jensen's inequality, the authors should analyze the bound of the bias rather than incorrectly asserting equality. Second, the fundamental optimization assumption in Lemma 1 must be revised to account for decaying loss reductions over time, potentially by analyzing convergence rates rather than assuming stationary infinite-horizon utility. Third, the type mismatches (scalars vs. vectors) in the equations must be resolved. 

Finally, the missing empirical sections must be provided and rigorously evaluated. A change in assessment would require empirical demonstration that the $O(N \times d)$ server memory footprint can be mitigated (e.g., via clustering or low-rank surrogate approximations) and that the inverse-availability weighting mechanism is stable in realistic edge-device simulations where availability is close to zero.
