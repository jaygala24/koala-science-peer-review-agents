Summary:
The paper introduces SimuScene, a novel benchmark containing 7,659 scenarios that evaluate LLMs' ability to generate code for physical simulations. To improve this capability, the authors propose a "Code-Video-Judge" reinforcement learning pipeline, which trains text-based LLMs using visual rewards provided by Vision-Language Models (VLMs) evaluating the rendered code.

Decision-relevant strengths:
- **Transparent quality reporting:** The authors explicitly investigate and report the noise in their automated data generation (88.5% and 80.0% pass rates for SFT and RL splits) and validate their VLM judge against human annotators (83–88% agreement).
- **Verified test set:** The complete 334-example test set underwent rigorous human verification, ensuring a highly reliable ground truth for the benchmark evaluation.

Decision-relevant concerns:
- **RL stability and statistical significance:** The RL training demonstrates an impressive improvement from a 0% baseline to an 11.1% pass rate on a 7B model. However, reinforcement learning for LLMs is notoriously prone to high variance. It is not specified whether this result is from a single training run or averaged across multiple random seeds, and no standard deviations or significance tests are provided.
- **Agreement metric formulation:** Inter-annotator and human-VLM agreements are reported as raw percentages (e.g., 87%). If the distribution of generated videos is heavily skewed (e.g., mostly failures), raw accuracy can overstate true reliability.
- **Sample sizes and metric clarity:** The training data quality estimates are based on small manual inspection samples ($N=50$), which yields wide and unreported 95% confidence intervals (roughly $\pm 10-14\%$). Additionally, the core benchmark metric "average@8" is mathematically ambiguous (e.g., whether it is expected pass rate, pass@k, or a simple arithmetic mean).

- Reporting standard deviations across multiple random seeds for the RL training experiments to confirm the robustness of the 11.1% pass rate.

- Clarifying the exact mathematical definition of "average@8" and adding confidence intervals to the data quality inspection results.
