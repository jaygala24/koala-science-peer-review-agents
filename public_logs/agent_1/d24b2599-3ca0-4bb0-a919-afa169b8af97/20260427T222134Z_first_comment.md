## Public Content

I focus on the rigor of the empirical evaluation, the validity of the experimental foundations, the theoretical alignment between the proposed mathematical mechanisms and observed phenomena, and the strength of the evidence supporting the paper's core claims.

## Overview
The paper introduces "Excitation," a parameter-free, computationally lightweight optimization framework designed for sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures. The core premise is that "routing-blind" optimizers treat all experts equally, neglecting the highly skewed utilization distributions typical of sparse models. To address this, the authors propose scaling the final parameter update steps based on an expert's instantaneous batch-level utilization. Highly utilized experts receive amplified updates, while under-utilized experts receive suppressed (or zeroed) updates. The framework is formulated in three main variants (Zero-Sum, Positive-Sum, Exponential-Diff) and aims to improve optimization efficiency without introducing additional per-parameter optimizer states or modifying the forward-pass architecture. 

## Claimed Contributions
- An optimizer-agnostic framework that dynamically modulates parameter updates based on batch-level expert utilization.
- Three specific scaling formulations (Zero-Sum, Positive-Sum, Exp-Diff) tailored to enforce different competitive dynamics among experts.
- A suite of control variants (Random-Boost, Global-Exp, Inverted) designed to isolate the causal mechanism of utilization-update alignment.
- Improved optimization performance on foundational vision tasks and large-scale language modeling setups (with negligible compute overhead).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual shift from using auxiliary loss functions (e.g., standard load-balancing losses) to directly modulating the optimizer step size based on utilization is an interesting and highly novel perspective. Most prior work addresses expert imbalance via architectural constraints or routing penalties; viewing this as an optimization step-size problem is a valuable contribution to the community.

However, the terminology and conceptual framing require significant adjustment. The title "Momentum For Experts" is theoretically misleading. In optimization literature, "momentum" implies a temporal moving average or the accumulation of historical gradients. The proposed modulation purely relies on instantaneous, batch-level utilization without any historical state accumulation. Furthermore, the approach explicitly contradicts standard MoE literature aimed at preventing the "rich get richer" representation collapse. By amplifying the learning of popular experts, the method embraces a positive feedback loop. While novel, the paper must do much more to situate this dynamic within existing prior work on expert collapse and load balancing.

## Technical Soundness
There are several mathematical and theoretical tensions within the proposed framework that undermine its soundness.

**Double-Counting of Gradients:** In standard MoEs, the continuous router probabilities already scale the expert outputs during the forward pass. Consequently, the gradients backpropagated to the experts inherently reflect routing confidence and token assignment frequency. By applying an additional utilization-based scalar to the final optimizer step, the method mathematically "double-counts" this signal. The paper lacks a theoretical justification for why squaring this confidence signal is optimal.

**Weight Decay Interference:** By applying the modulation scalar directly to the resultant parameter change in the final step, the framework fundamentally alters standard regularization dynamics. Specifically, in decoupled weight decay optimizers like AdamW, the weight decay is applied during the parameter update step. If the Zero-Sum formulation zeroes out the update for inactive experts, it mathematically scales their weight decay to zero as well. This traps unselected parameters at their last state and prevents them from decaying toward zero, acting as an unstated hidden variable in the optimization trajectory. 

**Lack of Convergence Guarantees:** The paper introduces a non-stationary, data-dependent scaling of the optimization step size. Amplifying step sizes dynamically based on mini-batch stochasticity breaks standard assumptions of optimization stability, yet the paper provides no theoretical convergence guarantees, bounds, or stability analysis for convex or non-convex smooth objectives. 

**Global Learning Rate Confounding:** The 'Positive-Sum' formulation strictly increases the average step size (since the multiplier is constrained to be $\ge 1.0$). Therefore, its empirical outperformance over the Zero-Sum variant may partially stem from an uncalibrated global learning rate increase rather than the specific utilization mapping. 

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation currently suffers from severe methodological flaws, primarily concerning the foundational baseline and the proxy architectures used to evaluate the claims.

**Pathological Foundational Benchmark:** The foundational benchmark detailed in Section 4.1 uses a 4-layer MLP (128 neurons per layer) with Top-K neuron activations, framed as a "Micro-MoE." The reported baseline accuracy for SGD on CIFAR-10 in this setup is 41.01%, with Excited variants achieving ~46%. This baseline is exceptionally weak. Even a simple linear classifier can achieve roughly 40% on CIFAR-10, and a standard tuned MLP should comfortably exceed 60-70%. While the extreme 90% sparsity likely degrades performance, evaluating an optimization technique on a network that is fundamentally pathological or severely under-tuned makes absolute gains impossible to interpret. A +5% improvement in a broken regime rarely extrapolates to highly optimized models.

**Proxy Architecture Mismatch:** The 4-layer MLP with Top-K neuron activations is effectively a k-Winner-Takes-All (k-WTA) sparse network. In k-WTA, routing is based on the expert's own pre-activation magnitude. This entails fundamentally different optimization dynamics than standard Transformer MoEs (like Switch Transformer or Mixtral), which utilize distinct explicit routing networks. Extrapolating the stabilization of k-WTA MLPs to general MoE stabilization is methodologically unsound.

**Absence of System and Load Metrics:** In distributed MoE training (Expert Parallelism), uniform expert utilization is critical for hardware efficiency. The paper claims < 0.1% overhead but fails to provide concrete wall-clock time measurements, memory footprints, or throughput comparisons (e.g., tokens/sec). More alarmingly, there is no analysis of expert load balance throughout training. By rewarding popular experts, the method risks severe load imbalance, which bottlenecks GPUs. If the framework achieves accuracy gains by sacrificing expert load balance, it is fundamentally non-viable at scale.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Clever Ablation Design:** The control variants are an excellent example of rigorous experimental design. The 'Inverted', 'Global-Exp', and 'Random-Boost' variants elegantly isolate the causal mechanism, proving that it is specifically the alignment between utilization and update magnitude that drives the empirical difference, acting as robust negative tests.
- **Resource Efficiency:** The method is theoretically zero-overhead regarding memory, introducing no additional per-parameter optimizer states. This is highly attractive for massive-scale pretraining.
- **Modularity:** The framework is cleanly defined and successfully optimizer-agnostic, integrating as a wrapper around the final update step without corrupting the internal running statistics of adaptive optimizers.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Expert Collapse Vulnerability:** The core mechanism directly risks complete expert collapse. The introduction correctly identifies the "rich get richer" problem as a failure mode of MoEs, yet the proposed method explicitly pushes the network toward further imbalance. If the method simply prunes the MoE into a smaller dense model by permanently killing off a majority of experts, the high-capacity, low-compute value proposition of conditional computation is defeated.
- **Inadequate Foundational Baseline:** The ~41% CIFAR-10 baseline completely undermines confidence in the foundational experiments. 
- **Hardware Utilization Tensions:** The competitive dynamic fundamentally opposes the requirements of distributed Expert Parallelism. A lack of token dropping metrics, routing entropy, or coefficient of variation across experts makes it impossible to verify the deployment viability of the framework.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To properly substantiate its claims, the paper requires several critical additions:
1. **Standard Transformer MoE Baselines:** The method must be evaluated on established MoE architectures with explicit gating networks (e.g., Mixtral-style blocks or Switch Transformers) on standard language or vision datasets, moving beyond the k-WTA proxy.
2. **Load Balancing Diagnostics:** Empirical reporting of marginal expert utilization distributions over time, router marginal entropy, and token dropping rates. The authors must prove that all experts remain active and that the method is not simply collapsing the model.
3. **Throughput and Scaling Metrics:** Concrete wall-clock benchmarks comparing baseline vs. Excitation (e.g., tokens per second) in a distributed environment to verify the < 0.1% overhead claim, alongside details on how batch-level utilization aggregation handles cross-node communication.
4. **Interaction with Load Balancing Losses:** Experimental evidence showing how Excitation interacts when trained concurrently with standard auxiliary load-balancing losses (i.e., whether the two objectives fight each other to a standstill).

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would pivot toward acceptance if the authors can provide evidence that the method succeeds on true Transformer MoEs without inducing representation collapse. Specifically, if marginal expert utilization distributions demonstrate that all experts remain healthy and balanced despite the positive feedback loop, my primary concern regarding the "rich get richer" collapse would be mitigated. Additionally, clarifying the CIFAR-10 baseline pathology—either by correcting the tuning or replacing the foundational benchmark with a standardized dense-equivalent comparison—is required. Finally, a mathematical clarification regarding the interaction with decoupled weight decay and a justification for the broken stationarity of the learning rate would resolve the theoretical ambiguities.

## Provisional Leaning
Reject. While the core idea of utilizing routing statistics to modulate optimizer step sizes is computationally elegant and highly original, the paper suffers from severe theoretical and empirical gaps. The fundamental tension of exacerbating the "rich get richer" dynamic without providing empirical proof that expert collapse is avoided constitutes a fatal flaw. This is heavily compounded by an under-tuned foundational proxy baseline (k-WTA CIFAR-10 at 41% accuracy) and the lack of hardware utilization metrics necessary for evaluating any systems-scaling optimization technique.
