## Public Content

I focus on the theoretical soundness, statistical validity, and the empirical rigor of the proposed uncertainty quantification framework for medical shape modeling.

## Overview
PRISM introduces a 3D probabilistic neural representation tailored for interpretable, continuous medical shape modeling. The methodology elegantly bridges deterministic implicit neural representations with uncertainty-aware statistical shape analysis. By modeling displacement fields as heteroscedastic Gaussians (Eq. 1 and Eq. 2), the paper derives a closed-form Fisher Information metric (Eq. 9) that enables the analytical computation of spatially localized, temporal uncertainty via automatic differentiation. To circumvent the high computational cost of iterative optimization at test time, PRISM further proposes an amortized inverse encoder (Eq. 7) mapped to local observations to predict individual intrinsic developmental time.

## Claimed Contributions
- A probabilistic formulation of 3D shape implicit neural representations that utilizes a heteroscedastic Gaussian displacement field to model population-level structural variance.
- The derivation of a closed-form Fisher Information metric (Eq. 9) that analytically decomposes temporal uncertainty into mean progression precision and structural variability components, avoiding costly Monte Carlo sampling.
- An amortized inverse encoder designed for efficient test-time intrinsic developmental time estimation without iterative Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE).
- The application of this continuous uncertainty framework to clinical tasks, specifically Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) anomaly detection and spatiotemporal developmental analysis.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper correctly identifies a critical operational limitation in current deterministic shape models (such as NAISR) and traditional parameter-space atlas frameworks: the inability to quantify spatially continuous, covariate-dependent aleatoric uncertainty directly on the anatomical surface. By unifying information geometry with neural implicit fields, PRISM presents a highly novel and elegant approach. 

A particularly strong theoretical contribution is the analytical decomposition of the Fisher Information matrix (Eq. 9) into two distinct components: the precision of the mean trajectory evolution ($I_\mu$) and the inverse of the population structural variance ($I_\Sigma$). This mathematical formulation has profound clinical interpretability. By separating the rate of morphological change from the inherent cross-sectional population variance, the framework allows clinicians to systematically distinguish between areas of the anatomy that rapidly change during development versus areas that naturally exhibit high variance across different healthy individuals. This explicit decoupling represents a substantial step forward over existing non-probabilistic or computationally intensive baseline methods.

## Technical Soundness
While the mathematical foundation mapping intrinsic time inference to Fisher Information is elegant, several core statistical choices and modeling assumptions require rigorous scrutiny:

1. **Statistical Disconnect in Inverse Encoder Training:** The methodology frames individual intrinsic time estimation as an MLE problem (Eq. 5), which is asymptotically efficient and unbiased. However, to bypass the computational cost of test-time optimization, the amortized inverse encoder is trained using an expected L1 loss (Eq. 7) over synthetically generated coordinate-displacement pairs. Minimizing an L1 loss computes the conditional median, whereas MLE targets the mode. In symmetric distributions, these align, but in clinical populations where developmental delays or pathological anomalies introduce heavy-tailed or skewed distributions, the median and mode can diverge significantly. This disconnect may introduce systematic biases into the test-time temporal estimates.
2. **Cramér-Rao Lower Bound (CRLB) as an Uncertainty Proxy:** Using the CRLB (Eq. 8) as a direct proxy for actual temporal uncertainty intrinsically assumes that the estimator (the inverse encoder) is statistically efficient. If the L1-trained inverse encoder is not an efficient estimator—or if the median-mode divergence introduces bias—the CRLB will strictly underestimate the true temporal uncertainty. Relying on an underestimated variance bound could lead to overconfident predictions in high-stakes clinical scenarios.
3. **Point-wise Marginalization and Spatial Correlations:** Equation 2 models the probability of the displacement field conditionally per point $p$. While this point-wise marginalization aligns with the coordinate-based nature of implicit neural networks, it risks ignoring complex spatial cross-correlations (covariances between distinct template points $p$ and $p'$). Anatomical deformations are highly coordinated; explicitly discarding these off-diagonal spatial covariances raises questions about whether the generative model can sample anatomically coherent, global surface geometries without introducing localized, uncoordinated noise.
4. **Template Mapping Dependency and Epistemic Risk:** The framework models spatial displacements conditionally based on a pre-established template point correspondence. This correspondence relies entirely on a separate template-based registration model (deferred to Appendix A.1). Consequently, the "aleatoric uncertainty" modeled by PRISM may inadvertently absorb unquantified epistemic registration errors. It remains theoretically unclear how the model handles severe topological deviations (e.g., missing anatomy, tumors) that strictly violate the underlying homeomorphic template mapping assumption.
5. **Chronological Unbiasedness Assumption:** The framework assumes that the population-average intrinsic time exactly equals chronological time ($E_{p(d|p,t)}[\tau] = t$). In highly heterogeneous clinical cohorts heavily skewed towards specific developmental abnormalities, this unbiased estimator assumption may fail, potentially skewing the baseline trajectories.

## Experimental Rigor

To fully validate this probabilistic framework, the experiments must advance beyond qualitative visualizations. The evaluation of uncertainty calibration is paramount. Standard calibration metrics—such as Expected Calibration Error (ECE) or empirical coverage probability—must be reported to confirm whether the theoretically derived CRLB uncertainties align with actual empirical errors on unseen data. 

Furthermore, regarding the training data generation for the inverse encoder (Section 4.2), the text states it utilizes the mean displacement $d = \mu(p, \tau)$. It is mathematically ambiguous whether the learned covariance $\Sigma(p, \tau)$ is subsequently used to inject realistic noise during the generation of these synthetic training triplets. If only the mean trajectory is used without noise injection, the inverse encoder risks becoming highly brittle to the natural variance and noise present in real-world clinical observations at test time.

Finally, relying on an external template-registration pipeline demands rigorous ablations testing the framework's sensitivity to misregistration, out-of-distribution point correspondences, and varying degrees of topological artifacting. 

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Theoretical Elegance:** The derivation of a closed-form Fisher Information metric from a heteroscedastic Gaussian field to yield analytically tractable, localized temporal uncertainty is a major asset to the medical imaging community.
- **Computational Efficiency:** Formulating an amortized inverse encoder to bypass iterative MLE provides a highly practical test-time solution for individual intrinsic time estimation.
- **Clinical Interpretability:** By framing temporal anomalies and structural variances in the language of information geometry, the method provides distinct, interpretable spatial uncertainty maps suitable for clinician-in-the-loop diagnostic workflows.
- **Exemplary Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-structured. Figure 1 provides an outstanding conceptual summary of the method and its downstream applications, significantly reducing cognitive load. Furthermore, explicit formulations of the NLL and L1 objective functions reduce ambiguity for replication.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Covariance Parameterization Omission:** The main text lacks specific mathematical details regarding the neural parameterization of the covariance matrix $\Sigma(p, t)$ required in Equation 3. Maintaining positive definiteness and ensuring computational tractability for inverses and determinants during unconstrained neural network optimization are non-trivial implementation details critical for reproducibility.
- **Robustness to Topological Pathology:** Due to its strict reliance on template correspondences, the framework’s capacity to handle out-of-distribution pathological shapes—such as missing structures or severe clinical deformations—is a potential failure mode that is not preemptively addressed in the methodology.
- **Dataset Transparency and Ethics:** The application of this model to clinical Out-Of-Distribution anomaly detection necessitates strict dataset transparency. Missing details regarding the clinical dataset’s demographic distribution, Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval, and anonymization protocols represent a compliance and safety concern. Biased representation within the dataset could skew the estimated "normal" trajectories, yielding disparate OOD detection rates across different demographic subpopulations.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To substantiate the theoretical claims, the following empirical evidence is strictly required in the full manuscript:
- **Uncertainty Calibration Metrics:** Quantitative measurements (e.g., coverage probability) proving that the estimated spatial and temporal uncertainties are empirically well-calibrated and do not underestimate variance due to the CRLB efficiency assumption.
- **Registration Artifact Analysis:** Controlled experiments or ablations demonstrating how systemic errors in the initial template registration step propagate into the learned Fisher Information and downstream uncertainty maps.
- **Training Noise Injection Protocol:** Clarification in Section 4.2 regarding whether synthetic displacements are sampled utilizing the learned variance $\Sigma$ to ensure the inverse encoder is robust to test-time observational noise.
- **Implementation Specifications:** Explicit details documenting how the covariance matrix $\Sigma(p, t)$ is parameterized (e.g., Cholesky decomposition, diagonal vs. full matrix) to ensure valid, positive-definite outputs.
- **Ethical and Demographic Disclosures:** Comprehensive documentation of the clinical dataset's scale, source, ethical compliance (IRB), and demographic distributions to evaluate potential representational biases.
