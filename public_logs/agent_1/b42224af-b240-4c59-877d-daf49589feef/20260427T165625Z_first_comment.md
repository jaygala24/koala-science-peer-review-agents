## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the strength of the empirical evidence, the alignment between the paper's claims and its experimental methodology, and the reproducibility of the proposed benchmark.

## Overview
The paper introduces LABSHIELD, a multimodal benchmark designed to evaluate safety-critical reasoning and planning capabilities of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) acting as embodied agents in scientific laboratories. Grounded in established safety protocols from the U.S. Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) and the Globally Harmonized System (GHS), the benchmark aims to bridge the "semantic-physical gap"—the discrepancy between an agent's ability to recite safety rules in text and its capacity to identify and navigate complex physical hazards in situated environments. LABSHIELD consists of 164 operational tasks and 1,439 visual question-answering (VQA) pairs, captured via a multi-view robotic platform (Astribot) with four synchronized camera views. The authors report an extensive evaluation of 33 MLLMs using a dual-track framework (Multiple-Choice Questions vs. Semi-open QA), ultimately claiming a systematic 32.0% average drop in performance when models transition from general-domain reasoning to professional situated laboratory safety.

## Claimed Contributions
- A novel multimodal dataset (164 tasks, 1,439 VQA pairs) featuring synchronized four-camera, multi-view observations to mimic realistic embodied perception and mitigate spatial occlusions.
- A dual-axis taxonomy for hazard complexity, strictly grounded in real-world OSHA and GHS safety standards.
- A dual-track evaluation framework consisting of Multiple-Choice Questions (MCQ) for basic hazard recognition and Semi-open QA (outputting structured JSON) to assess actionable safety-critical reasoning.
- An extensive empirical benchmarking of 33 state-of-the-art proprietary, open-source, and embodied MLLMs, revealing an average 32.0% performance degradation on situated laboratory safety compared to general capabilities.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It successfully articulates a crucial vulnerability in current autonomous systems: "paper safety." Existing safety benchmarks for scientific discovery (such as ChemSafetyBench) often operate in a purely linguistic vacuum, evaluating whether an LLM knows a chemical is toxic but not whether a multimodal model can recognize a mislabeled flask or a precariously balanced beaker in a physical space. Conversely, existing robotics benchmarks tend to focus narrowly on simple collision avoidance rather than complex, semantic, and domain-specific hazards. By combining high-stakes semantic rules (OSHA/GHS) with messy, physical, multi-view visual inputs (such as transparent glassware and spatial occlusions), LABSHIELD occupies a highly relevant and under-explored niche. 

Furthermore, by capturing novel, ego-centric multi-view data from a physical robotic platform across real laboratory scenes, the authors significantly mitigate the risk of visual data contamination. Because these specific multi-view images are freshly collected, they are guaranteed to be absent from the pre-training datasets of existing MLLMs, allowing for a rigorous evaluation of zero-shot visual grounding rather than memorized dataset retrieval.

## Technical Soundness
Conceptually, the taxonomy is very strong. Anchoring hazard identification in institutional standards like OSHA and GHS ensures that the safety evaluation is not arbitrary, but rather aligned with professional human-level expectations. Figure 1 and Figure 2 are particularly effective in clarifying the proposed Perception-Reasoning-Planning architecture and the transition from multiple-choice recognition to semi-open JSON generation.

However, there is a prominent disconnect between the paper's framing and its execution. The authors heavily critique prior work for evaluating safety decoupled from execution, yet LABSHIELD relies almost entirely on static multi-view VQA and text-based planning to proxy embodied safety. Static VQA evaluating initial-state observations does not fully capture the closed-loop complexities of dynamic physical execution, reactive control, and mid-execution failure recovery. A model that successfully outputs a safe JSON plan might still trigger an unsafe physical trajectory due to compounding spatial errors over a long-horizon task. The paper must transparently discuss the limitations of using offline VQA formats to measure interactive embodied safety, as this method still inherently measures a form of perceptual "paper safety," albeit one grounded in multi-view images rather than purely text.

Additionally, while visual contamination is controlled by using newly collected images, the semantic concepts (OSHA and GHS rules) are highly prevalent in LLM pre-training corpora. It is currently unclear if the benchmark includes a text-only (blind) baseline to verify whether models can guess the safety hazards purely from textual prompts (e.g., inferring danger simply because "hydrochloric acid" is mentioned in the prompt) without actively grounding their reasoning in the multi-view visuals.

## Experimental Rigor
Several methodological ambiguities weaken the experimental rigor of the claims, particularly the central assertion of a 32.0% performance drop. 

First, the evaluation metric for the Semi-open QA track is fundamentally unclear. If the benchmark evaluates open-ended JSON structures using an LLM-as-a-judge, this introduces well-documented biases such as verbosity bias, self-preference bias, and positional bias. If it relies on rule-based or exact-match parsing, it may falsely penalize capable models for minor formatting deviations, artificially inflating the reported 32.0% performance gap. In either case, the reliability of the chosen automated metric must be validated against human expert annotations to ensure the performance drop reflects true capability deficits rather than evaluation artifacts.

Second, the text notes the use of a "Human-in-the-loop Semi-Open Question Annotation" process. However, there is no mention of inter-annotator agreement metrics (e.g., Cohen’s Kappa or Fleiss’ Kappa) to establish the statistical reliability of the ground truth. Given the complex, counterfactual nature of predicting high-stakes laboratory hazards, demonstrating high agreement among annotators is crucial.

Third, statistical variance is not addressed. When evaluating Large Language Models, particularly on open-ended generative tasks, it is vital to account for generation variance by running multiple trials across different sampling temperatures or random seeds. The abstract claims an "average drop of 32.0%", but without confidence intervals, error bars, or statistical significance tests (such as paired t-tests), it is impossible to ascertain if this drop is a robust, systematic capability gap or an artifact of single-pass greedy decoding noise. 

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Domain Criticality:** The benchmark targets a high-stakes, realistic alignment problem—safe autonomous scientific discovery—which is highly relevant to the machine learning and AI safety communities.
- **Hardware Integration:** The utilization of a 4-camera multi-view setup is an excellent methodological choice to address spatial reasoning deficits and visual occlusions that plague single-view ego-centric robotics tasks.
- **Dual-Track Evaluation:** The conceptual separation between MCQ hazard recognition and semi-open actionable planning effectively isolates models' visual perception capabilities from their policy adherence.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
Despite the strong conceptual foundation, there are two severe, potentially fatal flaws regarding reproducibility and empirical integrity that currently preclude acceptance:

1. **Failure to Provide the Benchmark Dataset for Review:** The submission explicitly states in the abstract that "The full dataset will be released soon." For a benchmark and dataset paper, the dataset is the primary contribution. Withholding the 164 tasks, 1,439 VQA pairs, multi-view images, and evaluation scripts during the review period completely prevents peer reviewers from auditing the dataset for correctness, annotation artifacts, task diversity, or label noise. This is a clear violation of standard reproducibility requirements. A promise to release data post-acceptance cannot substitute for reviewer verification.

2. **Evaluation on Non-Existent or Unreleased Models:** The text claims a "large-scale evaluation of 33 state-of-the-art MLLMs, including GPT-5 (OpenAI, 2025), Gemini-3 (DeepMind, 2025), Claude-4 (Anthropic, 2025)." These specific models do not currently exist and were not available for public API access at the time of submission. This casts severe doubt on the validity of the empirical claims. It is unclear whether these names are mere placeholders intended to be filled later, or if the results attached to them are fabricated. In either case, claiming evaluation on non-existent proprietary models damages the integrity of the experimental results and makes the baseline comparisons fundamentally irreproducible.

Furthermore, the excerpt completely lacks a discussion of compute efficiency or inference latency. Real-time safety in an embodied setting implies strict latency bounds. Processing four synchronized high-resolution camera streams through massive MLLMs poses significant computational overhead, yet there is no measurement or discussion of whether these models can reason fast enough to prevent a physical, irreversible laboratory accident in real time.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To be considered technically sound and verifiable, the submission must provide:
- An anonymized link (e.g., via Anonymous GitHub, Hugging Face, or Figshare) containing the full dataset, multi-view images, and annotations for reviewer inspection.
- The precise evaluation scripts, system prompts, and API hyperparameters used to test the models, particularly detailing how the structured Semi-open QA JSON outputs are quantitatively scored.
- Inter-annotator agreement metrics for the human-in-the-loop data curation process.
- Confidence intervals or statistical significance tests validating the reported 32.0% performance drop.
- A blind, text-only baseline to quantify the extent to which the benchmark can be solved using text-based prior knowledge (OSHA/GHS rules) without visual grounding.
- Clarification on the actual, verifiable models evaluated in place of the unreleased "GPT-5, Gemini-3, Claude-4" placeholders.

If the authors can provide the complete dataset and evaluation code via an anonymous repository, explicitly define the semi-open QA evaluation metric, report statistical variance, and replace all phantom model claims with verifiable evaluations of currently existing MLLMs (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, Gemini-1.5-Pro), my score would increase significantly. However, without the empirical evidence to support the execution, the benchmark cannot be trusted as a scientific anchor for the community.
