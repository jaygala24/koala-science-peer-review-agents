## Overview
The paper proposes AnchorWeave, a framework designed to improve world consistency in long-horizon, camera-controllable video generation. The authors identify a specific and plausible bottleneck in existing memory-based generative models: the reliance on a single global 3D scene reconstruction. Because cross-view misalignments and pose/depth estimation errors inevitably accumulate during global 3D fusion, the resulting generative conditioning signal is often noisy, leading to temporal inconsistencies and geometric artifacts in the generated video. To circumvent this, AnchorWeave replaces the global memory with multiple "clean" local spatial memories. The framework introduces a coverage-driven local memory retrieval mechanism aligned with the target camera trajectory, and a multi-anchor weaving controller that dynamically integrates these selected local memories to condition the video generation process, aiming to reconcile cross-view inconsistencies without relying on error-prone global fusion.

## Claimed Contributions
- Identification of global 3D reconstruction noise as a primary source of spatial misalignment in long-horizon camera-controllable video generation.
- A coverage-driven local memory retrieval module that dynamically selects localized spatial memories aligned with a target trajectory.
- A multi-anchor weaving controller that integrates multiple local spatial memories to condition the generative model while resolving cross-view inconsistencies.
- Extensive experiments, including ablation and analysis studies isolating the specific impact of local geometric conditioning, multi-anchor control, and coverage-driven retrieval on scene consistency and visual quality.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual shift from leveraging a single, unified (but noisy) global memory to utilizing multiple localized (but clean) spatial memories is an insightful and compelling approach. It addresses a well-known physical and algorithmic artifact in 3D-conditioned generation—error accumulation in pose and depth estimation—by bounding the conditioning signal to localized views. This is a highly logical alternative to increasingly complex and computationally heavy global fusion algorithms. However, assessing the true novelty of the "weaving controller" requires careful comparison against existing multi-view conditioning mechanisms or temporal attention modules used in baselines like CameraCtrl or MotionCtrl. The originality of the contribution is strong, provided the architectural execution uniquely solves the overlapping blending problem without simply reintroducing the artifacts it aims to fix.

## Technical Soundness
The underlying premise—that localized memory retrieval yields a lower-variance and cleaner conditioning signal than a globally fused scene—is physically grounded and theoretically sound. However, the soundness of the execution heavily depends on the operational details of the multi-anchor weaving controller.

A fundamental risk in substituting a single global memory with multiple overlapping local memories is the introduction of new generation artifacts. When local memories inevitably disagree on the geometry or texture of overlapping regions (due to depth estimation errors, occlusion, or dynamic objects), the weaving controller must resolve these conflicts. If the controller relies on simple averaging or standard cross-attention without explicit spatial alignment, it heavily risks producing ghosting, blurring, or sudden visual shifts when the generation transitions between memory anchors. The theoretical soundness of this framework hinges on whether the mathematical formulation of the weaving controller can gracefully reconcile these cross-view inconsistencies. This mechanism must be clearly defined in the architecture rather than treated as a black box.

Furthermore, the exact data representation of a "local geometric memory" (e.g., posed RGB-D frames, unprojected partial point clouds, or latent feature maps) dictates the theoretical bounds of what the model can achieve and must be rigorously specified in the text to validate the claims.

## Experimental Rigor
The paper's commitment to ablation and analysis studies that isolate key components (retrieval vs. weaving) is a strong indicator of experimental rigor. However, evaluating "long-term scene consistency" in generative video is notoriously difficult and highly prone to metric misalignment. 

If the evaluation relies solely on standard, short-term video quality metrics (like FVD) or frame-wise image metrics (like FID), the core claim of the paper remains unsupported. Rigorous validation of "world-consistent" generation over long horizons requires specific 3D or geometric consistency metrics. Appropriate evidence would include multi-view reprojection errors, long-term optical flow warping consistency, or long-term point tracking accuracy (e.g., using TAPVid or CoTracker across distant frames). Evaluating long-term consistency on standard short video clips (e.g., 16-24 frames), where global memory accumulation errors are largely negligible, would constitute a severe methodological flaw.

Moreover, video generation improvements frequently suffer from high variance across random seeds and trajectories. To rigorously substantiate the claim that AnchorWeave "significantly improves" scene consistency, it is critical that statistical variance (e.g., standard deviations or confidence intervals across multiple evaluation seeds) is reported. If human evaluation is utilized to measure visual quality, it should include statistical significance testing (e.g., p-values) and inter-rater reliability scores to ensure the preference ratios are statistically meaningful.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Reproducibility and Missing Code:** No code repository or reproducibility materials are mentioned in the provided metadata. Given the vast complexity of implementing 3D memory retrieval pools, depth/pose estimation pipelines, and a custom multi-anchor weaving controller, the lack of open-source code is a major barrier to verifying the claims and reproducing the baselines.
- **Computational and System Scaling Overheads:** While global reconstruction is avoided, the framework shifts the computational burden directly to the generative inference phase. Maintaining a growing database of local memories and simultaneously conditioning the model on multiple anchors via the weaving controller may introduce severe scaling issues. There is a verified risk that VRAM requirements scale linearly with the number of retrieved anchors, potentially leading to Out-Of-Memory (OOM) errors on standard hardware accelerators for long-horizon generation. The inference latency overhead of the retrieval step must be clearly accounted for to justify real-world utility.
- **Risk of Data Leakage:** In evaluating "world consistency," the construction of the test-time memory retrieval pool is critical. If the test-time local memory bank inadvertently includes spatial memories, camera poses, or views drawn from training scenes, it constitutes data leakage, artificially inflating the spatial consistency metrics.
- **Ethical and Policy Risks:** High-fidelity, camera-controllable video generation carries substantial dual-use risks, including the creation of convincing disinformation or unauthorized synthetic media. Furthermore, training datasets involving indoor scenes (e.g., ScanNet or RealEstate10K) often raise privacy and consent concerns. The submission must include a broader impact statement addressing these dual-use risks and dataset privacy considerations to align with community ethical standards.

1. **Strict Metric Definitions:** Explicit mathematical definitions or references for the quantitative metrics used to measure "long-term scene consistency" versus standard visual quality.
2. **Compute Profiling:** A quantitative comparison of VRAM usage, memory pool size, and inference latency (FPS) between AnchorWeave and global-memory baselines, clearly broken down by the length of the generated horizon.
3. **Baseline Fairness:** Confirmation that the baseline models used for comparison (e.g., existing global-memory approaches) were evaluated under identical computational budgets, network capacities, and sequence lengths.
4. **Dataset Train/Test Splits:** Clear documentation of train and test splits, with explicit algorithmic guarantees on how the local memory bank is constructed during inference to strictly prevent spatial data leakage from the training set.
5. **Limitations Discussion:** A dedicated discussion of the failure modes of the multi-anchor weaving process (e.g., what happens visually when local memories severely conflict or when retrieval fails to cover the target trajectory).
