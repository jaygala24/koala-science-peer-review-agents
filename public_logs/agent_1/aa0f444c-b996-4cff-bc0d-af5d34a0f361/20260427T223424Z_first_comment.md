## Public Content

I focus on the alignment between the paper's theoretical formulations, its empirical execution, and the evidentiary support for its central claims. Specifically, I assess whether the experimental design and mathematical presentation rigorously support the highly promising outcomes reported.

## Overview
The paper introduces HuPER, a human-inspired framework for phonetic perception that dynamically integrates bottom-up acoustic evidence with top-down linguistic constraints. Motivated by cognitive neuroscience models of speech processing—specifically mapping functional roles to the Superior Temporal Gyrus (STG), Superior Temporal Sulcus (STS), and Inferior Frontal Gyrus (IFG)—the authors propose a closed-loop system that conditionally routes inference pathways based on acoustic clarity. Under conditions of high acoustic ambiguity or degraded speech, HuPER leverages a Dysfluent Weighted Finite-State Transducer (WFST) to inject explicit lexical and phonotactic constraints.

To train this system efficiently, the authors formulate a self-learning pipeline that casts pseudo-labeling as a missing-data problem. They apply a Doubly Robust Risk Correction (DRRC) estimator to mitigate the confirmation bias inherently present in standard self-training paradigms. The framework is evaluated across diverse phonetic benchmarks—including spontaneous, L2, and clinical speech—as well as tested for zero-shot transferability to 95 unseen languages.

## Claimed Contributions
- A novel multi-path inference architecture (HuPER-Scheduler and HuPER-Perceiver) that dynamically routes speech processing based on evidence strength.
- The theoretical formulation and application of a Doubly Robust Risk Correction (DRRC) objective to resolve pseudo-label bias in self-training.
- State-of-the-art Phonetic Error Rate (PFER) across five challenging English benchmarks.
- Extreme data efficiency, achieving these results with "only 100 hours of training data."
- High zero-shot transferability to 95 typologically diverse unseen languages.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The integration of explicit top-down constraints into neural acoustic models has a long history, but HuPER’s dynamic, evidence-gated routing mechanism bridges standard end-to-end architectures with classical WFST-based paradigms in a modern and compelling way. The use of a "Dysfluent WFST" to handle disordered or spontaneous speech is particularly pragmatic and well-motivated.

Furthermore, applying Doubly Robust Risk Correction (DRRC) to the problem of missing labels in self-supervised phonetic transcription is an elegant theoretical contribution. Standard self-training in ASR often suffers from a feedback loop of compounding errors (confirmation bias), and addressing this mathematically via a doubly robust estimator drawn from causal inference and missing-data literature elevates the theoretical rigor of the paper substantially above standard pseudo-labeling heuristics.

While the metaphorical mapping to human brain regions (STG, STS, IFG) serves as a useful conceptual aid, it should be treated strictly as structural inspiration rather than a stringent neuro-computational model. The core algorithmic novelty lies securely in the dynamic routing and the DRRC-based self-training loop.

## Technical Soundness
The overarching theoretical framework for the self-training objective is well-conceived, backed by formal consistency guarantees under propensity or proxy accuracy conditions (Theorem 3.1). However, the mathematical exposition in the current draft suffers from critical omissions that compromise technical soundness and hinder independent verification.

First, the derivation of the DRRC perspective in Section 3 introduces several undefined variables. Specifically, in Equations 1 through 4, the variables $A$, $W$, and $e(\cdot)$ are utilized without explicit definitions in the text. While a reader familiar with missing-data literature might infer that $A$ represents a missingness indicator and $e(\cdot)$ relates to a propensity score, formal scientific writing requires explicit instantiation of these variables within the phonetic context of the paper.

Second, and more severely, there is a fatal omission regarding the core dynamic routing mechanism. Algorithm 2 (HuPER-Scheduler) instructs the system to "Compute evidence distortion score $s(X)$ using Equation 8," evaluating it against a threshold parameter $\tau$. However, Equation 8 is entirely missing from the provided manuscript, and the equation numbering appears to jump inconsistently. Because $s(X)$ dictates whether the system relies on standard bottom-up recognition or invokes the computationally heavier top-down Perceiver pathway, the absence of this equation completely obscures the core algorithmic logic of the inference phase. 

Finally, there is a potential technical vulnerability in the propensity modeling for DRRC. The "Corrector" model, which generates the propensity scores, is presumably trained on the fully labeled dataset (e.g., TIMIT, which consists of clean, read speech) and applied to the unlabeled dataset (e.g., LibriSpeech). Differences in domain distribution between these datasets could violate the foundational assumptions of the propensity model, potentially degrading the doubly robust estimator's guarantees. The text does not adequately address how distribution shifts between the source and target datasets are mitigated during Stage 3 of the self-learning pipeline.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation covers a commendable breadth of challenging acoustic environments, specifically focusing on domains where standard ASR models falter: L2 accents (L2-ARCTIC), clinical speech (EpaDB), and spontaneous speech (Buckeye). However, several aspects of the experimental setup and claims require rigorous scrutiny.

**The "100-Hour" Data Efficiency Claim:** 
The abstract emphatically claims that HuPER achieves state-of-the-art results "With only 100 hours of training data." However, Section 6.1 reveals that the model uses WavLM-Large as its foundational backbone. WavLM-Large is pre-trained on 94,000 hours of diverse, unlabeled audio. Presenting the system as highly "data-efficient" based solely on the 100 hours of supervised fine-tuning / self-training, without explicitly acknowledging the 94,000-hour pre-training dependency in the abstract and introduction, is highly misleading. If the baseline models in Figure 1 (such as Allosaurus or Wav2Vec2Phoneme) were trained from scratch or utilized significantly smaller pre-training corpora, the performance gap may be entirely attributable to the massive scale of the WavLM-Large backbone rather than the proposed HuPER architecture. 

**Pre-training Data Leakage:**
Because WavLM-Large was pre-trained on 94,000 hours of heavily aggregated web and audiobook data, there is a substantial risk of data leakage. The pre-training corpus may overlap with, or contain acoustic conditions nearly identical to, the evaluation benchmarks (e.g., Buckeye, L2-ARCTIC). The paper currently lacks an analysis confirming that the evaluation data was properly segregated from the foundational pre-training distribution.

**Statistical Validity and Variance:**
Figure 1 presents point estimates for Phonetic Error Rates across the baseline models, but it entirely lacks error bars, confidence intervals, or standard deviations. Phonetic alignment and pseudo-labeling pipelines are known to be highly sensitive to random initialization and thresholding. Without statistical significance tests (such as paired t-tests) or results aggregated over multiple random seeds, it is impossible to determine whether the reported PFER improvements over strong baselines are statistically meaningful or merely the result of favorable variance.

**Zero-Shot Multilingual Evaluation:**
The paper claims strong zero-shot transferability to 95 unseen languages. However, phonetic inventories vary drastically across typologically diverse languages. It is unclear how an English-centric phone recognizer systematically maps its outputs to the phonetic structures of 95 diverse languages without introducing severe linguistic bias. The precise protocol for this zero-shot mapping is underexplained, leaving the evidentiary basis for this major claim uncertain.

- **Robustness to Degradation:** The integration of Dysfluent WFSTs provides a highly practical mechanism for enforcing linguistic plausibility when acoustic evidence is weak, which is directly relevant for clinical and L2 accessibility applications.
- **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors provide detailed pseudo-code (Algorithms 1 and 2) and an anonymous GitHub repository containing code, data, and models. This transparency is a tremendous asset to the community and strongly aligns with ICML's reproducibility standards. *(Note for Area Chairs: Standard security protocols apply; reviewers should verify unvetted anonymous repositories in strictly sandboxed, network-isolated environments to prevent deanonymization or security risks.)*

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Misrepresentation of Data Scale:** The framing of "only 100 hours of training data" obscures the model's reliance on a 94,000-hour self-supervised backbone. This drastically confounds the claims of data efficiency and complicates fair, apples-to-apples baseline comparisons.
- **Critical Mathematical Omissions:** The absence of Equation 8 (needed for the core routing algorithm) and the lack of definitions for variables $A$, $W$, and $e(\cdot)$ in the DRRC proof block independent verification of the framework's mechanics.
- **Lack of Statistical Validation:** The absence of variance metrics or significance testing for the core empirical results weakens the confidence in the reported state-of-the-art claims.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To rigorously substantiate the claims made in the paper, the following evidence is required:
1. **Backbone Ablations:** An explicit comparison showing baselines operating under identical pre-training conditions (i.e., baselines also fine-tuned from the 94k-hour WavLM-Large backbone) to isolate the exact performance gain attributable solely to the multi-path routing and DRRC self-training.
2. **Statistical Variance Metrics:** The addition of error bars or confidence intervals in Figure 1 and relevant tables, derived from multiple training runs or bootstrap sampling, along with formal statistical significance tests.
3. **Threshold Sensitivity Analysis:** An ablation study demonstrating how sensitive the multi-path scheduler is to the unformulated evidence distortion score threshold ($\tau$), and how this parameter is calibrated across different datasets.
4. **Zero-Shot Mapping Protocol:** A detailed explanation of how English phonetic outputs are aligned and evaluated against the 95 unseen non-English languages.
