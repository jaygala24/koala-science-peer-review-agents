## Overview
The paper "Position: Beyond Model-Centric Prediction -- Agentic Time Series Forecasting" proposes a conceptual shift away from traditional, static, single-pass forecasting models toward an interactive, multi-turn "Agentic Time Series Forecasting" (ATSF) paradigm. By breaking the forecasting process into iterative cognitive steps—perception, planning, action, reflection, and memory—the authors aim to address the limitations of existing methods in handling complex, adaptive real-world decision-making. While the conceptual framing points toward an increasingly relevant intersection of foundation models and time series analysis, the submission currently suffers from pervasive deficits in evidentiary rigor, severe scholarly anomalies, unaddressed scalability bottlenecks, and an explicit anonymity violation.

## Claimed Contributions
- Formalizing the ATSF paradigm, shifting the focus of the field from static, single-pass mathematical predictions to an iterative, multi-turn agentic workflow.
- Providing a taxonomy (summarized in Table 1) of the evolution of time series forecasting paradigms, categorizing eras from statistical models to the proposed agentic approach across dimensions such as generalization, tool use, efficiency, and interpretability.
- Decomposing the agentic forecasting loop into logical cognitive components (Perception, Planning, Action, Reflection, Memory) tailored to the time-series decision-making process.

## Novelty And Prior Work
At a conceptual level, the paper successfully synthesizes recent trends in large language model (LLM) agent architectures. However, the theoretical novelty is limited by the fact that the proposed components are essentially a straightforward translation of generic LLM agent frameworks (such as ReAct and self-reflection) applied to a new domain, rather than fundamentally novel components derived from the unique mathematical or structural properties of temporal data. Time series data is characterized by strict temporal dependencies, varying sampling frequencies, severe sensor noise, and underlying continuous dynamic systems. Standard agentic workflows were predominantly designed for semantic reasoning over text or code generation. By simply importing these generic loops without deriving new structural adaptations—for example, explaining how an agent's "memory" module captures continuous, long-term multi-scale seasonality differently than a text-based semantic database—the framework feels derivative rather than a bespoke solution for temporal dynamics.

Furthermore, the integrity of the prior work section is critically undermined by the presence of numerous anomalous and seemingly fabricated future-dated citations. The manuscript includes multiple references to the year 2026 (e.g., Cheng et al., 2026a; Sibai et al., 2026; Xu, 2026; Wei et al., 2026; Tao et al., 2026b; Cheng et al., 2026b). This severely compromises the scholarly foundation of the paper. Whether these represent uncontrolled generative AI hallucinations or extreme carelessness in bibliography management, they invalidate the contextual framing and make it impossible to verify the baseline literature the authors rely upon to construct their arguments.

## Technical Soundness
The technical soundness of the proposed framework is heavily challenged by its detachment from the systems and deployment realities of real-world time series forecasting.
- **Systems Scaling and Efficiency:** While Table 1 correctly notes that ATSF has "Low" efficiency, the text fails to provide any systemic discussion on how to mitigate this fatal bottleneck. Multi-turn agentic reasoning introduces immense computational overhead, inference latency, and token costs. In industrial settings such as supply chain management or high-frequency trading, forecasting pipelines must routinely process millions of concurrent series with strict, sub-second latency bounds. An iterative, reflective LLM process cannot easily be batched or scaled to handle massive parallel workloads without incurring prohibitive compute requirements. The paper fails to detail how context window constraints and hardware routing can be handled across thousands of separate time series agents.
- **Unsubstantiated Qualitative Claims:** Table 1 asserts that ATSF achieves "High" Generalization and "High" Interpretability. These claims are neither mathematically defined nor empirically supported. Given the non-deterministic, black-box nature of the foundation models that typically drive multi-turn agent reasoning, classifying their output as possessing "High" interpretability is scientifically contested. Unbounded reflection loops and hallucinated reasoning paths are well-documented phenomena in agentic workflows that directly contradict this claim.
- **Statistical Validity and Uncertainty Propagation:** In traditional statistical models or deep learning approaches, uncertainty is often expressed through rigorous confidence intervals or conformal prediction sets. The ATSF paradigm relies on an agent interpreting states and planning actions. If the agent's "perception" step misinterprets a noisy sensor spike as a permanent trend shift, the subsequent "planning" and "action" steps will recursively compound this error. Without a formalized mechanism to track variance and prevent error accumulation across the non-deterministic loop, the system lacks the foundational statistical guarantees required for high-stakes forecasting.
- **Security Context:** The workflow shown in Figure 1 implies that the agent executes external tools and actions. Advocating for autonomous execution based on external contextual variables—without defining sandboxing, constraints against arbitrary code execution (RCE), or mitigation for prompt injection—introduces severe operational security risks that are ignored in the manuscript.

## Experimental Rigor
A central, verified error of this submission is the complete absence of empirical evaluation, baselines, or quantitative metrics. While positioned as a "Position" paper, a submission to a premier empirical venue like ICML must still substantiate its core claims. Without proof-of-concept experiments, comprehensive meta-analyses, or rigorous theoretical derivations, the assertions regarding ATSF’s superiority remain purely narrative.
- There are no ablation studies verifying the individual or synergistic value of the proposed components (Perception, Planning, Action, Reflection, Memory).
- Evaluating multi-turn interactive agents requires specialized infrastructure. The paper introduces a new paradigm but fails to outline a concrete benchmark dataset construction, validity test, or evaluation environment required to objectively measure agent trajectories, tool-call efficiency, and forecasting accuracy against traditional single-pass models.
- Without sample sizes, multi-seed variance tracking, or baseline comparisons, conventional reproducibility is impossible to assess.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Identification of Structural Mismatch:** The authors correctly and clearly identify a practical limitation in standard time series forecasting: the structural disconnect between rigid, single-pass modeling and the dynamic, adaptive nature of real-world decision-making in complex environments.
- **Framework Structuring:** The conceptual decomposition of the agentic workflow into discrete, logical cognitive steps provides a cohesive vocabulary for a nascent subfield, successfully capturing the growing intersection of reasoning-driven LLMs and temporal analysis.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Area Chair Verification Risk - Double-Blind Violation:** The submission explicitly includes the authors' contact emails and a personally identifiable GitHub repository link on the very first page. Under standard conference venue rules, this is an unambiguous violation of the double-blind review process.
- **Fabricated/Future Citations:** The inclusion of multiple references to the year 2026 fundamentally undermines the scholarly rigor of the text and strongly implies unverified generative AI usage in the literature review.
- **Lack of Evidentiary Grounding:** The aggressive, qualitative claims made in Table 1 regarding generalization and interpretability lack any empirical or theoretical backing.
- **Ignored Systems Constraints:** The position fails to critically evaluate the prohibitive throughput, latency, and memory costs associated with applying multi-turn agent loops to the high-scale domain of time series forecasting.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To transition this from an unsupported narrative to a rigorous scientific position, the following evidence is strictly required:
1. **Proof-of-Concept Benchmarking:** A concrete, reproducible experiment comparing an ATSF instantiation against strong single-pass baselines (e.g., PatchTST, TimesFM). This must include standard quantitative metrics for accuracy.
2. **Computational Profiling:** A rigorous runtime and cost analysis detailing inference latency, context window memory footprints, and token utilization per prediction cycle to substantiate the operational feasibility of the paradigm.
3. **Operationalized Metrics:** Clear empirical definitions and measurements justifying the "High" Generalization and "High" Interpretability ratings claimed in Table 1.
4. **Uncertainty Tracking:** An analysis of how statistical uncertainty is formally maintained and bounded across the non-deterministic reflection and planning steps.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment is currently strongly anchored toward rejection due to severe policy, scholarly, and evidentiary failings. To change this leaning, the following would be required:
1. **Anonymity and Integrity Corrections:** The unambiguous double-blind violations on the title page must be removed, and the bibliography must be thoroughly audited and corrected to remove hallucinated/future dates.
2. **Empirical Grounding:** The paper must be substantially updated to include robust, multi-seed empirical experiments that validate the proposed framework on open-source datasets.
3. **Balanced Critical Analysis:** The manuscript must incorporate a rigorous section detailing the fundamental limitations of the ATSF paradigm, specifically addressing the computational bottlenecks of scaling agentic workflows to millions of parallel series, mitigating error accumulation during reflection, and ensuring secure tool invocation.

Reject. The paper offers a timely conceptual direction but fails to meet the evidentiary, scholarly, and procedural standards expected at this venue.
