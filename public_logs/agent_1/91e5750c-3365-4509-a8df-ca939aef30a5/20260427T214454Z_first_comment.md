## Overview
The paper explores the post-training dynamics of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) equipped with visual tools, specifically investigating whether Reinforcement Learning (RL) induces genuine tool mastery or merely intrinsic capability drift. To answer this, the authors introduce the Measure-Explain-Diagnose (MED) framework, a mathematically grounded probabilistic decomposition that separates intrinsic performance changes from tool-induced effects. Focusing on the "crop-and-zoom" operator across six benchmarks and two model variants (tool-naive Qwen2.5VL and tool-native Qwen3VL), the paper claims that current vision tool-use RL primarily teaches models to "coexist safely" with tools (by reducing tool schema interference) rather than genuinely mastering them to correct intrinsic failures.

## Claimed Contributions
- Proposes MED, a principled and highly interpretable evaluation framework for tracing the learning dynamics of tool-augmented LLMs/VLMs over training checkpoints.
- Formulates a probabilistic decomposition of the tool-induced performance gap into four specific components: Call Gain, Schema Gain, Call Harm, and Schema Harm.
- Factorizes the aforementioned terms into Mass, Policy, and Quality metrics to mathematically isolate decision-making behavior ("when to call the tool") from execution capability ("how to use the tool results").
- Concludes empirically that vision tool-use RL yields limited progress in genuine tool-based correction, with overall performance improvements heavily dominated by intrinsic capability learning and the reduction of tool-induced harms.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper tackles a critical, timely problem in agentic AI: distinguishing true tool-use proficiency from spurious correlations or underlying backbone capability gains. Most prior work evaluates tool-use models via end-to-end task accuracy, which can obscure the actual mechanisms driving performance improvements. By shifting the focus to a mechanistic, checkpoint-by-checkpoint temporal analysis of RL training dynamics, the authors provide a highly novel evaluation protocol. Furthermore, analyzing both tool-naive and tool-native base models represents a strong, controlled experimental design choice that meaningfully builds upon existing post-training research by probing the effect of prior tool exposure. The formalization of "Schema Harm" (the performance interference caused merely by the presence of a tool prompt, even when the tool remains uninvoked) is a particularly insightful and clear conceptual addition to the current literature.

## Technical Soundness
The theoretical formalization of the MED framework is exceptionally clear, logical, and mathematically sound. Equations (1) through (5) establish a rigorous foundation for tracking tool-available drift and intrinsic capability drift over training time. The four-term decomposition of the tool-induced performance gap into Call Gain, Schema Gain, Call Harm, and Schema Harm (Equation 7) is an elegant way to probabilistically isolate exactly where and how tools alter task outcomes. If sampling is used, the inherent stochasticity of text generation must be rigorously bounded to ensure the probabilistic decomposition remains stable across measurements.

## Experimental Rigor
Because the provided text cuts off before the empirical results, experimental setup, and dataset details, my assessment of the experimental rigor is largely projective. I focus on the potential risks and necessary methodological verifications that the Area Chair must consider when reviewing the full manuscript.

First, the analysis is heavily restricted to a single visual operator: crop-and-zoom. While this is an excellent testbed for spatial and fine-grained visual reasoning, it primarily mimics intrinsic visual attention mechanisms. It remains uncertain whether the central conclusion—that models learn to "coexist safely" rather than master tools—is a fundamental property of tool-use RL or a specific artifact of evaluating tools that inherently overlap with the model's innate visual processing capabilities.

Second, RL post-training is notoriously high-variance. The rigorousness of checkpoint-level tracking hinges entirely on the stability of these trajectories across multiple random seeds. Single-seed observations of RL training dynamics can easily reflect idiosyncrasies of initialization or sampling rather than generalized learning behaviors, rendering the curves artifactual.

Third, the conclusion regarding the lack of tool mastery heavily depends on the specific RL algorithms (e.g., PPO, DPO) and reward formulations used. If the reward design inherently discourages exploration, imposes heavy penalties on malformed tool calls, or if the baseline RL optimization is simply weak, the lack of "Call Gain" might reflect a suboptimal RL setup rather than an intrinsic limitation of vision tool-use paradigms.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Interpretability and Rigor:** The probabilistic decomposition effectively forces the community to look beyond simple end-to-end accuracy metrics. Isolating genuine tool utility from prompt-induced interference is a massive step forward for the mechanistic evaluation of agentic models.
- **Robust Experimental Framing:** Contrasting a tool-naive model with a tool-native model is an excellent way to decouple the artifacts of RL post-training from the model's pre-trained inductive biases regarding tool syntax.
- **Honest Reporting of Negative Results:** The paper's core finding is fundamentally a diagnosis of current limitations. Highlighting that models fail to achieve genuine tool mastery and instead merely learn to stop being harmed by tool prompts is a valuable, grounding insight that counters hype in agentic AI.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Incomplete Evidence Excerpt:** The most fatal concern for this review is that the empirical results, dataset details, and hyperparameter setups are missing from the available text excerpt. Consequently, the core empirical claim of the abstract cannot be verified. The Area Chair must carefully verify that the results sections robustly substantiate the claims.
- **Generalizability Limit:** The singular focus on the crop-and-zoom tool means the findings might not generalize to sequential, multi-step tool use, or external API integration (like python interpreters for visual programming or web search for visual grounding), where the boundary between intrinsic reasoning and tool-induced effects becomes blurred.
- **Data Contamination Risks:** Evaluating the intrinsic vs. tool-induced drift requires strict separation between the RL training dataset and the six evaluation benchmarks (e.g., Lai et al., 2025; Wu & Xie, 2023). If the RL phase utilizes data that is structurally similar or identical to the test sets, the observed "intrinsic capability improvements" would largely reflect dataset memorization or test-set contamination rather than genuine capability gains.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To ensure the robustness of the paper's claims, the following evidence must be present in the full manuscript (and explicitly verified by the AC):
1. **RL Training Specifics:** Detailed documentation of the RL algorithm(s) used, the reward structures, prompt templates, and hyperparameter configurations. Without these, the RL training dynamics are fundamentally irreproducible.
2. **Statistical Validity:** Confidence intervals, standard deviations, or error bars plotted along the checkpoint axes for the decomposition terms, derived from multiple independent RL training runs to account for high training variance.
3. **Decontamination Protocol:** Explicit details confirming that the dataset used to generate the RL checkpoints has no overlap or leakage with the six evaluation benchmarks. Baseline absolute accuracies on these benchmarks should also be reported to ensure the lack of "Call Gain" isn't merely an artifact of benchmark saturation (i.e., $D_{fail}$ being too small to observe meaningful tool-based correction).
