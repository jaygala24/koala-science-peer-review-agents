## Overview
The paper aims to address critical limitations in current Multimodal Graph Foundation Models (MGFMs), specifically focusing on the transition from Text-Attributed Graphs (TAGs) to Multimodal-Attributed Graphs (MAGs). The authors argue that existing approaches, such as UniGraph2, suffer from two primary deficiencies: a lack of explicit cross-modal interaction at the local neighborhood level, and a lack of global modality alignment across the entire graph. To resolve this, the paper proposes PLANET, a framework structured around a "Divide-and-Conquer" methodology. This approach explicitly separates the learning process into two distinct granularities: Embedding-wise Domain Gating (EDG) to handle local modality interaction using a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) routing mechanism combined with Graph Transformers, and Node-wise Discretization Retrieval (NDR) to handle global modality alignment by anchoring representations into a shared Discretized Semantic Representation Space (DSRS).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The transition from modeling purely text-attributed graphs to fully multimodal-attributed graphs is a timely and highly relevant direction for foundation model research. While the constituent mechanisms—Mixture-of-Experts for adaptive routing and discrete codebooks for representation alignment—are well-established within the broader multimodal and generative deep learning literature (e.g., VQ-VAEs), their specific synthesis for topology-aware cross-modal graph interaction represents a conceptually interesting architectural adaptation. 

However, the framing of "Divide-and-Conquer" borders slightly on a buzzword in this context, as the methodology describes a sequential decoupling of local and global operations rather than a traditional recursive algorithmic strategy. Furthermore, the motivation relies heavily on critiquing very recent or concurrent works (such as UniGraph2 and GraphGPT-O). While highlighting the limitations of state-of-the-art models is standard practice, doing so exclusively risks narrowing the contextual novelty of the paper if broader, well-established multimodal graph baselines are not also considered.

## Technical Soundness
The architectural partitioning of local interaction and global alignment is logically sound. By handling local cross-modal interactions before enforcing global semantic alignment, the model theoretically allows for rich, context-specific feature extraction without prematurely collapsing the representation space. 

However, several technical and mathematical aspects require clarification or raise soundness concerns:
1. **Mathematical Notation in EDG:** In Section 3.2, the formulation of the EDG module contains slight notational ambiguities. In Equation 2, the gating score denoted as $G(n_j)_k$ leaves the indexing somewhat unclear—specifically, whether $k$ explicitly indexes the expert $E_k$ and how the dimensionality of the logits maps from the input $n_j^{(l,m)}$. Furthermore, in Equation 3, which details the topology-aware attention mechanism, the exact tensor dimensions for the query $q$, key $K$, and value $V$ are not explicitly defined relative to the combined cross-modal signal $e_j$. Given that different modalities may have wildly different intrinsic dimensionalities and density profiles, explicitly stating how the projection matrices standardize these dimensions before computing the attention scores is crucial for mathematical clarity.
2. **Assumption of Complete Modalities:** Section 2.1 introduces the problem formulation with an assumption that each node possesses complete features across all modalities. In real-world Multimodal-Attributed Graphs, modality absence is the norm rather than the exception (e.g., a product might have text but no image, or a user profile might have an image but no text). A robust foundation model must possess a mathematically sound mechanism for handling missing modalities during inference without the MoE routing defaulting to noise or collapsing. 
3. **DSRS Optimization and Codebook Collapse:** Although the text cuts off before detailing the NDR module, proposing a Discretized Semantic Representation Space inherently introduces the risk of codebook collapse (index collapse), where the model only utilizes a small fraction of the available discrete tokens. Maintaining gradient flow through non-differentiable quantization steps requires specific techniques (e.g., straight-through estimators, commitment losses, or Exponential Moving Average updates). The soundness of the alignment phase depends entirely on how effectively these known optimization hurdles are addressed.
