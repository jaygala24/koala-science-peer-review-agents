## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the experimental design, baseline stringency, data curation (with an emphasis on contamination risks), and the statistical validity of the empirical claims supporting the proposed method.

## Overview
The paper investigates an intriguing inference-time phenomenon in large reasoning models (LRMs): when prompted with multiple questions simultaneously, models naturally compress their Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning traces, a behavior the authors term "self-compression" induced by "multi-question contextual pressure." Building on this observation, the paper proposes ConPress, a self-supervised fine-tuning (SFT) pipeline. By querying the model with multi-question prompts, using rejection sampling to isolate correct but compressed reasoning traces, and subsequently fine-tuning the model to produce these compressed traces in single-question settings, the authors aim to improve inference efficiency. The paper claims substantial token usage reductions on standard mathematics reasoning benchmarks.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Empirical Phenomenon Identification:** The identification and characterization of the self-compression phenomenon in LRMs, supported by scaling analyses and specificity tests indicating that the multi-question format structurally induces shorter reasoning traces.
- **The ConPress Methodology:** A lightweight, self-supervised fine-tuning pipeline that eschews expensive reinforcement learning (RL) processes, complex manual pruning heuristics, or reliance on external, proprietary teacher models.
- **Efficiency Improvements:** Claimed reasoning token usage reductions of up to 59% on MATH500 and 33% on AIME25, utilizing a modest fine-tuning dataset of 8,000 examples, purportedly while maintaining "competitive accuracy."

## Novelty And Prior Work
The empirical observation that large reasoning models autonomously compress their reasoning paths under the "pressure" of a multi-question prompt is a novel, highly interesting, and potentially easily reproducible quirk of current LRM decoding dynamics. Most contemporary methods for reducing inference token consumption rely heavily on either explicit reinforcement learning (RLHF/RLAIF) with token-level penalties, or distillation from significantly larger, proprietary teacher models. The proposal to exploit the model's own natural response to a structural prompt modification as a self-supervised distillation signal is an elegant, practical alternative that merits serious consideration and bypasses heavy engineering overhead.

## Technical Soundness
The fundamental mechanism of ConPress is conceptually sound and clearly presented, leveraging a multi-to-single distillation approach. However, there are structural concerns regarding the rejection sampling step. The paper transparently documents that model accuracy degrades as the number of simultaneous questions increases. Consequently, filtering multi-question traces strictly for ground-truth final-answer correctness may introduce a severe selection bias. 

The 8,000 correct, compressed traces might disproportionately represent problems that the model found inherently easy, or instances where the model successfully employed memorized shortcuts rather than computing a sound but abbreviated logical path. If the model is effectively rewarded for taking unsound logical leaps or outputting memorized answers under pressure, the resulting single-question fine-tuned model may suffer in its ability to generalize true "efficient reasoning" to out-of-distribution or highly complex problems. The paper acknowledges an "accuracy-efficiency trade-off," but the technical soundness of the pipeline depends entirely on ensuring that the extracted compressed traces represent valid, rigorous reasoning rather than degenerate shortcuts.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical investigation of the phenomenon exhibits several strong points, alongside critical gaps in baseline comparisons and statistical reporting.

- **Strengths in Evaluation Setup:** The authors thoughtfully provide full density plots for reasoning length distributions, offering a much clearer picture of the variance and distributional shift than simple point estimates. The specificity analysis is highly effective in ruling out simple confounders (e.g., verifying that simply appending long text or a difficult single question does not induce the same compression). Evaluating on AIME25 is a strong methodological choice, as it significantly mitigates the benchmark contamination risks typically associated with evaluating math reasoning on modern LRMs.
- **Lack of Statistical Uncertainty:** Given the massive variance in reasoning lengths shown in the density plots (often ranging over thousands of tokens), reporting average metrics (e.g., token length, relative accuracy in the specificity analysis) without any measure of statistical uncertainty (standard deviations, confidence intervals, or significance tests) weakens the empirical claims. It is currently difficult to ascertain whether minor differences in compression rates across prompt modifications are statistically significant or merely sampling noise.
- **Confounding Hyperparameters:** Reasoning lengths in LRMs are notoriously sensitive to generation hyperparameters (e.g., temperature, top-p, and particularly `max_tokens`). It is not definitively established whether the self-compression under contextual pressure is a pure reasoning adaptation, or an artifact of the model implicitly estimating an absolute token generation budget (e.g., an 8k or 32k context window limit) and truncating its thoughts accordingly when it identifies multiple questions in the prompt.
- **Contamination Risks:** The paper utilizes questions drawn from the MATH dataset to create the 8,000 fine-tuning examples, while evaluating on MATH500 and AMC. The MATH dataset notoriously contains historical AMC problems. If the exact splits and stringent deduplication processes are not rigorously defined, there is a high risk of train-test leakage that could artificially inflate the final evaluations.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Novel Empirical Finding:** The self-compression observation is a compelling behavioral finding that contributes meaningfully to our understanding of LRM context and generation dynamics.
- **Simplicity and Accessibility:** By relying entirely on self-generated reasoning and standard SFT, the ConPress method lowers the barrier to entry for optimizing inference costs, avoiding the complex engineering overhead of RL.
- **Robust Specificity Analysis:** The control experiments effectively isolate the multi-question structure as the primary driver of the phenomenon, successfully anticipating and addressing obvious alternative hypotheses.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Absence of Critical Single-Question Baselines:** To prove that the multi-question contextual pressure is strictly necessary for extracting efficient training data, ConPress must be compared against standard single-question length-filtering baselines. For instance, a Best-of-N sampling strategy on a single question, where the model generates multiple traces and is fine-tuned only on the shortest correct one, or simply prompting the base model to "be concise" during SFT generation. Without this, the performance gains cannot be definitively attributed to the multi-question phenomenon.
- **Vague Accuracy Trade-offs:** The abstract's claim of "competitive accuracy" masks the precise magnitude of the performance drop. Given that the multi-question extraction step degrades accuracy, the community needs to see the exact accuracy-efficiency Pareto front on single-question decoding post-SFT, particularly on the hardest subsets of AIME25, to ensure the model has not simply lost its ability to perform deep Chain-of-Thought deliberation.
- **Potential Data Contamination (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** If the 8,000 SFT examples drawn from the MATH dataset overlap with the MATH500 test set, or contain historical AMC problems not strictly deduplicated from the AMC evaluation set, the reported results on these benchmarks would be invalid.
- **Generalization Beyond Mathematics:** The evaluation focuses heavily on structured math reasoning (MATH, AIME, AMC). It remains an open question whether the multi-question compression phenomenon and the ConPress methodology seamlessly generalize to other long-horizon tasks, such as complex coding or open-ended logical deduction, where correctness is harder to verify and reasoning steps are less strictly sequential.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully validate the claims, the following experiments and clarifications are necessary:
1. **Isolated Baselines:** An explicit empirical comparison between ConPress and a baseline fine-tuned on the shortest correct reasoning traces generated from standard single-question prompts (e.g., via rejection sampling of multiple generations per prompt). A baseline using explicit length-constraint prompting ("Be concise") during SFT data generation should also be included.
2. **Explicit Performance Deltas:** Precise, numerical accuracy figures for the fine-tuned models on MATH500 and AIME25 compared to the base models, rather than qualitative terms like "competitive."
3. **Data Contamination Assurances:** Explicit confirmation of which subsets/splits of the MATH dataset were used for the fine-tuning examples, and the exact deduplication protocols implemented to guarantee zero overlap with the MATH500 and AMC test sets.
4. **Statistical Reporting:** The inclusion of standard deviations, standard errors, or confidence intervals for the average token lengths and relative accuracies reported in the specificity and scaling analyses.
5. **Generation Constraints:** Clarification of the specific generation hyperparameters used during the multi-question inference phase, particularly the `max_tokens` parameter, to rule out implicit length budgeting artifacts.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift positively toward a strong accept if the authors can demonstrate that ConPress outperforms a naive single-question length-filtered baseline in terms of the accuracy-efficiency trade-off. Furthermore, definitive proof that the MATH fine-tuning dataset is strictly isolated from the MATH500/AMC evaluation sets is required to rule out a fatal flaw of data leakage. Providing statistical variance measures to back up the empirical claims, along with exact accuracy degradation numbers that confirm the model retains its ability to solve the hardest problems, would fully solidify the paper's standing. Conversely, if the method fails to outperform simple single-question length-filtering, or if severe train-test contamination is confirmed, the contribution of the proposed pipeline would be severely undermined.
