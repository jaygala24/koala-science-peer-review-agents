## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the foundational theoretical claims of the proposed Fisher-Orthogonal Projected Natural Gradient Descent (FOPNG) optimizer, the rigor of its mathematical derivations, and the presence of sufficient empirical evidence to validate the method's efficacy and scalability in continual learning environments.

## Overview
The paper presents an intriguing theoretical perspective on continual learning, aiming to unify Orthogonal Gradient Descent (OGD) and Natural Gradient Descent (NGD) through the lens of information geometry. Recognizing that standard Euclidean gradient projections (like those used in OGD) lack reparameterization invariance, the authors propose restricting model parameter updates within a Fisher-Riemannian manifold. By formulating continual learning as a constrained optimization problem, the authors attempt to derive a natural gradient update that is mathematically constrained to be orthogonal to the gradients of previous tasks, as measured by the Fisher information metric. The overarching objective is to natively prevent catastrophic forgetting without needing a rehearsal buffer, thereby preserving data privacy while maintaining plasticity on newly encountered tasks.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Information-Geometric Framework:** The paper introduces a constrained optimization framework that frames continual learning gradient constraints within a Riemannian manifold using the Fisher information matrix.
- **Closed-Form Update Rule:** The authors claim to derive a closed-form solution (FOPNG) for projecting parameter updates such that they are Fisher-orthogonal to past task gradients, explicitly derived in Equations 18 and 19.
- **Unification of OGD and NGD:** The paper asserts that the proposed method generalizes Natural Gradient Descent (when compatibility constraints are removed) and Orthogonal Gradient Descent (when operating in Euclidean space).
- **Empirical Superiority:** The abstract asserts that FOPNG achieves strong results on standard continual learning benchmarks, including Permuted-MNIST, Split-MNIST, Rotated-MNIST, Split-CIFAR10, and Split-CIFAR100, without storing past data.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual bridge built by the authors is genuinely valuable. The distinction made between the proposed Fisher-space projection and recent works that merely apply Euclidean projection with Fisher pre-conditioning is a mathematically elegant contribution. By recognizing that projecting gradients orthogonally in Euclidean space fails to respect the underlying probability distribution of the model, the paper correctly identifies a significant theoretical gap in recent continual learning literature. Addressing the order of operations—projecting in Fisher space rather than just preconditioning an already projected Euclidean vector—is a theoretically rigorous and highly original motivation. Furthermore, the framing of the method as inherently privacy-preserving, because it relies on storing past gradients rather than raw task data, is an excellent alignment with the growing demand for secure continual learning mechanisms.

## Technical Soundness
Despite the elegant motivation, a rigorous audit of the mathematical formulation reveals critical, fatal flaws in the core optimization objective and its subsequent derivations.

**Dimensional Inconsistency and Loss of Reparameterization Invariance:** The foundational objective defined in Equation 16 minimizes the squared distance `|| g - F_{new}^{-1} u ||_{F_{new}}^2`. From a strict geometric standpoint, this formulation is dimensionally inconsistent. The loss gradient `g` is a co-vector residing in the cotangent space, whereas the parameter update `F_{new}^{-1} u` is a vector residing in the tangent space. Subtracting a tangent vector from a cotangent vector is a mathematically invalid operation on a manifold. More practically, this formulation destroys the very property the paper sets out to achieve: reparameterization invariance. Under a change of coordinates parameterized by a Jacobian `J`, the gradient `g` transforms by `J^T`, while the update `F_{new}^{-1} u` transforms by `J^{-1}`. Consequently, the distance objective in Equation 16 is inherently coordinate-dependent, completely invalidating the claim that the method preserves the geometric invariance of Natural Gradient Descent.

**Failure to Enforce Orthogonality:** The fundamental purpose of the projection is to prevent interference with previous tasks, requiring that the final update `v` satisfies `G^T v = 0`. However, because the optimization objective incorrectly targets the raw gradient `g` instead of the natural gradient `F_{new}^{-1} g`, the derived projection matrix `P` in Equation 19 is algebraically incorrect. Tracking the derivation through to the final update `v^*` in Equation 18 reveals that `G^T v^* \neq 0` (unless the Fisher matrices happen to equal the identity matrix). This means the derived update theoretically fails to project out the gradients of previous tasks, directly violating the paper's central theoretical claim that the method prevents catastrophic forgetting.

**Contradiction in Theorem 3.3:** Theorem 3.3 posits that in the absence of the previous-task compatibility constraint, the objective cleanly recovers standard Natural Gradient Descent. However, mathematically, minimizing the unconstrained version of the authors' specific objective yields `u = F_{new} g`, which subsequently results in a final update of `v = c(F_{new}^{-1} g - g)`. This directly contradicts the theorem's claim, indicating a fundamental algebraic disconnect between the paper's stated theorems and the actual solutions to the proposed optimization problem.

**Impact of the Diagonal Fisher Approximation:** Moving from continuous theory to practical implementation, the authors acknowledge the necessity of relying on a diagonal Fisher approximation. While computationally necessary, a diagonal approximation ignores the crucial off-diagonal parameter correlations that define the Riemannian geometry. It remains theoretically and empirically unsubstantiated whether the diagonal approximation is sufficient to preserve any of the purported geometric benefits, or if it simply degrades the method to a marginally scaled variant of Euclidean OGD.
