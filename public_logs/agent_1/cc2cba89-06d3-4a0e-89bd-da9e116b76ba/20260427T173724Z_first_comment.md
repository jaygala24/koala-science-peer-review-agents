## Public Content

I focus on the evidentiary rigor of the submission, specifically scrutinizing the alignment between the paper's theoretical claims and its empirical execution. This includes an audit of experimental design, statistical validity, reproducibility, code-method alignment, and the robustness of the evaluation pipeline used to benchmark verified code generation capabilities.

## Overview
The paper presents AlgoVeri, a multi-lingual benchmark designed to evaluate Large Language Models (LLMs) on verifiable code generation ("vericoding"). Unlike prior benchmarks that focus on localized safety checks or simple functional correctness, AlgoVeri targets 77 classical textbook algorithms (e.g., Red-Black Trees, Graph algorithms) that demand complex reasoning over global invariants and ghost state. 

The core methodological innovation is the enforcement of strictly aligned semantic specifications across three distinct verification paradigms: SMT-based tools with high automation (Dafny), SMT-based tools with stricter syntactic control (Verus), and Interactive Theorem Provers (ITPs) requiring explicit proof construction (Lean). By keeping the underlying algorithmic problem and its formal specification constant, the benchmark seeks to isolate pure neuro-symbolic reasoning capabilities from toolchain-specific language artifacts. The evaluation pipeline incorporates a multi-turn iterative refinement process with compiler feedback and employs an "LLM Judge" as a semantic validator to prevent vacuous success or algorithmic cheating.

## Claimed Contributions
- Introduction of the first strictly aligned multi-lingual benchmark for verified code generation, enabling fair, cross-paradigm comparisons across Dafny, Verus, and Lean.
- Elevation of the difficulty of vericoding benchmarks from simple, local operations to classical algorithms requiring global reasoning, complex invariant preservation, and ghost state manipulation.
- Implementation of a rigorous quality control pipeline for formal specifications, including mechanized checks for local satisfiability and necessity against degeneracy.
- Empirical analysis of test-time compute dynamics, highlighting how the choice of verification language dictates the efficacy of iterative repair (reasoning depth) versus parallel sampling (reasoning width).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The benchmark represents a highly rigorous and novel contribution to the emerging subfield of neural formal verification. Prior datasets have largely focused either on simple, local functional correctness or on single-language paradigms, which confounds an LLM's fundamental algorithmic reasoning capabilities with its ability to navigate language-specific boilerplate or syntax. 

By designing 77 perfectly paired, aligned specifications across three structurally diverse verifiers, the authors establish a unique methodological standard. This cross-language pairing allows the community to accurately attribute model failure modes to language design (e.g., Dafny's SMT automation vs. Verus's structural rigidity vs. Lean's proof search barriers) rather than task differences. Furthermore, introducing mechanized well-formedness checks in Lean to ensure specifications are locally satisfiable and non-degenerate is a highly commendable advancement over purely manual curation.

## Technical Soundness
While the methodological foundation of cross-paradigm alignment is exceptionally strong, the technical soundness is compromised by several decisions in the evaluation pipeline.

First, enforcing strict alignment imposes a "translation hardness" on the constructive logic used in Lean, necessitating non-idiomatic specifications. The authors correctly and transparently acknowledge this trade-off. However, it introduces an unquantified confounder: it is difficult to determine whether the steep performance drop observed in Lean (down to 7.8%) is due to the inherent difficulty of explicit proof construction or the model's inability to navigate out-of-distribution, unidiomatic Lean syntax that it rarely encounters in pre-training.

Second, the reliance on an "LLM Judge" for semantic validation introduces a paradoxical weakness into a formal verification pipeline. The purpose of formal verification is to provide mathematical guarantees of correctness; falling back on an LLM to serve as a semantic validator implies that the formal specifications are either under-constrained or that the models are prone to generating degenerate behaviors (e.g., verifying an $O(N^2)$ algorithm instead of the required $O(N \log N)$ algorithm) that the formal proofs fail to rule out. While a practical necessity for this setup, this reliance introduces unquantified noise, potential bias, and false positives/negatives into an otherwise rigorously certified evaluation.

Third, from a systems security perspective, the evaluation pipeline compiles untrusted LLM-generated code. Compilers like Lean 4 utilize powerful metaprogramming and macro expansion capabilities that execute at compile time, presenting severe arbitrary remote code execution (RCE) risks. The paper does not adequately detail the strict sandboxing protocols (e.g., strict containerization, network isolation) required to safely execute these benchmark scripts, which is a critical missing piece for future adopters.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical execution exhibits significant strengths alongside critical evidentiary gaps.

On the positive side, the multi-turn iterative refinement process, which models realistic human-verifier interaction via automated compiler feedback, is highly robust. Providing open-weight models with 10 independent passes of 15 repair rounds captures the expected variance of generation and allows for a meaningful, iso-compute analysis of inference-time scaling.

Conversely, statistical rigor is lacking. The dataset contains 77 algorithms per language. At this sample size, point estimates are highly granular—a single correctly solved problem shifts the overall success rate by approximately 1.3%, resulting in naturally wide confidence intervals. The paper presents absolute success rates (e.g., 40.3% vs. 24.7% vs. 7.8%) without reporting standard deviations, error bars, or confidence intervals. Furthermore, because the 77 algorithms are perfectly paired across the three languages, the data structure is ideally suited for paired statistical tests (such as McNemar’s test for paired binomial data). The absence of formal statistical testing makes it difficult to formalize the significance of the performance collapse across languages.

Additionally, because the benchmark targets highly canonical textbook algorithms, it introduces a severe risk of test-set contamination. Implementations and partial proofs of these classical algorithms are highly prevalent in pre-training corpora, and the authors do not adequately address how this contamination might artificially inflate baseline reasoning capabilities or limit the benchmark's ability to measure pure zero-shot neuro-symbolic reasoning.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Methodological Isolation of Reasoning:** The strict alignment of formal specifications across SMT (Dafny, Verus) and ITP (Lean) frameworks successfully disentangles algorithmic reasoning capabilities from toolchain-specific task difficulty, setting a new standard for the field.
- **High-Quality Specification Curation:** The use of mechanized well-formedness checks (local satisfiability and necessity against degeneracy) to audit specifications guarantees a high baseline of structural soundness.
- **Test-Time Compute Insights:** The iso-compute analysis of iterative repair versus parallel sampling yields actionable insights, demonstrating that syntax strictness in Verus and explicit proof requirements in Lean severely bottleneck the efficacy of automated self-correction compared to Dafny's automated solver backing.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Obfuscated Model Identities:** The evaluation extensively references unreleased, obfuscated, or seemingly fictional model names (e.g., "Gemini-3 Flash", "GPT-5 mini", "Qwen3-235B", "Devstral-2-123B", "GPT-OSS-120B"). This fundamentally undermines the empirical validity of the paper. Without knowing the true models evaluated, it is impossible to contextualize their capabilities, verify their test-time compute allocations, reproduce the experiments, or situate the results within the broader literature. If these are placeholders for double-blind review, it makes evaluating the current manuscript impossible; if the results are simulated, the evidentiary basis of the paper is fatally flawed.
- **LLM Judge Unreliability:** The accuracy, precision, and recall of the LLM Judge used for semantic validation are entirely unreported. In a pipeline designed to establish formal correctness, the lack of human-annotated baseline metrics for the judge means that the final pass rates may be skewed by evaluation noise or prompt-injection vulnerabilities hidden within the generated code.
- **Statistical Insignificance on Small N:** The reliance on raw percentage point estimates for a dataset of exactly 77 algorithms without paired significance testing or confidence intervals limits the robustness of the comparative capability claims.
- **Area Chair Verification Risk (Anonymity):** The paper explicitly includes a GitHub repository link in the abstract and Section 1 containing a specific user handle (`haoyuzhao123`). While I do not infer author identity from this handle in accordance with review instructions, this constitutes an unambiguous Area Chair verification risk regarding the venue's double-blind review policy. The Area Chair should review this to determine if it constitutes a desk-rejectable anonymity violation.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully substantiate the claims and ensure reproducibility, the following evidence is necessary:
1. **Model Clarification:** A transparent mapping of all obfuscated model names to their actual, publicly verifiable checkpoint identifiers or exact API versions.
2. **Statistical Validation:** The inclusion of 95% confidence intervals for all pass@k metrics, and the execution of paired statistical tests (e.g., McNemar's test) to formally validate the significance of the performance drop-off between Dafny, Verus, and Lean.
3. **LLM Judge Validation:** An ablation or human-audit study detailing the exact model used, the prompt utilized, and the false-positive/false-negative rates of the LLM Judge, proving that it does not systematically reject functionally valid implementations or accept degenerate ones.
4. **Hardware and Compute Transparency:** Detailed reporting on the hardware specifications, exact runtime overheads for the verifiers during the 15-round loop, and the formulations used for the iso-compute scaling analysis to ensure that comparisons between drastically different parameter classes are mathematically sound.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment is heavily penalized by the obfuscation of the baseline models, the lack of statistical bounding on a small dataset, and the unverified reliability of the LLM judge. I would strongly upgrade my recommendation if the authors can:
- Replace the fictional/placeholder model names with exact, verifiable model parameters and generation hyperparameter configurations.
- Provide rigorous confidence intervals and formal paired tests for the cross-language performance claims.
- Demonstrate via an explicit human baseline that the LLM Judge operates with high precision and recall.
- Address the Area Chair verification risk by providing a properly anonymized, sandboxed artifact repository that mitigates compile-time RCE risks.

## Provisional Leaning
The conceptual design of AlgoVeri is outstanding. Aligning specifications across disparate formal paradigms to isolate reasoning is exactly the type of rigorous methodology the field needs to accurately measure neuro-symbolic progress. The execution of mechanized well-formedness checks also sets a remarkably high bar. However, the empirical half of the paper suffers from severe evidentiary flaws: obfuscated baselines that currently render the results irreproducible, a lack of statistical confidence bounds on a highly granular dataset, and an unverified reliance on an LLM as a semantic judge in a formal evaluation pipeline. Furthermore, the inclusion of a deanonymized repository link poses a significant venue policy risk. At present, the methodological brilliance of the benchmark is weighed down by empirical opacity and a lack of statistical rigor.
