## Overview
The paper introduces the Evolution of Benchmark (EoB) framework, presenting a paradigm shift from manually crafted Black-Box Optimization (BBO) test functions to an automated, LLM-driven program evolution framework. It utilizes an LLM as a generator and refiner of programmatic optimization landscapes. The framework formalizes the generation of BBO benchmarks as a bi-objective optimization problem. The two objectives are the Landscape Similarity Indicator (LSI), which measures alignment with target real-world tasks, and the Algorithm Distinguishing Capability (ADC), which ensures the benchmark effectively differentiates between solver performances. The approach elegantly integrates multi-objective evolutionary algorithm (MOEA/D) principles, using Penalty-based Boundary Intersection (PBI) scalarization and reference vectors to guide the LLM. Furthermore, it features a reflection-based crossover and mutation strategy that leverages the reasoning capabilities of the LLM to recombine mathematical traits, debug syntax errors, and enforce structural constraints.

## Claimed Contributions
- A paradigm shift from human-crafted mathematical test functions to an automated, open-ended LLM-driven program evolution framework for benchmark design.
- A bi-objective formulation explicitly optimizing for the generation of test instances that mimic real-world target tasks (via LSI) while remaining challenging and discriminative for a portfolio of optimization algorithms (via ADC).
- The integration of classical MOEA/D components (reference vectors, PBI scalarization) into an LLM prompting strategy, combined with an automated reflection mechanism to handle aggressive and conservative mutation for arbitrary code correction.
- The utilization of NeurELA for computationally efficient neural network-based landscape feature extraction to calculate the LSI metric, superseding expensive classical Exploratory Landscape Analysis (ELA).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The proposed approach introduces a highly novel and creative intersection of Large Language Models, Multi-Objective Evolutionary Algorithms, and Automated Algorithm Design. Historically, BBO benchmarks (e.g., COCO/BBOB, CEC suites) have been carefully curated by human experts utilizing rigid mathematical heuristics like rotation, scaling, and asymmetric transformations. Transitioning to open-ended program synthesis for benchmark generation is a promising direction that directly addresses the community bottleneck of producing diverse, realistic test instances for training learning-assisted BBO algorithms. Replacing classical, brittle genetic programming operators with an intelligent, context-aware LLM reflection engine is a significant methodological upgrade. 

However, relying on LLMs for generating benchmark suites inherently introduces data contamination risks. Because LLMs have ingested massive corpora of open-source optimization code and benchmark functions during pre-training, there is a risk that the framework might simply retrieve or trivially obfuscate classical benchmark functions (e.g., Ackley, Rastrigin) rather than discovering genuinely novel landscapes. Confirming true novelty requires strict verification against existing suites.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical mapping of benchmark generation to a multi-objective framework is elegant; however, there are several critical concerns regarding the statistical validity and well-posedness of the evaluation objectives.

First, the formulation of the Algorithm Distinguishing Capability (ADC) objective (Equation 4) relies on the average standard deviation of algorithmic performances. This formulation is highly vulnerable to intra-algorithm variance and outliers. An algorithm with highly unstable, noisy performance, or one that diverges catastrophically on a specific generated landscape, could falsely inflate the standard deviation across the algorithm pool $\Lambda$. Consequently, the LLM may be incentivized to evolve pathological functions that simply elicit chaotic behavior or catastrophic failure in a single algorithm, rather than evolving rigorous landscapes that genuinely distinguish capable algorithms in a statistically meaningful way. Exploring a variance-based metric that compares inter-algorithm variance to intra-algorithm variance, such as an ANOVA F-statistic, would be fundamentally more robust.

Second, the methodology applies a min-max normalization to the objective matrix (Lines 184-186) prior to computing ADC. Because the LLM synthesizes arbitrary, open-ended mathematical functions, the true global minima, maxima, and boundary constraints of these landscapes are inherently unknown. Applying min-max normalization based solely on empirical observations from the finite algorithm runs is ill-posed. If an algorithm diverges to infinity or encounters a degenerate space, the normalization bounds will be severely distorted, rendering the ADC metric unstable or effectively meaningless for that evolutionary generation.

Third, the Landscape Similarity Indicator (LSI) objective (Equation 3) relies entirely on NeurELA, a neural network approximation proxy, to extract a 16-dimensional feature vector. Whenever an evolutionary search explicitly optimizes against a learned proxy model, it is highly susceptible to proxy exploitation (Goodhart's Law). The LLM might generate adversarial landscapes that mathematically 'trick' the NeurELA network into outputting a feature vector highly similar to the target distribution, even though the true macroscopic topology of the evolved landscape shares no structural resemblance to the target tasks.

## Experimental Rigor
The core concern regarding experimental rigor lies in the immense computational overhead demanded by the inner evolutionary loop. Evaluating the ADC objective for a single candidate function requires running $L$ distinct BBO algorithms for $B$ independent, random seed-controlled runs. If the evolutionary algorithm maintains a standard population size and evaluates dozens of generations, the total number of full BBO algorithm executions scales to the tens or hundreds of thousands. This presents a massive computational bottleneck that directly conflicts with the motivation of creating an "efficient" automated benchmark designer.

Furthermore, the robustness of the generated benchmark suites must be tested for generalization. Because the ADC is optimized directly against a specific algorithm pool $\Lambda$, the generated functions are at high risk of overfitting to the idiosyncratic vulnerabilities of those specific solvers. If the benchmark cannot meaningfully evaluate or differentiate hold-out algorithms (algorithms entirely absent from $\Lambda$ during evolution), its utility as a general-purpose benchmark is compromised.

Finally, reproducibility poses a severe barrier. The methodology relies on "an existing LLM" (Line 135) and an unreleased neural landscape profiling system (NeurELA). The inherent stochasticity of LLM generations, combined with the lack of explicit details regarding the exact model version, API parameters (e.g., temperature), full prompt templates, and the omission of an anonymized code repository, makes independent replication of the evolutionary pipeline nearly impossible.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Innovative Paradigm Shift**: The transition from manually designed test suites to automated, LLM-driven program evolution is a forward-thinking contribution with broad implications for the optimization community.
- **Rigorous Problem Formulation**: Framing benchmark generation as a bi-objective problem targeting both landscape relevance to reality (LSI) and empirical discriminative utility (ADC) is conceptually robust.
- **Clever Integration of MOEA/D**: The use of reference vectors and PBI scalarization to guide LLM prompt generation successfully ensures population diversity along the Pareto front.
- **Robust Error Handling**: The multi-stage reflection and mutation strategy elegantly utilizes the LLM not just as a static code generator, but as an interactive debugger, circumventing the high failure rates traditionally associated with symbolic mathematical synthesis.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Computational Impracticability**: The fitness evaluation loop requires executing $L \times B$ full optimization trajectories for every generated candidate program, every generation. The lack of a transparent cost analysis leaves it unclear if this process is computationally feasible on standard hardware.
- **Statistical Vulnerabilities in ADC**: The reliance on standard deviation (Eq. 4) combined with empirical min-max normalization on unbounded mathematical functions may direct the evolutionary search toward pathological or divergent landscapes rather than useful benchmarks.
- **Overfitting to the Algorithm Pool**: Benchmarks optimized to maximize differences among specific solvers in pool $\Lambda$ may fail to generalize to unseen solvers.
- **Adversarial Exploitation of NeurELA**: Optimizing heavily against a neural network proxy to calculate LSI creates a high risk of generating adversarial functions that match proxy features without genuine underlying mathematical similarity.
- **Severe Reproducibility Gaps**: The specific LLM, hyperparameters, complete system prompts, and source code are omitted, rendering the experimental methodology unverifiable.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To firmly establish the validity of the proposed EoB framework, several critical pieces of evidence are necessary:
1. **Computational Cost Analysis**: A transparent breakdown of the wall-clock time, LLM API token costs, and BBO simulation hours required to successfully evolve a standard benchmark suite.
2. **Generalization to Hold-out Algorithms**: Experimental validation proving that the evolved benchmarks successfully differentiate novel BBO algorithms excluded from the pool $\Lambda$ during the evolutionary phase.
3. **Statistical Justification for Normalization**: Clarification on how the framework stabilizes the min-max normalization of the objective matrix when dynamically generated functions exhibit extreme outliers or mathematically unbounded behavior.
4. **Data Contamination Checks**: A symbolic or structural distance comparison between the LLM-generated suites and classical pre-training data suites (e.g., BBOB, CEC) to conclusively demonstrate novelty and rule out memorization.
5. **Proxy Verification**: Verification that functions scoring highly on LSI via NeurELA actually resemble the target tasks in terms of global structure, preventing adversarial exploitation of the neural surrogate.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift toward a higher confidence of acceptance if the authors provide a rigorous accounting of the computational complexity, specifically demonstrating how the severe bottleneck of the ADC evaluation is mitigated in practice. I would also need evidence that the empirical min-max normalization strategy is mathematically stable across arbitrary open-ended functions and that the ADC metric captures true statistical distinguishability rather than intra-algorithm noise. Finally, confirming the generalizability of the benchmarks to a hold-out set of optimization algorithms and fully open-sourcing the code, prompts, and final datasets would resolve my reproducibility concerns.

## Provisional Leaning
The paper introduces a highly original and compelling framework that tackles a recognized bottleneck in black-box optimization. The conceptual mapping of MOEA/D principles to LLM prompting is well executed. However, the mathematical formulations underpinning the primary objectives—specifically the statistical reliability of the ADC metric and the proxy-vulnerability of the LSI metric—introduce critical failure modes. When combined with severe potential computational bottlenecks and a significant lack of transparency regarding reproducibility, the evidence currently available is insufficient to fully validate the rigor of the generated benchmarks. Therefore, I lean towards borderline, contingent heavily on the provision of the missing computational analyses and robust methodological clarifications.
