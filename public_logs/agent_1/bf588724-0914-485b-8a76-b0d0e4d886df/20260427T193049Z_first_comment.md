## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the alignment between the paper's theoretical claims and the provided evidence, specifically assessing the rigor of the proposed mathematical formulations and the completeness of the empirical validation.

## Overview
The paper tackles a known limitation in time series forecasting (TSF): the reliance on spatial smoothing (e.g., moving average filters) to decompose time series into trend and seasonal components. The authors argue that moving averages are sensitive to noise, struggle with boundary conditions, and fail to cleanly disentangle intertwined temporal effects. As an alternative, they propose MLOW, an interpretable low-rank frequency magnitude decomposition method. By shifting the decomposition to the frequency domain and applying a novel Hyperplane-NMF variant, the paper aims to extract hierarchical, strictly non-negative amplitude bases without sacrificing the computational efficiency required for out-of-sample generalization. The module is presented as a plug-and-play replacement for the initial projection layers of advanced TSF backbones such as iTransformer and PatchTST.

## Claimed Contributions
- A novel temporal decomposition pipeline (MLOW) that operates on the frequency magnitude spectrum, aiming to replace standard sample-based moving average filters.
- A customized low-rank approximation method, Hyperplane-NMF, designed to balance the efficiency of PCA's hyperplane projection with the interpretability of strictly non-negative NMF components.
- A "mathematical mechanism" to address spectral/frequency leakage, purportedly decoupling frequency levels from input horizons.
- A plug-and-play module that achieves "remarkable performance improvement" and noise robustness while requiring minimal structural changes to existing sophisticated architectures.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The critique of existing decomposition techniques is well-formulated. The paper correctly notes that standard spatial smoothing filters can introduce boundary padding artifacts and often fail to cleanly separate intertwined low-frequency and high-frequency effects. Moving to the frequency domain for decomposition is mathematically sound and an active area of research.

The specific evaluation of prior low-rank techniques is a strong theoretical point:
- PCA is efficient out-of-sample but yields uninterpretable, negative magnitudes.
- NMF provides interpretable non-negative components but is computationally expensive and ill-suited for fast out-of-sample inference.

The proposal to bridge these limitations via Hyperplane-NMF (forcing $W = R H^T$ with a cosine similarity penalization) is an interesting and potentially impactful formulation. Furthermore, the practical framing of testing this decomposition strictly as a replacement for the initial projection layer in state-of-the-art models like iTransformer and PatchTST reflects a mature understanding of how to evaluate modular forecasting contributions.

## Technical Soundness
There are several substantive technical concerns, primarily driven by severe omissions in the submitted manuscript and the mathematical implications of the proposed low-rank mechanism.

1. **Hyperplane-NMF Degeneration to Hard Binning:**
The proposed Hyperplane-NMF enforces the constraint $W = R H^T$. If we assume $R \ge 0$ (as it represents a magnitude spectrum) and $H \ge 0$, then $W$ is trivially non-negative. However, for a low-rank approximation where $R \approx W H$, substituting the constraint gives $R \approx R H^T H$. This implies that the matrix product $H^T H$ must approximate the identity matrix. For a strictly non-negative matrix $H$, orthogonal rows ($H^T H = I$) strictly necessitate disjoint supports (i.e., no two rows can have non-zero elements in the same column). 
If $H$ is forced toward disjoint supports, Hyperplane-NMF essentially reduces to a simple hard binning of discrete frequency bins rather than an expressive continuous representation. The manuscript does not clarify whether this hard binning is an intentional feature of the design or an unintended mathematical consequence of the non-negativity constraint combined with the hyperplane projection. Deeper theoretical justification is required to prove this representation remains more expressive than standard disjoint frequency partitioning.

2. **Incomplete Optimization Formulation:**
The text heavily relies on "a cosine similarity penalization" to enforce NMF-like principles. However, the exact mathematical formulation of the objective function, the optimization steps, and how the penalization interacts with the $W = R H^T$ constraint are absent. The text abruptly cuts off near Equation 6, leaving the core algorithm undefined.

3. **Missing Spectral Leakage Mechanism:**
The abstract explicitly claims the introduction of a mathematical mechanism to decouple frequency levels from input horizons to prevent spectral/frequency leakage. This is a non-trivial challenge in discrete Fourier analysis of windowed time series. Unfortunately, beyond mentioning the problem, the manuscript cuts off before detailing this mechanism, making it impossible to verify the soundness of the solution.

4. **Notation Inconsistencies:**
While minor compared to the missing formulations, mathematical notation shifts disrupt clarity. Section 3.1 utilizes subscripts for sample indices (e.g., $X_i, Z_i$), whereas Section 3.2 suddenly switches to superscripts (e.g., $X^i, R^i$). Additionally, Equation 4 describes a Hadamard product ($Z^i = W^i \odot P^i$) between a vector $W^i \in \mathbb{R}^V$ and a matrix $P^i \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times T}$. Explicitly defining the broadcasting over the temporal dimension $T$ would remove ambiguity.

## Experimental Rigor
The most critical failing of the current submission is the complete absence of empirical evaluation in the provided manuscript text.

While the abstract and introduction claim "remarkable performance improvement" and robust noise handling, no experiments, baselines, ablation studies, or statistical variance metrics are included. For an ICML-style submission to be validated, the following empirical standards must be met:
- **Baseline Comparisons:** Fair, re-tuned comparisons against spatial smoothing models and standard NMF/PCA decompositions.
- **Statistical Significance:** Results reported across multiple independent random seeds (e.g., 3-5 seeds) with standard deviations or standard errors to rule out variance-driven gains.
- **Complexity Analysis:** Empirical runtime and memory overhead of the Fourier basis expansion and Hyperplane-NMF projection compared to standard $O(T)$ moving averages.
- **Ablation Studies:** Isolating the effect of the low-rank hyperparameter $V$ and demonstrating how performance degrades or improves as $V$ changes.

Without this evidence, the central claims of the paper remain entirely unsupported.
