- critical_reviewer: P=0.10, confidence=0.95.
  Fatal-flaw candidate: The mathematical claim that one can use a pure minimization rounding technique (Axelrod et al., 2020) to map a continuous fractional minimax strategy to a pure discrete minimax strategy is flawed. While a fractional solution $\tilde{x}$ can represent a mixed strategy over sets, rounding it to a single deterministic set $S$ based on the opponent's current strategy $\tilde{y}$ destroys the min-max guarantee (i.e., $\max_y f(S, y) \le \max_y f^L(\tilde{x}, y)$ is generally false). This invalidates the core claim of finding an $\epsilon$-saddle point for the original discrete problem.
  Concern: Fatal Flaw in Continuous-to-Discrete Reduction: The authors claim (Lines 158-166) that they can solve the continuous Lovász extension min-max problem (7) and 'recover the solution of the min-max Problem (1)' using a rounding trick from pure minimization (Axelrod et al., 2020). In a min-max setting, rounding a fractional saddle-point $\tilde{x}$ to a discrete set $S$ guarantees good performance against the specific $\tilde{y}$, but does NOT guarantee robustness against other $y \in Y$. Thus, solving (7) and rounding does not yield a valid pure $\epsilon$-saddle point for the discrete problem.
  Concern: Overly Restrictive and Unjustified Assumption: The authors assume that a pure discrete-continuous saddle point exists for problem (1) (Line 188). In mixed discrete-continuous min-max games, strong duality typically fails and pure saddle points do not exist. By assuming its existence without characterizing the highly specific class of problems where this holds, the scope of the paper is severely restricted.
  Strength: The problem of solving mixed discrete-continuous min-max problems (submodular-concave) is practically motivated and highly relevant to robust combinatorial optimization and adversarial machine learning.
