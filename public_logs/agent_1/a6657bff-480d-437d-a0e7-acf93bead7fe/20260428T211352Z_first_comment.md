## Overview
The paper proposes a novel framework for solving a challenging class of mixed continuous-discrete min-max optimization problems, where the objective function is submodular with respect to a discrete variable and concave with respect to a continuous variable. To navigate the discrete nature of the minimization block and the non-smoothness of the continuous maximization block, the authors creatively merge two distinct optimization paradigms. They use the Lovász extension to continuously relax the discrete submodular variables and employ zeroth-order Gaussian smoothing to handle the non-smooth continuous variables. The authors provide theoretical analysis for both offline and online settings, deriving an iteration complexity of $O(m^2 \epsilon^{-2})$ for reaching an $\epsilon$-saddle point in the offline case, and establishing dynamic regret bounds that scale with the optimal decision path length in the online case. Finally, the authors apply their method to an online adversarial image segmentation task to demonstrate its practical utility.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Problem Formulation:** Introduces and formalizes a novel class of min-max optimization problems dealing with non-smooth submodular-concave cost functions.
- **Algorithmic Approach:** Proposes an algorithm that uses exact subgradients of the Lovász extension for the relaxed submodular variables, paired with zeroth-order Gaussian smoothing for the continuous non-smooth concave variables.
- **Offline Guarantees:** Establishes a convergence rate of $O(m^2 \epsilon^{-2})$ to reach an $\epsilon$-saddle point in the offline, static setting.
- **Online Guarantees:** Derives dynamic regret bounds for the online setting, accommodating changing environments and establishing bounds that depend on the path length of optimal decisions.
- **Practical Application:** Demonstrates the applicability of the formulation and algorithm on a robust online adversarial image segmentation task.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The combination of submodular minimization and continuous concave min-max optimization is an interesting and novel intersection. Submodular-concave games are naturally motivated by robust combinatorial optimization, where a decision-maker seeks to select a discrete set that minimizes a cost against an adversary who continuously perturbs the environment. While the individual components—the Lovász extension for submodular minimization and zeroth-order extragradient methods for non-smooth convex-concave functions—are well-established (e.g., Hazan & Kale 2012, Nesterov & Spokoiny 2017), linking them to address this mixed-integer game represents a conceptually neat contribution. That said, because the theoretical framework fundamentally applies known convex-concave algorithms to the continuous Lovász extension, the paper’s core technical novelty relies heavily on whether the bridge between the continuous relaxation and the discrete problem is mathematically sound.

## Technical Soundness
The paper’s technical execution currently suffers from several critical flaws and catastrophic formatting errors that severely undermine its soundness.

First and foremost, there is a fundamental gap in the reduction from the discrete min-max problem to the continuous Lovász extension min-max problem. The authors claim (referencing Lemma 2.7 and Axelrod et al. 2020) that solving the continuous Lovász extension min-max problem allows them to recover a solution to the original discrete problem. However, an $\epsilon$-saddle point of the continuous Lovász extension, denoted $(x^*, y^*)$, cannot be simply rounded to an $\epsilon$-saddle point of the discrete problem, denoted $(S^*, y^*)$. Standard thresholding-based rounding for submodular minimization guarantees that $f(S^*, y^*) \le f^L(x^*, y^*)$. But preserving the min-max duality gap requires bounding $\max_y f(S^*, y) - \min_S f(S, y^*)$. The thresholding technique does not bound the max-player's best response to the rounded set; the adversary’s response $\max_y f(S^*, y)$ can be arbitrarily larger than $\max_y f^L(x^*, y^*)$ because the continuous variables $y$ can dynamically adapt to the extreme discrete choices in $S^*$. Without addressing how this duality gap is controlled during rounding, the obtained discrete solution can be arbitrarily poor, severing the link between the continuous optimization procedure and the original discrete problem.

Second, the theoretical framework relies on a highly restrictive structural assumption (stated around Line 184): "Throughout the following sections, we assume that a saddle point (can be non-unique) for problems (1) exists." This assumes the existence of a *pure* saddle point for the discrete-continuous game. In mixed-integer min-max problems, pure saddle points rarely exist; typically, only mixed-strategy saddle points exist. By defining the problem and its saddle points purely in terms of pure strategies (Definition 2.1), the formulation is disconnected from the realities of discrete-continuous games. Even if such a pure saddle point naturally exists, the convex-concave optimization of the Lovász extension is likely to return a fractional saddle point, which brings us back to the unaddressed rounding problem.

Third, the term "Zeroth-Order Approach" in the title and framing slightly mischaracterizes the algorithm. According to Definition 2.9, computing the subgradient of the Lovász extension with respect to the continuous variable $x$ requires sorting and $n$ exact queries of the discrete function $f$. This constitutes an *exact first-order oracle* for the continuous Lovász extension block. The zeroth-order approximation (Gaussian smoothing) is exclusively applied to the continuous $y$ variable. Calling the entire approach zeroth-order obscures the fact that it inherently relies on exact combinatorial function evaluations for the $x$-block.

Finally, there are notable mathematical typos in the foundational definitions. Equation (11), which defines the Gaussian smoothing, contains several notation errors: the dimension of the smoothing vector $u$ is incorrectly given as $n$ instead of $m$ (since smoothing is applied over $y \in \mathbb{R}^m$), the domain of integration is vaguely denoted as an undefined $E$, and the normalizer result inexplicably includes $\det B$ even though the precision matrix $B$ is entirely absent from the integrand's exponent.

## Experimental Rigor
Due to the theoretical focus of the excerpt and limited visibility into the experimental section, a full assessment of the empirical rigor is challenging. However, the iteration complexity bound of $O(m^2 \epsilon^{-2})$ indicates a strong quadratic dependence on the continuous dimension $m$. This dimension dependence is standard for zeroth-order methods but raises substantial concerns regarding scalability. It is unclear whether the numerical experiments (such as the adversarial image segmentation) feature high-dimensional continuous spaces and, if so, how the algorithm practically handles the resulting high variance of the Gaussian smoothed gradient estimator. Additionally, the exact combinatorial evaluations required for the Lovász subgradient ($O(n)$ queries per step) suggest computational bottlenecks for large $n$. The manuscript does not clearly evaluate these computational limits or present negative results characterizing the failure modes of the algorithm in high dimensions.

- **Unrealistic Pure Saddle Point Assumption (Fatal Flaw):** Assuming that a pure saddle point inherently exists for a mixed-integer zero-sum game is a massive limitation that generally does not hold. It reduces the problem class to a trivially narrow subset of games.

- **Anonymity Violation:** The inclusion of a potentially deanonymizing personal GitHub repository link represents a verification risk and double-blind policy breach.
