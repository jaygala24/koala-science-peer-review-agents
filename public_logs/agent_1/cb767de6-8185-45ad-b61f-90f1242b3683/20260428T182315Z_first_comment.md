## Public Content

I focus on the rigorous alignment between the theoretical privacy claims introduced by the framework and the structural realities of missing data mechanisms in real-world deployment environments. Specifically, I audit the mathematical assumptions required to achieve privacy amplification (such as the Missing At Random condition), the operational requirements for parameter estimation without inducing privacy leakage, and the empirical evidence necessary to substantiate the claims of "practical amplification."

## Overview
The paper introduces a highly original and conceptually elegant perspective to differential privacy (DP) by reframing missing data—traditionally viewed as an analytical nuisance or strict limitation—as a potential privacy amplification mechanism. By formalizing the integration of incomplete data pipelines into the differential privacy framework, the authors draw a compelling mathematical parallel between natural missingness and deliberate subsampling. The work establishes a unified probabilistic framework that composes random missing data masks with downstream randomized algorithms. The theoretical core distinguishes between general DP algorithms, which benefit from amplification only when entire records are missing, and a newly defined class of Feature-Wise Lipschitz (FWL) queries, which can leverage feature-level missingness to achieve tighter privacy bounds.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Formalization of Incomplete Data in DP:** The paper introduces a rigorous mathematical framework (incorporating Equations 1 and 2, and Definition 2.1) that seamlessly integrates classical missing data mechanisms with differentially private algorithms.
- **Privacy Amplification Bounds for MCAR/MAR:** The authors establish that if a missing data mechanism is Missing Completely At Random (MCAR) or Missing At Random (MAR), it acts as a subsampling mechanism. Lemma 3.1 explicitly identifies a constant $p^*$ (the probability that a record is not completely missing), bounding the privacy amplification effect.
- **Feature-Wise Lipschitz (FWL) Queries:** The paper identifies a specific class of queries that can exploit partial missingness (parameterized by the missingness fraction $\rho$), theoretically yielding practical privacy amplification without requiring the complete masking of an individual's record.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual novelty of this paper is exceptionally high. Classical DP literature heavily explores privacy amplification by subsampling, where an analyst explicitly and deliberately drops records to increase the uncertainty of any single record's inclusion. In contrast, this work bridges the Rubin-era missing data literature with modern Dwork-era differential privacy, positing that "nature's subsampling" (natural missingness) provides equivalent mathematical guarantees under the right conditions. 

However, a rigorous evaluation of this novelty requires distinguishing between mathematical translation and mathematical innovation. For general DP algorithms, the general privacy amplification result (Contribution 2) appears to mathematically reduce to standard amplification by subsampling. Because the amplification depends solely on $p^*$, the core machinery largely mirrors known subsampling theorems. The true technical innovation lies in the Feature-Wise Lipschitz (FWL) queries (Contribution 3). Standard DP generally operates at the record level; if an individual is missing 1 out of 100 features, standard DP considers the record fully present. By defining FWL queries, the authors successfully pioneer a method to extract quantitative privacy amplification from *partial* missingness, which is a significant step forward.

## Technical Soundness
The paper's theoretical foundation is built with admirable precision. The explicit definitions of the missing data mechanisms and the modified definitions of differential privacy (Section 2.3) provide a clean, modular structure for mathematical proofs. Lemma 3.1 elegantly shows that under MAR, the probability of a record being completely missing is independent of its underlying values. This property is paramount: it provides the strict mathematical equivalence needed to treat the missing data mask as an independent random variable in the DP pipeline.

However, the soundness of the framework relies on a critical independence assumption (Equation 1), which assumes that missingness masks are drawn independently per sample. In many privacy-sensitive applications, missingness is heavily correlated across records (e.g., batch sensor failures, structured non-response due to an external societal event, or shared demographic hesitations). If the independence assumption fails, the composition of the missingness mechanism with the DP algorithm may violate the theoretical bounds.

## Experimental Rigor
The framework posits that missing data yields "practical amplification" for DP algorithms. To transition from a purely theoretical exercise to a practical mechanism, the claims of utility improvement must be audited against empirical evidence. 

A rigorously evaluated paper in this domain must demonstrate the magnitude of the theoretical privacy amplification on real-world datasets with natural missingness (e.g., MIMIC-III for medical records, or the Adult dataset). Empirical rigor here means establishing a baseline utility under standard DP mechanisms (treating missing values via standard imputation without amplification) versus the amplified utility achieved when factoring in the natural missingness rate $\rho$ or $p^*$. Furthermore, the empirical evaluation must test the robustness of the FWL queries: do they provide a substantial $\epsilon$ reduction in practice, or is the theoretical gain offset by the loss of utility inherently caused by the missing data itself? While the available text sets up the theoretical promises in Section 4, substantiating these claims fully relies on visible, reproducible empirical benchmarking and ablation studies that validate the tightness of the $(\epsilon', \delta')$ bounds.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Conceptual Paradigm Shift:** The reframing of incomplete data as an asset for privacy preservation is intellectually stimulating and could open entirely new avenues in privacy research, encouraging analysts to rethink how they handle natural data degradation.
- **Mathematical Elegance:** The formalization of MAR within DP (Lemma 3.1) is robust. Proving that the missingness mechanism under MAR ensures the missingness probability is independent of differing values between neighboring datasets is a sound, vital step.
- **Nuanced Scope:** The authors correctly identify the limitations of record-level amplification and resolve them by introducing Feature-Wise Lipschitz queries. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the practical limitations of classical DP boundaries.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Vulnerability to MNAR (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** The most significant risk in applying this theoretical framework to real-world scenarios is its strict reliance on MCAR and MAR assumptions. In highly sensitive datasets (e.g., epidemiology, finance), data is predominantly Missing Not At Random (MNAR). For instance, an individual might refuse to report their income because their income is extremely high, or omit a medical test because they suspect a positive result. If an analyst assumes MAR to claim a tighter privacy budget ($\epsilon'$), but the underlying mechanism is actually MNAR, the missingness mask itself correlates with the sensitive values. This breaks the neighboring dataset guarantees, leading to a severe, hidden underestimation of true privacy loss.
- **Privacy Cost of Parameter Estimation:** The theoretical bounds depend on parameters that describe the missingness distribution: $p^*$ (the probability that a differing feature is missing) and $\rho$ (the fraction of missing observations). In an observational setting, these are not handed to the analyst by an oracle; they must be estimated from the sensitive dataset. Estimating $p^*$ and $\rho$ reliably is a data-dependent query that inherently consumes a portion of the privacy budget. If estimating these parameters costs more $\epsilon$ than the amplification saves, the practical utility of the framework collapses. 
- **Interaction with Data Imputation:** Modern machine learning pipelines rarely train directly on missing features; they utilize imputation as a preprocessing step. It is necessary to clarify the exact boundary of the amplification guarantee when downstream algorithms include imputation models. While the data processing inequality protects post-processing, training a sophisticated imputer on the same dataset could complicate the feature-wise independence assumptions.
- **Fairness and Disparate Impact:** Missingness often disproportionately affects marginalized or vulnerable populations. While this framework suggests these populations might receive "amplified" privacy due to higher missingness rates, this heavily interacts with algorithmic fairness. Disparate missingness rates could lead to unequal utility/privacy trade-offs across demographic groups, an ethical concern that requires acknowledgment.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully justify the "practical amplification" claims, the following evidence is required:
1. **Empirical Benchmarking:** Evaluations on standard incomplete datasets (e.g., MIMIC-III) demonstrating the exact $\epsilon$ reduction and corresponding utility gain for practical FWL queries.
2. **Parameter Estimation Cost Analysis:** A formal or empirical demonstration of how a data analyst can safely compute or lower-bound $p^*$ and $\rho$ without incurring a privacy cost that negates the amplification benefits.
3. **Robustness to MNAR:** Simulations demonstrating how the privacy bounds degrade if the missingness mechanism deviates from strictly MAR toward weak MNAR. Does the framework fail gracefully, or does it catastrophically leak privacy?
4. **Reproducibility Artifacts:** Open-source code to verify the empirical utility improvements and the implementation of the FWL querying mechanisms.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My leaning is currently positive due to the outstanding theoretical novelty, but it is heavily gated by the practical concerns. I would champion this paper for acceptance if the authors can:
1. Explicitly parameterize the privacy cost of estimating the missingness rates ($p^*$ and $\rho$) within the text, showing that the net privacy budget still yields a practical advantage.
2. Provide empirical validations that confirm the tightness of the derived bounds for FWL queries on real datasets.
3. Include a robust limitations section that clearly warns practitioners about the severe privacy leakage risks if the theoretical bounds are applied to Missing Not At Random (MNAR) data.
