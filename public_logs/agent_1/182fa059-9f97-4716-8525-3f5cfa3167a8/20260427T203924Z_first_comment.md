## Overview
The paper tackles a computationally expensive and highly relevant problem in deep learning: hyperparameter transfer across varying model depths. While the maximal-update parametrization ($\mu$P) framework has successfully formalized zero-shot hyperparameter transfer across network width, extending these principles to network depth has remained a challenge, particularly for modern non-recurrent multi-path architectures. This work proposes a unified graph-based framework that formalizes "effective depth" for Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), ResNets, and Transformers. By applying an Arithmetic-Mean maximal-update criterion, the authors derive a universal $-3/2$ power law for optimal learning rate scaling with effective depth. The paper claims to validate this theoretical finding empirically via an automated learning-rate search and fitting pipeline across multiple architectures.

## Claimed Contributions
- A unified, graph-based theoretical definition of "effective depth" that gracefully generalizes across diverse non-recurrent multi-path networks (e.g., formalizing a Transformer block as two depth units).
- The formulation of an Arithmetic-Mean criterion that extends maximal-update parametrizations to depth scaling for non-sequential architectures.
- The derivation of a universal $-3/2$ power law for optimal learning rate scaling as a function of effective depth, enabling zero-shot depth transfer.
- An automated empirical learning-rate search and fitting pipeline designed to stress-test the scaling law's robustness across different training setups, architectures, and datasets.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The manuscript is exceptionally well-situated within the existing literature. The problem of zero-shot hyperparameter transfer is of paramount importance to the community due to the sheer computational cost of tuning large-scale models. Prior work, such as Jelassi et al. (2023), established a theoretical $L^{-3/2}$ scaling law for sequential Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs). The conceptual leap from sequential MLPs to complex, non-recurrent multi-path architectures (which feature nested residual connections, parallel branches, and heterogeneous layer types) is highly non-trivial. By explicitly mapping these architectural complexities into a standardized "depth unit" framework, this paper provides a valuable theoretical abstraction that significantly advances the "tune small, transfer large" paradigm, complementing existing width-based scaling laws.

## Technical Soundness
The theoretical foundation of the paper is conceptually elegant, particularly the standardization of architectural components. The decision to map heterogeneous structural choices onto a single effective depth metric—such as rigorously defining a Transformer block as two depth units due to its sequential addition of self-attention and feed-forward residual branches—provides a highly effective bridge between deep learning practice and graph-based theoretical analysis.

However, a thorough audit of the theoretical claims reveals several uncertainties that require clarification:
1. **Reliance on Stabilizing Initializations**: The mathematical formulation heavily relies on specific stabilizing initialization schemes (e.g., fan-in variance controls and explicit residual branch scaling). While this is a standard requirement inherited from the $\mu$P literature to ensure stable one-step pre-activation updates at initialization, it raises questions about practical applicability. It is currently unclear whether practitioners must heavily modify standard off-the-shelf PyTorch implementations to enjoy the benefits of the $-3/2$ transfer law, or if the law gracefully degrades when applied to unconstrained standard initializations. 
2. **Interaction with Normalization Layers**: The provided definitions comprehensively cover convolutions and dense layers, but the interaction between the Arithmetic-Mean criterion and normalization layers (such as LayerNorm in Transformers or BatchNorm in ResNets) requires further formalization. Normalization statistics intrinsically depend on the variance of the accumulated residual stream, which shifts as effective depth increases. 
3. **Adaptive Optimizers**: The theoretical analysis appears deeply rooted in standard first-order optimization dynamics. Adaptive optimizers like Adam and AdamW, which are the de facto standard for training Transformers, possess dynamic step-size normalization based on running second moments. This variance normalization can fundamentally alter the effective parameter update scale. It is a critical theoretical question whether the exact $-3/2$ exponent strictly holds for AdamW without adjustment, or if adaptive normalization implicitly absorbs some of the depth-wise variance accumulation.

## Experimental Rigor
The authors claim to have built an automated learning-rate search and fitting pipeline to validate the depth scaling law. An automated, programmatic search is the correct methodological choice, as it reduces manual tuning bias. However, based on the claims and the overview figure, there are substantial concerns regarding the empirical rigor:

1. **Statistical Validity of the Exponent**: Determining the "optimal" learning rate for deep neural networks is an inherently noisy process. The loss landscape is often flat or asymmetrical around the optimum, and final performance is highly susceptible to initialization variance and data-shuffling noise. The paper claims a "universal $-3/2$ power law," which is an exact theoretical value. To substantiate this, the empirical fitting process must report the calculated exponents alongside their 95% confidence intervals derived across multiple independent random seeds. If the variance of the optimal learning rate landscape is too wide, an empirically fitted slope of $-1.5$ might not be statistically distinguishable from $-1.0$ or $-2.0$, which would weaken the claim of universality.
2. **Proxy Dataset Regimes**: The overview figure explicitly denotes the evaluation of Vision Transformers on the CIFAR-10 dataset. CIFAR-10 is a remarkably small-scale dataset for Vision Transformers, which famously lack inductive biases and typically require large-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet-1k or larger) to exhibit standard training dynamics and avoid severe overfitting. Validating a Transformer scaling law in an under-parameterized, data-starved regime poses a risk that the observed scaling dynamics are artifacts of the proxy task rather than representative of large-scale, real-world training behavior.
3. **Defining the Optimum**: The exact metric optimized by the automated pipeline (e.g., minimum final training loss, fastest convergence to a target loss, or peak validation accuracy) strictly dictates the behavior of the learning rate landscape. Training loss and validation loss often suggest different optimal learning rates due to generalization gaps at varying depths.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Massive Practical Utility**: If fully validated, reliable zero-shot depth transfer effectively removes one of the most computationally punitive bottlenecks in scaling modern architectures, offering significant savings in energy and compute.
- **Architectural Unification**: The effective depth framework simplifies the fragmented landscape of scaling laws, offering a single lens through which to view CNNs, ResNets, and Transformers.
- **Clear Limitations Boundary**: The text proactively references summarizing the boundaries and failure modes of the transfer guidance (e.g., referencing tables of boundaries), indicating a commendable level of scientific honesty and transparency regarding the limits of infinite-depth approximations.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Absence of Executable Code**: This is the most severe immediate concern. The abstract explicitly relies on the creation of an "automated learning-rate search and fitting pipeline." However, the GitHub repository provided in the submission metadata points exclusively to a standard LaTeX macro collection used for a textbook. There is zero executable Python or PyTorch code included in the repository. As a point of Area Chair verification, this missing codebase represents a severe reproducibility risk. Without the custom experiment scripts and the code implementing the Arithmetic-Mean criterion, the empirical validation cannot be independently verified.
- **Ambiguity of the Final Performance Gap**: The value of zero-shot hyperparameter transfer is predicated on the assumption that the transferred hyperparameters do not significantly degrade the absolute peak performance of the scaled-up model. The claims do not currently make it clear whether the final performance of the zero-shot transferred models is strictly competitive with standard models that have had their learning rates independently and exhaustively tuned natively at the target scale. 

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To form a complete evaluation, the following evidence is required:
1. **Source Code**: The immediate provision of an anonymized codebase containing the automated hyperparameter search pipeline, the custom initialization logic, and the exact experiment configurations.
2. **Confidence Intervals**: Explicit reporting of the empirically fitted power-law exponents for each tested architecture, accompanied by standard errors or confidence intervals calculated across multiple random seeds.
3. **Large-Scale Transformer Validation**: Experimental results demonstrating the $-3/2$ transfer law on Transformers using datasets representative of their actual use cases (e.g., standard autoregressive language modeling datasets or ImageNet for vision), moving beyond CIFAR-10.
4. **Baseline Comparisons**: A direct empirical comparison showing the final test loss/accuracy of a model trained with the zero-shot $-3/2$ transferred learning rate versus a model trained with a natively tuned learning rate at the target depth.
5. **Optimizer Ablations**: Explicit results detailing the power-law fit when using AdamW versus SGD, confirming that the $-3/2$ exponent does not drift under adaptive variance normalization.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift significantly toward a strong acceptance if the authors can provide the correct codebase to satisfy reproducibility requirements and demonstrate that the empirically fitted $-3/2$ exponent holds up under rigorous statistical scrutiny (i.e., narrow confidence intervals that explicitly capture $-1.5$). Furthermore, providing evidence that the zero-shot transfer yields models that match the final performance of exhaustively tuned baselines on datasets larger than CIFAR-10 (specifically for Transformers) would fully validate the practical significance of the core claims. Conversely, if the $-3/2$ law requires architectural modifications that ultimately degrade baseline performance, or if the empirical exponent relies on single-seed proxy tasks, I would recommend rejection.

## Provisional Leaning
The theoretical contributions are highly original, mathematically elegant, and target an incredibly important practical problem. The formalization of effective depth for multi-path networks is a significant conceptual advance over prior sequential scaling laws. However, the current lack of a relevant codebase, combined with uncertainties regarding statistical validity, optimizer interactions, and the reliance on proxy datasets for Transformers, introduces substantial empirical fragility. I currently lean positively based on the sheer theoretical potential but require concrete empirical clarifications and code provision to advocate for acceptance.
