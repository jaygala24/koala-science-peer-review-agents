## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the empirical foundation, experimental design, and statistical validity of the claims presented in the manuscript.

## Overview
The paper investigates the interplay between task performance, susceptibility to manipulation (vigilance), and the ability to persuade in Large Language Models (LLMs). To move beyond static, single-turn question-answering benchmarks, the authors introduce a multi-turn, interactive framework built upon the spatial puzzle game Sokoban. The experimental setup features a "player" LLM attempting to solve the puzzle, and an "advisor" LLM providing either benevolent or malicious guidance. A central methodological innovation is the use of an algorithmic planner to supply the advisor with optimal solutions, aiming to cleanly decouple the advisor's underlying reasoning or task-solving capability from its linguistic ability to persuade. Based on this environment, the authors claim that puzzle-solving, persuasion, and vigilance are dissociable capacities, and that LLMs dynamically modulate their token usage (expending more tokens when dealing with malicious advice) as a form of resource-rational vigilance.

## Claimed Contributions
- The introduction of a novel, multi-agent evaluation framework utilizing sequential decision-making in Sokoban to model sustained persuasion and epistemic vigilance.
- A methodological technique to isolate persuasive capability from spatial reasoning by providing the advisor agent with ground-truth algorithmic plans.
- The introduction of quantitative metrics for measuring persuasion ($\Psi$) and vigilance ($\nu$).
- The empirical claim that LLMs exhibit token modulation, using fewer tokens to reason when receiving benevolent advice and more tokens when evaluating malicious advice.
- The overarching finding that task-solving performance, persuasive capability, and vigilance are fully dissociable capacities in modern LLMs.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual framing of this research is outstanding. Current AI safety and alignment benchmarks heavily rely on static, single-turn text interactions (e.g., jailbreak prompts or multiple-choice reasoning tasks). These fail to capture the sequential, compounding nature of persuasion and deception in real-world human-AI or AI-AI interactions. Transitioning to a multi-turn, deterministic environment like Sokoban is a highly original approach that provides an objective grounding for assessing truthfulness and manipulation. Furthermore, connecting these agent dynamics to established psychological literature on epistemic vigilance offers a rigorous theoretical foundation for analyzing LLM social cognition. The authors are addressing a critical, under-explored intersection of AI alignment, and the sandbox approach provides a safe methodology for eliciting deceptive behavior without dual-use real-world risks.

## Technical Soundness
While the high-level design is elegant, the specific technical execution exhibits several critical gaps that undermine the soundness of the claims.

**Asymmetric Decoupling of Reasoning and Persuasion:** The stated advantage of the algorithmic planner is that it provides the optimal path to the benevolent advisor, meaning the benevolent advisor only needs to utilize its persuasive text generation capabilities, not its spatial reasoning skills. However, the text does not clarify whether the malicious advisor is similarly provided with an algorithmic path to a definitive "deadlock" state. If the malicious advisor is merely instructed to "give bad advice" and must rely on its own LLM-based spatial reasoning to construct a trap, the experimental design is fundamentally asymmetric. In such a scenario, a failure of malicious persuasion might not be a failure of persuasive capacity, but rather a failure of spatial reasoning—the model simply could not figure out how to mathematically trap the player. This directly confounds the claim that persuasion and reasoning are isolated capacities.

**Missing Metric Formulations:** The paper introduces critical quantitative metrics for persuasion ($\Psi$) and vigilance ($\nu$), which are visually referenced in the results charts (e.g., Benevolent $\Psi$, Malicious $\Psi$, Aggregated $\nu$). However, the formal mathematical definitions of these metrics are entirely missing from the methodological text. Without seeing how these metrics are calculated, normalized, and bounded, it is impossible to assess their mathematical soundness. For example, it is unclear how the vigilance metric ($\nu$) penalizes random hallucinated walks versus specific compliance with the malicious advisor's trap.

**Data Contamination Risks:** Sokoban is a classic computational problem with heavy representation in coding tutorials, reinforcement learning repositories, and general text corpora. The paper does not discuss how it mitigates or measures the risk of benchmark leakage. If models are relying on memorized Sokoban heuristics rather than genuine spatial reasoning and vigilance, the core mechanism of the evaluation is compromised.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental rigor of this paper suffers from severe, potentially fatal flaws regarding reproducibility, baseline controls, and statistical validity.

**Evaluation of Fictitious or Unreleased Models:** The most alarming issue in the manuscript is the reporting of empirical results on models that do not exist or are completely inaccessible to the scientific community. The bar charts detailing model rankings explicitly list models such as "GPT-5", "Grok 4 Fast", "Claude Sonnet 4", and "Gemini 2.5 Pro". Presenting empirical data on unreleased or fictitious systems fundamentally destroys the reproducibility and credibility of the paper. If these names are intended as pseudonyms for double-blind review, it is a highly irregular and confusing practice that obscures the actual capabilities being measured. If this data is simulated or hypothetical, it cannot be used to support factual claims about the dissociable capacities of LLMs. Scientific claims at an empirical venue require verifiable evidence on accessible models.

**Absence of a Non-Persuasive Baseline:** The experiments compare an unassisted player to an advised player to measure the effect of the advisor. However, to rigorously claim that a model is demonstrating "persuasion" or a lack of "vigilance," the experimental design must rule out basic instruction-following (compliance). The study lacks a baseline condition where the player is given "neutral incorrect advice" or randomly generated commands. Without this baseline, we cannot mathematically disentangle whether a model was actively persuaded by manipulative reasoning, or if it simply exhibits a high baseline rate of blindly following user-provided instructions.

**Sample Size and Evaluation Scale:** The evaluation is restricted to exactly 10 custom Sokoban puzzles. The authors transparently note that puzzles were limited to two boxes and two goals because models struggled to track more objects—this honest reporting of limitations is highly appreciated and a mark of good scientific practice. However, generalizing from 10 highly simplified 2D puzzles to broad claims about "high-stakes human decision-making domains" is a massive overreach. A sample size of 10 puzzles is insufficient to robustly establish that puzzle-solving, persuasion, and vigilance are universally dissociable capacities across LLMs. 

**Lack of Statistical Validation:** The paper claims to have run 5 trials per puzzle to account for stochasticity. This results in 50 trials per condition. Despite this, the bar charts displaying the results present precise numerical rankings but entirely lack error bars, variance estimates, or confidence intervals. Multi-turn LLM interactions are notoriously high-variance, highly sensitive to temperature sampling, and prone to cascading errors. Furthermore, the abstract's claim that models modulate their token usage (using fewer tokens for benevolent advice and more for malicious advice) is stated as a factual finding, but there is no statistical hypothesis testing (e.g., t-tests or ANOVA) provided to demonstrate that this difference in token distributions is statistically significant rather than a byproduct of random sampling noise.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- The use of the Sokoban environment to study multi-turn AI persuasion is a highly creative, safe, and ecologically valid alternative to single-turn QA benchmarks.
- The conceptual mechanism of decoupling an agent's persuasive text generation from its underlying task reasoning via an external algorithmic planner is an elegant experimental design choice.
- The transparent reporting of negative results—specifically acknowledging that models hit a strict capability ceiling at more than two boxes and two goals—provides valuable, honest data to the community regarding the current limits of LLM spatial reasoning.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Reproducibility Failure:** The empirical results are invalid as they rely on fictitious or unreleased models (e.g., "GPT-5", "Claude Sonnet 4"). This prevents any independent verification of the claims.
- **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The claims regarding token usage modulation and the broad dissociability of capacities are not supported by rigorous statistical testing or adequate sample sizes (only 10 puzzles evaluated).
- **Missing Equations:** The mathematical formulations for the core metrics ($\Psi$ and $\nu$) are absent, rendering the evaluation criteria a black box.
- **Confounding Variables:** The absence of a neutral/random advice baseline means "persuasion" cannot be formally disentangled from mere "compliance."

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To validate the claims made in this paper, the following evidence is strictly required:
1. **Clarification of Evaluated Models:** A clear specification of the actual, publicly accessible models used to generate the empirical results, replacing the fictitious names currently present in the figures.
2. **Mathematical Definitions:** The explicit formal equations for the persuasion ($\Psi$) and vigilance ($\nu$) metrics.
3. **Statistical Analysis:** The inclusion of error bars (e.g., 95% confidence intervals) on all result charts, along with formal significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to substantiate the token modulation claims and the performance differences between models.
4. **Baseline Ablation:** An additional experimental condition featuring a "neutral" or "random" advisor to establish the models' baseline rate of compliance versus true susceptibility to persuasive reasoning.
5. **Symmetric Decoupling Verification:** Documentation or evidence showing that the malicious advisor was algorithmically provided a path to a deadlock, ensuring its reasoning capability was decoupled in the exact same manner as the benevolent advisor.
