## Overview
The paper introduces "SurfelSoup," a novel end-to-end learned geometry compression framework for point clouds. The core idea relies on moving away from traditional rigid voxel or point-wise representations toward a structured, probabilistic surface-based representation called "pSurfel." The authors model local point occupancies using a bounded generalized Gaussian distribution. To optimize the rate-distortion (R-D) trade-off, these pSurfels are organized into an adaptive hierarchical structure, the "pSurfelTree," which is dynamically refined using a "Tree Decision" module. By adaptively selecting the optimal surfel granularity, the framework avoids redundant point-wise compression in smooth, homogeneous geometric regions, aiming to yield compact and coherent surface reconstructions. The method is evaluated under the MPEG common test conditions against generic "voxel-based baselines" and the specific MPEG standard G-PCC-GesTM-TriSoup.

*(Note: There is a typographical error in the title of the manuscript. "Probablistic" should be corrected to "Probabilistic" for publication).*

## Claimed Contributions
- The formulation of **pSurfel**, a probabilistic surface representation that models local point occupancies using bounded generalized Gaussian distributions.
- The introduction of **pSurfelTree**, an adaptive octree-like hierarchical representation.
- A **Tree Decision** module designed to optimize rate-distortion by dynamically selecting surfel granularity, explicitly mitigating redundant compression in smooth geometric regions.
- Empirical superiority under standard MPEG common test conditions, claiming visually superior, smooth, and coherent surface reconstructions, alongside consistent R-D gains over G-PCC-GesTM-TriSoup and unspecified voxel-based learned baselines.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual transition from the traditional TriSoup (Triangle Soup) geometry representation utilized in standard MPEG codecs to a fully learned "SurfelSoup" is elegant and theoretically well-motivated. Point clouds inherently possess high degrees of spatial redundancy, particularly along flat or smoothly curved surfaces. Representing these surfaces via localized probabilistic distributions (the pSurfels) instead of disjointed points or rigid voxels demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of both geometric modeling and information theory.

However, a critical gap in the contextualization of this novelty is the exact definition of the "voxel-based baselines" referenced in the abstract. Over the past 3-4 years, the field of learned point cloud geometry compression (PCGC) has advanced rapidly, producing highly efficient sparse convolutional frameworks (such as SparsePCGC, OctSqueeze, and subsequent variants). If the paper's definition of prior work only encompasses older, simple voxelized neural networks or strictly non-learned MPEG standards, the novelty and significance of the R-D gains are severely contextualized. An ICML-level contribution in this domain requires direct benchmarking against recent (2021-2024) state-of-the-art *learned* models, not just traditional codecs or rudimentary voxel-based precursors.

## Technical Soundness
The probabilistic modeling of point occupancies via a bounded generalized Gaussian distribution provides a theoretically robust foundation. It naturally accommodates the inherent sensor noise and uncertainty present in raw point cloud data.

However, several technical uncertainties remain regarding the end-to-end optimization of the proposed pipeline:
1. **Discrete Optimization in Tree Subdivision:** The pSurfelTree relies on adaptive subdivision governed by a Tree Decision module. Hierarchical subdivision is an inherently discrete, non-differentiable operation. The paper must clearly elucidate how the gradients are approximated or bypassed during end-to-end training to ensure the rate-distortion optimality of the surfel granularity selection.
2. **Hyperparameter Overhead:** Fitting a bounded generalized Gaussian distribution requires learning and subsequently transmitting its defining parameters (e.g., shape, variance, bounds). The manuscript must clarify how these continuous parameters are quantized for entropy coding and whether the overhead of transmitting them negates the bitrate savings at lower bitrates.
3. **Behavior on High-Frequency Geometry:** While the method elegantly targets smooth regions, the assumption of a generalized Gaussian distribution may fail catastrophically on high-frequency geometric features, sharp edges, sparse foliage, or highly irregular LiDAR scans. The technical soundness of the representation hinges on how the Tree Decision module behaves when no smooth surface can be reliably fitted.

## Experimental Rigor
The decision to evaluate SurfelSoup under the rigorous, standardized MPEG common test conditions (CTC) is a significant strength. The MPEG CTC provides a recognized, standardized framework for comparing point cloud compression algorithms, ensuring that the datasets, bitrates, and metric calculations are consistent with industry standards.

Despite this excellent methodological foundation, the claims of experimental rigor are currently undermined by an absence of concrete quantitative reporting and transparency:
1. **Ambiguous Quantitative Gains:** The claim of a "consistent gain" is insufficient for rigorous evaluation. The paper must explicitly report Bjøntegaard Delta (BD) Rates. Furthermore, because the method focuses on surface structures (Surfels), it is critical that both **D1 (point-to-point)** and **D2 (point-to-plane)** PSNR metrics are reported. A method optimizing for smooth surfaces may show artificially inflated D2 metrics while performing poorly on D1 due to exact point placement inaccuracies. Both metrics are required to provide a complete picture of the rate-distortion performance.
2. **Computational Complexity Evaluation:** End-to-end learned compression frameworks often suffer from severe computational bottlenecks compared to their traditional counterparts. There is no mention of the encoding or decoding latency, nor the memory footprint. The computational overhead of querying the Tree Decision module and parameterizing the pSurfels must be strictly quantified against G-PCC-GesTM-TriSoup. 
3. **Statistical Validity:** Learned codecs are sensitive to network initialization. The paper must ensure that the R-D performance metrics do not merely represent a single, "lucky" training run but are robust. If the margins of improvement over the baseline are small, statistical significance testing (or reporting variance across random seeds) is required.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Principled Probabilistic Approach:** The introduction of pSurfels using a bounded generalized Gaussian distribution is mathematically sound and represents a meaningful evolution in modeling local geometry for compression.
- **Adaptive R-D Optimization:** The pSurfelTree coupled with the Tree Decision module explicitly models and penalizes spatial redundancy, addressing a fundamental inefficiency in rigid octree and voxel-based compression pipelines.
- **Standardized Evaluation Paradigm:** The utilization of the MPEG common test conditions is highly commendable, anchoring the method to real-world, industry-standard benchmarks (G-PCC) and allowing for reliable comparisons.
- **Ethics and Safety:** The research focuses on domain-agnostic data compression. The broader impacts are overwhelmingly positive (reduced bandwidth, lower energy consumption). As long as standard MPEG datasets were used (and no sensitive, non-anonymized human scan PII was exposed), the ethical standing is highly sound.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Insufficient Baseline Identification (Potential Fatal Flaw):** Without knowing exactly which state-of-the-art *learned* baselines are used, the paper's primary claims of superiority are in doubt. Outperforming standard G-PCC-TriSoup is necessary but not sufficient for an ICML venue; it must be shown that SurfelSoup outperforms contemporary neural compression models.
- **Lack of Open-Source Code (Reproducibility Risk):** The architecture involves custom, complex components (pSurfelTree formulation, parameterization of generalized Gaussian distributions, discrete Tree Decision thresholds). Without a repository or detailed supplementary code, reproducing this end-to-end framework from scratch is highly improbable for the community.
- **Hidden Computational Costs (Potential Fatal Flaw):** If the encoding time required to optimize the Surfel granularity makes the codec impractically slow (e.g., orders of magnitude slower than standard G-PCC without proportional R-D gains), the utility of the method is compromised.
- **Potential Failure Modes on Sharp Geometry:** The emphasis on "smooth and coherent surface reconstructions" strongly suggests the method oversmooths sharp geometric features or fails on sparse/noisy point clouds. A robust limitations section investigating these edge cases is essential.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To cross the threshold for acceptance, the following evidence is required:
1. **Direct comparison against State-of-the-Art Learned Baselines:** The results must include comparisons against recent, well-regarded neural point cloud compression frameworks (e.g., sparse convolutional methods).
2. **Complete R-D Metrics:** Explicit tables detailing D1 BD-Rate and D2 BD-Rate across the evaluated MPEG test sequences. 
3. **Runtime and Memory Profiling:** A formal comparison of encoding/decoding times (in milliseconds/seconds) and peak memory usage compared to G-PCC-GesTM-TriSoup.
4. **Ablation Studies:** Experiments isolating the performance contributions of the probabilistic surface modeling (pSurfel) versus the hierarchical structural adaptation (Tree Decision module).
5. **Code Availability:** Anonymous release of the training and evaluation codebase, including the custom tree subdivision mechanisms, to allow for Area Chair verification and future community reproducibility.
