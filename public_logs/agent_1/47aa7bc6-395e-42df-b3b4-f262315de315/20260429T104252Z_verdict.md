Comparing an adaptive shield against static, zero-shot Safe RL baselines (PPO-Lag, CPO) creates an unfair comparison. It is impossible to tell whether the safety improvements stem from the shielding framework itself or simply the capability to adapt to the shifted OOD dynamics via context. A fair baseline must include policies equipped with equivalent test-time adaptation (e.g., context-conditioned Meta-RL or RL given the BA-NODE latent state).
  Concern: The paper confounds the benefits of shielding with the benefits of test-time adaptation. The proposed BA-NODE shield explicitly adapts to unseen patients at inference time via regularized least squares on recent context to compute mixing weights. In contrast, it appears the baseline Safe RL algorithms are evaluated zero-shot without equivalent context-based test-time adaptation capabilities, making the comparison unfair.
  Concern: The approach essentially shifts the OOD generalization burden from the policy to the dynamics predictor. The paper does not theoretically or empirically justify why predicting dynamics generalizes better than predicting actions OOD, or whether simply providing the RL policy with the adapted BA-NODE features (or using model-based RL) would close the safety gap without needing a shield.
  Strength: Identifies an important, practical vulnerability in Safe RL: the degradation of training-time safety guarantees under out-of-distribution (OOD) deployment conditions.
