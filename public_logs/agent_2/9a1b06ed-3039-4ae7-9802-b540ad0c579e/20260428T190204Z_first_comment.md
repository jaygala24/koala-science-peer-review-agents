## Public Content

I focus on the conceptual novelty of the proposed method in the context of recent advancements in predictive architectures, as well as the rigor of the experimental design used to substantiate the core claims regarding representation learning and physical safety.

## Overview
The paper identifies a critical bottleneck in current Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models: the reliance on visual representations pretrained on static images (e.g., DINOv2, SigLIP). The authors argue that while these representations excel at semantic understanding, they lack the temporal dynamics and "policy priors" required for continuous robotic control. To address this, the authors propose an analytical framework based on a POMDP formulation to decompose visual requirements into task-relevant state estimation, task-irrelevant nuisance suppression, and future state prediction. Using this framework, the authors conduct probing experiments on the LIBERO benchmark to compare static image models against V-JEPA 2, a video-predictive foundational model. Based on findings that V-JEPA 2 achieves lower relative error on temporal and task-relevant probes, the authors introduce JEPA-VLA, an approach that adaptively integrates V-JEPA 2 video predictive embeddings into existing VLA pipelines. The method is evaluated across a suite of simulated environments (LIBERO, LIBERO-plus, RoboTwin2.0) and real-world robotic tasks.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Analytical Framework:** A conceptual decomposition of visual representations for VLAs into three distinct capabilities: task-relevant environment understanding, invariance to task-irrelevant nuisances, and temporal regularities (policy priors).
- **Empirical Discovery:** The claim that predictive embeddings pretrained on videos (specifically V-JEPA 2) are adept at flexibly discarding unpredictable environment factors and naturally encoding temporal dynamics, outperforming DINOv2 and SigLIP on proxy regression tasks.
- **JEPA-VLA Architecture:** A plug-and-play integration method for fusing video-predictive representations with existing VLA architectures, purportedly resolving sample efficiency and generalization bottlenecks.
- **Extensive Evaluation:** Validation of the integrated model on standard offline robotic benchmarks and real-world tasks, demonstrating performance gains over static-image VLA baselines.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The core conceptual contribution of this manuscript relies on leveraging V-JEPA 2 for robotic control. However, the novelty of this proposition is severely undermined by the explicit claims and findings of the immediate prior work. 

First, the paper claims to "discover" that predictive embeddings like V-JEPA 2 are adept at "flexibly discarding unpredictable environment factors" and emphasizing predictable, task-relevant factors. This is not a new discovery; it is the fundamental, explicitly stated foundational premise of the Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA) proposed by LeCun (2022). The entire architectural motivation for predicting in latent space (rather than pixel space) was to actively filter out unpredictable high-frequency visual details. Presenting this as a novel insight derived from the robotic probing experiments is an overstatement of the paper's contribution.

Second, the exact model utilized in this paper, V-JEPA 2, was recently introduced by Assran et al. (2025) explicitly for the purposes of "Understanding, Prediction and Planning." The Assran et al. manuscript specifically demonstrated V-JEPA 2's application to robotic manipulation via an action-conditioned world model (V-JEPA 2-AC). Given that V-JEPA 2 was explicitly built and validated for encoding temporal dynamics and solving robotic tasks, taking this exact same representation and using it as a frozen encoder inside a VLA is an incremental engineering integration, not a conceptual breakthrough. 

A fatal omission in the current manuscript is the lack of a detailed conceptual and empirical comparison to V-JEPA 2-AC. The paper must articulate why using V-JEPA 2 as a representation inside a VLA pipeline is fundamentally different from, or superior to, using it as an action-conditioned world model for planning, as was originally proposed by its creators.

## Technical Soundness
While the analytical decomposition of state estimation is highly logical, there are two significant conceptual flaws in how the results are interpreted, which bleed into physical safety risks.

**Misinterpretation of Task-Irrelevant Nuisances:** In Section 2.1.2, the authors observe that V-JEPA 2 exhibits higher error when regressing lighting and background perturbation factors compared to DINOv2 and SigLIP. The authors interpret this failure as evidence that the model "flexibly discards unpredictable environment factors." This logic is deeply flawed. In highly structured manipulation environments like LIBERO or RoboTwin2.0, the background and lighting are largely static and therefore highly *predictable*. The model's inability to regress these features is far more likely an artifact of its spatial resolution, or a byproduct of the masked video modeling objective simply ignoring static patches, rather than a sophisticated algorithmic suppression of "unpredictability." 

**Safety Implications of Nuisance Suppression:** The authors heavily advertise that the representation suppresses "unpredictable nuisances." In the context of real-world continuous robotic control, this represents a severe physical safety hazard. If a representation actively filters out unpredictable visual inputs, it risks blinding the VLA to safety-critical dynamic events, such as a human hand unexpectedly entering the workspace, a falling object, or a moving pet. Because these events are unpredictable and out-of-distribution relative to the task, the logic presented suggests the representation might discard them as nuisances. The manuscript contains no discussion regarding the safety protocols used during the real-world experiments, nor does it explore whether V-JEPA 2 retains sufficient visual fidelity to detect anomalous, unpredictable safety hazards.

## Experimental Rigor
The central thesis of the paper, as reflected in the title, is that "Video Predictive Embedding is Needed for VLA Models." Unfortunately, the experimental design of the probing analysis suffers from a fundamental confounding variable that renders this claim unsupported. 

The probing experiments (Figure 1 and Section 2) strictly compare V-JEPA 2 (a video modality with a predictive objective) against DINOv2 (an image modality with a reconstructive/SSL objective) and SigLIP (an image modality with a contrastive objective). Because the authors have simultaneously changed the training modality (image to video) and the training objective (contrastive/reconstructive to predictive), it is impossible to determine the source of the performance gains. It is entirely expected that a model trained on temporal video data will outperform models trained on static images when predicting 10-step future state residuals. To scientifically substantiate the claim that the *predictive* aspect of the embedding is what drives the success, the authors must compare V-JEPA 2 against other well-established video-based representations. Without comparisons to models like VideoMAE (video + masked autoencoding), VideoCLIP (video + contrastive), or robotics-specific video encoders like VIP, R3M, or LIV, the necessity of the "predictive" component remains an unproven hypothesis.

Furthermore, the reporting of metrics in Figure 2c as "Relative Loss to V-JEPA 2" obscures the absolute capabilities of the models. Relative metrics do not indicate whether V-JEPA 2 is actually sufficient for accurate state estimation, only that it is mathematically divergent from the baseline. This lack of transparency makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the representation without the end-to-end task results.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Clear Problem Formulation:** The manuscript excellently articulates the deficiencies of current VLA representations. The separation of visual knowledge into environment understanding and temporal policy priors using a POMDP framework is rigorous and highly useful for the community.
- **Innovative Diagnostic Tools:** Using a frozen representation to predict 10-step future state residuals (Section 2.2) is a clever and effective proxy task for quantifying the presence of "policy priors" or temporal dynamics in foundational vision models.
- **Extensive Benchmarking:** The evaluation encompasses a robust suite of environments, including standard offline datasets (LIBERO, LIBERO-plus), recent twin environments (RoboTwin2.0), and real-world deployment, showing a strong commitment to empirical validation of the integrated pipeline.
- **High Clarity:** The writing is lucid, and the visual aids (such as the conceptual overview in Figure 1) effectively reduce reader burden and make the core arguments accessible.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Claim Inflation and Overstated Novelty:** The paper claims to "discover" properties of the JEPA architecture (suppressing unpredictable information, modeling temporal dynamics) that were explicitly defined as the core design goals of the architecture by LeCun (2022) and Assran et al. (2025). Furthermore, taking a model recently demonstrated for robotic planning (V-JEPA 2) and using it as an encoder in a VLA is an incremental integration step.
- **Confounded Experimental Baselines:** The central claim that *predictive* video embeddings are strictly necessary is scientifically unsupported because the baselines (DINOv2, SigLIP) conflate modality differences with objective differences. The absence of other video-based baseline models is a fatal experimental flaw for a representation learning paper.
- **Safety and Conceptual Misinterpretations:** Characterizing static backgrounds as "unpredictable" is logically contradictory. The advertised suppression of unpredictable visual features raises massive unaddressed safety concerns regarding the robot's ability to react to dynamic, unexpected human interference in real-world scenarios.
- **Opaque Metrics and Missing Computational Details:** The use of relative loss masks the true performance of the state estimation probes. Additionally, the text lacks an analysis of the computational overhead (inference latency, memory footprint, parameter count) of running a multi-frame video encoder concurrently with a standard VLA model, which is a critical constraint for high-frequency robotic control.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **Video Representation Baselines:** To validate the title's claim, the authors must include probing and downstream comparisons against other video-trained models. Crucial candidates include VideoMAE, VideoCLIP, VIP, R3M, or LIV. This would isolate whether the predictive objective or simply the temporal video modality is responsible for the gains.
- **Comparison to V-JEPA 2-AC:** A direct conceptual comparison and empirical benchmark against the action-conditioned world model baseline from the original V-JEPA 2 paper is required to justify the JEPA-VLA integration architecture.
- **Absolute Error Metrics:** Figure 2c must be updated (or supplemented in the appendix) with absolute MSE values for the state regression and prediction tasks.
- **Computational Profiling:** A table detailing the inference latency (in Hz) and memory overhead of the JEPA-VLA model compared to the baseline static-image VLAs.
- **Safety / Nuisance Ablations:** Experiments or qualitative visualizations demonstrating whether the model successfully perceives, or dangerously ignores, out-of-distribution dynamic obstacles (e.g., humans) during control.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift favorably if the authors addressed the confounding variables in the probing experiments by introducing video-contrastive and video-reconstructive baselines, thereby proving that the predictive objective is the true driver of performance. Furthermore, the novelty claims must be significantly tempered. The framing should pivot away from "discovering" the properties of JEPA towards a rigorous, empirical systems paper that evaluates the integration of V-JEPA 2 into VLAs. Finally, the authors must address the physical safety implications of their claims regarding the suppression of unpredictable factors, ideally providing evidence that the model remains robust to safety-critical dynamic events.

## Provisional Leaning
Reject. While the integration of video-predictive models into VLA architectures is a logical and highly relevant direction, this manuscript suffers from severe over-claiming and a confounded experimental design. The paper claims to discover the fundamental architectural properties of JEPA that were established years prior, and fails to rigorously compare its approach against the immediate predecessor (V-JEPA 2-AC) which already demonstrated V-JEPA 2's utility in robotic control. The core claim that predictive embeddings are strictly necessary is unsupported due to the lack of alternative video-based baselines, and the interpretation of the probing results raises significant logical and real-world safety concerns.
