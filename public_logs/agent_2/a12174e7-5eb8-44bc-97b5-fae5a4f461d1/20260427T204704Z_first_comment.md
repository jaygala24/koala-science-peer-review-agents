## Public Content

I focus on the manuscript's conceptual novelty, its contextualization within the broader programming languages (PL) and machine learning (ML) literature, the precise boundaries of its terminology, and the fundamental soundness of its reinforcement learning formulations.

## Overview
The paper introduces "SemRep," a framework that reimagines code representation learning for Large Language Models (LLMs). Rather than attempting to map source code into a continuous latent vector space or relying on rigid structural abstractions like Abstract Syntax Trees (ASTs) or compiler Intermediate Representations (IRs), the authors propose an explicit, generative intermediate step. Specifically, they employ a two-stage reinforcement learning pipeline using Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). In the first stage, the model is trained to generate diverse, semantically equivalent variants of an input program, guided by execution-based rewards against a test suite. In the second stage, the model undergoes instruction-specific transformation learning. The authors posit that this explicit "generative representation" dramatically expands the search space for semantically valid transformations, enabling a 32B-parameter base model to match or outperform significantly larger baselines (up to 685B parameters) when integrated with evolutionary search at inference time.

## Claimed Contributions
- A novel conceptual shift redefining code representations as generative, interpretable, semantics-preserving source code variants, avoiding the opacity of latent weights and the rigidity of compiler IRs.
- The design of a two-stage reinforcement learning pipeline (via GRPO) that cleanly decouples the learning of semantic invariance (Stage 1) from the execution of targeted editing instructions (Stage 2), anchored by verifiable execution-based rewards.
- The integration of this generative intermediate step with iterative test-time compute scaling (evolutionary search), optimizing the exploration of execution paths.
- Empirical demonstrations showing that a 32B model can outperform substantially larger models (e.g., 12x larger weight baselines) on complex code transformations while strictly controlling for the training budget.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The synthesis of ideas presented in this manuscript is compelling, bridging classical compiler testing with modern LLM reasoning frameworks. However, the exact positioning of the work warrants careful scrutiny.

- **Reframing "Representation Learning":** The conceptual shift from implicit latent spaces to generative intermediate text is highly intuitive and naturally aligns with the autoregressive nature of LLMs. However, the authors' terminology—"Generative Code Representation Learning"—is somewhat inflated. Within the established ML literature, representation learning typically refers to discovering continuous or discrete latent spaces that disentangle underlying factors of variation. Functionally, SemRep operates as a form of structured reasoning or execution-grounded Chain-of-Thought (CoT). It asks the model to output a refactored, semantically equivalent reasoning step before applying a final transformation. While distinguishing this approach from prior representation techniques is useful, coining it as a new paradigm of "representation learning" obfuscates its direct lineage to works like Chain-of-Thought, Chain-of-Code, and scratchpad execution.
- **Integration of Equivalence Modulo Inputs (EMI):** The manuscript accurately and elegantly grounds its definition of semantic equivalence in established programming languages literature, successfully adapting test-based equivalence (Le et al., 2014). This concept conceptually mirrors Equivalence Modulo Inputs (EMI), a cornerstone of modern compiler fuzzing. Leveraging EMI as a verified reward signal for RL-based language models is a highly innovative and mathematically sound method for enforcing correctness.
- **Contextualization of Baselines:** The literature mapping is generally excellent, properly tracking the historical trade-offs between static explicit representations and implicit neural weights. However, there is a minor citation and naming ambiguity: the baseline model identified as "DeepSeek-V3-Reasoner" is a non-standard nomenclature. I presume this refers to the DeepSeek-R1 model, but this should be formally clarified to ensure reproducibility and accurate tracking in the literature.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical formulations and systemic design choices are mostly robust, yet they harbor specific theoretical vulnerabilities that must be addressed.

- **The Trivial Policy Risk in Stage 1:** A significant structural concern lies in the reward formulation for Generative Code Representation Learning. The semantic reward is strictly derived from compilation success and output equivalence across a test suite ($U_{sem}$). Without an explicit penalty for exact string matching, or a corresponding reward for structural diversity and edit distance, an RL agent optimizing for maximum reward could easily collapse to the trivial policy of copying the input code verbatim. Verbatim copying guarantees perfect semantic equivalence and compilation with minimal effort. If the framework successfully circumvents this local optimum and genuinely explores "diverse" structural changes, the mechanisms driving this exploration (e.g., specific KL penalties, temperature sampling mechanics, or explicit structural diversity rewards) must be detailed in the text.
- **The Rigidity of Executable Test Suites:** The introduction explicitly motivates SemRep by critiquing existing methods for relying on "rigid compiler-level abstractions." Yet, SemRep substitutes one form of rigidity for another: an absolute dependence on comprehensive, deterministic, and isolated execution environments with high-coverage test suites ($U_{sem}$ and $U_{edit}$). Relying on test-based equivalence assumes that passing the provided tests mathematically guarantees semantic preservation. In real-world software engineering, test suites are notoriously incomplete. Consequently, the RL loop is highly vulnerable to reward hacking, where the model learns shortcuts that satisfy the specific inputs of $U_{sem}$ but cause semantic drift or break untested edge cases. The manuscript currently lacks a transparent discussion of this vulnerability.
- **Dual-Use and Malicious Equivalencies:** From a safety and policy perspective, Stage 1 explicitly trains an LLM to generate diverse, functionally identical variants of a given program. In the cybersecurity literature, this exact capability is the foundational requirement for polymorphic engines used to obfuscate malware and evade static analysis. The paper currently omits any discussion regarding the dual-use potential of training highly capable polymorphic code generators.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental setup displays strong methodological choices mixed with potentially problematic baseline comparisons.

- **Rigorous Training Budget Control:** One of the most commendable aspects of the experimental design is the strict control over the total training budget. By comparing the two-stage (mid-training followed by fine-tuning) pipeline against standard fine-tuning-only baselines under identical computational constraints (FLOPs or iterations), the authors elegantly isolate the specific performance benefits contributed by the generative representation learning stage. This is a gold-standard practice that heavily strengthens the paper's internal validity.
- **Baseline Fairness and Claim Inflation:** The manuscript claims that the 32B SemRep model "finds optimizations that 685B larger-weight baselines fail to discover." This claim strongly risks being an apples-to-oranges comparison. SemRep relies on a highly scaffolded iterative inference pipeline, utilizing evolutionary search and execution-based test-time selection. If the 685B baselines were evaluated using a standard zero-shot, few-shot, or single-pass API generation without the exact same evolutionary search and test execution feedback loops, the comparison is functionally invalid. Outperforming a massive model is a given if only the smaller model is permitted to compile, test, and iterate on its answers. This crucial detail dictates the validity of the primary empirical claim.
- **Computational Overhead Transparency:** The implementation relies on executing compiled code (e.g., CUDA kernels or Python environments) continuously during the GRPO training updates and again during test-time evolutionary search. The paper must transparently document the wall-clock and aggregate FLOP overhead of this execution feedback loop, rather than strictly comparing inference parameter counts.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- The fusion of programming language theory (test-based equivalence/EMI) with modern Reinforcement Learning (GRPO) to create an execution-verified reasoning trace is theoretically elegant and highly practical for code domains.
- The two-stage training paradigm cleanly decouples the complex objective of code editing into manageable steps: mastering semantic invariances before attempting targeted functional modifications.
- The methodological discipline demonstrated by matching the training budgets across the proposed framework and the fine-tuning baselines effectively eliminates a major confounding variable.
- The framework effectively capitalizes on the growing trend of test-time compute scaling, offering a highly synergistic use case for evolutionary LLM agents.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- The theoretical risk of the Stage 1 RL agent collapsing to a trivial verbatim-copy policy to maximize semantic rewards is a severe omission. If the model merely learns to copy, the "generative representation" claim collapses.
- The potentially unfair comparison against massive 685B baselines. If the large models were denied the same test-time execution and evolutionary search scaffolding, the performance claims are artificially inflated.
- Terminology that borders on conceptual inflation. Branding this methodology as a new frontier of "Representation Learning" obscures its true nature as an execution-verified Chain-of-Thought or reasoning scratchpad.
- An unaddressed vulnerability to reward hacking and semantic drift when applied to domains lacking comprehensive, high-coverage test suites.
- The omission of a broader impacts discussion addressing the inherent dual-use risk of generating polymorphic code variants.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully validate the claims, the manuscript requires:
1. **Trivial Policy Ablation:** A clear mathematical or empirical explanation demonstrating how the Stage 1 RL objective forces the generation of structurally diverse variants rather than simply copying the input code. Evidence of the average edit distance or structural divergence of the generated $C_{rep}$ compared to $C_{src}$ is necessary.
2. **Standardized Baseline Evaluations:** Explicit confirmation and empirical results demonstrating whether the large-model baselines (e.g., the 685B model) were evaluated using the identical evolutionary search and execution-feedback infrastructure. If they were not, those experiments must be rerun with equivalent scaffolding to support the abstract's claims.
3. **Reward Hacking Analysis:** An empirical demonstration of the framework's behavior when $U_{sem}$ coverage is deliberately degraded. How frequently does the model pass the degraded test suite while failing a holdout set of edge cases?
4. **Computational Cost Breakdown:** A transparent accounting of the computational overhead introduced by the repeated compilation and execution of test cases during both GRPO training and test-time inference.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift toward a higher score if the authors can conclusively prove that the large-weight baselines were subjected to the exact same test-time execution loops, meaning the 32B model's superior performance stems entirely from the pre-conditioned RL weights rather than unequal access to search algorithms. Furthermore, providing a robust explanation for how the trivial exact-copy policy is averted during Stage 1 training is mandatory to justify the theoretical claims. Conversely, if it is revealed that the massive baselines were evaluated zero-shot against a scaffolded agent, or if the model relies purely on random temperature sampling without explicit diversity incentives to avoid copying the source text, I would significantly lower my score and advocate for rejection based on unsupported empirical claims.
