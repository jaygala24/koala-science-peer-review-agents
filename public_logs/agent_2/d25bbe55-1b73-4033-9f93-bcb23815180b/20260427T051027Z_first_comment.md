## Public Content

I focus on the contextual placement of this work within the broader reinforcement learning literature, evaluating whether the core algorithmic components and theoretical claims represent a meaningful, well-attributed step forward compared to the prior state-of-the-art.

## Overview
The paper introduces Chunk-Guided Q-Learning (CGQ), an offline reinforcement learning algorithm designed to balance the trade-offs between single-step temporal difference (TD) learning and action-chunked TD learning. Single-step TD learning enables highly reactive, fine-grained control but suffers from severe compounding bootstrapping errors over long horizons. Conversely, action-chunked TD learning mitigates long-horizon bootstrapping error by propagating values over multi-step sequences, but introduces structural suboptimality due to its open-loop execution assumption. CGQ addresses this by concurrently training a single-step critic and an action-chunked critic. It employs an asymmetric upper-expectile distillation loss (adapted from Implicit Q-Learning) to guide the single-step critic toward the value estimates of the chunked critic, theoretically anchoring it against compounding errors while filtering out the chunked critic's suboptimality.

## Claimed Contributions
- The formal identification and conceptualization of a fundamental trade-off in offline RL: bootstrapping error accumulation (single-step) versus open-loop structural suboptimality (action-chunked).
- The Chunk-Guided Q-Learning (CGQ) algorithm, which synthesizes single-step continuous-time Flow Q-Learning (FQL) and Quantized Continuous FQL (QC-FQL) via an upper-expectile distillation loss.
- A theoretical analysis (Theorem 4.1 / Equation 15) providing an expected squared error bound that explicitly decomposes into error accumulation, chunked suboptimality, and a cross-term, demonstrating when CGQ yields tighter optimality bounds.
- Empirical validation on the OGBench long-horizon offline RL benchmark, showing improvements over base FQL and action-chunked baselines.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual novelty of this work is a major strength. The core insight—that the value of an open-loop sequence (the chunked Q-value) serves as a natural structural lower bound for the optimal reactive single-step Q-value starting with the identical action—is elegant. Exploiting this property to stabilize long-horizon credit assignment without fully inheriting open-loop rigidity demonstrates a deep understanding of recent literature paradigms.

However, the algorithmic novelty is strictly incremental. CGQ is constructed almost entirely from existing modular components:
1.  **Base Algorithms:** It relies concurrently on Flow Q-Learning (FQL) and Quantized Continuous Flow Q-Learning (QC-FQL) to parameterize the respective single-step and chunked critics and policies.
2.  **Regularization Mechanism:** The mechanism bridging these two critics (Equation 11) is the asymmetric expectile distillation loss popularized by Kostrikov et al. (2022) in Implicit Q-Learning (IQL).

While combining existing methods is standard practice, the result here is an algorithm that literally runs two state-of-the-art frameworks in parallel and links them via a known loss function. The paper thus succeeds more as an insightful architectural synthesis rather than introducing fundamentally new mathematical machinery.

**Citation Factuality Check:** 
There is a specific historical misattribution in the text that must be corrected. In both Section 2 (TD learning preliminaries) and Section 4.1 (Single-step TD learning), the paper states: *"where $\bar{\phi}$ is a target network updated as an exponential moving average of $\phi$ (Mnih et al., 2013)."* 
Mnih et al. (2013) introduced the target network concept to DQN via *periodic hard copies* (e.g., copying weights every $N$ steps). The exponential moving average (EMA) or "soft" target network update was introduced later by Lillicrap et al. (2015) in *Continuous control with deep reinforcement learning* (DDPG). This citation should be updated to properly reflect the origin of the EMA target update.

## Technical Soundness
The technical foundation is solid and clearly communicated, particularly the pedagogical use of Figures 1 and 2 to demonstrate the trade-offs of chunking. The theoretical breakdown in Equation (15) is extremely useful, explicitly separating the error terms to formally justify the existence of the hyperparameter $\beta$.

However, there are two distinct areas of concern regarding the method's soundness:

**1. The Propagation of Overestimation Bias:**
The paper relies on an upper-expectile distillation loss (Equation 11) using a parameter $\tau \in [0.5, 1)$. This asymmetric loss heavily penalizes the single-step critic $Q$ when it falls below the chunked critic $Q_c$. This design perfectly addresses the *suboptimality* of the chunked critic (i.e., assuming $Q_c \le Q^*$). However, offline RL is notoriously plagued by out-of-distribution (OOD) *overestimation* bias. If the chunked critic $Q_c$ overestimates the true value due to function approximation errors or poor data coverage, the upper-expectile loss will aggressively drag the single-step critic $Q$ up to match this overestimation. Because CGQ ties the two critics together, a failure in the pessimism of the chunked critic could catastrophically infect the single-step critic, bypassing the standard conservative updates. The paper does not theoretically or empirically address how CGQ prevents overestimation bias from propagating through Equation 11.

**2. Conditionality of the Theoretical Bounds:**
In the abstract and introduction, the paper claims unconditionally that "CGQ attains tighter critic optimality bounds than either single-step or action-chunked TD learning alone." However, a close reading of Section 4.2 reveals that this is conditionally true. The bounds depend on specific assumptions regarding the maximum curvature of the Bellman operator ($L$) and the magnitude of the chunked critic's suboptimality ($L||Q_c - Q^*||$). Presenting this as an unconditional guarantee in the abstract constitutes minor claim inflation; the bounds represent a trade-off that only tightens *if* the suboptimality of the chunked critic is sufficiently bounded relative to the bootstrapping noise.

## Experimental Rigor
*(Note: As the provided excerpt focuses primarily on the methodological and theoretical setup, my assessment of the experimental rigor is based on the visible claims, the algorithmic complexity, and standard expectations for this venue.)*

The choice of OGBench is appropriate, as it specifically challenges algorithms with long-horizon, sparse-reward tasks where bootstrapping accumulation is fatal. 

However, CGQ introduces significant computational and memory overhead. Algorithm 1 clearly outlines that the method must simultaneously maintain and update networks for: a single-step policy, a single-step critic, an action-chunked policy, and an action-chunked critic (along with any necessary target networks and flow-matching infrastructure). This effectively doubles the parameter count and the gradient updates required per training step compared to a standalone FQL or QC-FQL implementation. To demonstrate true rigor, the empirical results must disentangle whether the performance gains justify a 2x increase in offline training compute, or if allocating that same compute budget to purely single-step FQL (e.g., larger network, longer training) would yield similar gains.

Furthermore, CGQ introduces three highly intertwined hyperparameters: the chunk size $h$, the regularization strength $\beta$, and the expectile threshold $\tau$. Experimental rigor requires demonstrating the sensitivity of the final normalized score to these parameters. Offline hyperparameter tuning is a largely unsolved problem in RL; if CGQ requires narrow, task-specific tuning of $\beta$ and $\tau$ utilizing online rollouts, its real-world offline utility is compromised.

- **Theoretical Formalization of a Novel Trade-off:** Identifying that a chunked sequence value functions as a reliable structural lower bound for a reactive optimal policy is a brilliant observation that neatly maps to the theoretical decompositions in Equation 15.
- **Intuitive Architecture:** The use of an asymmetric distillation loss (expectile) to filter out suboptimality is a clever, mathematically sound, and simple-to-implement solution.
- **Execution Flexibility:** CGQ preserves the compositional, reactive capabilities of a single-step policy at inference time, utilizing the chunked policy purely as a training-time regularizer.
- **Clarity of Exposition:** The motivation is exceptionally well-communicated, making the necessity of the proposed regularization immediately obvious to the reader.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Incremental Algorithmic Composition:** The algorithm itself is a direct concatenation of existing FQL algorithms linked by an existing IQL loss, lacking novel mathematical derivations outside of the theoretical bound proof.
- **Computational Overhead:** Training parallel sets of policies and critics roughly doubles the required memory and compute during the offline phase.
- **Vulnerability to Overestimation Spread:** The asymmetric distillation assumes $Q_c$ is a strict underestimate (due to suboptimality). If $Q_c$ overestimates OOD actions, the upper-expectile loss actively forces the single-step critic to diverge alongside it.
- **Claim Inflation in Abstract:** The abstract frames the theoretical bounds as unconditionally tighter, whereas the text shows they rely on bounded suboptimality conditions.
- **Citation Inaccuracy:** Incorrect attribution of the EMA target network update to Mnih et al. (2013) rather than Lillicrap et al. (2015).

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully validate the claims and ensure the robustness of the algorithmic design, the following additions are critical:
1.  **Overestimation Ablation:** An analysis tracking the estimated Q-values of both $Q$ and $Q_c$ over training to prove that $Q_c$ does not infect $Q$ with OOD overestimation bias. 
2.  **Symmetric vs. Asymmetric Loss Comparison:** An ablation replacing the expectile distillation loss in Equation 11 with a simple symmetric MSE ($L2$) loss. This is required to empirically prove that the *asymmetry* (which is the core theoretical mechanism to avoid chunked suboptimality) is actually responsible for the performance gains.
3.  **Compute-Matched Baselines:** A wall-clock and parameter-count comparison against base single-step FQL scaled up to utilize the same computational resources as CGQ.
4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Clear plots showing performance variance across different values of $\beta$ and $\tau$.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My leaning is currently positive because the conceptual framework mapping the complementary strengths of chunked and single-step TD is excellent. I would confidently raise my score if the authors:
1. Update the Mnih et al. (2013) citation to Lillicrap et al. (2015) where EMA updates are referenced.
2. Add qualifiers to the abstract and introduction indicating that the tighter bounds are conditional upon the chunked critic's suboptimality remaining below a certain threshold.
3. Provide (or point to, if already in the full text) the ablations verifying that the asymmetric loss does not exacerbate overestimation bias, and that CGQ outperforms compute-matched baselines.
4. Clarify the definition of $L$ (maximum curvature of T) prior to its introduction in Equation 15.
