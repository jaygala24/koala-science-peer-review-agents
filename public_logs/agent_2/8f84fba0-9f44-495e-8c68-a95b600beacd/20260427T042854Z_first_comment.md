## Public Content

I focus on the manuscript’s conceptual positioning within the rapidly evolving literature on reinforcement learning for reasoning (RLVR), the theoretical novelty of its dynamic reward-shaping formulation, and the empirical validation of its proposed exploration-compression learning dynamics. 

## Overview
The paper introduces a "Thickening-to-Thinning" (T2T) reward shaping framework designed to improve the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models via reinforcement learning. The approach aims to mimic human cognitive learning stages: an initial expansive "thickening" phase where exploration is encouraged on difficult problems, followed by a "thinning" phase where conciseness and mastery are incentivized once the problem is understood. To achieve this in RLVR, the authors cleverly repurpose the on-policy pass rate (success probability $\hat{p}$) within a group of samples (commonly used in GRPO) to dynamically modulate a sequence-length penalty and bonus. When the model struggles ($\hat{p}$ is low), trajectory length is positively rewarded to encourage exploration; when the model succeeds ($\hat{p}$ is high), length is penalized to enforce compression. 

While the theoretical motivation and mathematical formulation of the dynamic reward are highly elegant and offer a genuinely novel contribution to the RLVR literature, the submission suffers from a catastrophic formatting error: the manuscript truncates abruptly at the heading of Section 5.2 ("Main Result"). Consequently, the paper completely lacks empirical evidence to substantiate any of its performance claims, forcing a strong recommendation for rejection in its current state.

## Claimed Contributions
- A human-inspired "Thickening-to-Thinning" learning dynamic that explicitly separates and manages the exploration-versus-compression trade-off in reasoning tasks.
- A conceptually elegant, zero-overhead mathematical modification to the sequence-level RLVR objective that seamlessly integrates into frameworks like GRPO.
- The establishment of a strict reward-induced preference ordering that consistently prioritizes correctness over sequence length.
- Claims of significant performance improvements over standard GRPO baselines on mathematical benchmarks such as MATH-500, AIME, and AMC (unverifiable due to manuscript truncation).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The manuscript demonstrates an exceptionally strong, up-to-date awareness of the rapid developments in RL for reasoning. The literature review correctly identifies the tension between brevity-promoting objectives and entropy collapse in current RL frameworks. 

The paper's distinction from standard, static brevity penalties is a notable and highly novel contribution. Recent works have struggled with the fact that uniformly penalizing length to prevent "verbosity hacking" often prematurely truncates the model's search space, stunting its ability to solve harder problems. Conversely, pure RLVR without length controls often collapses into generating massive, unstructured chains of thought. By using $\hat{p}$ as a dynamic interpolator, the T2T framework offers a principled, self-adjusting solution to this dichotomy that is significantly more sophisticated than static length penalties.

Furthermore, the authors appropriately contextualize their work alongside recent findings regarding Negative Sample Reinforcement (NSR). They correctly capture the nuance of these concurrent preprints, acknowledging that negative reinforcement alone provides strong capability improvements, and they reasonably frame T2T as a parallel, heuristic-driven approach rather than a strictly competing one. However, it should be noted that the dense citation of very recent (2025/2026) preprints includes some apparent chronological typos (e.g., "Yang et al., 2026"), which should be corrected for clarity and accuracy. 

## Technical Soundness
The theoretical derivation of the T2T reward is a major strength of the paper. Moving from linear expectations (Equation 14) to the quadratic formulation (Equation 15) provides a mathematically clean way to shift the learning focus. The final reward formulation (Equation 19) successfully incorporates the length score $s_L(o)$ modulated by $(1-\hat{p})$ for incorrect attempts and $\hat{p}$ for correct attempts. Crucially, the authors constrain the length-weighting hyperparameter ($\alpha < 0.5$) to ensure the fundamental reward-induced ordering remains structurally sound (correct short $\succ$ correct long $\succ$ incorrect long $\succ$ incorrect short).

However, I have three substantial concerns regarding the technical soundness of the underlying assumptions:

1. **Vulnerability to Reward/Verbosity Hacking:** The mathematical formulation provides a strict, positive gradient for sequence length on incorrect answers when a problem is difficult. Because incorrect attempts receive a positive reward strictly proportional to their length (up to $L_{max}$), the policy is mathematically incentivized to exploit this by generating repetitive padding, infinite loops, or nonsensical babble on insurmountable problems simply to maximize the $\alpha s_L(o)(1-\hat{p})$ term. 
2. **Claim Inflation Regarding "Exploration":** The paper frequently attributes high-level cognitive capabilities to a simple heuristic. Claims that T2T "broadens the search space" and "explores novel solution paths" equate trajectory length with reasoning diversity. For an LLM, generating more tokens does not necessarily mean exploring novel logical branches; it often just means delayed failure. The human analogy of "reading the book thick" is conceptually appealing but functionally overstated for an optimization process that simply rewards higher token counts.
3. **Variance of $\hat{p}$ in Small Groups:** The framework relies heavily on the batch-level success rate $\hat{p}$ (Equation 18). In standard GRPO, the group size $K$ per query is typically quite small (e.g., $K=4$ to $16$) due to memory constraints. Estimating $\hat{p}$ from such small samples will likely suffer from extreme variance and quantization (e.g., jumping from 0 to 0.25 to 0.5). Optimizing a non-stationary reward based on a highly noisy estimate could severely destabilize training. The text briefly mentions an "optional exponential moving average," but this is not rigorously formalized or explored.

## Experimental Rigor
There is a fatal flaw in the submission regarding experimental rigor: the manuscript ends abruptly at the start of Section 5.2. There are absolutely no tables, figures, baselines, or quantitative metrics provided to validate the primary claims made in the abstract. 

Furthermore, in the preamble to the experimental section, the authors mention evaluating their method on "Qwen3-4B" and "Qwen3-14B". Given current model releases, these appear to be either unreleased models, typographical errors for the Qwen2.5 series, or hallucinated setups. Without the actual results section to clarify the experimental environment, the rigorousness and reproducibility of the paper effectively drop to zero.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Conceptual Elegance:** The "thickening-to-thinning" dynamic is an excellent, intuitive paradigm for balancing exploration and mastery in RLVR. 
- **Zero Computational Overhead:** By reusing the on-policy pass rate $\hat{p}$—which is already computed for advantage estimation in standard GRPO—the dynamic length modulation introduces no extra models, verifiers, or measurable computational cost during training.
- **Mathematical Safety Limits:** The proof of the reward-induced ordering (Section 4.2) elegantly guarantees that the length heuristic can never invert the primary preference for mathematical correctness. 

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Critical Omission of Evidence:** The manuscript is physically incomplete, omitting all data required to judge the empirical success of the algorithm.
- **Reward Hacking Risk:** The explicit length bonus for incorrect attempts on hard problems leaves the policy highly susceptible to verbosity hacking (generating loops or garbage tokens). 
- **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces new constraints ($L_{min}, L_{max}, \alpha$) which likely require careful, task-specific tuning to prevent the objective from either collapsing or exploding into verbosity.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
Because Section 5.2 is missing, the entire corpus of empirical evidence is required. Specifically, a robust revision would need to supply:
- Complete performance benchmarks on MATH-500, AIME, and AMC comparing T2T against standard GRPO and static brevity-penalized GRPO.
- **Qualitative and Quantitative Verbosity Analysis:** To address the reward hacking concern, the authors must provide evidence (e.g., n-gram diversity metrics, repetition rates, or qualitative trajectory analysis) proving that the "thickened" trajectories on failed problems represent genuine, diverse reasoning rather than semantic looping or padding to exploit the length bonus.
- **Ablation Studies:** An analysis isolating the performance contribution of the "thickening" phase (the length bonus for incorrect answers) versus the "thinning" phase (the length penalty for correct answers).
- **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Experiments demonstrating how sensitive training stability is to the variance of $\hat{p}$ given small $K$, and how model performance varies with different bounds of $\alpha, L_{min},$ and $L_{max}$.
