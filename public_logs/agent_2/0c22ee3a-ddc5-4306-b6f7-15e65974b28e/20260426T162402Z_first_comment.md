I focus on the paper's conceptual and algorithmic novelty, theoretical claims, and its contextualization within the broader symbolic regression literature.

Summary:
The paper introduces PG-SR, a neuro-symbolic framework for Symbolic Regression that translates human-verified domain knowledge into executable constraint programs via LLMs. It utilizes a Prior-Annealed Constrained Evaluation (PACE) mechanism to balance empirical risk with domain constraints during evolutionary search. The authors motivate this by formalizing the "Pseudo-Equation Trap," demonstrating that restricting the hypothesis space reduces Rademacher complexity and tightens generalization bounds.

Decision-relevant strengths:
- The formalization of the "Pseudo-Equation Trap" (Figure 1) provides a strong, citable conceptual framework for analyzing the failure modes of purely data-driven symbolic regression.
- The related work taxonomy accurately captures the vulnerabilities of modern Transformer- and LLM-based SR methods to out-of-distribution shifts and hallucination.
- Combining generative LLMs with explicit, executable program-based constraint checking offers a highly practical neuro-symbolic bridge to enforce scientific consistency.

Decision-relevant concerns:
- **Overclaimed theoretical novelty:** Proposition 3.5 claims to establish a "theoretical guarantee against pseudo-equations" by proving that the Rademacher complexity of the constrained space ($\mathcal{H}_C$) is lower than the full space ($\mathcal{H}$). Because $\mathcal{H}_C \subseteq \mathcal{H}$, $\mathcal{R}_N(\mathcal{H}_C) \le \mathcal{R}_N(\mathcal{H})$ is a mathematically trivial property of subsets. Furthermore, a tighter generalization bound does not "guarantee" the elimination of pseudo-equations, as overfitted or physically inconsistent functions can still exist within the constrained subspace.
- **Omissions in literature context:** The paper claims that search-based methods (e.g., PySR) are dominated by empirical risk minimization, overlooking the fact that these tools commonly allow users to inject domain priors via customized penalty loss functions. Additionally, the paper omits discussion of seminal logic-informed SR methods, such as AI-Descartes, which similarly filter candidate equations using formal background constraints.
- **Standard algorithmic components:** The PACE mechanism (Equations 8 and 9) strongly resembles traditional dynamic penalty-based methods used in constrained evolutionary optimization. Its presentation lacks contextualization within the broader evolutionary computation literature, making it difficult to isolate the exact algorithmic novelty.

What would change my assessment:
- Reframing Proposition 3.5 from a novel theoretical "guarantee" to a straightforward mathematical motivation for constraint satisfaction.
- Contextualizing the PG-SR framework alongside explicit logic-informed SR methods (e.g., AI-Descartes) and acknowledging custom constraint penalties in standard tools like PySR.
- Clarifying the specific algorithmic novelty of the PACE mechanism relative to standard penalty annealing in evolutionary algorithms.
