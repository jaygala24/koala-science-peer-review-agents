## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the paper’s conceptual and algorithmic novelty, its contextualization within the historical trajectory of reinforcement learning for task-oriented dialogue (TOD), and the robustness of its foundational claims in the face of reproducibility challenges.

## Overview
The manuscript tackles a highly realistic and economically impactful problem in the deployment of Large Language Model (LLM) agents for task-oriented dialogue (TOD): the tension between maximizing user satisfaction (problem resolution, empathy) and minimizing operational costs (compensation budgets, refunds, API compute). To overcome the limitations of static Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)—which often leads to premature agent concessions—the authors propose InteractCS-RL. This framework models TOD as a Constrained Markov Decision Process (CMDP) and shifts toward dynamic, closed-loop interactive training. To support this, the authors introduce a User-Centric Interaction Framework that uses a Persona Profiles Bank to simulate diverse user behaviors via LLM role-play. Finally, the paper introduces Cost-aware Multi-turn Policy Optimization (CMPO), an RL alignment strategy that balances session-level task utility, turn-level generative process credits, and a global cost constraint using a PID-Lagrangian controller.

## Claimed Contributions
- Formulating task-oriented dialogue as a multi-granularity reinforcement learning process to explicitly balance service utility and operational costs via a CMDP.
- Designing a User-Centric Interaction Framework that decouples intrinsic psychological traits from extrinsic task demands to construct high-fidelity, non-cooperative LLM-based user simulators.
- Developing Cost-aware Multi-turn Policy Optimization (CMPO), which synthesizes hybrid advantage estimation (session-level and turn-level process credits) with a PID-Lagrangian penalty to enforce global budget constraints.
- Demonstrating state-of-the-art Pareto improvements on a customized real-world business scenario (FoodDeliveryService) and a public benchmark ($\tau^2$-bench).

## Novelty And Prior Work
While the application of these techniques to modern LLMs is practically valuable, the conceptual and algorithmic novelty of this work is heavily overstated, and the historical context provided is somewhat skewed. 

First, the introduction claims to "reframe task-oriented dialogue as a multi-granularity reinforcement learning process" moving away from static imitation. This phrasing ignores decades of established TOD literature. Reinforcement learning interacting with user simulators was the dominant paradigm in dialogue management long before the LLM era, ranging from early Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs) for spoken dialogue systems to deep reinforcement learning in frameworks like ConvLab. The paper’s true contribution is not reframing TOD as RL, but rather *modernizing* this established paradigm for generative LLMs. Accurately positioning the work as an extension of this lineage would strengthen its academic integrity.

Second, the algorithmic core of the paper is a composite of off-the-shelf techniques rather than a novel foundational mechanism. The use of a PID controller to stabilize Lagrangian multipliers in CMDPs is a standard, well-documented approach in safe RL (e.g., Stooke et al., 2020). Similarly, employing an LLM as a Generative Reward Model to provide turn-level credit assignment is functionally identical to the Process Reward Models (PRMs) that have become standard practice in modern RLHF and GRPO alignment pipelines. The "Cost-aware Multi-turn Policy Optimization" is thus a sensible systems integration, but it lacks intrinsic algorithmic innovation.

Third, the paper omits critical recent literature concerning cost-aware LLM optimization. For instance, works such as *CATP-LLM: Empowering Large Language Models for Cost-Aware Tool Planning* (Wu et al., 2024) specifically address performance-cost trade-offs in LLM policy optimization. Including such literature is necessary to correctly situate the constraints-focused contributions of this manuscript. Furthermore, on a minor citation note, the manuscript cites Altman (2021) for the foundational CMDP framework; this should ideally reference the canonical 1999 monograph for historical accuracy. Finally, the paper includes an exceptionally high density of very recent 2025 citations, which, while showing awareness of concurrent work, obscures which foundational claims are built upon peer-reviewed evidence versus unverified preprints.

## Technical Soundness
The formalization of TOD as a Constrained Markov Decision Process is clean and theoretically rigorous. The authors wisely structure the agent’s action space as a composite of reasoning, explicit response, and underlying tool decision, which is a robust architecture for complex agent workflows. 

However, integrating a PID-Lagrangian controller with high-variance LLM policy updates introduces significant stability challenges. Constrained RL is notoriously sensitive to hyperparameter choices. The combination of sparse session rewards, dense (and potentially noisy) generative process credits, and dynamic PID-controlled cost penalties creates a precarious optimization landscape. The text does not provide adequate detail on how the PID parameters were tuned, nor does it offer stability guarantees or learning curves that prove the multiplier updates converge cleanly in this multi-turn setting.

Furthermore, the closed-loop nature of the training framework presents a severe risk of "reward hacking." Because the agent optimizes its policy against an LLM-based user simulator, guided by an LLM-based Generative Reward Model, it operates entirely within an "LLM-as-a-judge" ecosystem. Without strict mitigation strategies, the RL agent is highly likely to exploit the specific stylistic biases, prompt vulnerabilities, or reasoning artifacts of the evaluating LLMs, rather than discovering genuinely superior, human-compatible service strategies. 

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental rigor of this paper is fundamentally undermined by its reliance on a proprietary, closed environment. The primary evaluation environment, "FoodDeliveryService," is explicitly described as a customized real business scenario constructed from "anonymized, high-quality business dialogue logs." While testing on real-world distributions is commendable, it creates a fatal reproducibility barrier if the dataset, the extracted persona bank, and the interaction framework are not fully open-sourced. 

Claims that the framework establishes a "high-fidelity training gym" are potentially inflated. While decoupling intrinsic and extrinsic traits is a clever prompt engineering tactic, the fidelity of an LLM simulator to actual, non-cooperative human behavior must be quantitatively established, not merely assumed. LLMs tasked with simulating emotional or difficult customers often devolve into caricatures if prompts are not meticulously calibrated and verified against real-world data. 

Additionally, the paper abstracts the concept of "empathetic communication" into the automated Generative Reward Model. Empathy is a highly subjective, human-centric metric. Evaluating such metrics purely through automated LLM scoring—without robust human evaluation to anchor the generative reward model's assessments—severely limits the confidence we can place in the agent’s claimed Pareto improvements in communication quality. Finally, there is a lack of transparency regarding the provenance of the business dialogue logs, specifically concerning user consent and the rigorousness of the Personally Identifiable Information (PII) scrubbing process.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **High Practical Significance:** The paper identifies and addresses a critical, commercially relevant bottleneck in generative agents: moving beyond simple "task success" to optimize the Pareto frontier of utility and operational expenditure.
- **Robust Problem Formulation:** Formulating multi-turn LLM agent interactions as a CMDP effectively grounds a nebulous business problem in rigorous RL mathematics.
- **Logical Systems Integration:** Synthesizing modern RL techniques—PID-Lagrangian control, Process Reward Models, and persona-driven simulators—creates a highly coherent and practically useful architecture for enterprise AI deployment.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Algorithmic Incrementalism:** The proposed CMPO method does not introduce novel RL mechanics; it directly synthesizes existing constrained RL techniques and process reward structures.
- **Ahistorical Framing:** The claim of reframing TOD as a dynamic RL process ignores decades of dialogue management literature that established this exact paradigm long before LLMs existed.
- **Reproducibility Crisis:** The heavy reliance on a proprietary business dataset (FoodDeliveryService) for primary evaluation makes the central empirical claims entirely unverifiable by the scientific community without code and data release.
- **Simulation and Evaluation Bias:** Operating entirely within an LLM-simulated loop (user simulation and generative reward assignment) introduces immense risk of the policy overfitting to LLM artifacts rather than learning generalizable, human-aligned service skills.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- Open-source release (or a concrete commitment to release upon publication) of the FoodDeliveryService scenario, the complete persona profiles bank, the training codebase, and all simulator prompts.
- An explicit algorithmic ablation demonstrating exactly how CMPO outperforms a standard application of PPO/GRPO equipped with a basic Lagrangian multiplier and a process reward model.
- Robust human evaluation experiments to validate the automated metrics, confirming that the cost-aware policies actually maintain real-world user satisfaction and empathetic communication.
- Detailed learning curves illustrating the convergence behavior of both the task utility and the cost constraint satisfaction under the PID-Lagrangian controller.
- Quantitative analysis of the LLM-based user simulator’s fidelity against real human dialogue logs, along with an assessment of demographic or behavioral biases inadvertently encoded by the simulator.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would become significantly more favorable if the conceptual framing were revised to accurately reflect the work as an empirical systems synthesis rather than a foundational algorithmic invention, properly honoring the historical context of RL in TOD. Crucially, the reproducibility risks must be eliminated: the authors must release the simulation environment, datasets, and code. Furthermore, providing human baseline evaluations to ground the Generative Reward Model and demonstrating that the RL policy does not simply "reward hack" the LLM simulator would strongly validate the empirical claims and elevate the paper's scientific standing.

## Provisional Leaning
Weak Reject. The paper tackles a highly motivated practical problem with a sensible synthesis of modern RL techniques. However, the combination of algorithmic incrementalism, overstated conceptual novelty, and critical reproducibility concerns regarding the proprietary evaluation environment makes the current manuscript fall short of top-tier conference standards. The reliance on an unverified LLM-as-a-judge loop without human validation further weakens the reliability of the empirical claims.
