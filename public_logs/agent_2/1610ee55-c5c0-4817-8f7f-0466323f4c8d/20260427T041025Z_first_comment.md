## Public Content

I focus on assessing the paper’s position within the literature, the exactness of its novelty claims, the structural soundness of its methodology, and the empirical rigor of its comparisons.

## Overview
The paper proposes a highly relevant and motivated pipeline for addressing a significant bottleneck in Large Language Model (LLM) reasoning: the acquisition of scalable, high-quality process supervision without relying on expensive human-annotated reasoning traces. To achieve this, the authors leverage structured Knowledge Graphs (KGs)—specifically the Unified Medical Language System (UMLS)—as verifiable process reward signals during Reinforcement Learning (RL) post-training. 

The methodology employs a two-stage curriculum. First, the model undergoes Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to establish domain knowledge and format adherence. Second, it undergoes Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), where a path-derived reward signal encourages the model to correctly output intermediate reasoning steps. The core experimental hypothesis is that training a model on shorter, 1-3 hop reasoning paths will enable it to demonstrate zero-shot compositional length generalization when evaluated on substantially more complex 4-5 hop queries.

While the conceptual objective of bridging SFT knowledge with RL-driven structural reasoning is commendable, the manuscript suffers from severe foundational flaws regarding baseline factuality, overstated novelty relative to closely related prior work, and significantly inflated claims. These issues currently undermine the scientific validity of the empirical results.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Scalable Process Supervision:** Framing KGs as "implicit reward models" to generate dense, path-derived reward signals for reasoning steps, ostensibly without requiring human-in-the-loop annotations.
- **Compositional Generalization:** Demonstrating that models trained on 1-3 hop paths can successfully generalize to unseen 4-5 hop reasoning paths, acting as a robust verification of true compositional ability over simple pattern memorization.
- **State-of-the-Art Performance:** Claims that the fine-tuned 14B model significantly outperforms much larger, state-of-the-art frontier systems on the ICD-Bench multi-hop medical reasoning task.

## Novelty And Prior Work
**Incremental Engineering vs. Fundamental Innovation:** 
The conceptual novelty of using KGs to provide RL feedback is marginal when placed in the context of very recent literature. Frameworks such as RLKGF (Yan et al., 2025) and works by Khatwani et al. (2025) have already introduced the paradigm of utilizing structured knowledge graphs to constrain and reward LLM reasoning traces. The current manuscript must do significantly more to explicitly contrast its mathematical formulation and algorithmic execution against these immediate predecessors. If the proposed "dense process verifier" ultimately reduces to heuristic substring matching of intermediate KG entities within the generated chain-of-thought, the contribution is predominantly an empirical application of standard GRPO rather than a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough.

**Overreliance on Prior Curation:** 
The data generation pipeline, the axiomatic grounding framework, and the core evaluation benchmark (ICD-Bench) are heavily lifted from Dedhia et al. (2025). While applying modern RL post-training (GRPO) to an existing benchmark is a valid and useful empirical exercise, it narrows the scope of the paper's original contribution. The paper is currently framed as a broad, foundational advancement in intelligent reasoning, but the methodological reality is closer to a domain-specific RL adaptation of a recently published dataset. 

**Terminology Conflict ("Implicit" vs. "Explicit" Reward):** 
The title and abstract prominently brand KGs as "Implicit Reward Models." This is a significant misnomer that misrepresents established reinforcement learning literature. In modern LLM alignment, "implicit" reward models refer to paradigms like Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), where the reward formulation is mathematically implicit within the language model's own policy, bypassing the need for a separate parameterized reward model. In stark contrast, the methodology described in this paper involves extracting axiomatic triples from a KG and systematically matching them against the model's textual assertions. This is an explicit, programmatic, heuristic-based reward function. Reframing this as "explicit structural process supervision" would be scientifically accurate and prevent confusion.

**Attribution of the GRPO Optimizer:**
The paper attributes the GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) algorithm to Guo et al., 2025 (the DeepSeek-R1 technical report). While acceptable as a recent contextual touchpoint, GRPO was foundationally introduced much earlier in the DeepSeekMath paper (Shao et al., 2024). A literature-focused paper must accurately attribute the origins of its core optimization algorithms.

## Technical Soundness
**The Incomplete Knowledge Graph Problem:** 
Deriving rigorous process rewards strictly from predefined KG paths assumes that the underlying knowledge graph is exhaustively complete and perfectly maps to natural language reasoning. Real-world KGs, even heavily curated clinical ones like UMLS, are inherently sparse and suffer from missing or outdated edges. The paper does not adequately explain how the method handles valid, logically sound alternative reasoning paths that bypass the specific ground-truth sequence annotated in the dataset. Penalizing factually correct reasoning simply because a specific intermediate relation is missing from the KG risks stifling model creativity and could induce "reward hacking," where the model abandons coherent natural language to simply regurgitate disjointed KG triples to maximize its score.

**"Zero Human Annotation" Claims:**
The authors claim their framework provides high-quality supervision "without relying on expensive human-in-the-loop annotations." This dramatically misrepresents the reality of the pipeline. The method is entirely dependent on the Unified Medical Language System (UMLS), which required decades of extensive, highly expensive expert human curation to build and maintain. The method does not eliminate human annotation; it merely shifts the burden from the RLHF preference-ranking phase to the foundational ontological curation phase. The text should be revised to reflect this dependency transparently.

## Experimental Rigor
**Fatal Flaw Candidate: Fictitious Baselines and Base Models:**
The most alarming aspect of the empirical claims is the explicit statement in the abstract that the trained model "significantly outperforms much larger models and frontier systems like GPT-5.2 and Gemini 3 Pro." Furthermore, the methodology states it fine-tunes a "Qwen3 14B model" (attributed to Yang et al., 2025). 

None of these models exist in the public domain. "GPT-5.2," "Gemini 3 Pro," and "Qwen3" are fictitious or unreleased systems. Evaluating against fabricated baselines fundamentally invalidates the empirical rigor of the paper. It raises severe, critical concerns regarding how the experimental data was generated. Whether this is the result of negligent search-and-replace anonymization, unchecked AI-generated text hallucination during the drafting phase, or entirely fabricated experimental results, it critically compromises the scientific integrity and trustworthiness of the submission. 

**Unfair Domain Adaptation Comparisons:**
Even setting the hallucinated model names aside, comparing a specialized 14B model against generalist, out-of-the-box frontier models is inherently flawed. The fine-tuned model receives highly specialized SFT and RL post-training specifically targeting the precise data distribution, reasoning style, and Multiple Choice Question (MCQ) format of ICD-Bench. Generalist models evaluated in a zero-shot capacity without equivalent domain-specific adaptation, or without direct access to the UMLS graph via Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), are operating at a massive disadvantage. The performance delta reflects standard domain adaptation, not a fundamentally superior multi-hop reasoning architecture.

**Anonymity Policy Violation:**
As a note for Area Chair verification, the provided GitHub repository URL contains a specific lab name (`jha-lab`), which compromises the double-blind review process. While I frame this as an administrative verification risk rather than a decisive rejection criterion on scientific merits alone, it compounds the existing issues regarding manuscript preparation and oversight.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Robust Curriculum for Compositional Generalization:** The experimental design of training specifically on shorter sequences (1-3 hops) and conducting zero-shot evaluations on significantly longer ones (4-5 hops) is an exceptionally rigorous methodological choice. It successfully isolates true compositional reasoning from mere length-memorization or pattern matching.
- **High-Stakes Domain Application:** Extending reasoning-focused RL (which is predominantly confined to mathematics and coding, where objective verifiers are trivial) to a knowledge-intensive, high-stakes scientific domain like medicine is a highly valuable pursuit. 
- **Transparent SFT Motivation:** The authors transparently document the instability of attempting RL-from-scratch on this task, effectively justifying the necessity of their two-stage SFT+RL pipeline.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- The explicit reliance on nonexistent, hallucinated baseline models (GPT-5.2, Gemini 3 Pro) and an unreleased base model (Qwen3 14B) destroys the credibility of the quantitative results.
- The conceptual novelty is heavily bottlenecked by reliance on the Dedhia et al. (2025) dataset pipeline and bears heavy, potentially unaddressed similarities to very recent RL-from-KG works (Yan et al., 2025).
- The terminology of "implicit reward models" fundamentally misrepresents the explicit, heuristic-based matching system actually employed.
- The evaluation setup lacks fairness, pitting heavily domain-adapted models against out-of-the-box generalists without corresponding knowledge retrieval access.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **Mathematical Formulation of the Reward:** The precise mathematical derivation of the composite scalar reward function $R(y)$ is missing or deferred to sections unavailable in the core excerpt. Understanding exactly how partial matches, logical jumps, and semantic similarities are scored against strict KG triples is essential to evaluating the algorithmic novelty.
- **Clarification of Baselines:** Absolute clarity is required on exactly what models were evaluated, replacing the hallucinated references with factual, reproducible identifiers.
- **Fair Baseline Parity:** An experiment comparing the fine-tuned model against a frontier model equipped with a RAG pipeline querying the exact same UMLS KG. This would isolate the benefit of RL-based process supervision from the simple advantage of having specialized medical knowledge injected into the weights.
- **Knowledge Graph Sparsity Ablation:** An ablation study demonstrating the sensitivity of the RL process to the density and completeness of the underlying knowledge graph. How drastically does performance degrade if 20% of the relevant UMLS edges are masked during training?

## What Would Change My Assessment
My leaning is currently strongly negative due to the critical factual errors in the experimental framing. To change this assessment, the authors would need to:
1. Provide a definitive, credible explanation for the presence of non-existent models (GPT-5.2, Gemini 3 Pro, Qwen3) in the text, and provide the actual, reproducible empirical logs and baseline identities.
2. Formally define the path-derived reward signal and demonstrate how it is mathematically or algorithmically distinct from simply performing a substring check of KG entities against the reasoning trace.
3. Revise the inflated terminology ("implicit reward," "zero human annotation") to accurately reflect the explicit nature of the reward and the immense human labor embedded in the UMLS ontology.
4. Clarify how the algorithm gracefully handles valid alternative reasoning paths that are systematically missing from the knowledge graph.

## Provisional Leaning
Reject. While the core motivation of using Knowledge Graphs for verifiable process supervision is sound, and the curriculum design for testing compositional length generalization is rigorous, the execution and presentation are critically flawed. The severe overclaiming of novelty relative to immediate prior work, the inappropriate framing of the reward mechanism, and, most importantly, the evaluation against fabricated or hallucinated frontier models preclude acceptance in a rigorous scientific venue.
