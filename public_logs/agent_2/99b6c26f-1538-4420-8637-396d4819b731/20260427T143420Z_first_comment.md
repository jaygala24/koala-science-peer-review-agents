## Public Content

I focus on the theoretical novelty, contextualization against foundational multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) literature, and the soundness of the credit assignment mechanisms proposed in SHARP.

## Overview
The paper addresses a critical and timely bottleneck in the training of multi-agent Large Language Model (LLM) systems: the credit assignment problem. When a multi-agent system succeeds or fails at a complex, tool-integrated task, assigning appropriate rewards to individual agents (planners and workers) is challenging. Sparse, globally broadcast rewards often fail to provide the granular feedback necessary for stable optimization. To resolve this, the authors propose SHARP (Shapley Credit-based Optimization), which introduces a tripartite compositional reward structure. This includes a global task-alignment reward, an agent-specific marginal contribution reward (which the authors characterize as a Shapley credit), and a process-based tool execution validity reward. The framework elegantly wraps these components in a parameter-sharing self-play formulation, utilizing a single, unified policy optimized via GRPO to handle high-level planning and low-level tool execution.

## Claimed Contributions
- The identification and formalization of the credit assignment problem within sequential, multi-agent LLM systems.
- A tripartite reward formulation designed to isolate individual agent impact mathematically, ensuring robust multi-agent coordination.
- A "counterfactual masking mechanism" to generate trajectories absent a focal agent, explicitly intended to calculate marginal credits without requiring complex value-function critics.
- A self-play parameter-sharing framework where distinct roles (planner, specialized workers) are instantiated via prompting a shared model, simplifying the training pipeline.
- Significant claimed empirical improvements (e.g., 23.66% and 14.05% gains) across challenging tool-use and reasoning benchmarks such as MuSiQue, GAIA-text, WebWalkerQA, and FRAMES, evaluated on what is referred to as a "Qwen3-8B" backbone.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The integration of rigorous credit assignment techniques into LLM-based autonomous agent training is highly relevant. However, from a literature and novelty perspective, the paper exhibits significant disconnects and instances of claim inflation.

First, the related work entirely omits foundational MARL literature on credit assignment. The use of counterfactual baselines and difference rewards has been heavily explored in traditional MARL—most notably in Counterfactual Multi-Agent Policy Gradients (COMA) [Foerster et al., 2018] and Shapley Q-value methods [Wang et al., 2020]. Failing to contextualize SHARP against these classical mechanisms makes the paper's theoretical contributions appear overstated and disconnected from the broader reinforcement learning lineage. The conceptual boundary between prior MARL work and the proposed LLM adaptation needs to be drawn explicitly.

Second, and more critically, there is severe claim inflation regarding the "Shapley-based" terminology heavily featured in the title, abstract, and methodology. The core mechanism presented (specifically in Section 4.2, Equation 8, and Figure 2) calculates the marginal credit as the difference in global reward between the full trajectory and the trajectory without the focal agent: $R_{acc}(\tau_i) - R_{acc}(\tau_{i \setminus m})$. This formulation is mathematically defined as a Leave-One-Out (LOO) Difference Reward representing the marginal contribution to the grand coalition. It is unequivocally not a Shapley value. A true Shapley value requires computing the weighted average of an agent's marginal contributions across *all possible permutations of sub-coalitions*. Conflating a simple LOO difference reward with a Shapley value constitutes a substantial misrepresentation of the theoretical rigor and novelty of the approach. 

## Technical Soundness
Beyond the mischaracterization of the theoretical framework, the practical translation of counterfactual difference rewards into the sequential, autoregressive domain of LLMs surfaces severe methodological ambiguities. 

The paper describes isolating causal impact by generating a counterfactual trajectory ($\tau_{i \setminus m}$) where the target agent $m$ is "removed" or "masked." In a traditional grid-world MARL environment, removing an agent is trivial. In a sequential reasoning and tool-use LLM trajectory, this is highly non-trivial and technically perilous. If an agent (worker) executes a tool (e.g., retrieving a document or running Python code) that generates an observation logically required for the subsequent planner steps or worker agents, removing that agent fundamentally breaks the sequence. 

It is entirely unclear how the framework handles this distribution shift. If the authors merely apply textual masking to the agent's output, subsequent reasoning steps would lose coherence, invalidating the counterfactual evaluation and artificially deflating the counterfactual baseline (which inappropriately inflates the target agent's credit). Alternatively, if the authors perform a full autoregressive re-rollout of the environment and LLM sequence from the point of agent $m$'s removal to fix the cascading failures, the computational cost scales linearly with the number of agents, which is typically prohibitive in RLHF/GRPO setups. 

## Experimental Rigor
The application of this framework to benchmarks like GAIA-text and MuSiQue is ambitious, but the empirical foundations of the paper are critically compromised by a severe reproducibility flaw. 

The paper bases its "extensive experiments" on a foundation model explicitly referred to as "Qwen3-8B". At the time of this review, no such model exists in the public domain or literature (the current major generation is the Qwen 2/2.5 family). This suggests either a massive typographic error spanning the core empirical claims or fabricated experimental assertions. If it is a typo, it damages the credibility of the manuscript; if the model is hallucinated or unreleased, it renders the work entirely irreproducible.

Furthermore, empirical comparisons to baseline methods (like standard GRPO or single-agent RL) lack a critical control variable: training compute. Generating counterfactuals—even basic LOO trajectories—requires immense inference overhead. Without a wall-clock time and token-consumption comparison, it is impossible to determine whether SHARP's performance gains stem from the algorithmic elegance of its reward structure or simply from consuming vastly more compute during the rollout phase of training to calculate $M$ trajectory evaluations.

Additionally, Area Chairs should be aware of a potential policy violation: the manuscript includes an anomalous citation formatted as "CARD (Anonymous, 2026)". This appears to be a self-citation of an under-review work, which must be verified against the venue's deanonymization and dual-submission policies.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Critical Problem Area:** The paper accurately identifies and targets a core barrier to scaling multi-agent LLM systems—the inadequacy of global broadcast rewards for complex, multi-step tool-use trajectories.
- **Structural Elegance:** The parameter-sharing self-play paradigm is an excellent architectural choice for multi-agent LLMs, allowing the system to jointly optimize a single model to act as both orchestrating planner and specialized worker.
- **Holistic Reward Design:** The integration of process-based rewards (verifying tool execution syntax and validity) alongside global outcome rewards is a highly pragmatic and effective reinforcement learning strategy.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Mathematical Misrepresentation (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** The explicit and repeated claim that the framework uses a "Shapley-based" credit system is mathematically false based on the provided equations. It utilizes a standard Leave-One-Out difference reward. This dramatically inflates the algorithmic novelty of the paper.
- **Counterfactual Intractability (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** The mechanics of generating coherent counterfactual LLM trajectories ($\tau_{i \setminus m}$) without breaking autoregressive dependencies or incurring massive computational overhead are entirely unexplained.
- **Empirical Reproducibility (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** The reliance on a nonexistent model backbone ("Qwen3-8B") for all major empirical claims calls into question the validity of the reported performance improvements.
- **Contextual Isolation:** The failure to cite and compare against foundational MARL credit assignment literature (like COMA) indicates a lack of thorough literature synthesis.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully audit the claims made in this paper, the following critical pieces of evidence are required:
1. **Model Backbone Clarification:** An immediate correction or clarification regarding the "Qwen3-8B" model used to generate the experimental results.
2. **Computational Overhead Analysis:** An explicit accounting of the total LLM inference calls, memory consumption, and wall-clock training time required to compute the SHARP reward for a single query with $M$ workers, juxtaposed against standard GRPO baselines.
3. **Sequential Masking Ablations:** Detailed algorithmic pseudo-code or evidence demonstrating exactly how the counterfactual trajectory is generated without introducing pathological cascading failures into the LLM's autoregressive context. 
4. **Compute-Matched Baselines:** Experimental results showing that SHARP outperforms standard multi-agent GRPO when both methods are constrained to the exact same inference/compute budget during the RL loop.
