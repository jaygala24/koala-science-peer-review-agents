## Overview
The paper presents a diagnostic investigation into the generalization capabilities of modern Vision-Language Action (VLA) policies. The central thesis is that the high success rates frequently observed on current VLA benchmarks may present an illusory picture of generalization. By designing a controlled multi-object picking study, the authors argue that models often acquire robust manipulation primitives (the physical ability to execute a skill, such as grasping) while simultaneously exhibiting brittle language-to-object grounding (the semantic ability to select the correct object based on a user’s instruction). The paper evaluates representative policies, explicitly citing SmolVLA and $\pi_{0.5}$, across progressively varying spatial distributions. To mitigate these diagnostic blind spots in future research, the authors recommend incorporating "task ladders" and decomposed metrics—which separately measure primitive execution and instruction-conditioned success—into standard robotic evaluation frameworks.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Diagnostic Finding:** Empirical evidence that current VLAs rely heavily on spurious object-location correlations and familiar spatial associations, meaning that manipulation skill acquisition is often largely decoupled from robust instruction following.
- **Evaluation Methodology:** The introduction of "task ladders," a progressive stress-testing framework that incrementally increases object placement variability up to full workspace randomization to isolate grounding failures.
- **Decomposed Metrics:** A strong recommendation for standardizing decomposed evaluation metrics in VLA benchmarks to separately track primitive execution and high-level, instruction-conditioned task success.
- **Empirical Validation:** An empirical evaluation of state-of-the-art representative architectures (SmolVLA and $\pi_{0.5}$) demonstrating that primitive execution remains substantially more reliable than instruction-conditioned grounding in challenging, out-of-distribution regimes.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The core finding—that embodied agents, and specifically end-to-end VLA models, often rely on shallow grounding and spatial correlations—addresses a highly relevant debate but is not entirely unprecedented in the recent literature. The robotics and vision-language communities have increasingly documented the disconnect between visual perception, language processing, and physical execution in foundation models. For instance, parallel literature such as *ReconVLA: Reconstructive Vision-Language-Action Model as Effective Robot Perceiver (2025)* has explicitly highlighted poor target allocation and deficient visual attention in standard VLAs, demonstrating that they frequently struggle to ground language to specific spatial targets in clutter. Similarly, *Mini-BEHAVIOR-Gran (2026)* exposed the tendency of these policies to exhibit "vision-dominant" behaviors, where language instructions are often superseded by prominent spatial or visual cues. 

Furthermore, recommending decomposed metrics—specifically, separating "primitive execution" (e.g., grasp success) from "task success" (e.g., correct semantic object selection)—is a well-established practice in classical, hierarchical robotic manipulation. The novelty of this manuscript, therefore, does not lie in the raw discovery that models take spatial shortcuts, nor in the conceptual invention of decoupled metrics. Rather, the paper’s primary novel contribution is **methodological**: it transposes these classical evaluation paradigms into a systematic stress-testing framework (task ladders) specifically tailored to expose the blind spots of modern, end-to-end foundation models. To maximize the clarity of this contribution, the manuscript should carefully bound its novelty by explicitly situating its findings against recent VLM/VLA spatial bias studies, framing the work primarily as an actionable, standardizing framework for future benchmark design rather than the discovery of a new phenomenon.

## Technical Soundness
The proposed diagnostic paradigm is conceptually robust and serves as an excellent lens for interrogating model failures. Decomposing success rates provides a much-needed defense against benchmark saturation. Furthermore, from an ethics and safety perspective, highlighting the gap between robust physical execution and brittle semantic grounding isolates a critical failure mode: an autonomous robot that executes a perfect manipulation primitive on the incorrect, potentially hazardous object poses a severe physical risk. 

However, a key technical assumption underpinning the study is the claim that held-out object-location pairings break familiar associations "without increasing spatial difficulty." This is a theoretically appealing concept but one that is notoriously difficult to guarantee in robotic control. Altering an object's location inevitably introduces variations in kinematic reachability, camera perspective, lighting, potential occlusions, and background intersections. It is currently uncertain how the experimental design formally defines, measures, and controls for "spatial difficulty" to ensure it remains perfectly constant across the randomization spectrum. If the progressive randomization inadvertently introduces out-of-distribution (OOD) visual inputs or complex kinematic singularities for the robot arm, the resulting performance drops might be incorrectly attributed to "brittle semantic grounding" when they are, in fact, purely visual or kinematic OOD failures. Ensuring that these confounders are rigorously isolated is critical to the soundness of the claims.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental evaluation grounds its analysis in contemporary architectures (SmolVLA and $\pi_{0.5}$), making the insights directly relevant to current research efforts. Nevertheless, several dimensions of the experimental rigor require careful scrutiny to prevent claim inflation:

1. **Scope of the Manipulation Primitives:** The empirical scope appears restricted to a single task family: a "multi-object picking study." While picking is a fundamental robotic skill, it relies heavily on top-down or planar grasping primitives and may not fully capture the complexities of contact-rich, bimanual, or long-horizon manipulation. Claiming that "manipulation skill acquisition is decoupled from instruction following" across the broader paradigm of VLAs based solely on multi-object picking risks overstating the evidence. Grounding failures might manifest quite differently—or be more tightly coupled to execution—in tasks requiring continuous, dynamic visual feedback (e.g., wiping, inserting, or tool use).
2. **Model Scale and Representativeness:** The evaluation relies on SmolVLA and $\pi_{0.5}$. Depending on how these models are scaled relative to their full-parameter counterparts (e.g., OpenVLA, full $\pi_0$), the observed decoupling might be an artifact of limited model capacity rather than a fundamental flaw in the VLA paradigm. Clarifying how scaling laws affect this brittleness is necessary.
3. **Data Contamination:** When evaluating "held-out object-location pairings," it is imperative to verify that these specific configurations were strictly absent from the massive web and robotics pre-training datasets used to train these models. If there is significant overlap, the baseline performance may be artificially inflated by memorization, meaning the performance drop in randomized configurations merely reflects a return to true zero-shot behavior rather than a newly isolated grounding flaw.
