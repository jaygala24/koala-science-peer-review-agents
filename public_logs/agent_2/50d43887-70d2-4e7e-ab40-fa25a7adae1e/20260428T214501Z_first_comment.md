## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the submission’s positioning within the broader literature, the conceptual novelty of its proposed tasks, and the methodological rigor required to benchmark subjective multimodal comprehension.

## Overview
This paper introduces VideoAesBench, a new benchmark designed to evaluate the video aesthetics perception capabilities of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs). Sourcing videos from a diverse array of origins—including User-Generated Content (UGC), AI-Generated Content (AIGC), Robot-Generated Content (RGC), compressed, and gaming videos—the dataset categorizes aesthetics across 12 dimensions spanning Visual Form, Style, and Affectiveness. To systematically probe LMMs, the authors construct questions in multiple formats (True/False, Single Choice, Multiple Choice, and Open-Ended) and benchmark 23 current open- and closed-source LMMs. The evaluation highlights significant capability gaps, noting that modern models possess only rudimentary aesthetic perception, struggling particularly with multiple-choice and open-ended formats.

## Claimed Contributions
- The formulation of the first comprehensive benchmark specifically targeting video aesthetics perception in LMMs, distinguishing the task from standard perceptual video quality assessment.
- A taxonomy of 12 fine-grained aesthetic dimensions (Visual Form, Style, and Affectiveness) applied to a highly diverse set of video domains.
- A multi-format question-answering dataset containing 1,804 question-answer pairs corresponding to source videos.
- Extensive baselining of 23 LMMs, revealing key performance bottlenecks in open-source and closed-source models when addressing nuanced, subjective aesthetic queries.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The transition from image aesthetics (e.g., AesBench) and video perceptual quality (e.g., Q-Bench-Video) to *video aesthetics* represents a logical and highly relevant progression for multimodal evaluation. The inclusion of modern synthetic sources like AIGC and RGC is a forward-looking design choice that aligns the benchmark with current multimedia consumption trends. 

However, when viewed through the lens of literature and conceptual novelty, the benchmark's transition from the image domain to the video domain appears heavily incremental and potentially missing the core attributes of the new modality.

**The "Video" Gap in Video Aesthetics:**
The novelty of this work is somewhat constrained by its direct adaptation of image aesthetics frameworks. The 12 proposed dimensions (e.g., composition, lighting, color, visual subject) are primarily spatial, frame-level attributes. A foundational differentiation between an image aesthetics benchmark and a *video* aesthetics benchmark should be the explicit inclusion of temporal dynamics. Crucial video-specific aesthetic elements—such as camera motion, pacing, editing rhythm, temporal consistency, and motion blur—do not appear to be explicitly represented or prioritized in the provided taxonomy. If the benchmark primarily evaluates spatial attributes that happen to be in motion, it risks operating as an image aesthetics benchmark applied to video frames, leaving it uncertain whether LMMs are actually processing temporal context or merely sampling a single representative frame to answer the questions.

**Distinction from Perceptual Quality and Recent Frameworks:**
The conceptual boundary between "perceptual quality" (e.g., compression artifacts, noise) and "aesthetic quality" (e.g., emotional resonance, stylistic framing) is notoriously fuzzy. While the related work section effectively covers traditional visual quality assessment, it misses recent advancements in LMM-based VQA reasoning frameworks—such as LMM-VQA (2024)—which also construct Q&A pairs for video assessment. A stronger literature review must theoretically and empirically isolate aesthetic perception from underlying perceptual distortions that might dominate an LMM's attention mechanism.

**Mischaracterization of Prior Work (AesBench):**
There are factual inaccuracies in the description of the closest prior work, AesBench. In Section 2.1, the authors state that AesBench covers "three key capabilities including aesthetic perception, aesthetic empathy, and aesthetic interpretation." However, the AesBench literature explicitly defines four capabilities: Perception (AesP), Empathy (AesE), Assessment (AesA), and Interpretation (AesI). Furthermore, Table 1 claims AesBench evaluates "8 dimensions." Without clarification on how the authors are re-categorizing the original work's taxonomy, this risks misrepresenting the established literature to artificially inflate the comparative breadth of VideoAesBench.

## Technical Soundness
Benchmarking aesthetic perception is inherently difficult due to its subjective, culturally dependent nature. The current manuscript presents several methodological uncertainties that impact its technical soundness.

**Subjectivity and Inter-Annotator Agreement (IAA):**
Aesthetic judgments cannot be treated as objective "ground truth" without rigorous statistical validation. The text relies on a "human-in-the-loop strategy" for QA pair creation and manual aesthetic attribute annotation. However, it lacks essential details on how inter-annotator disagreement was resolved, particularly for highly subjective dimensions within "Style" and "Affectiveness." Without reported Inter-Annotator Agreement (IAA) metrics (e.g., Cohen's Kappa or Fleiss' Kappa), it is impossible to determine whether the ground truth reflects a verifiable consensus or the idiosyncratic bias of a single annotator. 

**Dataset Scale and Evaluation Depth:**
With 1,804 QA pairs across 1,641 (or 1,804, as stated in the abstract) videos, the benchmark provides roughly exactly one question per video. This 1:1 ratio is unusually sparse for evaluating fine-grained multimodal comprehension. Evaluating an LMM's ability to navigate 12 distinct aesthetic dimensions is severely constrained if each video only probes one specific aspect. By comparison, image benchmarks like AesBench contain upwards of 8,400 QA pairs, allowing for a much denser evaluation matrix. A 1:1 ratio limits the benchmark's statistical power to thoroughly profile a model's holistic aesthetic understanding of any single video.

## Experimental Rigor
While the empirical evaluation of 23 models provides a substantial baseline for the community, several elements of the experimental design require clarification to ensure reproducibility and rigor.

**Open-Ended Evaluation Protocols:**
The inclusion of open-ended questions is highly commendable, as aesthetics often defy rigid multiple-choice categorization. However, automated evaluation of free-form, subjective text generated by LMMs is notoriously challenging. The manuscript lacks detail on the exact metrics and protocols used to score these open-ended aesthetic descriptions against the ground truth. If an "LLM-as-a-judge" paradigm (e.g., using GPT-4) was employed, the authors must discuss how they mitigated the judge model's own aesthetic biases and aligned it with human preference. 

**Dataset Imbalance:**
The sampling strategy from the source datasets appears highly imbalanced and lacks clear justification. For example, Table 2 indicates that only 4 videos were sampled from the LIVE-Compress dataset. Such extreme sparsity from specific distributions undermines the claim of a comprehensively diverse benchmark and raises questions about the statistical significance of results parsed by video source.

**Anonymity Policy Risk:**
The abstract and introduction contain a direct GitHub link (`https://github.com/michaelliyunhao/VideoAesBench`) that includes what appears to be an author's name (`michaelliyunhao`). From a reviewing standpoint, I am framing this as an Area Chair verification risk. While this breaches the venue's double-blind policy and often constitutes grounds for desk rejection, I leave the procedural enforcement of this violation to the ACs and focus my technical assessment on the assumption that an anonymized artifact will be provided if permitted.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Emerging Modalities:** The deliberate inclusion of AIGC, RGC, and gaming videos addresses a critical gap, ensuring the benchmark evaluates models on the types of synthetic and diverse media they will encounter in modern deployment.
- **Question Formatting:** Moving beyond simple scoring to include multiple-choice questions—where LMMs must actively reason among distractors to select aesthetic attributes—introduces a rigorous and challenging evaluation paradigm that clearly exposes current model limitations.
- **Baseline Utility:** The benchmarking of 23 models establishes a highly useful leaderboard and performance baseline that future literature in multimodal aesthetic reasoning will inevitably cite.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Missing Temporal Aesthetics:** The 12 dimensions fundamentally mirror spatial image aesthetics. The lack of explicit evaluation for camera motion, editing rhythm, pacing, and temporal coherence weakens the claim that this is truly a *video* aesthetics benchmark.
- **Subjectivity Verification:** The absence of Inter-Annotator Agreement metrics for the human-in-the-loop annotations casts doubt on the reliability of the ground-truth answers for inherently subjective queries.
- **Evaluation Sparsity:** A ratio of approximately one question per video limits the capacity to evaluate a model's deep, holistic understanding of a given video asset.
- **Literature Misrepresentation:** Factual inaccuracies regarding the capabilities and dimensions of AesBench, alongside typographical errors in citation years (e.g., 2026, 2025).
- **Anonymity Violation:** The presence of a non-anonymized GitHub repository link in the text.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To strengthen the manuscript and address the methodological gaps, the authors should provide:
1. **Inter-Annotator Agreement Metrics:** Quantitative evidence (e.g., Fleiss' Kappa) demonstrating consensus among human annotators during the creation of ground-truth QA pairs, especially for the Style and Affectiveness dimensions.
2. **Temporal vs. Spatial Analysis:** An analysis or breakdown of whether the existing 1,804 questions strictly require temporal understanding to answer correctly, or if they can be solved by an LMM sampling a single still frame.
3. **Open-Ended Scoring Protocol:** Explicit details on the evaluation framework used to score open-ended responses, including measures taken to align automated judges (if used) with human aesthetic baselines.
4. **Clarification of Dataset Statistics:** Resolution of the numerical discrepancies between the Abstract (1,804 videos), Introduction, and Table 1 (1,641 Source Videos vs. 1,804 QA pairs).

## What Would Change My Assessment
My leaning is currently negative, driven primarily by the concern that adapting spatial image aesthetics to moving frames does not sufficiently capture the novelty of *video* aesthetics, combined with missing validations for subjective ground truth. 

I would be willing to raise my score if the rebuttal can:
1. Demonstrate that the dataset meaningfully evaluates *temporal* aesthetic properties (pacing, rhythm, motion), or update the claims to explicitly scope the benchmark to spatial aesthetics applied to video.
2. Provide convincing IAA statistics that prove the subjective ground truth is reliable.
3. Correct the factual inaccuracies regarding AesBench in Section 2.1 and Table 1.
4. Satisfactorily explain the evaluation mechanism for the open-ended questions.
