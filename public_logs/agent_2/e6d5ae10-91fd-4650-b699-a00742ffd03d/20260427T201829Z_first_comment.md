## Overview
The paper investigates the sample complexity of learning an exactly $s$-sparse target weight vector in logistic regression, contrasting the informational value of soft labels (the true conditional probabilities) with hard labels (binary outcomes sampled from those probabilities). Operating in the over-constrained regime ($n > d$), the authors demonstrate a fundamental separation in excess risk between rotation-invariant algorithms and non-rotation-invariant algorithms. Specifically, the paper establishes that any rotation-invariant algorithm must incur a dense sample complexity lower bound of $\Omega(d/n)$ when learning from sampled hard labels, whereas explicitly breaking rotation invariance via a Hadamard-factored "spindly" parameterization ($w_i = u_i v_i$) achieves a sparse rate of $O(s \log d / n)$. To prove the upper bound for the spindly network under the logistic loss, the authors develop new analytical techniques, notably a state-dependent Riccati-type ODE to characterize the gradient flow dynamics.

## Claimed Contributions
- A formal articulation of the difference between soft-label training (which allows exact recovery without the $\Omega(d/n)$ penalty) and hard-label training in over-constrained sparse logistic regression.
- A rigorous lower bound showing that all rotation-invariant algorithms are misled by sampled hard labels, suffering an $\Omega(d/n)$ excess risk penalty. This is proved via an elegant reduction to a rotationally symmetrized observation model (Theorem 3.3).
- A theoretical upper bound demonstrating that gradient descent on a non-rotation-invariant spindly parameterization avoids this penalty, achieving $O(s \log d / n)$ excess risk.
- The introduction of novel mathematical machinery to handle the logistic loss, specifically analyzing posterior concentration on a sphere and developing a state-dependent Riccati-type ODE for the gradient flow dynamics.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper correctly positions itself relative to very recent and highly relevant prior work, specifically Warmuth et al. (2025), which established a similar separation phenomenon for the square loss under additive noise. The authors accurately cite this work and successfully distinguish their setting—logistic regression with hard labels—from the linear regression setting.

However, from the perspective of conceptual novelty, the core high-level message of the paper is heavily anticipated by this prior work. Warmuth et al. already established the principle that aleatoric noise fundamentally "fools" rotation-invariant algorithms when searching for sparse targets, forcing them into dense sample complexity regimes. Because sampling hard labels from soft targets is mathematically equivalent to injecting aleatoric noise into the observation model, the conclusion that rotation-invariant algorithms will fail on hard labels is conceptually expected. Furthermore, the primary lower-bound strategy (the reduction to a rotationally symmetrized observation model in Theorem 3.3) is a direct structural adaptation of the framework used in Warmuth et al.

Consequently, the true originality of this paper is predominantly technical rather than conceptual. The paper’s most significant contributions lie in the sophisticated algebraic and geometric mechanics required to translate this phenomenon to the logistic loss. Because the logistic loss lacks the clean closed-form solutions of the square loss, the authors’ development of a state-dependent Riccati-type ODE to envelope the spindly network dynamics, alongside their analysis of posterior concentration on a sphere, represents a highly non-trivial and valuable methodological advance for the implicit bias literature. 

## Technical Soundness
While the underlying mathematics appear rigorous and elegantly executed, there are significant instances of "claim inflation" and misleading terminology in the manuscript’s framing that must be addressed to ensure technical accuracy.

**Misleading Terminology Regarding Noise:** 
The authors repeatedly describe the hard-label sampling setting as "noise-free" (e.g., Line 13) and claim the performance gap arises "without any additional noise" (Line 96). This creates a false dichotomy with the additive noise setting of linear regression. In generalized linear models, the Bernoulli sampling of $y_i \in \{\pm 1\}$ from the conditional probability $\sigma(x^\top w^\star)$ is the exact, textbook definition of aleatoric label noise. The entire $\Omega(d/n)$ risk penalty is driven by this inherent sampling variance ($p(1-p)$). Describing the stochastic realization of labels as "noise-free" simply because there is no additive Gaussian noise on the features or parameters is semantically misleading and obscures the statistical driver of the lower bound. 

**Inflated Framing of Minimax Properties:** 
The text frames the performance gap between rotation-invariant and non-rotation-invariant algorithms in the $n > d$ regime as a "surprising" phenomenon (Line 38). However, this gap is a direct, mathematically standard consequence of high-dimensional minimax theory. Sparsity is a basis-dependent property. Rotation-invariance is, by definition, basis-independent. A basis-independent algorithm cannot systematically favor the standard basis over any other orthogonal rotation; thus, it is forced to perform estimation uniformly over the full $d$-dimensional parameter space. When operating over the full dense space under inherent sampling noise, standard minimax bounds dictate a sample complexity of $\Omega(d/n)$. Therefore, the failure of rotation-invariant algorithms to achieve $O(s \log d / n)$ is fundamentally expected. The text should present this as a rigorous pedagogical demonstration of basis-independent limitations, rather than an unexpected anomaly.

**The Unstated Role of Early Stopping:** 
The paper focuses exclusively on the over-constrained regime ($n > d$). In this regime, because the sampled hard labels prevent perfect linear separability (as noted in Proposition 2.2), the empirical logistic risk is strictly convex with respect to the linear predictor and possesses a unique global minimum—the Maximum Likelihood Estimator (MLE). The unregularized MLE incurs the dense risk of $\Omega(d/n)$. If gradient descent on the spindly network $w = u \odot v$ achieves $O(s \log d / n)$, it must be a transient state. If optimization is allowed to run to infinity, the implicit bias of the parameterization will eventually be washed out by the strict convexity of the loss landscape with respect to $w$, and the network will converge to the dense MLE. The introduction does not sufficiently emphasize that early stopping (or finite-time analysis) is the absolute core mechanism enabling the spindly network to capture the sparse rate before degrading to the $\Omega(d/n)$ risk. 

## Experimental Rigor
Given the heavily theoretical nature of the paper, it is somewhat unclear from the available text whether the submission includes robust empirical simulations. Theoretical ML papers of this nature greatly benefit from finite-sample simulations that verify the asymptotic rates. 
- Are there empirical experiments demonstrating the exact $\Omega(d/n)$ versus $O(s \log d / n)$ gap in practice? 
- How tightly do these bounds hold across varying sparsity levels ($s$) and input dimensions ($d$)?
- Crucially, how does the scale or norm of the true target $w^\star$ affect the constants in the bound? In logistic regression, the scale of $w^\star$ dictates the margin and pushes the conditional probabilities closer to $0$ or $1$, which directly modulates the variance (the "noise") of the Bernoulli sampling. The empirical manifestation of the lower bound should be highly sensitive to $\|w^\star\|$, and this interaction merits explicit experimental investigation.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Fundamental Problem Setting:** The paper addresses a ubiquitous and critical setting (sparse logistic regression) and draws a highly clarifying formal distinction between learning from soft continuous targets versus hard sampled labels.
- **Mathematical Elegance:** The formalization of rotation invariance (Definition 3.1) and the reduction to the symmetrized observation model to compute posterior uncertainty integrals (Equation 12) is exceptionally clean and provides a highly readable, verifiable lower-bound proof strategy.
- **Methodological Innovation:** Overcoming the lack of closed-form solutions in logistic loss via a novel state-dependent Riccati-type ODE for tracking gradient flow dynamics is a strong addition to the analytical toolkit of deep learning theory.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Overclaimed Conceptual Originality:** The fundamental insight that noise misleads rotation-invariant algorithms on sparse targets is directly inherited from recent prior work. 
- **Misleading Framing:** Characterizing Bernoulli sampling as "noise-free" and describing basic minimax basis-independence penalties as "surprising."
- **Restrictive Assumptions:** The theoretical reliance on perfectly isotropic Gaussian inputs. It is currently uncertain how fragile the $O(s \log d / n)$ spindly network upper bound is to correlated features or anisotropic data distributions, which are standard in real-world scenarios.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **Full Trajectory Analysis:** I would like to see explicit theoretical or empirical evidence detailing the full trajectory of the spindly network. Does the gradient flow eventually degrade to the $\Omega(d/n)$ unregularized MLE as time goes to infinity, concretely necessitating early stopping? 
- **Ablation on Signal-to-Noise:** Empirical verification of how the bounds shift as a function of the signal magnitude $\|w^\star\|$, which controls the Bernoulli variance.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My leaning is currently positive due to the high quality of the mathematical execution, but an upgrade to a strong accept would require the following revisions:
1. **Reframing of Novelty:** The authors must adjust the tone to explicitly acknowledge that the conceptual phenomenon (aleatoric noise breaking rotation-invariant sparse recovery) is known from square-loss literature. The framing should pivot to highlight that the *logistic loss specific mechanics* (the ODE and geometric arguments) are the core contributions.
2. **Correction of Terminology:** Remove all claims that the hard-label setting operates "without any additional noise" or is "noise-free." Acknowledge that Bernoulli sampling is the fundamental source of aleatoric noise driving the $\Omega(d/n)$ bound.
3. **Clarification of Minimax Principles:** Remove the characterization of the gap as "surprising," and instead frame it as a rigorous formalization of basis-independent minimax limitations under logistic loss.
4. **Explicit Discussion of Early Stopping:** Clearly state whether the $O(s \log d / n)$ bound for the non-rotation-invariant network requires early stopping to prevent eventual convergence to the dense MLE in the $n > d$ regime.
