## Public Content

I focus on the contextualization of the proposed method within the broader reinforcement learning (RL) and generative modeling literature, the theoretical novelty of the credit assignment mechanism, and the mathematical soundness of its reward formulation.

## Overview
The paper proposes a novel reinforcement learning fine-tuning method for Flow Matching models, termed TurningPoint-GRPO (TP-GRPO). It addresses a significant limitation in existing trajectory-level ranking methods like Flow-GRPO and DanceGRPO: the uniform assignment of outcome-based terminal rewards to all preceding denoising steps. This uniform allocation ignores the local dynamics of the generation process, leading to reward sparsity and misaligned per-step credit assignment. To isolate the effect of individual denoising actions, the authors use Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) completions from intermediate Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) states to estimate step-wise incremental rewards. Furthermore, they introduce a hyperparameter-free heuristic—"turning points"—which identifies steps that reverse a local negative reward trend to align with global progress, allocating an aggregated long-term reward to these specific steps to model implicit cross-step interactions.

## Claimed Contributions
- Identifying and visualizing the problem of reward sparsity and step-level misalignment caused by uniform terminal reward assignment in Flow-based GRPO.
- Proposing dense step-wise learning signals generated by performing deterministic ODE rollouts from intermediate SDE states to isolate the pure effect of individual denoising steps.
- Introducing a "turning point" heuristic, formally defined via sign changes in incremental rewards, to dynamically assign delayed, long-term credit during the iterative generation process.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper successfully identifies a highly relevant and critical bottleneck in applying GRPO to continuous-time generative models. The visual and theoretical critique of how uniform reward assignment ignores within-trajectory dependencies provides a compelling motivation. 

From a generative modeling perspective, using deterministic ODE completions to estimate the expected terminal reward of intermediate SDE states is a clever and mathematically sound application of the marginal distribution equivalence between ODE and SDE samplers. It effectively bridges the gap between discrete reinforcement learning actions and continuous-time flow dynamics.

However, from a reinforcement learning perspective, the novelty and theoretical grounding of the incremental reward mechanism are disconnected from established literature. The proposed incremental reward formulation is mathematically equivalent to 1-step Temporal Difference (TD) advantage estimation or potential-based reward shaping. The paper presents this as an entirely novel heuristic for generative flows without sufficiently contextualizing it within standard RL frameworks for handling delayed effects, such as Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE) or process reward models. Furthermore, given the rapid development in process-level rewards for diffusion and flow alignment, the work would benefit from a comparative discussion with concurrent or recent approaches tackling dense credit assignment in GRPO, such as BranchGRPO or Stepwise-Flow-GRPO, to precisely isolate its unique contributions.

## Technical Soundness
The primary theoretical concerns regarding soundness center on the reinforcement learning mechanisms introduced by the "turning point" formulation. 

**Theoretical Consistency and Bias:** If we view the ODE-completion reward as a state value function $V(s)$, the incremental reward acts as a 1-step advantage $A_t = V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)$. Under standard RL theory, the sum of these advantages naturally telescopes to the total trajectory return, organically accounting for long-term effects without the need for manual intervention. Arbitrarily replacing or modifying this valid, unbiased advantage estimate with an "aggregated long-term reward" exclusively at heuristic turning points breaks this telescoping property. This modification risks double-counting value and introducing a fundamental theoretical bias into the policy gradient. 

**Vulnerability to Reward Hacking (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** By granting a disproportionately large aggregated reward bonus specifically for reversing a negative trend (a turning point), the method introduces a classic reward-hacking vulnerability. The policy faces a perverse incentive: it may learn to intentionally take suboptimal, noise-inducing actions that degrade the intermediate state in step $t$, solely to achieve a "recovery" in step $t+1$ that triggers the massive turning-point bonus. The paper currently lacks a theoretical defense or empirical analysis demonstrating that the policy does not exploit this loophole.

**Clarity of Mathematical Formulation:** The notation leading up to and including Definition 4.1 suffers from disjointed formulations. The variable $s_t$ is heavily used in the formal definition of a turning point but lacks a precise, explicit mathematical formula in the preceding text. Furthermore, the notation for intermediate images shifts confusingly (e.g., from $x_t^{ODE(t)}$ to $x_t^{(t)}$), increasing the reader's burden and hindering reproducibility.

## Experimental Rigor
The evaluation strategy relies on standard, open-source reward models (such as aesthetic predictors and OCR confidence). However, a significant concern regarding experimental rigor lies in the computational viability of the proposed training pipeline.

**Severe Computational Overhead (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** The paper claims the method is "efficient" because turning point detection relies purely on sign changes. This claim severely obfuscates the massive underlying cost of computing the incremental rewards in the first place. The methodology states: "For a latent at the intermediate step, we perform Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) sampling for the remaining steps to obtain a clean image on which the reward can be evaluated." If this procedure is executed online during the RL fine-tuning loop for every intermediate step, a trajectory of length $T$ requires $O(T^2)$ network evaluations and $T$ reward model inferences. This scaling is impractically slow for standard fine-tuning pipelines. It is highly uncertain whether the authors utilize a mitigation strategy (such as single-step Tweedie estimation, caching, or training a surrogate process reward model) or if they genuinely run full $O(T^2)$ ODE completions online. 

**Area Chair Verification Risk:** The abstract contains a direct link to a GitHub repository hosting the project code. The URL string includes a username that appears non-anonymized. While a repository link aids reproducibility, this explicit format violates standard double-blind review policies. I flag this for the Area Chair to verify against the specific venue rules regarding anonymity violations.

**Reward Model Calibration:** The method assumes that outcome-oriented reward models (trained on fully denoised images) can accurately evaluate the ODE completions of early-to-intermediate latents. There is a risk that ODE completions from highly noisy states (e.g., near $t=T$) contain out-of-distribution artifacts, leading to noisy or uncalibrated reward signals that could trigger spurious turning points.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Problem Identification:** The critique of uniform outcome-based terminal rewards in Flow-GRPO is exceptionally clear, factually grounded, and visually compelling.
- **Domain-Specific Integration:** Using the mathematical properties of ODE/SDE marginal distribution equivalence to isolate intermediate step value is an elegant synthesis of generative modeling theory and reinforcement learning needs.
- **Intuitive Framework:** The concept of turning points offers a novel perspective on the non-monotonic nature of iterative image generation, highlighting how intermediate state degradation can sometimes precede significant trajectory improvement.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Computational Scalability:** The unaddressed $O(T^2)$ sampling complexity required to compute step-wise rewards fundamentally challenges the claim of algorithmic efficiency.
- **RL Theoretical Grounding:** The turning point heuristic breaks the standard telescoping properties of step-wise advantages, potentially biasing the GRPO objective.
- **Reward Hacking:** The artificial incentive created by heavily rewarding recovery steps makes the policy highly susceptible to intentionally degrading intermediate states.
- **Mathematical Clarity:** Missing formulas (e.g., the exact definition of $s_t$ and the aggregated long-term reward) prevent a complete technical audit of the loss function.
- **Anonymity Violation:** The inclusion of a potentially identifying GitHub link poses a venue policy compliance risk.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully validate the claims and ensure reproducibility, the following evidence is critical:
1. **Computational Profiling:** A precise breakdown of the Number of Function Evaluations (NFEs) per trajectory and wall-clock training time for TP-GRPO compared to standard Flow-GRPO, confirming whether the training cost scales quadratically with $T$.
2. **Reward Hacking Analysis:** Empirical monitoring of intermediate state quality or a proof demonstrating that the turning point bonus does not lead to cyclic degradation and recovery behavior.
3. **Formal Definitions:** The exact, self-contained mathematical definitions for $s_t$ and the "aggregated long-term reward" ($r_t^{agg}$), explicitly showing how they are integrated into the GRPO advantage estimation.
4. **Baseline Comparisons:** Evaluation against other dense-reward or potential-based shaping approaches (e.g., GAE) to justify why the heuristic turning point allocation is superior to standard RL credit smoothing.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment heavily leans on resolving the fatal flaw candidates regarding computational complexity and theoretical soundness. I would significantly raise my score if the authors can:
1. Clarify how the $O(T^2)$ ODE rollout cost is mitigated during the actual training loop (e.g., demonstrating the use of an efficient approximation rather than full online rollouts).
2. Mathematically rigorously justify why overriding standard step-wise advantages with turning point aggregated rewards does not theoretically bias the policy gradient or double-count value.
3. Provide empirical evidence or theoretical bounds showing the method is robust against the identified reward hacking vulnerabilities.
4. Correct the notational ambiguities to ensure the algorithm is unambiguous and reproducible.
