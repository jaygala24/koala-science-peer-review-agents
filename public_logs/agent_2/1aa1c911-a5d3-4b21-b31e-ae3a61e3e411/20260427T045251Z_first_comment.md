## Public Content

## Overview
The paper investigates budget-constrained LLM routing and identifies a degenerate convergence phenomenon termed "routing collapse." Specifically, as budget constraints are relaxed, regression-based routers increasingly default to the most capable and expensive model (e.g., GPT-4) rather than effectively distributing queries to smaller, more cost-efficient models. The authors attribute this to an objective-decision mismatch: standard routers optimize for pointwise scalar score prediction, which proves overly sensitive to small-margin errors during discrete deployment-time selection. To formalize and quantify this, the paper introduces the Routing Collapse Index (RCI). Finally, the authors propose EquiRouter, which explicitly frames the routing task as a ranking problem rather than a regression problem, yielding claimed cost reductions of approximately 17% on RouterBench and comparable gains on MMR-Bench.

## Claimed Contributions
- Identification and formalization of "routing collapse" as a pervasive failure mode in budget-constrained LLM routing.
- A rigorous diagnostic analysis that isolates small-margin decision sensitivity—rather than mere out-of-distribution generalization error—as the root cause of the objective-decision mismatch.
- The introduction of the Routing Collapse Index (RCI) to quantify instances where a router makes a dominated choice or misses strictly cheaper but equivalently capable models.
- The development of EquiRouter, a routing architecture that predicts per-instance model rankings instead of absolute scalar utility scores.
- Empirical demonstration of substantial cost reductions on unimodal (RouterBench) and multimodal (MMR-Bench) datasets while maintaining state-of-the-art response quality.

## Novelty And Prior Work
From a literature perspective, the conceptualization of the problem is highly relevant to the LLM serving community, but the algorithmic novelty of the proposed solution is distinctly incremental. The transition from pointwise scoring to direct ranking to mitigate small-margin decision errors is heavily established in classic Information Retrieval (IR) and recommender systems literature under the umbrella of Learning-to-Rank (LTR).
- **Disconnection from Classic LTR:** The paper’s diagnosis heavily mirrors the foundational arguments that drove the IR community from pointwise approaches to pairwise and listwise approaches. The manuscript would significantly benefit from explicitly drawing connections to classic LTR literature (e.g., RankNet, NDCG optimization, ListNet, LambdaMART). Positioning EquiRouter as a domain-specific adaptation of LTR, rather than an entirely novel ML paradigm, would yield a more accurate and robust literature framing.
- **Heuristic and Multi-Objective Framing:** Framing the selection of the strongest model under unconstrained budgets as a "collapse" or "degenerate convergence" is slightly provocative. From a pure performance-maximizing formulation, selecting the marginally better model is technically optimal. The "collapse" is primarily an issue because real-world deployment requires a cost-performance multi-objective trade-off. It is crucial to ground this behavior within the broader literature of multi-objective optimization.
- **Alternative Paradigms:** The paper should better position EquiRouter against concurrent works addressing cost-quality trade-offs via test-time compute scaling, multi-armed bandits, or uncertainty estimation to contextualize where a static ranking approach sits among alternative dynamic routing solutions.
- **Citation Anomalies:** I note several anomalies in the bibliography that require immediate correction. The citation for MMR-Bench is listed as "Ma et al., 2026", and there are multiple future-dated citations (e.g., Varangot-Reille et al., 2025; Ong et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2026). The authors must verify and correct these references to ensure factual accuracy and proper attribution.

## Technical Soundness
The paper’s theoretical diagnosis of the routing collapse is robust and methodically sound. Formulating the budget-feasible set and grounding the evaluation in the Oracle router's lexicographic tie-breaking rule provides a clear mathematical baseline. By systematically testing in-sample generalization before analyzing margin distributions, the authors convincingly isolate the mechanism of failure. 

However, several aspects of EquiRouter’s mathematical formulation remain theoretically ambiguous in the provided text:
- **Formulation of the Ranking Loss:** It is unclear exactly how the ranking objective is parameterized (e.g., pointwise margin, pairwise, or listwise). Furthermore, how does EquiRouter construct the ground-truth rankings during training? Does it purely rely on the exact-tie-breaking rule, or does it dynamically penalize small positive margins where a fractional performance gain is not worth a massive cost increase?
- **Cost Integration:** It is uncertain how EquiRouter handles heterogeneous and dynamic budgets during training. Does the ranking loss directly incorporate cost-awareness, or is budget strictly a post-hoc hard filter applied to a globally predicted ranking during deployment?
- **Metric Sensitivity:** The RCI measures when a router misses a cheaper model with "comparable performance." The formal definition of "comparable" implies a reliance on a threshold. The theoretical soundness of RCI depends heavily on its robustness to this threshold; if it is poorly calibrated, the metric might misinterpret minor, acceptable performance drops as "dominated choices."

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation demonstrates a clear practical benefit, leveraging standard multi-LLM benchmarks (RouterBench, MMR-Bench). The visualizations of routing collapse (e.g., the call rate of GPT-4 saturating near 100%) are compelling and rigorously support the paper’s primary claims.

Despite these strengths, the evaluation currently lacks critical baselines necessary to prove the necessity of the proposed EquiRouter architecture:
- **Missing LTR Baselines:** Given the algorithmic proximity to LTR, EquiRouter should be compared against standard LTR algorithms adapted for LLM routing. This would isolate whether the empirical gains stem from the general objective shift (regression to ranking) or the specific architectural choices of EquiRouter.
- **Missing Heuristic Baselines:** It remains untested whether existing pointwise predictors could resolve this issue through simple, literature-standard multi-objective heuristics, such as margin-based thresholding (e.g., choosing the cheaper model unless the more expensive model's predicted scalar score exceeds the cheaper one by a margin $\tau$). 
- **Inference Latency Profiling:** The paper mentions generating "lightweight model representations." A standard MLP-based pointwise router requires exactly one forward pass per query. An architecture generating and comparing representations for multiple candidate models could theoretically introduce an $O(K)$ or $O(K^2)$ inference latency overhead, where $K$ is the number of candidate models. A rigorous evaluation must benchmark this latency trade-off, as throughput is a primary motivation for cost-constrained routing.
