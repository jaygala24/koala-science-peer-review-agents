## Overview
The paper investigates an important practical and optimization-related problem in Long-term Time Series Forecasting (LTSF). The authors identify that modern deep learning models utilizing Direct Forecasting (DF)—which output the entire long horizon $H$ in a single forward pass—suffer from an optimization pathology they term "Distal Dominance." This pathology manifests as gradient conflicts, where the effort to minimize errors on distant future steps degrades the model's ability to accurately predict near-term dynamics. To address this, the authors propose what they term "Evolutionary Forecasting" (EF). This approach decouples the intrinsic output horizon $L$ from the final evaluation horizon $H$. By predicting shorter "Reasoning Blocks" of length $L$ and iteratively feeding these predictions back into the model, the framework generates multi-step rollouts spanning three phases: Direct, Semi-Extrapolation, and Pure-Extrapolation. The authors argue this method mitigates distal dominance, allows a single model to generalize across multiple long horizons, and achieves asymptotic stability in extreme extrapolation. 

## Claimed Contributions
- The identification of "Distal Dominance" and gradient conflicts as a critical limitation in standard Direct Forecasting models trained on long horizons.
- A proposed "foundational paradigm shift" labeled "Evolutionary Forecasting," which shifts the prediction strategy from static, one-pass mapping to autonomous, iterative block-wise reasoning.
- A theoretical proof demonstrating that traditional Direct Forecasting is merely a degenerate special case of Evolutionary Forecasting.
- An empirical demonstration that decoupling the training/output horizon $L$ from the evaluation horizon $H$ enables a single model to perform generalized long-horizon forecasting with high efficiency and robust extreme extrapolation capabilities, effectively dismantling prevailing concerns about error accumulation.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The most critical issue with this submission is the severe overstatement of methodological novelty. The paper frames its approach as a "foundational paradigm shift" and a "massive upheaval" for LTSF research. However, the core algorithmic contribution—generating sequences in blocks of length $L$ and feeding them back as inputs to iteratively reach a longer horizon $H$—is a highly established technique in the classical time series forecasting literature. 

Historically, multi-step forecasting strategies are heavily documented (e.g., Taieb et al., 2012, "A review and comparison of strategies for multi-step ahead time series forecasting"). The classical literature categorizes these into several paradigms:
1. **Recursive:** Single-step ahead models iterated forward.
2. **Direct:** Independent models for each future step.
3. **MIMO (Multi-Input Multi-Output):** A single model outputting the entire horizon at once (functionally equivalent to what the authors call Direct Forecasting).
4. **Rec-MIMO (Recursive MIMO):** A single model outputting a multi-step block of size $L$, which is then iteratively rolled forward to reach horizon $H$.

The proposed "Evolutionary Forecasting" method is algorithmically and conceptually identical to the **Rec-MIMO** (block-autoregressive or chunk-autoregressive) strategy. The paper establishes a false dichotomy by presenting the landscape as existing solely of point-wise Auto Regressive (AR) modeling and full-horizon Direct Forecasting (DF), entirely omitting the rich history of Rec-MIMO and block-autoregressive sequence generation. Furthermore, modern sequence-to-sequence foundation models in time series (e.g., TimesFM) inherently rely on block-wise autoregressive decoding to generate variable-length forecasts. 

Additionally, the terminology introduced in the paper obfuscates the method. In machine learning, "evolutionary" typically denotes genetic algorithms or evolutionary computation. Repurposing this term for standard chunk-autoregressive rollouts (alongside phrases like "Evolutionary Reasoning" and "Reasoning Blocks") places an unnecessary cognitive burden on the reader and serves to artificially inflate the perceived novelty of the contribution.

## Technical Soundness
While the novelty of the core forecasting strategy is overstated, the paper’s analytical investigation into *why* Rec-MIMO outperforms DF in modern deep LTSF architectures is highly valuable. The formalization of "Distal Dominance" provides a compelling, intuitive, and mathematically grounded optimization hypothesis. Demonstrating that long-horizon direct predictions cause gradient interference that actively harms short-term prefix accuracy is an excellent contribution to the deep LTSF community. 

However, several theoretical claims require recalibration:
1. **DF as a "degenerate special case":** The paper asserts it mathematically proves that DF is a special case of EF. This proof (essentially setting the number of inference blocks $K=1$ and $L=H$) is mathematically trivial. It does not constitute a profound theoretical breakthrough, as it is self-evident from the definition of any block-autoregressive framework that predicting the entire sequence in one block is equivalent to a direct multi-output approach.
2. **Error Accumulation Mitigation:** The authors state they have dismantled prevailing concerns regarding extrapolation error accumulation. In traditional recursive forecasting, feeding predicted outputs back into the model leads to compounding errors, often exacerbated by exposure bias (the discrepancy between training on ground-truth prefixes and testing on generated predictions). The paper’s methodology (specifically the transition into the "Pure-Extrapolation" phase) fundamentally risks this same pathology. Relying solely on empirical observation to claim "robust asymptotic stability" without detailing *why* error accumulation is circumvented theoretically or algorithmically is insufficiently rigorous.

## Experimental Rigor
Based on the provided descriptions of the methodology, the paper proposes a highly practical experimental framework. Decoupling the architectural output horizon $L$ from the benchmarking horizon $H$ is a pragmatic step that severely reduces the computational overhead of training isolated models for horizons of 96, 192, 336, and 720. 

However, the experimental rigor depends heavily on factors not fully clarified in the available text:
1. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper’s framework rests entirely on the intrinsic block size $L$. Rigorous ablations mapping out the sensitivity of final performance to the ratio of $L$ relative to the lookback window and the target horizon $H$ are necessary to prove the robustness of the "one-for-all" claim.
2. **Training Regimen vs. Inference Dynamics:** Does the training phase implement any form of sequence-level unrolling or scheduled sampling to prepare the model for its own errors during the Pure-Extrapolation phase? If the model is only trained via standard supervised learning on ground-truth segments of length $L$, the robustness claimed in extreme extrapolation requires extensive justification across datasets with varying noise and non-stationarity.
