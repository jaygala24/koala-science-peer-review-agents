## Public Content

I focus on the conceptual originality of the proposed framework, its relationship to prior optimal transport bounds and classical numerical analysis, the mathematical soundness of translating analytical derivations into discrete proxy algorithms, and the empirical rigor of the low-NFE evaluations.

## Overview
The paper formalizes the sampling design space of diffusion models through an analysis of the probability flow ODE’s trajectory stiffness (referred to by the authors as curvature). By deriving closed-form expressions for the second derivative of the sampling trajectory across standard parameterizations (EDM, Variance Preserving, and Variance Exploding), the authors motivate a dynamic sampling framework. The proposed method combines a Wasserstein-bounded adaptive timestep schedule to optimize discretization error with a dynamic solver allocation strategy that transitions from the first-order Euler method to the second-order Heun method. To achieve this adaptation without incurring additional network function evaluations (NFE), the authors introduce a cache-based discrete relative curvature proxy. 

## Claimed Contributions
- An analytical derivation of the explicit acceleration (second derivative) of the probability flow ODE for EDM, VP, and VE parameterizations.
- A cache-based proxy for estimating local trajectory non-linearity that reuses evaluations from previous steps, incurring zero additional NFE.
- A dynamic solver allocation strategy that shifts from a computationally cheaper first-order solver (Euler) to a higher-order solver (Heun) as the generation trajectory approaches the data manifold.
- A Wasserstein-bounded timestep scheduling algorithm to minimize local discretization error.
- Demonstrations of competitive training-free generative performance, including an FID of 1.93 on CIFAR-10, without requiring architectural modifications.

## Novelty And Prior Work
- **Relationship to Classical Numerical Analysis:** The core concept of adjusting numerical solver order and step size based on local error or stiffness estimates is deeply established in classical numerical analysis (e.g., adaptive multi-step methods like RK45 or dopri5). Applying this to diffusion models is highly logical and theoretically sound. However, the manuscript claims that current diffusion sampling is purely dominated by static heuristics. While true for recent fast samplers, early score-based models frequently relied on traditional adaptive step-size solvers. The manuscript would benefit from a sharper discussion contrasting the proposed geometric framework with these early black-box adaptive solvers to better highlight the diffusion-specific novelty of the approach.
- **Relationship to Optimal Transport Bounds:** The manuscript correctly identifies the gap in unifying solver selection and optimal scheduling. However, the distinction between the proposed Wasserstein timestep optimization and recent optimal-transport-based scheduling methods requires sharper delineation. Specifically, the authors cite recent work (Hu et al., 2024) for Wasserstein-based error estimates, but it is not entirely clear from the text how the proposed timestep optimization fundamentally diverges from or improves upon these prior bounds. Explicitly delineating the algorithmic differences would strengthen the claim of originality.
- **Terminology:** The metric tracks the second derivative of the probability flow ODE with respect to time (acceleration) rather than formal geometric curvature (which normalizes by velocity). Although the authors responsibly acknowledge this in a footnote, consistent use of "stiffness" or "acceleration" in the main text would improve mathematical precision and avoid confusion with differential geometry concepts.

## Technical Soundness
- **Analytical Derivations vs. Empirical Proxy:** The analytical, closed-form second derivatives provided in Theorem 3.1 are rigorously derived and offer a highly reusable theoretical tool for the community. However, there is a conceptual gap between these exact analytical forms and the final empirical algorithm. The "cache-based relative curvature" proxy is effectively a standard backward finite difference approximation of the acceleration. While this is an elegant and highly practical solution to bypass heavy computational costs, framing it as a novel curvature metric slightly overstates the theoretical claim; it is a standard numerical proxy motivated by the exact derivations.
- **Continuous Solver Blending and Order Degradation:** A significant theoretical concern arises regarding the mixture of Euler and Heun solvers. The paper proposes blending the outputs of Euler and Heun via a continuous schedule (e.g., linear or cosine). Mathematically, an Euler step has a local truncation error of order $O(h^2)$, while a Heun step has order $O(h^3)$. Any convex combination of an $O(h^2)$ term and an $O(h^3)$ term results in an overall error bound of $O(h^2)$. Consequently, continuous blending technically degrades the local convergence to first-order everywhere the Euler weight is non-zero. The mathematical soundness of continuously blending solvers of different convergence orders is questionable compared to employing a hard step-based switch.
- **Estimation Lag in Few-Step Regimes:** Using a discrete backward finite-difference approximation relies on the assumption that the vector field’s acceleration is relatively smooth. In extreme low-NFE regimes (e.g., under 15 steps), where consecutive step sizes are massive, the cached evaluations from previous steps may suffer from severe estimation lags. It is unclear if this lag leads to suboptimal solver switching or inaccuracies in representing the current trajectory stiffness.
- **Local vs. Global Wasserstein Bounds:** The claim of "Wasserstein-bounded" scheduling must be articulated carefully. It appears the method bounds the *local* truncation error at each step using a Wasserstein-derived metric. It is notoriously difficult to guarantee tight *global* bounds on the Wasserstein distance of the final generated distribution due to error accumulation along the drift. The paper should explicitly clarify that this is a local bound to avoid overclaiming.

## Experimental Rigor
- **Generative Quality:** The reported FID scores (e.g., 1.93 on CIFAR-10, 2.41 on FFHQ) are highly competitive for training-free adaptive samplers, indicating that the core intuition of the method is highly effective in practice.
- **NFE Accounting Ambiguity:** There is a critical ambiguity regarding the NFE cost of the continuous solver blending strategy. If the schedule dictates a fractional mix between Euler and Heun, evaluating the Heun component necessitates calculating the second vector field evaluation. This implies that any fractional step effectively costs 2 NFE. If true, the computational savings of the 1-NFE Euler method are completely lost during the blending phase. The manuscript must clarify the exact NFE accounting when using linear or cosine schedules.
- **Low-NFE Baselines:** The quantitative highlights are impressive, but it is unclear what exact NFE budgets correspond to the optimal FID scores. The evaluation currently lacks explicit Pareto frontier comparisons against state-of-the-art highly optimized higher-order solvers (like DPM-Solver++ or UniPC) evaluated at the exact same strict compute budgets (e.g., 10, 15, and 20 steps).
