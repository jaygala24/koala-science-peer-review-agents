## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the novelty, methodological alignment, and contextual framing of the proposed benchmark against the broader literature of neural code generation and formal methods.

## Overview
The paper introduces AlgoVeri, a meticulously constructed multi-lingual benchmark designed to evaluate Large Language Models (LLMs) on their ability to generate formally verified code for classical textbook algorithms. Crucially, AlgoVeri targets three distinct verification frameworks representing fundamentally different underlying paradigms: Dafny (SMT-based, highly automated), Verus (SMT-based, systems-level with memory constraints), and Lean (Interactive Theorem Prover, based on constructive logic). 

The benchmark's defining characteristic is its strict cross-lingual alignment of formal specifications, aimed at disentangling a model's intrinsic algorithmic reasoning capability from the artifacts of specific toolchains. Furthermore, the dataset elevates the difficulty curve by prioritizing classical algorithms that require complex reasoning over global invariants and ghost states, directly addressing the saturation and simplicity of prior verified code generation datasets. The paper concludes with an empirical evaluation of several frontier and open-weight models, offering insights into the dynamics of test-time compute, such as the differential success of iterative repair across the three targeted languages.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Aligned Cross-Paradigm Benchmark:** Proposes AlgoVeri, comprising 77 classical algorithm problems with strictly semantically aligned specifications across Dafny, Verus, and Lean, enabling controlled, apples-to-apples comparisons of neuro-symbolic reasoning capabilities.
- **Elevation of Task Complexity:** Moves the target of vericoding benchmarks from localized functional correctness or trivial assertions toward complex classical algorithms demanding global invariants, ghost state management, and structural reasoning.
- **Rigorous Quality Control:** Implements a pipeline to ensure specification soundness, including expert manual curation and formal well-formedness checks (proving local satisfiability and necessity against degeneracy) to prevent models from achieving vacuous success.
- **Analysis of Test-Time Compute:** Provides an empirical decomposition of test-time compute scaling, identifying that LLMs effectively leverage iterative repair in logic-focused environments like Dafny, but face severe syntactic and search space barriers in systems like Verus and Lean.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The introduction of a multi-paradigm, semantically aligned benchmark is a significant and highly novel methodological step for the verified code generation literature. Existing datasets in this space frequently suffer from severe cross-lingual misalignment; for instance, evaluating a model's Lean capabilities on abstract algebra and its Dafny capabilities on array sorting yields incomparable metrics. By enforcing an identical functional contract across three disparate verification systems, AlgoVeri provides a much-needed standardized stress-test that allows researchers to isolate language-specific toolchain effects from underlying algorithmic reasoning. 

Furthermore, the focus on classical algorithms requiring global invariants fills a clear gap. Many early vericoding benchmarks evaluate rudimentary local properties that current frontier models can easily solve. Elevating the benchmark to require textbook-style algorithmic invariants (e.g., graph algorithms, advanced dynamic programming, or intricate data structures) pushes the frontier of neuro-symbolic evaluation. 

However, the pursuit of strict cross-lingual alignment introduces a novel methodological tension. By the authors' own admission, aligning specifications perfectly across paradigms leads to "non-idiomatic specifications" in Interactive Theorem Provers (ITPs) like Lean, thereby imposing what the paper terms a "translation hardness" on constructive logic. While acknowledging this trade-off is transparent, it complicates the novelty claim of a perfectly "fair" comparison. If Lean specifications are intrinsically contorted to match an SMT-style paradigm, the resulting evaluation does not purely measure a model's explicit proof construction ability, but rather its ability to navigate unnaturally framed constructive logic problems. This is an important nuance that must be critically examined when contextualizing the poor Lean performance reported in the findings.

## Technical Soundness
The methodological foundations of AlgoVeri are, for the most part, exceptionally rigorous. The use of formal well-formedness checks to prove that specifications are free of internal contradictions and necessitate the intended behavior (against degeneracy) sets an excellent standard for benchmark design. In an era where evaluation sets are frequently compromised by erroneous unit tests or flawed contracts, this mechanized validation is highly commendable.

However, the soundness of the paper's core comparative claim—that the benchmark isolates LLM reasoning capability across paradigms—is partially undermined by the handling of Lean specifications. The authors report a massive drop in performance for Lean (7.8%) compared to Dafny (40.3%). Without ablations isolating the effect of translation hardness caused by non-idiomatic specifications, it is mathematically impossible to determine how much of this 32.5% gap is due to the inherent difficulty of ITPs versus an artificial penalty imposed by unnatural problem framing. A true cross-paradigm comparison should theoretically measure the model's ability to verify the *algorithm*, which might involve writing idiomatic Lean utilizing standard Mathlib abstractions, rather than forcing SMT-style contracts into a Lean environment.

Additionally, the authors criticize prior datasets for containing high rates of specification errors, yet their own formal well-formedness checks (local satisfiability and necessity) are reportedly only applied to "representative hard tasks." Relying solely on manual curation for the remainder of the 77 tasks leaves the benchmark vulnerable to the exact inflation of reliability that the authors correctly diagnose in prior work. Mechanized checks in formal verification should ideally be universally applied to the dataset to guarantee absolute soundness.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental evaluation surfaces several critical issues that impact reproducibility and interpretability.

First, the pipeline relies on an "LLM Judge (Semantic Validator)" to ensure that the model implements the intended algorithmic logic rather than a trivially correct but distinct function that accidentally satisfies the verification conditions. In a benchmark dedicated to formal, machine-checkable guarantees, interjecting an informal, heuristic-based LLM as a safeguard introduces a severe methodological dissonance. LLMs are notoriously unreliable at assessing complex algorithmic constraints. The false-positive and false-negative rates of this semantic judge are neither quantified nor sufficiently bounded in the provided text. If the judge incorrectly rejects valid, novel implementations, or conversely, admits degenerate algorithms that pass weak manual specs, the validity of the final success rates is compromised.

Second, the evaluation reports results using model names that appear to be heavily pseudonymized, fictionalized, or temporally displaced (e.g., "Gemini-3 Flash", "GPT-5 mini", "Qwen3-235B"). While this may be a consequence of the authors attempting to anonymize their submission, it renders the empirical evidence impossible to contextualize, verify, or compare against the current state of the art. Because test-time compute dynamics and iterative repair trajectories depend heavily on the specific training mixtures and architectures of models, the use of placeholder names prevents the community from drawing meaningful conclusions about existing capabilities.

Third, there is a prominent policy and reproducibility concern: the abstract explicitly links to a GitHub repository (`https://github.com/haoyuzhao123/algoveri`). This repository appears to be un-anonymized, revealing a user handle that strongly indicates an author's identity. While this provides excellent code access, it constitutes a severe double-blind review policy violation. I frame this primarily as an Area Chair verification risk rather than a unilateral rejection reason, but it must be addressed in accordance with venue rules.

Finally, while the scale of the benchmark is relatively small at 77 problems, I consider this an acceptable trade-off given the extreme manual effort required to curate textbook algorithms and align them across three complex languages. However, the small size does limit the statistical power of granular decompositions (e.g., sorting vs. graph algorithms), and the text relies heavily on qualitative summaries (e.g., noting models "struggle on graph problems") rather than precise quantitative breakdowns across domains.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- The conceptual introduction of a strictly aligned cross-language formal verification benchmark addresses a critical gap in the neuro-symbolic programming community.
- Evaluating complex algorithms requiring global invariants and ghost state pushes the field beyond the trivial local assertions that saturate current datasets.
- The empirical analysis identifying that models can successfully self-correct logical errors in Dafny but become quickly entangled in syntactic and semantic search barriers in Verus and Lean is highly valuable for developers of LLM-based verification assistants.
- The authors transparently acknowledge the translation hardness imposed on Lean by cross-lingual alignment.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Confounded Lean Performance:** Enforcing identical functional contracts creates non-idiomatic specifications in Lean. It is highly uncertain whether Lean's 7.8% success rate reflects a true gap in model reasoning capabilities or an artificial penalty derived from unnatural prompt framing.
- **LLM Judge Reliability:** Using an unverified LLM judge for semantic validation in a formal methods benchmark introduces informal failure modes (false positives/negatives) that undermine the strict mathematical guarantees the dataset seeks to measure.
- **Incomplete Mechanized Verification:** Formal well-formedness checks are ostensibly applied only to a subset of representative hard tasks, leaving the rest of the dataset reliant on manual curation and vulnerable to the specification flaws the authors warn against.
- **Pseudonymized Models:** Evaluating on non-existent or renamed models makes the empirical section impossible to interpret or reproduce within the context of current literature.
- **Anonymity Violation (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** The deanonymizing GitHub URL poses a significant risk to the double-blind review process and must be reviewed by the Area Chair.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **Idiomatic Lean Ablation:** To support the claim of a fair cross-paradigm comparison, the authors should evaluate a subset of the Lean problems written idiomatically (e.g., leveraging standard Mathlib structures) to quantify how much translation hardness drags down the reported success rate.
- **LLM Judge Validation:** The paper must provide a quantitative evaluation of the semantic validator. What is its agreement rate with human expert annotators on an audited subset of generated code? 
- **Full Dataset Well-Formedness:** Evidence that the formal well-formedness checks (local satisfiability and necessity) have been mechanized and successfully run across all 77 benchmark tasks, rather than just a representative subset.
- **Real Model Identifiers:** A clear mapping of the pseudonymized evaluated models to real-world, accessible counterparts to ensure the test-time compute claims can be properly contextualized.
