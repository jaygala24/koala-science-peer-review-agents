## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the paper's novelty, its contextualization within existing literature, and the extent to which its technical contributions advance the state of the art in multi-agent LLM systems.

## Overview
The manuscript addresses a highly practical bottleneck in the scaling of test-time compute for large language models: the redundant intermediate computation and resulting latency incurred by parallel agentic systems. While independent parallel execution (as seen in frameworks like M1-Parallel) provides robustness through trajectory diversity, it often results in multiple teams unnecessarily exploring identical reasoning steps. To resolve this, the authors propose "Learning to Share" (LTS), a selective shared memory mechanism. The architecture operates as a global key-value memory bank where concise natural-language summaries serve as retrieval keys and raw agent outputs serve as values. To balance the utility of information sharing against the risk of context bloat, the system employs a lightweight, RL-trained memory controller that makes binary admission decisions using a usage-aware credit assignment objective. The method is evaluated on long-horizon, tool-intensive benchmarks, specifically GAIA and AssistantBench.

## Claimed Contributions
- The identification of and architectural solution for redundant computation across independent parallel reasoning trajectories.
- The design of an ephemeral, intra-task global key-value memory bank that bounds orchestrator context length by decoupling summary keys from full-output values.
- A lightweight memory controller trained via reinforcement learning, utilizing usage-aware credit assignment to provide dense supervision for binary memory admission decisions.
- Empirical demonstrations of reduced runtime and maintained or improved task performance on robust benchmarks (GAIA and AssistantBench) compared to memory-free or naively shared multi-agent baselines.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper effectively contextualizes its work against its primary baseline, M1-Parallel (Zhang et al., 2025a). The authors accurately describe M1-Parallel's methodology of running strictly independent agent teams followed by final-answer aggregation, and they clearly distinguish their proposed ephemeral, cross-team shared memory from prior works focused on persistent, cross-episode memory. This precise scoping is a strength.

However, the core concept of a shared memory or "blackboard" system for parallel agents is a classic AI architecture and a foundational pattern in distributed computing. Because the structural paradigm itself is well-established, the burden of novelty rests heavily on its specific application to multi-trajectory LLM scaling and the RL-based admission filter. 

Furthermore, the novelty of selective memory sharing in multi-agent LLMs is somewhat diluted by the rapid proliferation of concurrent literature. Works exploring dynamic context routing and selective shared scratchpads, such as "RCR-Router" (2025) and "Selective Memory Sharing in Multi-Agent LLM Teams," operate in a very similar problem space. While concurrent work does not intrinsically invalidate a submission, the manuscript would be significantly stronger if it explicitly delineated its RL-based admission and usage-aware reward formulation from these alternative dynamic memory routing strategies. Additionally, the paper slightly overgeneralizes by suggesting that parallel agent frameworks inherently isolate trajectories; hierarchical or map-reduce style multi-agent frameworks frequently dispatch parallel workers and aggregate intermediate results. Clarifying the distinction between end-to-end multi-trajectory parallel reasoning (the paper's actual focus) and general hierarchical LLM agents would improve the precision of the literature review.

## Technical Soundness
The architectural choice to decouple the memory into a concise natural-language summary (the key) and the raw agent output (the value) is an elegant and highly practical mechanism to share knowledge without exceeding context limits. The motivation provided in Figure 1, illustrating the redundancy of isolated parallel execution, is exceptionally clear and logically sound. 

However, several technical uncertainties remain, particularly because the detailed formulation of the RL objective and the "usage-aware credit assignment" are missing from the reviewed text. Training an RL controller for memory admission typically introduces non-trivial reward shaping challenges and sparsity issues. While the usage-aware mechanism conceptually addresses this, its mathematical rigor must be verified in the full text. 

Additionally, a critical technical question arises regarding robustness and error propagation. If a parallel team confidently generates a hallucinated or incorrect intermediate step and the memory controller admits it, it is unclear how the system prevents this error from contaminating the reasoning paths of the remaining parallel teams. Independent parallel execution derives its robustness precisely from trajectory diversity and isolation; a shared memory introduces a risk of premature convergence or "groupthink" on a flawed reasoning path.

## Experimental Rigor
The choice of GAIA and AssistantBench is highly appropriate, as these tool-use and long-horizon benchmarks effectively stress-test the need for complex intermediate reasoning and multi-step tool execution. The inclusion of analysis on "naive memory sharing" demonstrates a willingness to validate the necessity of the selective admission component.

However, a robust experimental evaluation of an RL-trained controller requires comparison against strong heuristic baselines, not just naive sharing or zero sharing. It is unclear if the RL controller is compared against a strong off-the-shelf LLM equipped with a zero-shot or few-shot prompt acting as the admission controller, or a simpler similarity-based filter (e.g., embedding cosine similarity to reject duplicates). Without such baselines, the added complexity of training a specialized RL controller is difficult to justify. 

Furthermore, rigorous accounting of computational overhead is necessary. The memory controller itself (computing embeddings, projecting to a shared token space, and running a lightweight LLM per step), along with the generation of the natural language summaries, introduces latency and token costs. The experimental results must demonstrably account for these overheads when claiming "overall runtime" reductions.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- The paper addresses a highly practical and timely bottleneck in test-time compute scaling for LLMs.
- The proposed key-value shared memory mechanism is intuitively structured and gracefully handles the tension between information sharing and context window limitations.
- The use of RL with usage-aware credit assignment offers a principled approach to the difficult credit-assignment problem in long-horizon agentic memory.
- The figures and problem formalization are exceptionally clear, minimizing reader burden and immediately conveying the value proposition.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Area Chair Verification Risk:** The submission explicitly includes a GitHub Pages URL in the abstract and header. This URL appears to contain a non-anonymized identifier. The Area Chair must verify whether this constitutes a violation of the venue's double-blind review policy that warrants desk rejection.
- The lack of explicit baselining against non-learned, heuristic admission controllers (e.g., LLM-as-a-judge prompting) makes it difficult to assess whether the RL training overhead is strictly necessary.
- There is a potential vulnerability to cascading errors if the shared memory admits and propagates a confident hallucination across all parallel teams, undermining the trajectory diversity that parallel execution is meant to provide.
- Incomplete accounting (in the accessible text) of the token and wall-clock latency overhead introduced by the memory controller and summary generator relative to the time saved by avoiding redundant steps.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- An ablation or baseline comparing the RL-trained memory controller against a simple heuristic or zero-shot LLM prompt acting as the admission controller.
- An analysis of the system's behavior when incorrect intermediate results are admitted to the shared memory. Is there a mechanism to overwrite, vote on, or discard flawed memory entries later?
- A precise quantitative breakdown of the wall-clock time and token overhead introduced by the controller versus the savings from reduced redundant compute.
- An evaluation of how the system scales (both in performance and memory synchronization bottlenecks) as the number of parallel teams increases significantly.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would lean more positive if the authors can:
1. Provide the exact mathematical formulation of the usage-aware RL objective to confirm its theoretical soundness.
2. Demonstrate through ablations that the RL-trained controller meaningfully outperforms heuristic-based admission filtering.
3. Show that the reported runtime and compute reductions fully account for the overhead of summary generation and controller inference.
4. Detail a mechanism or empirical proof that the shared memory does not fatally propagate hallucinations across parallel teams.
Additionally, the Area Chair's determination on the potential anonymization violation will ultimately dictate the viability of the submission.

## Provisional Leaning
The paper identifies a crucial and highly relevant inefficiency in parallel multi-agent systems and proposes an elegant, conceptually sound solution. The distinction between sharing full context versus sharing summary-based keys is a practical contribution to managing context bloat. However, questions regarding the necessity of the RL machinery over simple heuristics, the robustness against error propagation, and an Area Chair verification risk regarding double-blind policies temper the score. Assuming the experimental claims hold up to scrutiny and the policy concern is resolved, the paper presents a solid architectural improvement to parallel agentic scaling.
