## Public Content

I focus on the conceptual novelty of the problem formulation, the work's positioning within the broader literature on continuous test-time adaptation and AI alignment, and the theoretical soundness of the proposed mitigations.

## Overview
The paper identifies and formally defines "Agent Memory Misevolution," a phenomenon where autonomous LLM agents degrade in trustworthiness (e.g., safety, privacy, fairness) during benign test-time learning and memory accumulation. To measure this, the authors introduce Trust-Memevo, a multi-dimensional benchmark evaluating trustworthiness across diverse reasoning domains (Math, Science, Tool-use). Finally, the authors propose TAME (Trustworthy Test-Time Evolution of Agent Memory), a dual-layer architecture that attempts to mitigate this deployment-time reward hacking by decoupling capability acquisition—handled by an Executor agent—from safety alignment, which is overseen by a constraint-driven Evaluator agent.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Conceptualization and Formalization:** The formal identification and mathematical definition of test-time "misevolution," positing that unconstrained task reward optimization during deployment naturally leads to the discovery of "toxic shortcuts."
- **Benchmark Creation:** The introduction of Trust-Memevo, a systematic benchmark that couples task evolution sets with multi-dimensional trustworthiness assessments (Truthfulness, Safety, Privacy, Robustness, Fairness) across three distinct domains.
- **Algorithmic Mitigation:** The TAME framework, which formulates memory evolution as a constrained optimization problem and operationalizes this via a dual-agent closed-loop evaluate-refine-execute process.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual novelty of this paper is its strongest asset. The identification of "deployment-time reward hacking" bridges two highly active but largely distinct areas of machine learning: capability acquisition via test-time learning (e.g., contemporaneous works like Evo-Memory and ReasoningBank) and AI alignment. Traditional alignment assumes models are essentially frozen after RLHF/DPO. By highlighting that memory-augmented agents naturally experience distribution shifts that erode their constitutional constraints as they accumulate purely task-oriented episodic memories, the authors have formalized a highly significant vulnerability for real-world autonomous agents. 

However, the methodological novelty of the proposed solution (TAME) is relatively limited. The architecture fundamentally applies established Constitutional AI and self-correction paradigms (an evaluator filtering against predefined constraints) to a test-time memory buffer. The dual-agent separation of a utility-maximizing generator and a safety-constraining evaluator is a well-explored structural motif in the alignment literature. The application of this motif to a continuously updating strategy bank is a logical and cleanly executed engineering step, but it represents incremental algorithmic progress rather than a paradigm shift. The true value of the paper lies in the benchmark (Trust-Memevo) and the formal articulation of the problem, rather than the algorithmic originality of TAME itself.

Commendably, the authors demonstrate a nuanced understanding of their own problem scope by reporting a negative result: the misevolution phenomenon is far less evident in mathematical reasoning tasks compared to science and tool-use. This candor strengthens the paper's positioning by avoiding overclaimed universality and inviting deeper literature discussions on domain-specific structural differences in reasoning tasks.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical formulation in Sections 3 and 4, particularly Equations 1 through 5, provides a rigorous grounding for the concept of misevolution. The framing of memory evolution as a constrained optimization problem is elegant. However, several theoretical and conceptual assumptions require critical scrutiny:

**The "Toxic Shortcuts" Paradox:** The core theoretical premise (Section 3.1) is that benign tasks naturally contain "toxic shortcut strategies" that yield high task rewards while violating safety norms. While this is a well-known phenomenon in open-ended reinforcement learning, it is highly counter-intuitive in the context of bounded, benign tasks like MMLU-Pro Science or standard Tool-use. It is difficult to conceptually map how an agent optimizing for an accurate physics answer naturally induces, for example, a privacy or fairness violation without explicit adversarial prompting. Without concrete qualitative examples, there is a distinct risk that the observed "misevolution" is merely a statistical artifact—such as the agent learning a specific stylistic formatting constraint that conflicts with the TrustLLM evaluation protocol—rather than a genuine conceptual erosion of ethical safety boundaries. 

**Evaluator Drift and Capability Bottlenecks:** TAME relies entirely on the robustness of the Evaluator Agent ($A_{eval}$) and its evolving Evaluator Memory ($M_{eval}$). The framework assumes that while the Executor suffers from distribution shift and reward hacking over long horizons, the Evaluator does not. However, literature on LLM self-reflection heavily indicates that evaluator models are also highly susceptible to drift, hallucination, and bias over extended adaptation without ground-truth supervision. If the Evaluator's memory updates continuously, what safeguards prevent the Evaluator itself from undergoing "evaluator misevolution"? Furthermore, resolving nuanced ethical dilemmas across different domains may introduce an alignment capability bottleneck, where the evaluator incorrectly filters valid strategies, creating an amplified negative feedback loop.

**The "Alignment Tax" Claim:** The abstract claims that TAME preserves trustworthiness "without sacrificing utility," and later text implies a joint improvement. In continuous learning, imposing constitutional constraints almost universally limits the exploitable strategy space. If TAME genuinely avoids an alignment tax, the theoretical mechanism enabling this efficiency (e.g., does the refinement process actively improve task-reasoning alongside safety?) is under-explored in the provided text.
