## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the paper's contextualization within the broader representation learning and relighting literature, scrutinizing the novelty of its core empirical claims, and assessing the structural distinctiveness of the proposed Augmented Latent Intrinsics (ALI) framework relative to recent state-of-the-art methods.

## Overview
This paper investigates the impact of modern visual priors on the low-level vision task of image-to-image relighting. It explicitly challenges the prevailing assumption that stronger pretrained semantic encoders strictly benefit downstream tasks, hypothesizing instead that they can degrade relighting performance due to a fundamental tension between semantic abstraction and photometric fidelity. To address the shortcomings of high-level semantic features, the authors propose Augmented Latent Intrinsics (ALI). This framework seeks to regain lost dense photometric structure by fusing features from a pixel-aligned visual encoder into a latent-intrinsic pipeline. The approach specifically targets historically difficult specular materials like metal and glass. Furthermore, to overcome the persistent scarcity of paired ground-truth illumination data, the paper introduces a self-supervised refinement strategy trained on unlabeled real-world image pairs.

## Claimed Contributions
- Empirical probing experiments demonstrating that features from top-performing semantic encoders degrade relighting quality, thereby revealing a fundamental trade-off between semantic abstraction and photometric fidelity.
- The introduction of the Augmented Latent Intrinsics (ALI) framework, which structurally fuses a pixel-aligned visual prior with latent intrinsic representations to improve relighting realism, particularly on complex, specular materials.
- The development of a self-supervised refinement strategy that leverages unlabeled real-world image pairs, adapting the model to real-world illumination scenarios without requiring paired training data.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The explicit investigation of the failure modes of large semantic visual priors in dense, lighting-sensitive tasks is a welcome angle. However, the novelty of the core empirical claim must be carefully weighed against established principles in representation learning. 

The finding that "stronger semantic encoders harm relighting" is presented as a counter-intuitive discovery. Yet, from a theoretical standpoint, this phenomenon is highly predictable. Models optimized for semantic understanding—such as CLIP (via contrastive text-image matching) or DINOv2 (via self-supervised feature distillation)—are explicitly trained to achieve invariance to low-level scene variations, including lighting, shading, and viewpoint. A representation that successfully abstracts away illumination to recognize an object will naturally perform poorly when tasked with reconstructing or manipulating that very illumination. Therefore, the "surprising" nature of this claim depends entirely on the depth of the probing. If the paper merely demonstrates that lighting-invariant models fail at lighting tasks, the scientific novelty is limited. To constitute a significant contribution to the literature, the paper must dissect *why* and *how* these features fail, separating the intentional invariance of the semantic objective from incidental architectural artifacts (such as the loss of high-frequency spatial details due to downsampling).

Regarding the architectural contribution, the proposed ALI framework operates within the domain of latent intrinsic representations. Recent literature, notably works such as LumiNet (CVPR 2024), has already successfully explored the intersection of latent intrinsics and diffusion models for indoor scene relighting. The primary architectural novelty of ALI appears to be the injection of a "pixel-aligned visual encoder." The paper must explicitly contextualize ALI against models like LumiNet to clarify its structural distinctiveness. If the pixel-aligned fusion is essentially the addition of early-layer skip connections to an existing latent backbone, the algorithmic novelty may be viewed as overly incremental for an ICML audience. 

## Technical Soundness
The hypothesis underlying the method—balancing semantic context with dense photometric structure—is conceptually sound. However, the technical rigor of the execution relies heavily on the specific mechanisms of feature fusion. The model must integrate dense pixel-aligned features without overwriting or discarding the valuable disentanglement properties (e.g., separating albedo, shading, and lighting) that latent intrinsic representations provide.

The proposed "self-supervised refinement strategy" is a highly pragmatic approach to the problem of paired data scarcity. However, its formulation invites technical scrutiny. Learning relighting without ground-truth paired data is mathematically underconstrained. It is currently unclear how the objective function prevents degenerate solutions, such as the model learning trivial identity mappings or ignoring the target illumination conditioning entirely. The soundness of this refinement strategy rests on whether it genuinely learns from unlabeled data or if it covertly relies on hidden supervision—such as synthetic pretraining, pseudo-labels extracted from off-the-shelf normal/depth estimators, or strong multi-view constraints disguised as "unlabeled pairs."

## Experimental Rigor
A rigorous probing of visual priors requires comprehensive ablation across multiple distinct families of encoders. To validate the claim about "top-performing semantic encoders," the experiments should contrast architectures trained for invariance (e.g., CLIP, DINOv2) against those that might preserve low-level structure (e.g., MAE, standard ResNets or ConvNeXts). Furthermore, the evaluation must strictly control for spatial resolution to ensure that the observed degradation is inherently tied to semantic abstraction rather than a simple disparity in feature map sizes.

Evaluating relighting performance on "complex, specular materials" introduces significant measurement challenges. Standard full-image metrics like PSNR or SSIM are notoriously insensitive to the localized, high-frequency nature of specular highlights and reflections. Claims of "largest gains" on these materials necessitate rigorous validation, ideally incorporating specialized perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) or comprehensive human perceptual studies to confirm that the numerical improvements translate to visual realism.

- **Architectural Incrementality:** The lack of specificity regarding the "pixel-aligned visual encoder" raises concerns. If the proposed fusion mechanism is standard, the methodological contribution may be viewed as a marginal engineering upgrade over existing latent intrinsic models rather than a fundamental innovation.
- **Ambiguity in Methodological Scope:** The reliance on abstract terms ("top-performing semantic encoders," "unlabeled real-world image pairs") obscures the actual scope, generalizability, and reproducibility of the findings.

- **Resolution Control Experiments:** Controlled experiments that artificially equate the spatial resolution of the semantic encoders with the pixel-aligned prior to definitively isolate the impact of semantic abstraction from downsampling artifacts.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would become more positive if the full text demonstrates that the probing analysis isolates the semantic abstraction penalty from trivial spatial downsampling artifacts. A rigorous demonstration that the ALI framework significantly and perceptually outperforms recent baselines like LumiNet on established benchmarks would strengthen the case for its architectural novelty. Finally, a transparent and mathematically sound explanation of the self-supervised refinement objective—showing it avoids degenerate solutions without relying on hidden supervised priors—would heavily bolster the paper's technical standing.
