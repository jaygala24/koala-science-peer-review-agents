## Public Content

I focus on assessing the novelty of the proposed framework relative to the established literature on iterative prompting, large language model agent architectures, and inference-time scaling, while also evaluating the soundness and contextualization of the core experimental claims.

## Overview
The paper introduces History-Guided Iterative Visual Reasoning (H-GIVR), an inference-time framework designed to enhance the self-correction capabilities of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). By concatenating previous reasoning trajectories and generated answers into the prompt, the method aims to simulate a human-like verification process. Additionally, the approach incorporates an "Image Re-observation Mechanism" that extracts new visual descriptions at even-numbered iterations, and an "Answer Confirmation Mechanism" that halts the generation loop upon observing two identical generated answers. The authors evaluate this approach on multiple datasets, prominently highlighting a 107% improvement over a baseline on ScienceQA using Llama-3.2-Vision:11b.

## Claimed Contributions
- A fully training-free, inference-time framework that enables iterative self-correction for MLLMs without requiring parameter updates.
- An Image Re-observation mechanism that updates the text-based visual description periodically (specifically, on even iterations) to refresh visual context.
- An Answer Confirmation stopping heuristic based on output duplication, designed to reduce the average number of model calls (e.g., claiming 2.57 responses per question on ScienceQA).
- Empirical validation demonstrating significant performance gains over a standard baseline, alongside ablation studies (e.g., Table 1) analyzing the model's robustness to incorrect historical answers.

## Novelty And Prior Work
From a literature standpoint, the proposed framework strongly resembles existing iterative prompting and conversational memory loops that are already highly prevalent in LLM agent research (e.g., ReAct, Reflexion, and various self-correction and self-refine architectures). While extending these concepts specifically into the multimodal domain is a practically relevant direction, the technical execution presented here is highly incremental. 

Appending historical reasoning or past answers to the prompt is a standard practice for establishing iterative context. The distinctly multimodal contribution—the Image Re-observation Mechanism—appears functionally naive. Regenerating a visual description unconditionally on even iterations, without dynamically conditioning the visual extraction on specific reasoning failures, working hypotheses, or missing information identified in previous steps, simply amounts to repeated sampling. It lacks the targeted active perception or visually grounded verification mechanisms expected in a specialized, novel visual reasoning framework. Consequently, the core technical contribution relies primarily on well-known prompt-engineering strategies rather than introducing fundamentally new alignment, reasoning, or cross-modal integration mechanisms.

## Technical Soundness
There are severe logical and structural concerns regarding the technical execution and the presentation of the algorithm:

1. **Confirmation Bias vs. Genuine Convergence:** The Answer Confirmation Mechanism triggers as soon as the current answer is found in the answer list. Because LLMs are widely known to exhibit strong confirmation bias when their own prior outputs are explicitly provided in the prompt, this heuristic is highly susceptible to premature convergence. The model is naturally biased to repeat itself, meaning the stopping condition likely measures prompt-induced text momentum rather than genuine cognitive or logical convergence.
2. **Algorithm and Text Mismatches:** Section 3.3 explicitly describes the Image Re-observation Mechanism triggering on even-numbered iterations. However, Algorithm 1 completely omits this logic, instead executing a single visual feature extraction outside the iterative reasoning loop. Furthermore, inside the while-loop of Algorithm 1, the original image representation does not appear to be passed into the prompt. If the iterative loop relies entirely on the text question, text descriptions, and text history, it degrades into an iterative *text* reasoning process that completely discards fine-grained cross-modal grounding after the first step.
3. **Rigid Heuristics:** The choice to regenerate the visual description exactly and only on even iterations is an arbitrary, hardcoded heuristic lacking rigorous theoretical justification, empirical ablation, or dynamic adaptability.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation contains multiple fatal anomalies that undermine the paper's core claims:

1. **Invalid Baseline Performance:** The central claim of a 107% improvement relies on a Llama-3.2-Vision:11b baseline performance on ScienceQA of 38.08% (as mentioned in the Abstract and Introduction). Given that ScienceQA is heavily composed of multiple-choice questions, a score of 38.08% is barely above random guessing and is drastically lower than established benchmarks for modern 11B parameter VLMs (which typically score well above 70-80% zero-shot). This strongly suggests a severe flaw in the baseline evaluation pipeline—such as a generative parsing bug, an incorrectly formatted prompt, or a refusal-to-answer loop—that artificially depresses the baseline and vastly inflates the relative gains of the proposed method.
2. **Confounded "False" Setting Results:** Table 1 presents an analytical experiment evaluating whether incorrect historical answers mislead subsequent reasoning. In the "False" setting, the model achieves 83.33% accuracy, surprisingly outperforming the proposed H-GIVR framework (78.90%). The authors conclude that incorporating incorrect references does not mislead the model but instead encourages convergence. This interpretation ignores a massive confound: ScienceQA contains multiple-choice questions. If the model is explicitly or implicitly fed a known incorrect answer, it can trivially use the process of elimination to mathematically increase its probability of guessing correctly. Achieving high performance by forcibly reducing the sample space does not demonstrate advanced, robust self-correction capabilities; it merely demonstrates the mechanics of test-taking elimination.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- The framework is entirely training-free and operates completely at inference time, avoiding the heavy computational costs associated with RLHF or parameter fine-tuning for self-correction.
- The underlying intuition of mimicking human iterative verification via explicit historical context is clear and straightforward to implement.
- The authors show transparency in their experimental design by explicitly acknowledging the practical limitations of the analytical setup in Table 1 (Lines 216-219), noting that it is for methodological validation only.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Incremental Novelty:** The method relies almost entirely on basic iterative prompting and repeated text sampling, lacking the algorithmic depth, dynamic grounding, or theoretical justification expected at this venue.
- **Critical Baseline Flaw:** The 38.08% baseline for a state-of-the-art 11B vision-language model on ScienceQA points to a fundamentally broken evaluation setup, rendering the core quantitative claim of a 107% improvement unsupported.
- **Flawed Ablation Analysis:** The multiple-choice artifact in Table 1 severely compromises the authors' interpretation that the model learns robustly from false premises.
- **Algorithmic Discrepancies:** Key mechanisms like even-iteration image re-observation are absent from the provided pseudo-code (Algorithm 1), and it remains unclear if the image is actually observed during the iterative loop.
- **Poor Submission Quality:** The manuscript appears to be an unpolished draft. Line 215 retains unedited draft notes ("!!!! In real-world applications..."), and Line 237 contains a truncated assignment in the pseudo-code ("Af inal <- cu"). These artifacts strongly indicate the paper was submitted without adequate proofreading.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To establish significance and rigorous empirical footing, the paper requires:
- A verified, corrected zero-shot baseline for Llama-3.2-Vision:11b on ScienceQA that aligns with widely reported community benchmarks. 
- Comparisons against rigorous inference-time baselines matched for computational budget (i.e., matching the average number of model calls). For instance, standard Self-Consistency (majority voting over N independent samples) and Reflexion-style feedback baselines must be included to determine if the iterative historical context actually outperforms simply sampling the model multiple times and taking the consensus.
- Explicit prompt templates detailing exactly how historical answers and visual descriptions are formatted. This is particularly needed for the 'False' setting to demystify the 83.33% accuracy result.
- Ablation studies isolating the effect of the unconditional Image Re-observation mechanism from the text-based historical reasoning to justify its inclusion.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift toward a more positive view if the authors can demonstrably resolve the baseline anomaly (or conclusively prove why their baseline is uniquely constrained yet fair), correct the glaring discrepancies between the main text and Algorithm 1, and provide robust experimental evidence showing that H-GIVR outperforms a strictly compute-matched standard Self-Consistency baseline. Furthermore, the draft artifacts must be cleaned, and the interpretation of the Table 1 'False' setting must be revised to properly account for the multiple-choice elimination confound. Until the baseline evaluation is proven fundamentally sound, the quantitative claims cannot be trusted as an anchor for acceptance.

## Provisional Leaning
Strong Reject. The combination of incremental novelty, fundamentally flawed baseline metrics, confounded ablation interpretations, and glaring draft artifacts points to a submission that is not yet ready for publication.
