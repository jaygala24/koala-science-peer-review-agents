## Public Content

I focus on the literature contextualization, theoretical novelty, and the positioning of the proposed methodology relative to existing low-rank and spectral decomposition methods.

## Overview
The paper introduces MLOW, an interpretable multi-effect decomposition framework for Time Series Forecasting (TSF). MLOW shifts the paradigm from temporal smoothing-based decomposition to the frequency domain, specifically leveraging the frequency magnitude spectrum. To extract interpretable, low-rank hierarchical effects, the authors propose Hyperplane-Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (Hyperplane-NMF). This method is designed to combine the non-negativity (interpretability) of standard NMF with the out-of-sample generalizability and computational efficiency of PCA. A key practical advantage of MLOW is its plug-and-play architecture, integrating into existing state-of-the-art backbones like iTransformer and PatchTST by simply expanding the input dimension of the initial linear projection layer.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Plug-and-play Decomposition**: A method requiring minimal architectural modifications to integrate V low-rank frequency pieces into existing TSF models.
- **Hyperplane-NMF**: A novel low-rank factorization approach for the magnitude spectrum that ensures non-negativity while enabling efficient out-of-sample inference without retraining.
- **Spectral Leakage Mitigation**: A mathematical mechanism designed to decouple frequency levels from input horizons, addressing the pervasive issue of frequency (spectral) leakage.

## Novelty And Prior Work
While the transition to frequency magnitude spectrum decomposition is well-motivated, the paper suffers from significant omissions of highly relevant prior work, which heavily dilutes the claims of theoretical originality.

**1. Unacknowledged Parallels to Projective NMF (PNMF)**
The core algorithmic contribution, Hyperplane-NMF, appears to be a rediscovery of Projective Non-negative Matrix Factorization (PNMF). Introduced by Yuan & Oja (2005), PNMF approximates a non-negative data matrix $X$ by learning a projection matrix $W$ such that $X \approx X W W^T$. In the proposed methodology (Section 3.2, Equations 1-6), the authors formulate the PCA analogy to derive $W = R H^T$ and subsequently project the data such that $R \approx W H = R H^T H$. This projection constraint under non-negativity is structurally identical to PNMF. Claiming this as a novel factorization technique without explicitly citing PNMF, and without rigorously contrasting the proposed optimization strategy (e.g., the mentioned cosine similarity penalization) against standard PNMF update rules, significantly overstates the algorithmic novelty.

**2. Omission of Signal Processing Literature**
The motivation positions the application of NMF to the magnitude spectrum as a highly novel concept for separating multiple "effects." However, decomposing frequency magnitude spectra using NMF is a foundational technique in signal processing and audio source separation (e.g., Smaragdis & Brown, 2003). While TSF presents distinct challenges, treating this magnitude factorization as entirely novel ignores a massive body of directly analogous work. Furthermore, the paper dismisses standard NMF's ability to generalize to unseen data (Section 2.1), completely ignoring well-established out-of-sample extension techniques in the literature, such as "folding-in" (keeping the learned dictionary fixed and solving only for the activations of new data).

**3. Contextualization Against Traditional Spectral Leakage Mitigation**
The abstract and introduction heavily promote a "mathematical mechanism" that solves frequency leakage by allowing flexible selection of frequency levels. While promising, the novelty of this mechanism cannot be evaluated in a vacuum. It must be explicitly compared against traditional, widely used spectral leakage mitigation strategies such as windowing functions (e.g., Hann, Hamming), zero-padding, or fractional Fourier transforms. 

**4. Broad Dismissal of Prior TSF Literature**
The paper engages in slight claim inflation by stating that "existing TSF models cannot effectively learn interpretable multi-effect decomposition." This broadly dismisses substantial prior work, such as N-BEATS and its variants, which are explicitly designed for interpretable trend and seasonality decomposition. Additionally, grouping all prior frequency-domain models (like FEDformer or FreTS) merely as uninterpretable complex-value transformations glosses over their specific mechanisms for isolating periodicities.

## Technical Soundness
The conceptual framing of multi-effect decomposition via the frequency magnitude spectrum is logically sound, and the authors accurately diagnose the limitations of temporal moving-average filters (e.g., boundary effects, noise sensitivity). However, the mathematical formalism in Section 3 contains several notational inconsistencies that impact clarity and rigor:
- **Dimensional Mismatches**: In Equation (1), $R^i \in \mathbb{R}^K$ and $B^i \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times T}$. For the product $R^i B^i$ to be valid and result in a shape matching $X^i$, $R^i$ must be explicitly defined as a $1 \times K$ row vector. 
- **Implicit Broadcasting**: In Equation (4), $Z^i = W^i \odot P^i$ uses a Hadamard product between a vector $W^i \in \mathbb{R}^V$ and a matrix $P^i \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times T}$. While broadcasting is common in programming, mathematical notation requires more rigor (e.g., writing this as $\text{diag}(W^i)P^i$).
- **Missing Transpose**: In Section 3.1, the decomposed output $\dot{X}_i = [X_{im}, X_{ir}, Z_i]$ is stated to be in $\mathbb{R}^{T \times (V+2)}$. Because $X_{im}$ and $X_{ir}$ have shape $T$ (or $T \times 1$) and $Z_i$ has shape $V \times T$, concatenating them along the feature dimension requires $Z_i$ to be transposed to $T \times V$. 

Most critically, the provided manuscript text truncates abruptly in Section 3.2 ("makes finding quality low-rank c"). Consequently, the specific formulation of the mathematical mechanism to address frequency leakage, the optimization procedure for Hyperplane-NMF, and the handling of phase information cannot be verified for soundness.
