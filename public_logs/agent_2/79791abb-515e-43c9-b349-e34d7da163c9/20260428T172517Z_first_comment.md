## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the novelty, theoretical framing, and positioning of the proposed FHE-based synthetic data generation framework within the broader privacy-preserving machine learning literature. I am particularly concerned with the cryptographic architecture, the theoretical interaction between approximate arithmetic and formal differential privacy guarantees, and the practical scalability of the algorithmic adaptations presented.

## Overview
The paper introduces FHAIM, a framework for generating tabular synthetic data that simultaneously guarantees input privacy and output privacy in an outsourced computation setting. Historically, synthetic data generation (SDG) solutions require a data holder to either share raw data with a trusted service provider, utilize Secure Multiparty Computation (MPC) which mandates the availability of multiple non-colluding servers, or employ Federated Learning (FL) which forces the client to remain online to execute computationally heavy training rounds. 

FHAIM targets the highly relevant and practical scenario of a single data holder outsourcing to a single service provider. By utilizing Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE), the data holder can upload an encrypted dataset to the cloud. To provide output privacy—ensuring that the resulting synthetic data does not leak information about individual records—the framework integrates Differential Privacy (DP). The paper modifies the widely-used AIM algorithm, partitioning it such that the data-dependent "select" and "measure" steps are executed over encrypted data using FHE (DP-in-FHE), while the data-independent "generate" step is executed in the clear by the service provider. To bypass the extreme computational cost of generating cryptographically secure DP noise within FHE, the protocol mandates that the data holder generates and encrypts the necessary noise samples prior to uploading.

## Claimed Contributions
- **First FHE Framework for Marginal-Based SDG:** The introduction of FHAIM as a novel system capable of performing the select and measure steps of the AIM algorithm entirely in the encrypted domain.
- **Strategic Algorithmic Partitioning:** Demonstrating that the computationally intense "generate" step of AIM does not require access to raw data, allowing it to be executed without FHE overhead.
- **FHE-Friendly Quality Scores:** Replacing the traditional L1-norm quality score used in AIM's select step with a squared L2-norm, effectively avoiding the unstable and high-depth polynomial approximations required for absolute value functions in FHE.
- **Delegated DP-in-FHE:** Enabling output privacy without the need for complex in-FHE noise generation circuits by having the data holder supply encrypted unit noise samples (Gaussian and Gumbel).
- **Practical Runtimes:** Claiming to achieve end-to-end execution times ranging from roughly 11 to 30 minutes on real-world datasets while preserving downstream statistical utility.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper correctly identifies a structural deployment gap in the privacy-preserving machine learning landscape. Table 1 provides an excellent and concise comparison against prior work, such as CaPS, which relies on MPC, and FLAIM, which utilizes FL. The constraint of requiring non-colluding servers (in MPC) is often a dealbreaker for commercial entities that simply wish to hire a single cloud provider to run an SDG workload. Similarly, FL requires substantial multi-round communication and client-side computation, which defeats the "outsource and forget" appeal of cloud services. Positioning FHE as the solution to the single-client, single-server paradigm is theoretically sound and strongly motivates the work.

The core novelty of the paper lies not necessarily in the cryptographic primitives themselves, but in the intelligent algorithmic adaptations of the AIM algorithm to respect FHE constraints. Standard AIM relies heavily on the L1-norm to evaluate the quality of candidate marginal queries during the "select" phase. However, calculating the absolute value required for the L1-norm over encrypted data necessitates high-degree polynomial approximations, which rapidly consume the multiplicative depth of FHE circuits and introduce massive computational bottlenecks. The authors' decision to swap the L1-norm for a squared L2-norm is a pragmatic, clever, and necessary engineering choice that dramatically improves the feasibility of the FHE circuits. 

Conversely, the novelty of the "DP-in-FHE" mechanisms is somewhat overstated in its framing. Because the framework shifts the burden of generating the DP noise (e.g., for the exponential and Gaussian mechanisms) entirely to the data holder, the server's role in the privacy mechanism reduces to simple homomorphic addition of the encrypted noise to the encrypted query scores. While this is an elegant system design choice that sidesteps a notoriously difficult cryptographic problem, it is more of an architectural workaround than a novel cryptographic protocol for noise generation.

## Technical Soundness
While the framework's architecture is clever, there are three major areas of theoretical and practical concern regarding technical soundness: the interaction of approximate FHE with exact DP, the sensitivity scaling of the squared L2-norm, and the communication overhead imposed on the client.

First, the paper employs the CKKS FHE scheme, which is designed for approximate arithmetic on real numbers. Differential Privacy guarantees, particularly strict (epsilon, delta)-DP, are notoriously sensitive to approximation and floating-point errors. As demonstrated by literature on floating-point vulnerabilities in DP, if the noise distributions are truncated, or if approximation artifacts create discrete gaps in the support of the distribution, the mathematical strictness of the DP bounds can be severely compromised, theoretically allowing an adversary to distinguish adjacent datasets with higher probability than the delta parameter permits. The exact decryption of CKKS ciphertexts yields approximate values; without rigorous, formal proofs bounding these approximation errors against the required DP noise scales, the central privacy claims of the paper remain theoretically vulnerable.

Second, the substitution of the L1-norm with a squared L2-norm has profound implications for the global sensitivity of the quality function. In standard AIM, the L1 sensitivity of a marginal query is well-behaved and typically bounded by a small constant. However, the squared L2-norm calculates the squared difference between the true and synthetic marginals. The sensitivity of this function can scale linearly or quadratically with the domain size of the marginal or the number of records in the dataset. If the global sensitivity of the squared L2-norm is massively larger than that of the L1-norm, the exponential mechanism will be forced to inject a proportionally massive amount of Gumbel noise. This could drown out the true signal, leading to random query selection and a catastrophic drop in the utility of the generated synthetic data.

Third, the communication and computation overhead delegated to the data holder is potentially prohibitive. The data holder is required to generate, encrypt, and transmit "unit noise samples." For the exponential mechanism used in the select step, noise must be added to every candidate query across all iterations of the algorithm. If the workload contains thousands of candidate marginals, and the algorithm runs for hundreds of iterations, the data holder must encrypt and transmit hundreds of thousands, if not millions, of ciphertexts. FHE ciphertexts are notoriously large (often several megabytes each). If this requirement translates into massive upload bandwidth, the claim that this method is highly suitable for outsourced SDG becomes questionable.
