## Public Content

I focus on the manuscript's relationship to existing literature, the precise nature of its conceptual novelty, and the validity of its empirical claims relative to established interpretable machine learning baselines.

## Overview
This paper addresses a highly relevant and persistent gap in translational clinical machine learning: the disparity between the complex, continuous outputs of traditional predictive models and the rigid, operational constraints of bedside clinical environments. To bridge this gap, the authors propose AgentScore, a framework for the "autoformulation" of Deployable Clinical Scoring Systems. Instead of learning continuous probability regressors or even integer-weighted points systems, AgentScore strictly constrains its hypothesis space to unit-weighted, arithmetic-free, N-of-M checklists (e.g., "if 3 of these 5 conditions are met, trigger intervention"). 

To construct these checklists without sacrificing predictive power, the methodology leverages a neuro-symbolic approach: it uses Large Language Models (LLMs) to propose semantically meaningful clinical rules (encompassing temporal changes, physiological ratios, and logical compositions) and subsequently filters these candidates through a deterministic, data-grounded verification-and-selection loop. The authors assert that AgentScore generates scoring systems that maintain the stringent structural requirements of clinical guidelines while achieving predictive discrimination comparable to or exceeding baseline models across eight clinical tasks (spanning MIMIC-IV and eICU) and two externally validated datasets.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Formalization of Deployability:** The authors formalize the structural, cognitive, and operational constraints of bedside guidelines (Definition 2.1) and systematically define a rich Rule Language (classes i-viii) capable of handling physiological thresholds, ratios, and temporal logic.
- **Taxonomy of Interpretable Clinical ML:** The paper effectively maps the current landscape of interpretable scoring (Table 2), clearly delineating the architectural and optimization differences between AgentScore and prior art such as CORELS, AutoScore, RiskSLIM, and FasterRisk.
- **LLM-Guided Combinatorial Optimization:** The paper proposes combining the semantic priors of LLMs (to navigate the combinatorial explosion of feature construction) with a deterministic, data-grounded loop to guarantee statistical validity.
- **Empirical Superiority:** The abstract and introduction claim strong performance across eight clinical tasks and two external tasks, purportedly outperforming existing interpretable score-generation methods.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual framing of this paper is exceptionally strong. Table 2 provides a masterful summary of the interpretable scoring literature, accurately classifying established methods by their score forms, weight constraints, and optimization strategies. 

The primary novelty lies in treating operational deployability not as an afterthought or a post-hoc rounding procedure, but as a hard, non-negotiable modeling primitive. Existing methods fall short of strict bedside utility in distinct ways: methods like CORELS learn sequential rule lists (which can suffer from high variance and cognitive overload if deep), while MINLP (Mixed-Integer Non-Linear Programming) approaches like RiskSLIM and FasterRisk learn integer-weighted linear models that still require bedside mental arithmetic. 

Furthermore, a major limitation of prior work is the reliance on a fixed, manually engineered set of binary input features. Algorithms like RiskSLIM focus exclusively on optimizing the subset selection and integer weights; they do not construct the features natively. By introducing LLMs to act as a semantic feature proposal engine—generating complex derived variables such as physiological ratios (e.g., PaO2/FiO2) or temporal trajectories—AgentScore actively constructs the feature space. 

However, from a novelty standpoint, using LLMs to propose semantic features or rules for tabular data has been explored recently in general automated feature engineering frameworks (e.g., CAAFE). While applying this paradigm to the rigid unit-weighted checklist constraints of clinical scoring is a valuable specialization, the authors should explicitly acknowledge the broader literature on LLM-assisted tabular feature engineering to properly situate their contribution.

## Technical Soundness
While the problem formulation is theoretically elegant, the technical execution raises several critical questions regarding optimization and computational complexity. 

The paper establishes in Equation 1 that selecting an optimal subset of rules from a candidate dictionary to form a checklist is an NP-hard subset selection problem. In existing literature, this is typically solved using specialized branch-and-bound algorithms, cutting-plane methods, or greedy approximations. The provided text frequently references a "deterministic, data-grounded verification-and-selection loop," but it remains entirely opaque on how this loop computationally scales. If the LLM generates hundreds of complex derived rules ($\mathcal{R}$), evaluating the combinatorial combinations of unit-weighted scores is non-trivial. Does the algorithm employ a greedy heuristic, an Integer Linear Program (ILP), or an exhaustive search bounded by a parsimony penalty? Without mathematical clarity on the specific solver or heuristic used to optimize Equation 1, the technical soundness of the search strategy cannot be fully verified.

Additionally, the mathematical constraint of unit-weights naturally sacrifices expressivity compared to integer weights. An integer-weighted model strictly subsumes a unit-weighted model. Consequently, any performance parity or superiority AgentScore achieves over integer-weighted baselines cannot originate from the optimization formulation itself; it must be driven entirely by the superiority of the LLM-derived candidate rules. 

## Experimental Rigor
The reliance on LLMs introduces substantial risks regarding dataset contamination and baseline fairness, which must be rigorously audited.

**1. Baseline Fairness and Feature Space Confounding:**
The paper claims to outperform methods like RiskSLIM, AutoScore, and CORELS. However, a severe confounding factor arises if the experimental design conflates the *feature engineering* phase with the *score learning* phase. As noted in the novelty section, RiskSLIM does not natively construct physiological ratios or temporal rules; it requires them as input. If the empirical evaluation restricts RiskSLIM to the raw, un-engineered clinical variables, but grants AgentScore access to the highly predictive, LLM-generated semantic rules, the comparison is fundamentally unfair. To prove the efficacy of the "verification-and-selection loop" as an algorithm, the baselines (RiskSLIM, FasterRisk) must be evaluated on the exact same expanded dictionary of LLM-generated semantic candidate rules. Otherwise, the paper only proves that LLM feature engineering improves model performance, not that the AgentScore subset-selection algorithm is superior.

**2. Benchmark Contamination and "Discovery" Claims:**
The tasks evaluated heavily rely on MIMIC-IV and eICU. These datasets, along with their associated clinical prediction tasks (e.g., mortality, sepsis, AKI), have been published extensively and are thoroughly represented in the pre-training corpora of modern LLMs (such as GPT-4 or Llama-3). There is a significant risk that the LLM is not dynamically "discovering" rules from the data distribution, but is instead reciting established clinical guidelines or published predictive features memorized during pre-training. To substantiate the claim of "autoformulation," the evaluation must demonstrate that the method works on novel or synthetic datasets where the LLM's medical priors are uninformative, or at least provide ablations comparing the LLM's proposals against random or purely heuristic grammar-based generation.

**3. Privacy and Data Use Agreements:**
The methodology integrates clinical data with an LLM for data-grounded verification. The authors must explicitly state what information is transmitted to the LLM API. If individual patient trajectories or raw records from restricted datasets (MIMIC-IV, eICU, UK CF Registry) are sent to external commercial APIs, this would violate standard Data Use Agreements (DUAs) and pose a severe ethical and policy flaw.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Confounded Baseline Comparisons:** The high likelihood that baselines were evaluated on raw variables while AgentScore utilized an LLM-enriched feature space, muddying the source of the performance gains.
- **Missing Optimization Details:** The absence of concrete algorithmic and mathematical details on how the deterministic verification loop solves the NP-hard subset selection problem formulated in Equation 1.
- **LLM Memorization vs. Discovery:** The unaddressed risk that the LLM is simply reciting pre-trained clinical guidelines from the MIMIC-IV and eICU literature rather than algorithms dynamically mining features.
- **Reproducibility and Stochasticity:** The lack of transparency regarding LLM sensitivity, generation temperature, and the specific prompts utilized to navigate the combinatorial rule space. 

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To definitively secure an accept decision, the full manuscript should contain the following evidence:
1. **Fair Baseline Experiments:** Results showing the performance of RiskSLIM, AutoScore, and FasterRisk when provided with the identical set of candidate rules ($\mathcal{R}$) generated by the LLM for AgentScore. This isolates the effectiveness of the selection algorithm.
2. **Algorithmic Complexity Details:** Explicit mathematical details of the deterministic search algorithm, including bounds, convergence, or the specific heuristic utilized to filter the LLM proposals.
3. **LLM Ablations:** An analysis of the stability of the generated checklists when substituting the underlying LLM (e.g., switching from a frontier proprietary model to a smaller open-weights model) or varying the prompt structure.
4. **Data Privacy Assurances:** Explicit confirmation that no individual-level restricted data was transmitted to external LLM APIs, and that the methodology complies with standard hospital DUAs.
