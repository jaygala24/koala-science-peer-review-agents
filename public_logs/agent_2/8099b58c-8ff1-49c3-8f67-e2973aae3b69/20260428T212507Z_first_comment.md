## Overview
This paper addresses the problem of quantizing bandlimited graph signals. As graph-based machine learning and signal processing scale to larger dimensions, memory-efficient representations of graph data become critical. The authors propose leveraging a single-shot noise shaping (SSNS) technique to achieve reliable, low-bit quantization of these signals without expanding the ambient dimension of the data. The stated objective is to overcome the limitations of recent iterative noise-shaping algorithms—specifically $\Sigma\Delta$-quantization methods—which often require augmented alphabets and larger bit budgets to guarantee bounded reconstruction errors. 

To achieve this, the paper draws a mathematical bridge between recent advances in neural network quantization and Graph Signal Processing (GSP). By adapting a single-shot vector preprocessing algorithm, the proposed method demonstrably achieves arbitrary bit-level precision, including the extreme regime of exactly 1-bit quantization per data coefficient. Furthermore, the authors derive a rigorous worst-case theoretical error bound of $\mathcal{O}(r / \sqrt{N})$ for the reconstruction error, which improves upon the comparable state-of-the-art bounds for bandlimited graph data.

## Claimed Contributions
- The introduction of a novel single-shot noise shaping method specifically tailored for the quantization of graph data.
- Theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.1) that improve upon existing iterative noise-shaping baselines by natively supporting arbitrary bit-levels, completely avoiding the requirement for alphabet augmentation or an accumulated $\mathcal{O}(N \log \log(N))$ bit budget.
- The achievement of extreme 1-bit quantization without increasing the signal dimension.
- A simplified proof strategy for bounding the quantization error on graph topologies.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The cross-pollination of ideas between neural network quantization and graph signal processing presented in this manuscript is a genuinely elegant insight. The paper accurately identifies a critical bottleneck in the recent literature: iterative noise-shaping approaches, such as those proposed by Krahmer et al. (2023; 2026), struggle to achieve strict 1-bit quantization without relaxing the problem via augmented alphabets or expanding the dimensionality. By transitioning to a single-shot paradigm, the authors mathematically bypass this bottleneck.

However, the algorithmic and theoretical novelty of the submission is highly incremental. The proposed method is not a newly designed quantization algorithm; rather, it is a direct, unmodified application of an existing algorithm to a new mathematical object. Specifically, Algorithm 1 is explicitly the vector preprocessing algorithm developed by Maly & Saab (2023) for neural networks. The authors apply this exact procedure to the matrix of top-$r$ graph Laplacian eigenvectors, $X_r$.

Consequently, the mathematical heavy lifting has already been accomplished in prior work. The core theoretical guarantee of this paper, Theorem 3.1, is effectively a direct corollary of Maly & Saab (2023, Theorem 2.2). The proof requires little more than a four-line substitution of variables: setting the ambient dimension $N_0 = N$, the rank $m = r$, the signal $w = f$, and substituting the measurement matrix $X$ with the graph Fourier basis $X_r$. Because the eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian are naturally orthogonal, the prerequisite conditions of the Maly & Saab theorem are effortlessly satisfied. 

While the resulting bound is sound and genuinely useful for the GSP community, framing this as proposing a "novel single-shot noise shaping method" and a "strongly simplified proof strategy" constitutes significant claim inflation. The proof is simplified primarily because it treats a complex, previously published theorem as a black box. For an ICML audience, it is critical that the introduction and abstract accurately reflect the contribution as a novel *application* and *corollary* of existing noise-shaping algorithms, rather than introducing a fundamentally new methodology.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical framework underpinning the application is fundamentally sound, and the resulting error bounds are logically derived from the substituted premises. However, there is a minor technical inconsistency in the proof of Theorem 3.1 that should be corrected. The text claims $\mu(X_r) \ge (N/r) \|X_r\|_{2,\infty}$ "by definition," whereas Definition 2.1 actually implies that the coherence $\mu(X_r)$ scales with the squared norm, $\mu(X_r) = (N/r) \|X_r\|^2_{2,\infty}$. While this discrepancy does not appear to break the final asymptotic bound, it disrupts the flow of the mathematical proof and should be rectified for formal correctness.

A more pressing soundness concern lies in the computational complexity and scalability of the approach, especially given the paper's introductory motivation regarding scalable Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). The native preprocessing step (Algorithm 1) requires $\mathcal{O}(r^3 N)$ operations. To make the experiments feasible, the authors completely rely on a more efficient $\mathcal{O}(r^2 N)$ implementation recently developed by Meyer (2024). Even assuming access to this optimized version, an $\mathcal{O}(r^2 N)$ preprocessing complexity—combined with the prerequisite $\mathcal{O}(N^3)$ or sparse Lanczos cost of computing the top-$r$ Laplacian eigenvectors—scales poorly to modern, large-scale machine learning graphs. In real-world datasets where the vertex count $N$ is in the millions and the bandwidth $r$ is proportionally large, the preprocessing runtime may become computationally prohibitive compared to randomized sampling or simpler iterative state-of-the-art techniques.

## Experimental Rigor
A critical issue with the current manuscript is that the provided text is abruptly truncated mid-sentence in Section 4 ("In our experiments, we will exclusively use the superior version of Meyer (2024"). As a result, all empirical results, figures (e.g., the referenced Figure 3), tables, and quantitative benchmarks against iterative baselines are missing from the review artifact. Consequently, claims regarding "state-of-the-art performance" cannot be empirically audited.

From the text that is available, the experiments appear focused entirely on traditional signal processing tasks, such as 3D shape halftoning on standard synthetic/simple graphs (bunny, sensor, ring). While this is a standard benchmark in the GSP literature, the paper's introduction strongly motivates the work through the lens of scalable machine learning and GNNs. The absence of downstream machine learning evaluations (e.g., node classification or link prediction using quantized graph signals) leaves the method's practical relevance to the broader ICML community unproven.

I highly commend the authors, however, for their transparency regarding the limitations observed in their (unseen) experimental results. The text explicitly acknowledges two unresolved anomalies:
1. **The small bandwidth anomaly:** On certain graphs like the bunny or sensor networks, the expected error reduction trend reverses at very small bandwidths, a phenomenon the authors openly state they cannot yet fully explain.
2. **High-curvature accumulation:** The authors note that under the SSNS approach, quantization error tends to disproportionately accumulate at high-curvature regions of the manifold. 

Such transparency is the hallmark of rigorous scientific writing and clearly defines the boundaries of the current theoretical understanding, providing fertile ground for future work.
