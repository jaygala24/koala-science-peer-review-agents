- critical_reviewer: P=0.15, confidence=0.95.
  Fatal-flaw candidate: The Fourier reconstruction experiment (Figure 2) is conceptually invalid and reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of the role of positional embeddings, invalidating a central pillar of the paper's motivation.
  Concern: Fundamental conceptual error regarding positional encoding vs. image representation: The core motivation and visualization in Figures 1 and 2 conflate the frequency of the position embedding with the spatial frequency of the input image itself. Masking an image's 2D FFT using RoPE frequencies and applying an inverse-FFT is physically meaningless. RoPE frequencies dictate the oscillation of attention logits over relative token distances; they do not serve as Fourier basis functions to reconstruct pixel intensities. The claim that Axial RoPE 'fails to faithfully reconstruct input image structures such as circles' represents a deep misunderstanding of how Transformers process images, as the visual features (e.g., circles) are captured by the patch embeddings and value vectors, not the positional encodings.
  Concern: Disruption of RoPE's geometric frequency progression: The authors claim 'no loss in multi-scale encoding capacity', which is mathematically false for any specific axis. By dividing the embedding into K=8 groups, the number of frequencies explicitly assigned to the x-axis (0 degrees) drops from d/4 to d/16. To compensate, a displacement along the x-axis will trigger rotations in the oblique groups, resulting in effective frequencies scaled by irrational projections (e.g., $\theta_j \cos(22.5^\circ)$). Mixing the base geometric progression with these projection factors severely disrupts the carefully crafted exponential frequency decay of RoPE. The paper fails to analyze how this tangled frequency set affects the relative attention decay curve (the expected dot product) and its extrapolation properties.
  Strength: Identifies a valid structural limitation in standard Axial 2D RoPE, specifically its restriction to axis-aligned positional encoding, which limits its ability to model diagonal spatial relationships.
