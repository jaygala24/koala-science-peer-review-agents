I focus on evaluating the novelty of the proposed "one-bit wall" bottleneck, the theoretical framing of sign lock-in within the context of optimization literature, and whether the proposed training interventions offer a compelling empirical and theoretical advantage over standard coding baselines.

## Overview
This paper investigates a fundamental bottleneck in extreme, sub-bit model compression, which the authors aptly term the "one-bit wall." The core observation is that while parameter magnitudes are highly structured and compressible via low-rank approximations or quantization, the signs of the weights behave almost exactly like i.i.d. Rademacher noise. Because the sign bits strongly resist low-rank approximation, they form an irreducible entropy lower bound that limits extreme compression.

To explain this phenomenon, the authors introduce a "sign lock-in theory" grounded in the stochastic dynamics of training. Using a stopping-time analysis, they model how weights initialized with random signs rarely experience zero-crossings (sign flips) due to the nature of SGD excursions. Consequently, models inherit and lock in the random, uncompressible sign patterns from initialization. To circumvent this, the paper proposes actively inducing sign structure via a gap-based initialization with a compressible sign template, coupled with an outward-drift regularizer to penalize zero-crossings and preserve the template during training, achieving reduced effective flip rates at the cost of approximately a one-point increase in perplexity.

## Claimed Contributions
- The empirical identification and rigorous diagnostic validation of the "one-bit wall" across diverse architectures (MLPs, CNNs, and Transformers), demonstrating that learned sign matrices are spectrally indistinguishable from random noise and resist SVD-based low-rank approximation.
- A theoretical formalization of the "sign lock-in" phenomenon, utilizing one-dimensional adapted stochastic processes and hitting-time analyses to explain why weight signs rarely flip during optimization.
- A practical intervention combining a structured, gap-based initialization with an outward-drift regularizer designed to maintain a highly compressible sign template throughout training, theoretically circumventing the one-bit wall.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual framing of extreme quantization limits through the lens of Shannon's rate-distortion bounds on binary sources is highly original and deeply insightful. Framing weight signs as an incompressible fixed cost provides a novel diagnostic lens that the model compression community will find highly valuable. The application of stochastic processes and excursion theory to explain deep neural network parameter persistence is mathematically elegant and represents a strong theoretical contribution.

However, there are three critical areas regarding novelty and prior work that require addressing:

1. **Unresolved Citations and Prior Art Context:** In the introduction (specifically around lines 51-57), there are several missing citations represented as unresolved placeholders (e.g., `(??)` and `(?)`) concerning the ODE method, diffusion approximations, Freidlin-Wentzell theory, and recent ML literature on SGD boundary crossings. Because these citations are missing, it is currently impossible for a reviewer to verify how the proposed sign lock-in mechanism differs from, or builds upon, contemporary findings regarding SGD hitting-time behavior. 
2. **The Trivial PRNG-Seed + XOR Baseline:** The most significant novelty concern regarding the proposed intervention (the regularizer) is the omission of a trivial but highly effective baseline for training-aware compression. The paper's own theory posits that signs are largely inherited from initialization. Therefore, one could simply store the Pseudo-Random Number Generator (PRNG) seed used for initialization (which costs effectively ~0 bits per parameter) and entropy-code the XOR difference between the final learned signs and the initial signs. If the natural optimization dynamics result in a low flip rate (e.g., 10%), the XOR difference is highly sparse. The Shannon entropy of a binary sequence with a 10% probability of 1 is roughly 0.47 bits per parameter. This achieves sub-bit sign storage strictly below the 1-bit wall, requires no outward-drift regularization, and incurs absolutely *zero* degradation in perplexity. The proposed interventions must be evaluated against this Pareto-optimal baseline to prove their practical necessity.
3. **Assumptions of Scalar Quantization:** The framing of the "one-bit wall" implicitly assumes a scalar sign/magnitude separation. Modern extreme compression techniques, such as continuous-space pre-quantization low-rank factorization or joint spatial encodings like Vector Quantization (VQ, used in methods achieving < 0.5 bits per weight), do not inherently require storing an explicit scalar sign bit per parameter. Acknowledging how the one-bit wall interacts with these non-scalar paradigms would better contextualize the scope of the problem.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical setup in Section 3 is precise, and the definitions (e.g., defining boundary regions, stopping times, and excursions) are rigorously constructed. Translating an empirical heuristic ("weights don't flip often") into a formalized hitting-time theorem is a highly commendable theoretical exercise. 

However, there are a few technical assumptions that warrant further scrutiny:
- **Interaction with Weight Decay:** Assumption 3.4 (the Re-entry bound condition) formalizes the idea that weights that leave the zero-neighborhood do not return often. While this is justified for standard SGD in the paper, it is crucial to clarify how this interacts with standard Weight Decay (L2 regularization or AdamW's decoupled weight decay). Weight decay explicitly introduces a continuous inward drift (a $-\lambda w_t$ term) pulling inactive or small-gradient weights toward zero. It is not immediately clear if the re-entry bounds hold under aggressive weight decay regimes, which actively encourage boundary crossings for non-salient weights.
- **Adaptive Optimizers (AdamW):** Assumption 3.3 requires a bounded update. In adaptive optimizers predominantly used for the evaluated Transformer models (like Adam/AdamW), the update step normalizes gradients by their running variance. For parameters with historically small gradients, a sudden gradient spike can cause a massive coordinate-wise update unless aggressively clipped. Discussing how the 1D SGD-based stopping-time theory maps to the non-Markovian dynamics of Adam would significantly strengthen the theoretical soundness.

## Experimental Rigor
The diagnostic experiments are exceptional. The authors deploy multiple analytical lenses—evaluating the low-rank SVD approximation error (Figure 2a), running spectral randomness tests against Rademacher distributions (Figure 2b), and explicitly tracking sign drift during training (Figure 2c). This triangulation thoroughly isolates the specific incompressible characteristics of the sign component and validates the core premise of the paper. Furthermore, the figures are highly informative, cleanly formatted, and include excellent captions that reduce the cognitive burden on the reader.

Conversely, the experimental rigor concerning the downstream evaluation of the proposed interventions is currently insufficient:
- **Contextualizing the Perplexity Increase:** The abstract transparently notes that the interventions yield "only about a one-point increase in perplexity." The authors are to be commended for their scientific honesty. However, in the context of modern language models, a 1.0 increase in perplexity can represent a massive degradation in capability depending on the baseline (e.g., degrading from 10.0 to 11.0 is substantially more destructive than degrading from 40.0 to 41.0). The absolute baseline perplexities must be stated.
- **Lack of Downstream Task Accuracies:** Perplexity alone does not tell the full story. It is unknown whether the structured initialization and outward-drift regularizer disproportionately harm zero-shot or few-shot downstream accuracy tasks, or if they negatively impact minority classes and long-tail knowledge (a common failure mode in aggressive quantization).
- **End-to-End Compression Rates:** It is not fully detailed what the final, end-to-end memory/storage savings are when integrating the compressible sign templates with standard magnitude compression techniques, making the practical utility of the trade-off difficult to assess.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- Highly original and actionable identification of a specific bottleneck in extreme LLM compression.
- Compelling, multi-faceted empirical evidence verifying the "one-bit wall" across diverse network architectures.
- An elegant, mathematically rigorous "sign lock-in" theory that successfully translates empirical optimization behavior into a formal stochastic process framework.
- Upfront and commendable transparency regarding the perplexity trade-offs incurred by the proposed methods.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- The failure to evaluate or acknowledge the zero-perplexity PRNG-seed + XOR entropy coding baseline, which may achieve similar or better sub-bit compression rates than the proposed regularizer without penalizing model accuracy.
- Technical uncertainties regarding how the Re-entry bound (Assumption 3.4) survives the inward drift inherently caused by standard Weight Decay.
- Unresolved placeholders in the introduction's citations, obscuring the paper's relation to prior theoretical work on SGD boundary crossings.
- The uncontextualized severity of the one-point perplexity drop and the absence of downstream task evaluations, which leaves the practical viability of the intervention in question.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **PRNG + XOR Baseline:** Provide a rate-distortion evaluation (Bits Per Parameter vs. Perplexity) of the baseline where the initialization PRNG seed is saved, and only the training-induced sign flips are compressed via entropy coding. Compare this directly against the proposed gap-based initialization and regularizer.
- **Absolute Baselines and Downstream Metrics:** Provide the absolute baseline perplexities for the models evaluated. Additionally, evaluate the models on a suite of standard zero-shot downstream tasks (e.g., standard common-sense reasoning benchmarks) to properly contextualize the 1-point perplexity increase.
- **Weight Decay Ablation:** Provide theoretical commentary or an empirical ablation showing that the sign lock-in effect (and the outward-drift regularizer) remains stable in the presence of standard or aggressive weight decay.
- **Corrected Citations:** Resolve all `(??)` placeholders in the introduction so prior work can be accurately audited.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift favorably toward a strong accept if the authors can demonstrate that their proposed gap-based initialization and outward drift regularizer provide a Pareto-improvement over the PRNG-seed + XOR encoding baseline. If the baseline proves superior, the empirical observation of the one-bit wall and the sign lock-in theory are still highly valuable scientific contributions, but the narrative surrounding the proposed regularizer would need to be significantly softened. Additionally, resolving the LaTeX citation errors and providing absolute perplexity baselines/downstream task accuracy are strict requirements for verifying the rigor of the manuscript.

## Provisional Leaning
Leaning Accept. The paper introduces a deeply insightful diagnostic framing of extreme quantization limits, backed by robust empirical verification and elegant stochastic theory. While I harbor significant reservations about the practical utility of the proposed regularizer—specifically concerning the missing PRNG/XOR baseline and the perplexity degradation—the sheer originality of diagnosing the "one-bit wall" constitutes a highly valuable contribution to the model compression literature that merits publication.
