## Public Content

## Overview
The authors propose a Neural Network Parameterized Ising Machine (NPIM), aiming to solve NP-hard combinatorial optimization tasks—specifically, the Max-Cut and general Ising problems. They achieve this by algorithmically unrolling a dynamical continuous Ising machine, replacing fixed analytical update steps with parameterized node-wise local update rules governed by a lightweight, time-varying Multilayer Perceptron (MLP). Rather than allowing the MLP weights to vary arbitrarily at each unrolled step, the weights follow a temporal Fourier-basis schedule. This formulation is designed to mimic, and ideally improve upon, the time-varying annealing schedules of classical physics-inspired Ising heuristics. 

Because unrolling thousands of steps of a dynamical system frequently causes vanishing or exploding gradients under Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT), and because attributing rewards to specific actions over long horizons creates noisy policy gradients in Reinforcement Learning, the authors bypass gradient-based training entirely. Instead, they opt for a zeroth-order evolutionary optimization strategy to train the unrolled temporal weights.

## Claimed Contributions
- Bridging unrolled algorithm design (traditionally utilized in convex sparse coding) with continuous-time, physics-inspired dynamical systems for NP-hard combinatorial optimization.
- Designing an extremely parameter-efficient MLP mapped over a temporal Fourier basis to flexibly model time-varying local interaction rules while enforcing smoothness over the optimization trajectory.
- Introducing a training paradigm that completely avoids recurrent gradient instability through the application of zeroth-order evolutionary optimization.
- Claiming competitive solution quality and inference time compared to both existing heavy learning-based neural combinatorial models and strong classical Ising machine heuristics.
- Claiming that the learned dynamics successfully recover algorithmic structures characteristic of successful discrete solvers, explicitly highlighting the discovery of momentum-like behavior.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The most significant strength of this manuscript is its ability to thoughtfully bridge largely disjoint literatures. The connection between algorithm unrolling—most famous in the context of the Learned Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm (LISTA) for convex optimization—and highly non-convex, physics-inspired combinatorial algorithms is a genuinely creative step. It shifts the neural combinatorial optimization paradigm away from massive, parameter-heavy Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), transformers, or diffusion models, offering a path toward lightweight, physically grounded dynamical rules that are cheap to execute at inference time.

However, the positioning against the deep learning literature requires recalibration, particularly regarding the justification for zeroth-order optimization. The authors assert that backpropagation is infeasible for long trajectory dynamics due to instability and uninformative gradients. While this is entirely true for naive BPTT, the literature section overlooks crucial context on how the machine learning community typically handles continuous-time dynamical systems over long horizons. Specifically, Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (Neural ODEs) and adjoint sensitivity analysis were designed explicitly to bypass the memory and instability bottlenecks of unrolling. Adjoint methods compute gradients continuously backwards in time by solving a secondary ODE, operating with constant memory and avoiding discrete unrolling instabilities. A novelty argument centered on the failure of backpropagation is incomplete without explaining why adjoint methods are either unsuited, theoretically incompatible, or empirically inferior to zeroth-order optimization for the NPIM continuous relaxation.

Similarly, the dismissal of standard recurrent network mitigations feels slightly inflated. Truncated Backpropagation Through Time (TBPTT), gradient clipping, and implicit differentiation are standard methods for stabilizing recurrent network gradients that are not adequately discussed. Furthermore, the critique of Reinforcement Learning failures largely targets the noisy reward attribution of REINFORCE; modern implementations for long-horizon continuous planning often leverage Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) or Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), which should be acknowledged as the baseline standard for comparison.

Finally, the related work would benefit from discussing native stochastic neural network approaches to the Ising problem. Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBMs) are literally neural networks parameterized as Ising models, natively operating on an energy landscape equivalent to Max-Cut. Framing NPIM’s conceptual advantages—specifically its unrolled temporal dynamics—against classical RBM sampling or recent hardware-accelerated energy-based models would firmly anchor the novelty of the proposed dynamic approach.

## Technical Soundness
The architectural choices within the model are mathematically sound and thoughtfully derived from the physics of the Ising Hamiltonian. By enforcing spin-flip symmetry directly into the network—specifically by omitting biases to guarantee odd functions—the authors demonstrate a strong, domain-specific inductive bias that likely aids zero-shot generalization across diverse graph topologies.

Parameterizing the temporal weights of the MLP via a Fourier basis is an elegant formulation. The Fourier basis essentially acts as a low-frequency inductive bias. By restricting the temporal schedule to a summation over low-frequency sines and cosines, the model naturally enforces a smooth schedule over time, preventing the learned dynamics from devolving into chaotic, high-frequency oscillations at each unrolled step. This mimics the smooth annealing schedules found in simulated bifurcation machines and quantum annealers.

However, the choice to rely strictly on zeroth-order optimization introduces fundamental capacity bottlenecks that are not fully analyzed. Zeroth-order search (such as Evolutionary Strategies) estimates gradients by perturbing the parameter vector in random directions. The variance of this gradient estimator scales linearly with the dimensionality of the parameter space. While the MLP here is lightweight, the total parameter count depends multiplicatively on the hidden layer dimension and the number of Fourier basis modes. Even a moderate increase in capacity to handle complex, dense problem distributions risks pushing the model into a regime where zeroth-order search requires an exponentially scaling population size to accurately estimate the update direction. The lack of a theoretical or empirical breakdown of this precise dimensionality trade-off leaves a gap in the soundness of the scalability claims.

Additionally, there are minor notational ambiguities that must be resolved to ensure reproducibility. In the MLP update rule formulation, there is a subscript typo where the weight matrix is indexed as $W^1_{1,k}(t)$ despite being defined as a $1 \times D$ matrix; this should likely be updated to $W^1_k(t)$. The noise term $\eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ is also slightly ambiguous—the manuscript needs to explicitly state whether this noise is sampled independently per node and per time step, or if it is shared globally. Finally, the text must clarify whether the continuous variables $x_j(t)$ are used directly to compute the local coupling fields during recurrence, or if the discrete spins $\sigma_j(t) = \text{sign}(x_j(t))$ are substituted, as the latter is a common discretization step in classical simulated bifurcation.

## Experimental Rigor

To support the abstract's claim of "competitive solution quality and time-to-solution relative to recent learning-based methods and strong classical Ising-machine heuristics," the experimental section must evaluate NPIM against highly optimized classical continuous heuristics. Comparing NPIM solely against parameter-heavy GNNs or generic heuristics is insufficient. The gold standard baselines here are the Simulated Bifurcation Machine (SBM), Coherent Ising Machines (CIM), or commercial exact solvers like Gurobi evaluated at varied time-limits. 

Furthermore, the evaluation must include rigorous time-to-solution scaling analyses on standard combinatorial benchmarks (e.g., the Gset or strictly controlled random graphs of varying densities). Given that the proposed unrolled update relies on a history buffer, there may be hidden inference-time overhead compared to non-recurrent greedy algorithms or highly vectorized classical machines. 

The claim that NPIM automatically recovers "algorithmic structure, including momentum-like behavior" is phenomenological and highly specific. Experimental rigor requires explicit visualizations of the learned temporal weights, phase-space trajectory analyses, or ablation studies that mathematically demonstrate this momentum behavior. Simply observing that the model solves the problem well is not sufficient to claim that it mathematically reinvented momentum.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Conceptual Innovation:** Successfully translating algorithm unrolling into the domain of non-convex, physics-inspired combinatorial optimization is a significant and novel conceptual leap.
- **Extreme Parameter Efficiency:** By relying on a time-varying MLP rather than a massively overparameterized architecture, the method gracefully sidesteps the inference and memory bottlenecks of modern auto-regressive models or diffusion models for graphs.
- **Physical Grounding:** Building necessary symmetries directly into the model architecture shows a thoughtful integration of domain knowledge that significantly restricts the search space, enabling the zeroth-order optimizer to succeed.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Uncontextualized Optimization Claims:** The absolute rejection of gradient-based dynamical training ignores modern continuous-time deep learning solutions like Neural ODEs and adjoint sensitivity analysis. This makes the central justification for the zeroth-order approach read as slightly inflated and disconnected from adjacent machine learning subfields.
- **Scalability Limits of Zeroth-Order Training:** Because zeroth-order optimization scales poorly with parameter count, it is highly uncertain whether the NPIM framework can scale its hidden dimensions or history buffers to tackle significantly more diverse or challenging graph distributions without the training process becoming computationally intractable.
- **Verifiability of Structural Claims:** Bold claims regarding the automatic discovery of "momentum-like" behaviors risk being overly generous interpretations of a black-box temporal weight curve unless explicitly proven via rigorous state-space visualizations.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
