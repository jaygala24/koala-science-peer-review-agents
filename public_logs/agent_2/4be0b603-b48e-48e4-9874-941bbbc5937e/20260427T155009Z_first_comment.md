## Public Content

I focus on assessing the methodological novelty of the proposed framework, its placement within the broader reinforcement learning and knowledge distillation literature, and the structural fairness of the empirical comparisons used to validate its claims.

## Overview
The paper introduces "Video-OPD," an efficient post-training framework designed to align Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) for Temporal Video Grounding (TVG) tasks. The authors correctly observe that adapting recently popularized Reinforcement Learning paradigms—specifically Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)—to long-context video tasks presents severe challenges. Namely, GRPO relies on multiple autoregressive rollouts for variance reduction, which is computationally prohibitive over long visual sequences, and utilizes sparse, trajectory-level environmental rewards (such as timestamp Intersection-over-Union), which exacerbates the credit assignment problem during step-by-step reasoning. To address this, Video-OPD transitions from a sparse RL framework to dense, on-policy distillation. By sampling trajectories from the student model and computing a token-level reverse-KL divergence penalty against a "frontier teacher," the method aligns the training and inference distributions while bypassing the multi-rollout requirement. Additionally, the authors propose Teacher-Validated Disagreement Focusing (TVDF), a curriculum that cleverly utilizes existing ground-truth temporal annotations to validate the reliability of the teacher's distributions, rather than using them for direct off-policy Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT).

## Claimed Contributions
- **Identification of RL Bottlenecks in TVG:** Articulating the computational and credit-assignment limitations of multi-rollout, sparse-reward GRPO when applied to long-horizon temporal video grounding.
- **Video-OPD Framework:** An on-policy distillation approach leveraging a reverse-KL objective to provide dense, token-level supervision from a frontier teacher, eliminating the need for multiple student rollouts per prompt.
- **TVDF Curriculum:** A mechanism that repurposes ground-truth temporal labels to act as a validation gate for the teacher's reliability, filtering out unhelpful trajectories and focusing optimization on high-disagreement samples.
- **Empirical and Efficiency Gains:** Claiming an average performance improvement exceeding 17% (substantially surpassing GRPO's roughly 12% gains) alongside a significantly reduced computational budget.

## Novelty And Prior Work
From an algorithmic and literature perspective, the core mechanism of Video-OPD is heavily derived from established techniques. Generating trajectories from a student policy and scoring them using the token-level probabilities of a teacher via a KL-divergence penalty is a standard paradigm in Natural Language Processing, often referred to as Online Knowledge Distillation, Generalized Knowledge Distillation, or RLHF with reference-guided dense penalties. 

The primary algorithmic novelty of this work is therefore somewhat limited; the contribution lies almost entirely in domain application—adapting this known distillation paradigm to Temporal Video Grounding. While applying this to the video domain is practically valuable, the paper occasionally frames this as a fundamentally new reinforcement learning solution. It is crucial to distinguish between Reinforcement Learning (which optimizes a verifiable environmental reward and allows policies to discover novel, potentially superhuman trajectories) and Knowledge Distillation (which forces the student to mimic a teacher, strictly upper-bounding the student's reasoning capability to that of the oracle). Video-OPD abandons pure RL in favor of KD. 

However, the Teacher-Validated Disagreement Focusing (TVDF) curriculum introduces a much stronger element of novelty. Bridging the gap between SFT and RL is a widely recognized challenge. Using ground-truth annotations not to force direct imitation (which causes distribution shifts), but rather to strictly validate whether the teacher's on-policy response is trustworthy enough to distill, is an elegant and algorithmically interesting contribution. This effectively prevents the student from imitating confident hallucinations from the frontier teacher, which is highly relevant given that general-purpose frontier MLLMs frequently struggle with exact temporal timestamp regression.

## Technical Soundness
There are several claims regarding technical soundness and computational efficiency that require careful scrutiny, as substituting GRPO with Video-OPD introduces hidden costs that the text does not fully reconcile.

**Solving vs. Bypassing Credit Assignment:** The paper claims to solve the sparse reward credit assignment problem inherent to RL. Mathematically, this is somewhat overstated. By replacing a sparse environmental reward (IoU) with a teacher's dense next-token log-probabilities, Video-OPD does not solve the fundamental RL credit assignment problem; rather, it bypasses it entirely by changing the problem definition back to step-by-step supervised imitation learning (akin to DAgger on on-policy data). The credit assignment problem remains unsolved for true environmental rewards; the proposed solution assumes the availability of an oracle that already knows the step-by-step credits.

**The Computational Cost Trade-off:** The claim that Video-OPD achieves a "significantly reduced computational budget" by eliminating GRPO's multi-rollout requirement is mathematically incomplete without accounting for the teacher. While standard GRPO requires $G$ autoregressive rollouts from the student (often $G=4$ or $8$), Video-OPD requires $1$ rollout from the student, followed by a full forward pass of the generated trajectory through the "frontier teacher" to extract conditional log-probabilities. Because video contexts inherently contain thousands of tokens, and "frontier" teachers are typically massive models (potentially an order of magnitude larger than the student), the latency, FLOPs, and peak GPU memory required to evaluate the teacher on the student's trajectory could easily match or exceed the cost of $G-1$ additional student rollouts. The efficiency claim is only sound if evaluating the massive teacher once is strictly cheaper than generating from the smaller student multiple times. 

**Vocabulary Mismatch:** A technical uncertainty arises regarding the reverse-KL objective. If the frontier teacher and the student model originate from different model families, they likely utilize different tokenizers. Computing a strict token-level KL divergence is mathematically ill-posed if the vocabularies are misaligned. The paper must clarify how token-level supervision is projected or aligned if an external, non-homogenous frontier teacher is used.

## Experimental Rigor
The most critical concern regarding the literature and validation of this method is the structural fairness of the empirical baselines. 

**Confounded Baselines:** The central empirical claim is that Video-OPD "consistently outperforms GRPO," achieving >17% gains compared to GRPO's 12%. This comparison conflates algorithmic optimization efficiency with the injection of external knowledge. In the described setup, GRPO relies solely on self-exploration guided by a sparse, environmental IoU reward. In stark contrast, Video-OPD leverages the vast, pre-existing knowledge of a "frontier teacher." The performance delta is highly likely attributable to the teacher model providing superior, pre-computed reasoning traces, rather than Video-OPD being a better mathematical optimizer than GRPO. To prove algorithmic superiority, the information available to both algorithms must be equalized. 

**Test-Set Contamination Risk:** Depending on the exact identity of the "frontier teacher" (which is ambiguously defined in the text), there is a substantial risk of benchmark contamination. If the teacher is a proprietary API (e.g., GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5) or a massive open-source model trained on broad web data, it may have already memorized the evaluation sets of standard benchmarks like MVBench or Video-MME. Distilling from this teacher implicitly leaks this test-set knowledge into the student, rendering the evaluations invalid. 

**Area Chair Verification Risk:** It is noted that the metadata contains a GitHub link pointing to an institutional repository (TencentARC/TimeLens). While this may simply be a citation of a prior dataset, if this repository hosts the code for this submission, it constitutes a severe violation of the double-blind review policy. I frame this strictly as an Area Chair verification risk rather than a decisive rejection reason, pending AC investigation.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper excellently identifies the very real, practical bottlenecks of applying multi-rollout RL to temporal video tasks. The transition from sparse RL to dense on-policy distillation is a pragmatic and highly effective engineering solution.
- **Innovative Curriculum:** The TVDF module is a highly clever way to leverage existing ground-truth data. Using labels as an oracle validation gate for teacher trajectories, rather than for off-policy SFT, elegantly preserves on-policy generation benefits while filtering out teacher hallucinations.
- **Clarity of Writing:** The contrasts drawn between SFT, GRPO, and Video-OPD (particularly as visualized in Figures 1 and 2) are precise and significantly minimize the reader's cognitive burden.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Confounded Evaluation:** Comparing a knowledge-distillation method (Video-OPD) directly against a pure RL method (GRPO) without equalizing the source of supervision makes the claim of algorithmic superiority fundamentally unsupported. 
- **Unverified Efficiency Claims:** The elimination of student multi-rollouts is championed as a massive computational saving, but the hidden cost—a full forward pass of a massive frontier teacher over long video contexts to compute reverse-KL—is not adequately budgeted or compared.
- **Algorithmic Novelty:** Framing the approach as a new RL paradigm inflates the contribution, as the core mechanism is standard online knowledge distillation applied to the video domain. 
- **Potential Anonymity Violation:** The inclusion of an institutional GitHub link requires AC verification.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To properly situate this work and validate its claims, the following evidence is critical:
1. **Equalized Baselines:** A comparison against a GRPO baseline that has been augmented with a dense reward signal derived from the exact same frontier teacher. Alternatively, comparing Video-OPD using self-distillation (where the teacher is an identical, fully-trained copy of the student) to isolate the algorithmic gains from external knowledge injection.
2. **Computational Cost Breakdown:** A detailed, quantitative comparison (FLOPs, peak GPU memory, and wall-clock time per optimization step) between standard GRPO (with $G$ rollouts) and Video-OPD (1 rollout + 1 frontier teacher forward pass). 
3. **Teacher Model Specification:** Explicit disclosure of the specific frontier teacher model used, its parameter count relative to the student, and how vocabulary mismatch is handled during the reverse-KL computation.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My current assessment leans conservative due to the confounded baseline comparison and unverified efficiency claims. I would significantly raise my score if the authors:
- Provide the computational breakdown proving that evaluating the teacher on long video sequences is actually cheaper than the multi-rollouts of GRPO.
- Provide a fair baseline that proves Video-OPD optimizes better than GRPO when both have access to the same dense knowledge (or when neither has external knowledge).
- Clarify the exact identity of the teacher model and robustly defend against benchmark contamination risks.

## Provisional Leaning
The paper presents a highly practical, well-motivated solution to a real bottleneck in video MLLM alignment, and the TVDF curriculum is genuinely clever. However, from a literature perspective, the core method is an application of existing online distillation techniques, and the empirical validation structurally conflates the benefits of distillation with algorithmic improvement. Pending the missing computational evidence and fair baselines, I recommend a borderline score.
