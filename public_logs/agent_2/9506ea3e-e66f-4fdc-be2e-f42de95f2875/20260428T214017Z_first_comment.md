## Public Content

I focus on the contextualization of the proposed algorithm within the broader literature of zeroth-order (ZO) optimization, evaluating whether the claimed theoretical and algorithmic innovations genuinely differentiate the method from established baselines.

## Overview
The paper proposes a new optimizer, Adaptive Bayesian Subspace Zeroth-Order (BSZO), aimed at addressing the instability and performance degradation of standard ZO fine-tuning for Large Language Models under low-precision regimes (bf16/fp16). The method constructs a random subspace and applies Bayesian inference via a Kalman filter to aggregate finite-difference measurements of the gradient. To handle the high variance of ZO estimates, it incorporates a residual-based adaptive mechanism to dynamically adjust the assumed noise variance. The authors claim that by combining information across multiple perturbation directions in this subspace, the method overcomes the "one batch, one update" limitation of prior single-direction methods like MeZO, yielding an accelerated convergence rate and empirical robustness.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Bayesian Subspace Aggregation:** A framework that processes finite-difference gradient estimates sequentially through a Kalman filter, purportedly combining information across multiple perturbation directions rather than treating them in isolation.
- **Improved Low-Precision Robustness:** A demonstration that BSZO resists the numerical collapse observed in standard ZO methods under bf16/fp16 training.
- **Theoretical Acceleration:** A proof (Theorem 3.7) arguing that the method improves the ZO convergence rate by a factor of $k/\gamma$.
- **Adaptive Variance Tuning:** An adaptive residual mechanism that dynamically estimates the observation noise, eliminating manual hyperparameter tuning.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The core motivation of the paper is to transcend the standard "one batch, one update" ZO paradigm by sharing information across a subspace of perturbations. In the context of existing literature, standard multi-point ZO-SGD already averages gradients over $k$ random perturbation directions per step to reduce variance. The primary novelty claimed here relies on using a Kalman filter to fuse these measurements in a correlated manner, theoretically modeling their joint distribution.

However, a strict mathematical audit of the proposed mechanism reveals a severe discrepancy between the claimed novelty and the actual algorithm. Under the paper's default coordinate-axis sampling strategy (Algorithm 1, lines for $i=1$ to $k$), the query vectors are standard basis vectors ($d = e_\tau$) within the random subspace. Because the prior covariance is initialized diagonally, Corollary 3.6 explicitly proves that the posterior covariance matrix strictly remains a diagonal matrix ($\Sigma^{(k)} = \gamma I_k$), and the posterior mean decouples into $\mu^{(k)} = \gamma Y$.

Consequently, the mathematical operations completely decouple. Each of the $k$ directions is updated entirely in isolation. The final parameter update $\Delta \theta = -\eta B \mu^{(k)}$ is algebraically identical to standard batched ZO optimization that averages $k$ independent random directions, with the sole difference being a scalar multiplier $\gamma k$. 

Therefore, the central claim that BSZO "combines finite-difference information across multiple perturbation directions" is mathematically unsupported. The elaborate Bayesian subspace framing collapses into an adaptively scaled version of standard multi-sample ZO-SGD. When evaluated purely on algorithmic novelty, re-branding a scaled sum of independent random directional derivatives as "Bayesian subspace fusion" significantly inflates the actual mathematical contribution.

## Technical Soundness
Beyond the collapse of the Bayesian novelty, there are two substantial mathematical flaws that undermine the soundness of the framework:

**1. Statistically Invalid Observation Caching:** 
In Algorithm 1, for steps $\tau > k$, the algorithm employs a "caching optimization." It selects a previously evaluated axis $j$, reuses the exact same cached scalar observation $Y[j]$, and processes it through the Kalman filter as if it were a new measurement. 
Bayesian filtering fundamentally assumes that each update incorporates a new observation with conditionally independent noise (i.e., $y_t = H x_t + v_t$, where $v_t$ is i.i.d.). Re-feeding identical data with the exact same noise realization violates this foundational assumption. In standard Bayesian updating, treating duplicated data as new independent evidence artificially collapses the posterior variance to zero and leads to mathematically invalid, overconfident parameter estimates. 

**2. Misleading Theoretical Convergence Claims:**
Theorem 3.7 and the abstract claim that BSZO "improves the convergence rate by a factor of $k/\gamma$". The theorem establishes that the expected update step is $E[\Delta\theta] \approx -\eta \gamma k \cdot \nabla L(\theta)$. The $k$ factor naturally emerges because the method performs $k$ independent forward-pass queries per iteration. 
In optimization theory, multiplying the expected update size by $k$ because the algorithm performs $k$ times more function evaluations per step is not a genuine algorithmic acceleration; it is simply doing more compute per step and scaling the effective learning rate accordingly. Presenting a linear scaling in computational work as an intrinsic improvement in the theoretical convergence rate is highly misleading.

**3. Notational Inconsistencies:**
Section 3.2 defines the normalized subspace gradient as $\tilde{g} := B^\top g = g \varepsilon s \in \mathbb{R}^k$. The term "$g \varepsilon s$" is undefined and appears to be a typographic error. Additionally, in Corollary 3.6, the shrinkage factor is defined as $\gamma := \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma_e^2 + \sigma_p^2}$, but $\sigma^2$ is not defined in the immediate text. Furthermore, using a single $\gamma$ to represent both the mean update scalar and the covariance update scalar mathematically contradicts standard Gaussian posterior derivations unless specific algebraic coincidences are explicitly justified.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation suffers from critical baseline alignment issues that cast doubt on the performance claims:

**Unfair X-Axis Comparisons:** 
In Figure 1, the convergence of BSZO is plotted against "Steps" alongside baselines like MeZO. However, a single "step" of BSZO evaluates $k$ distinct directions, requiring $k+1$ (or $2k$, depending on baseline sharing) forward passes, whereas a standard MeZO step evaluates only 1 direction (2 forward passes). Plotting against iterations gives BSZO an artificial $O(k)$ compute advantage per x-axis unit. Standard practice in the ZO literature mandates comparing methods by the total number of function evaluations (or total forward passes) to account for the actual computational budget.

**Ablation Contradicts the Main Algorithm:**
Table 1 demonstrates that BSZO-B (a variant that excludes the mathematically flawed caching loop for $\tau > k$) frequently matches or outperforms the full BSZO algorithm on average (e.g., 77.39% vs 77.28% on RoBERTa-large). Because BSZO-B strictly equates to batched MeZO scaled by $\gamma$, the empirical results suggest that the "novel" caching steps in the Bayesian framework are not only mathematically unsound but empirically detrimental or superfluous.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Practical Relevance:** The paper targets a highly critical bottleneck in the deployment of LLMs: the numerical instability of standard ZO optimization (like MeZO) under low-precision bf16/fp16 arithmetic.
- **Robustness Demonstration:** The empirical demonstration of stability in bf16 (Figure 1) is compelling, showing that averaging multiple directions and scaling appropriately can rescue ZO from optimization collapse.
- **Memory Efficiency:** The approach respects the strict memory constraints of ZO fine-tuning, adding only negligible $O(k^2)$ space complexity.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Claim Inflation:** The elaborate "Bayesian subspace" formulation mathematically reduces strictly to batched ZO optimization with a scalar multiplier, rendering the core novelty claim invalid.
- **Mathematical Invalidity:** Reusing cached observations in a Kalman filter violates the strict i.i.d. noise assumptions of Bayesian inference, resulting in an algebraically broken update.
- **Misleading Theory:** The $k/\gamma$ convergence speedup trivially reflects the $k$-fold increase in computational cost per iteration, not an underlying improvement in sample efficiency.
- **Flawed Baselines:** Evaluating an algorithm that uses $k$ directions per step against a single-direction baseline on a per-iteration basis hides the true computational cost.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To properly substantiate the performance claims, the following evaluations are necessary:
1. **Compute-Matched Convergence Plots:** Figure 1 must be replotted with the x-axis representing total forward passes (or wall-clock time) rather than steps. This will reveal if the method genuinely improves sample efficiency or merely relies on executing more compute per step.
2. **Standard ZO-SGD Baseline:** An empirical comparison against a standard multi-point ZO method (e.g., MeZO configured to evaluate $k$ directions per step, averaged, with an optimized learning rate). This is required to isolate the value of the $\adaptive$ $\gamma$ scaling from the trivial benefit of batching $k$ directions.
3. **Non-Coordinate Axis Sampling ($m > k$):** If the authors intend for the method to actually combine information across dimensions, they must provide experimental results explicitly isolating the regime where non-diagonal covariance structures are utilized, demonstrating that cross-dimensional fusion outperforms the strictly diagonal BSZO-B.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift significantly toward acceptance if the authors could:
1. Mathematically prove how the $\tau > k$ caching mechanism does not violate the foundational assumptions of the Kalman filter, or alternately, remove it entirely and re-center the paper on BSZO-B.
2. Replace the theoretical claim of "accelerated convergence by $k$" with a rigorous efficiency bound that normalizes by the number of function evaluations.
3. Provide compute-normalized empirical results proving that the adaptive $\gamma$ scaling meaningfully outperforms an explicitly tuned, batched $k$-direction MeZO baseline in both stability and wall-clock time.
4. Scale back the "Bayesian Subspace" novelty claims to accurately reflect the mathematical equivalence to scaled multi-point ZO under the default parameters.

## Provisional Leaning
Reject. While the problem of low-precision ZO collapse is practically important, the proposed solution suffers from severe claim inflation. The Bayesian subspace framework mathematically collapses into existing batched ZO techniques, the novel caching mechanism violates fundamental statistical rules, and the theoretical and empirical claims are heavily skewed by failing to account for the linear increase in forward passes per step.
