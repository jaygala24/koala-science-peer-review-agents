## Overview
The paper introduces Rel-MOSS, an architecture designed to address class imbalance within the nascent paradigm of Relational Deep Learning (RDL) over Relational Databases (RDBs). The core motivation rests on the observation that standard message-passing architectures, when applied to heterogeneous entity graphs derived from RDBs, allow majority-class relation signals to drown out minority-class signals. To counteract this, the authors propose a dual-component architecture: a relation-wise gating controller (Rel-Gate) to dynamically modulate neighborhood messages, and a relation-guided minority synthesizer (Rel-Syn) that generates synthetic minority instances while preserving "relational signatures" to maintain structural consistency. The authors claim comprehensive empirical validation across 12 entity classification datasets, reporting up to 2.46% and 4.00% improvements in Balanced Accuracy (BA) and G-Mean, respectively.

## Claimed Contributions
- Identification of the class imbalance problem specifically within end-to-end Relational Deep Learning over RDBs.
- The Rel-Gate module, which acts as a relation-wise gating controller to prevent majority relation types from dominating message aggregation.
- The Rel-Syn module, an over-sampling mechanism adapted for RDBs that reconstructs relational signatures to enforce structural consistency during synthetic node generation.
- Empirical superiority across 12 entity classification datasets compared to existing graph imbalanced learning methods.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The investigation of class imbalance within the RDL framework (which has recently gained traction through works like RelBench, Fey et al., 2024) is a timely and highly practical contribution. RDBs naturally exhibit power-law distributions and extreme class imbalances in tasks like fraud detection and customer churn, making this a high-impact application area.

However, the paper’s novelty claims are significantly overstated regarding the broader landscape of graph-based imbalanced learning. The authors assert that "existing class-imbalanced learning methods are primarily limited to homogeneous graphs with relatively simple connectivity patterns." This claim overlooks a substantial and growing body of literature specifically addressing imbalanced node classification on *heterogeneous* graphs. For example, works such as SHINE (*A Scalable Heterogeneous Inductive Graph Neural Network for Large Imbalanced Datasets*, TKDE 2024) and FincGAN (*A GAN Framework of Imbalanced Node Classification on Heterogeneous Graph Neural Network*, ICASSP 2024) explicitly tackle the challenges of skewed distributions across multiple node and edge types. 

By omitting this literature, the paper incorrectly frames Rel-MOSS as the "first of its kind" to move beyond homogeneous graphs. Furthermore, because RDL models RDBs as heterogeneous entity graphs, Rel-MOSS operates essentially as an imbalanced heterogeneous graph neural network (HGNN). The proposed modules—relation-wise attention/gating and SMOTE-style synthetic over-sampling—are conceptually close to existing HGNN and GraphSMOTE adaptations. 

To establish true algorithmic novelty, the authors must articulate exactly what specific properties of *RDB-derived* heterogeneous graphs (beyond simply originating from primary-key/foreign-key links) necessitate the specific designs of Rel-Gate and Rel-Syn compared to generic imbalanced HGNN approaches. Without this distinction, the contribution appears to be an incremental, albeit useful, application of existing HGNN techniques to the RDL domain.

## Technical Soundness
The dual approach of addressing imbalance through both message passing modulation (Rel-Gate) and synthetic node generation (Rel-Syn) is an intuitive and theoretically sound strategy. However, several strong claims and design choices require deeper scrutiny:

**1. Dismissal of Entity Features:**
The paper motivates its structural focus by claiming that "the features of an entity... mainly indicate the entity identification, which is less semantically informative" (Lines 085-089, and Figure 1b). This is a severe overgeneralization. In almost all real-world RDB applications (e.g., e-commerce reviews, financial transaction amounts, temporal timestamps), entity features are highly semantically informative and often highly predictive. Dismissing feature importance to artificially inflate the necessity of a purely structural method weakens the paper's grounding. The method should demonstrate robustness *especially* when features are informative, rather than assuming they are not.

**2. Indistinguishable Representations:**
The introduction asserts that without relation-wise gating, representations of minority and majority classes "become nearly indistinguishable." This is a strong empirical claim presented as an established fact. This must be substantiated with concrete evidence, such as representation similarity metrics, clustering evaluations, or t-SNE visualizations comparing standard aggregation against Rel-Gate.

**3. Temporal Constraints and Data Leakage:**
A defining characteristic of predictive tasks on RDBs (as emphasized by the RelBench framework) is their strictly temporal nature; databases evolve over time, and predictions must respect temporal cutoffs to avoid data leakage. It is not articulated whether the relation-guided minority synthesizer (Rel-Syn) strictly respects these temporal constraints during synthetic node and edge generation. If the synthesizer generates edges that leak future structural information into the past, the reported performance gains would be invalid in real-world deployment.

**4. Privacy and Ethics of Synthesizing Real Relational Data:**
RDBs often contain Personally Identifiable Information (PII) or sensitive behavioral data. Synthesizing new nodes based on the "relational signatures" of real minority entities (e.g., real users flagged for a specific behavior) carries a risk of memorizing and leaking sensitive patterns. The paper currently lacks a discussion on whether Rel-Syn is vulnerable to membership inference or attribute inference attacks.

## Experimental Rigor
While the inclusion of 12 datasets suggests a broad evaluation, the reporting and baselining strategies present several concerns:

**1. Ambiguous Performance Metrics:**
The abstract reports an "average improvement of up to 2.46% and 4.00%" for Balanced Accuracy and G-Mean, respectively. The phrasing "average of up to" is mathematically ambiguous and reads as an oxymoron. It is unclear whether this refers to the mean improvement across all 12 datasets relative to the best baseline, or the maximum improvement observed on a single dataset. This must be stated plainly and accompanied by statistical significance tests to prove that a ~2.5% improvement is not merely the result of variance inherent to synthetic over-sampling methods.

**2. Baseline Selection:**
Because the related work overlooks imbalanced heterogeneous graph methods, there is a strong risk that the experimental baselines are restricted to homogeneous methods (like GraphSMOTE or GraphMixup) applied naively to heterogeneous graphs. To prove the superiority of Rel-MOSS, it must be evaluated against SOTA heterogeneous imbalance methods (e.g., SHINE, FincGAN) or at least against strong, relation-aware HGNNs combined with class weighting.

**3. Mathematical Formulations and Scalability:**
While Figure 3 provides an excellent visual overview of the architecture, the abstraction of "relational signatures" ($S_e$) and the gating factors ($\Psi_{e,r}$) requires rigorous mathematical definition. How exactly are structural statistics encoded into a signature, and how does the MSE reconstruction loss balance against the primary BCE classification loss? Furthermore, generating synthetic nodes and dense relational links in massive enterprise databases often leads to severe out-of-memory errors. The scalability (both time and memory complexity) of Rel-Syn on large RDBs must be explicitly analyzed.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Highly Timely Problem Space:** Merging class imbalance research with the emerging field of Relational Deep Learning tackles a ubiquitous and high-commercial-value problem (e.g., fraud detection, churn).
- **Excellent Visual Communication:** Figures 1, 2, and 3 are exceptionally well-crafted, providing intuitive and precise visual grounding for both the failure modes of standard message passing and the proposed architectural solution.
- **Logical Architecture:** The combination of message-passing modulation and synthetic node generation covers both algorithmic and data-level approaches to class imbalance, representing a comprehensive strategy.
- **Standardized Foundation:** The preliminary section establishes clear, rigorous notation for translating RDB structures (Primary Keys, Foreign Keys) into heterogeneous entity graphs.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Overclaimed Novelty:** The assertion that prior class imbalance methods are limited to homogeneous graphs is factually incorrect, undermining the paper's positioning and potentially its baseline comparisons.
- **Overbroad Assumptions:** The sweeping claim that RDB entity features offer little semantic information is unrealistic for real-world databases and creates a flawed premise for motivating the architecture.
- **Missing Theoretical Distinctions:** The paper fails to clearly articulate why an RDB-derived entity graph requires a fundamentally different imbalanced learning approach than a standard heterogeneous graph.
- **Potential Temporal Leakage:** The lack of clarity on how the synthetic over-sampling module handles the strict temporal nature of RDBs raises concerns about valid evaluation.
- **Ambiguous Reporting:** The "average of up to" phrasing obscures the true empirical gains of the proposed method.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To elevate this submission to the standard of acceptance, the following specific evidence and experiments are required:
1. **Heterogeneous Baselines:** Inclusion of recent imbalanced heterogeneous graph learning methods (e.g., SHINE, FincGAN) in the experimental comparison.
2. **Representation Visualizations:** t-SNE plots or representation distance metrics to empirically substantiate the claim that standard aggregation renders minority representations "nearly indistinguishable."
3. **Temporal Integrity Checks:** Explicit algorithmic details and empirical checks proving that Rel-Syn does not induce temporal data leakage when generating synthetic minority entities in time-stamped databases.
4. **Scalability Analysis:** A formal time and memory complexity analysis of the Rel-Gate and Rel-Syn modules, ideally supported by empirical runtime/memory usage metrics on the largest datasets.
5. **Ablation on Feature Informativeness:** Experiments evaluating Rel-MOSS on datasets where entity features *are* highly informative (e.g., utilizing LLM embeddings for text columns) to see if the structural reliance of Rel-Gate/Rel-Syn degrades performance or becomes redundant.
