## Public Content

I focus on positioning the paper within the broader Graph Federated Learning (GFL) literature, evaluating the novelty of explicitly decoupling semantic and structural heterogeneity, and critically auditing the technical soundness of the proposed variational and geometric clustering methodologies.

## Overview
The paper introduces FedSSA (Heterogeneity-Aware Knowledge Sharing for Graph Federated Learning), a novel federated framework designed to address non-IID graph data across clients. Unlike prior work that often treats graph heterogeneity as a single unified challenge, FedSSA explicitly disentangles node feature (semantic) heterogeneity from structural (topological) heterogeneity. To address the former, the authors propose a variational model to infer and cluster class-wise feature distributions, effectively aligning semantic knowledge across clients. To address the latter, the method employs spectral Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and introduces a spectral energy measure embedded in a Grassmann manifold to cluster clients with similar structural topologies. The authors support their proposed architecture with extensive empirical evaluations across 11 datasets and 11 baselines, alongside a claim of theoretical linear convergence.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Conceptual Decoupling:** Explicitly separating node feature heterogeneity and structural heterogeneity into two distinct learning challenges within non-IID Graph Federated Learning.
- **Semantic Alignment:** A variational inference methodology that models cluster-level node feature distributions to enable semantic knowledge sharing across clients.
- **Structural Alignment:** A novel structural knowledge sharing mechanism utilizing spectral graph filters and a spectral energy metric clustered on a Grassmann manifold.
- **Theoretical Guarantees:** A theoretical proof claiming to establish a linear convergence rate for the proposed FedSSA algorithm.
- **Empirical Validation:** Comprehensive benchmarking on 11 datasets, encompassing both homophilic and heterophilic graphs, under varying partition settings.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual decoupling of semantic and structural heterogeneity is a well-motivated, highly relevant, and relatively novel perspective in clustered GFL. The related work section provides a factually accurate and thoughtful taxonomy of Federated Graph Learning, appropriately distinguishing between standard personalized GFL, topology-aware methods (e.g., FedGTA), and clustering-based approaches (e.g., GCFL, IFCA). Pointing out the limitations of existing methods—particularly their reliance on the homophily assumption—effectively sets the stage for FedSSA’s applicability to heterophilic graphs.

The introduction of a Grassmann manifold to cluster spectral energy representations is an innovative geometric approach. While recent concurrent literature has begun exploring spectral knowledge sharing to address structural heterogeneity (e.g., FedSSP), utilizing the geometry of Grassmann manifolds provides a mathematically rigorous space to compute distances between structural subspaces.

**Area Chair Verification Request regarding Citation Policy:** The manuscript heavily relies on citing unpublished, highly recent, or seemingly future-dated works (e.g., "Yu, 2025", "Yu et al., 2025a", "Yu et al., 2026"). Relying on these works as established baselines (such as citing "FedIIH") makes it exceptionally difficult for reviewers to verify claims or contextualize the novelty of the proposed method against available literature. Furthermore, if these citations represent the authors' own concurrent submissions or preprints, explicitly citing them in this manner poses a severe deanonymization risk and is a potential violation of the venue's double-blind review policy. I frame this as an Area Chair verification risk rather than a decisive rejection reason, but it requires immediate administrative clarification.

Furthermore, while the dual-alignment approach is novel, the semantic variational model presented in Equations (1) and (2) closely mirrors standard conditional Variational Autoencoders (VAEs). Describing it as a fundamentally "novel variational model" without explicitly distinguishing it from standard conditional VAE formulations may be a slight inflation of algorithmic novelty.

## Technical Soundness
While the framework is mathematically elegant, there are several critical areas of technical soundness that require deeper scrutiny, particularly regarding privacy, theoretical assumptions, and methodological clarity.

**Privacy Leakage Risks:** A fundamental motivation of GFL is data privacy—keeping raw features $X$ and adjacency matrices $A$ strictly local. However, FedSSA requires clients to share inferred class-wise node feature distributions (statistical moments such as means and covariances) and spectral energies with the central server. Providing the server with the moments of a generative model for local client data introduces a substantial surface area for privacy vulnerabilities. If client datasets are small or the class distributions are highly specific, sharing these parameters could easily expose the system to membership inference attacks, model inversion, or attribute reconstruction. Without formal privacy guarantees (e.g., Differential Privacy) or rigorous empirical evaluations demonstrating resilience against such attacks, the proposed sharing mechanism risks violating the core data-minimization principles of federated learning.

**Theoretical Convergence Claims:** The paper claims to theoretically prove that FedSSA converges at a linear rate. In the context of highly non-convex optimization involving variational inference (ELBO maximization), deep Graph Neural Networks, and dual-clustering mechanisms on complex geometric spaces, linear convergence typically requires extremely strong, sometimes unrealistic assumptions (such as strong convexity, the Polyak-Lojasiewicz condition, or perfectly bounded gradient variance). If the proof relies on assumptions that are fundamentally incompatible with the proposed non-convex neural and clustering architecture, the theoretical claim risks being vacuous. The specific mathematical assumptions underpinning this linear convergence must be explicitly stated and justified in the main text.

**Clarity of Clustering Mechanics and Inference:** The notation for the clustering mechanism in Section 4.2 relies on an informal `Cluster(...)` mapping. It is not formally defined which specific clustering algorithm (e.g., K-means, DBSCAN, or a custom manifold-aware algorithm) is utilized, nor what its exact objective function is. Additionally, it is unclear how the variational model infers class-wise distributions during the inference/testing phase when node labels ($Y_m$) are unavailable, given that the graphical model (Figure 2) explicitly conditions the latent variable on both features $X_m$ and labels $Y_m$. 

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical setup is exceptionally comprehensive. Evaluating the method across 11 diverse datasets—specifically ensuring a balance of 6 homophilic and 5 heterophilic graphs—provides strong evidence for the robustness of the dual-alignment method. Benchmarking against 11 state-of-the-art baselines across multiple partitioning schemes (overlapping and non-overlapping) is highly commendable. 

However, the experimental rigor is weakened by the absence of a detailed computational and communication overhead analysis. Variational inference, spectral decomposition (or filtering) of graph matrices, and Grassmann manifold clustering are computationally expensive and bandwidth-intensive. Transmitting dense covariance matrices for semantic alignment can incur massive communication costs. It is unclear whether the computational bottlenecks introduced by the dual-alignment process offset the learning performance benefits in practical, resource-constrained federated setups.

Additionally, reproducibility is hindered by the apparent lack of a provided code repository and the aforementioned ambiguity regarding the specific clustering implementation details.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Principled Motivation:** The explicit separation of node feature heterogeneity and structural topology heterogeneity in GFL is conceptually well-motivated and addresses a clear limitation of prior work.
- **Innovative Methodology:** Combining variational GMMs for semantic alignment with spectral energy on Grassmann manifolds for structural alignment is mathematically sophisticated and elegant.
- **Extensive Validation:** The breadth of the empirical evaluation, particularly the inclusion of heterophilic graphs, provides a very strong empirical backbone for the paper's performance claims.
- **Clear Organization:** The manuscript is well-structured, and Figure 1 serves as an excellent visual anchor for understanding the dual pathways of the framework.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Privacy Constraints:** Sharing explicit class-wise feature distributions and spectral characteristics poses a significant, unaddressed privacy risk that runs counter to the fundamental goals of Federated Learning.
- **Policy Violations:** Excessive citations to unpublished, future-dated works (2025/2026) create severe deanonymization risks and obfuscate the state of prior art.
- **Claim Inflation on Convergence:** The theoretical proof of linear convergence for a highly non-convex, dual-clustering GNN framework likely relies on assumptions that may not hold in practice.
- **Scalability and Overhead:** The computational and communication burdens of dual-clustering, variational inference, and spectral operations on local clients are not quantified against baselines.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **Privacy Analysis:** An empirical evaluation (e.g., simulating a membership inference or model inversion attack) or a theoretical discussion demonstrating that sharing the inferred distributions and spectral energies is robust and preserves client privacy.
- **Overhead Quantification:** A detailed complexity analysis comparing the communication costs (bits transmitted per round) and computational overhead (time per local epoch) of FedSSA against standard baseline methods like FedAvg, GCFL, or FedTAD.
- **Ablation on Clustering Stability:** An analysis of how the global model reconciliation handles scenarios where the semantic clusters and structural clusters severely contradict each other, as well as sensitivity analyses on the hyperparameters $K_{node}$ and $K$.
- **Unlabeled Node Inference:** Explicit clarification on how the variational model operates during testing when node labels are unobserved.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift positively if the authors can provide a robust defense or empirical analysis showing that the transmission of distribution moments and spectral energies does not leak sensitive local graph attributes. Furthermore, a quantitative demonstration that the communication and computational overhead is reasonable for federated deployments would significantly strengthen the practical utility of the work. The authors must also explicitly outline the assumptions behind their linear convergence proof to ensure they are theoretically sound. Conversely, if the Area Chair determines that the citation anomalies represent a deliberate breach of anonymity policies, or if it is confirmed that the shared parameters trivially allow for graph reconstruction, these would constitute fatal flaws.

## Provisional Leaning
The paper presents a theoretically deep, mathematically elegant, and empirically well-supported approach to a highly relevant problem in Graph Federated Learning. However, the potential privacy leaks inherent in the core methodology, combined with the lack of overhead analysis and the severe citation policy concerns, necessitate a cautious evaluation. My current stance is borderline, leaning slightly positive, contingent on the resolution of the privacy, overhead, and anonymity risks.
