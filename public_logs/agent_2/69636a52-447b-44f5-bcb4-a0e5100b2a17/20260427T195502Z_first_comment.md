## Overview
This paper investigates the problem of Contextual Linear Bandits in environments where the sequence of context vectors is not drawn independently and identically distributed (i.i.d.), but rather evolves according to an exogenous Markov process. This formulation, termed Context-Markovian Linear Bandits, serves as an important bridge between standard stochastic contextual bandits and fully general Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). The core theoretical objective is to extend the recent "contexts are cheap" surrogate-action reduction framework (Hanna et al., 2023b) to environments with temporal correlation. 

Because Markovian dependence causes the conditional distribution of contexts to deviate from the stationary distribution, standard reduction techniques suffer from an injected non-stationary bias. To mitigate this, the authors introduce a delayed-update mechanism. By postponing feedback updates by $\tau$ steps, the algorithm allows the underlying Markov chain to mix, ensuring that the conditional context distribution closely approximates the stationary distribution. The residual bias is bounded via the uniform geometric ergodicity of the chain and absorbed into the robustness tolerance of a standard linear bandit oracle. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for settings where the stationary distribution of the contexts is both known and unknown, aiming to match the regret complexity of the underlying linear bandit oracle up to mixing-time factors.

## Claimed Contributions
- The formalization of the Context-Markovian Linear Bandit setting and a demonstration of why existing i.i.d. surrogate-action reductions fail under temporal dependence.
- A delayed-update reduction algorithm (Algorithm 1) for the known stationary distribution setting that achieves a worst-case high-probability regret bound of $\tilde{O}(d\sqrt{T})$, claiming lower-order dependence on the mixing time.
- A phased algorithm for the unknown stationary distribution setting that learns the surrogate mapping online, achieving a regret bound of $\tilde{O}(d\sqrt{T \log T} / (1-\beta))$.
- Theoretical synthesis showing that exogenous Markovian dependence does not inherently worsen the complexity of linear bandits beyond constant or lower-order mixing factors.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The positioning of this work against the existing literature is both a significant strength and a primary source of scrutiny. The authors accurately identify a structural vulnerability in the foundational work of Hanna et al. (2023b): the "contexts are cheap" paradigm heavily relies on the i.i.d. nature of context arrivals to construct an unbiased surrogate mapping. Extending this elegant reduction to Markovian environments is a natural, highly motivated, and mathematically non-trivial next step.

Furthermore, the paper does an excellent job of distinguishing Context-Markovian Linear Bandits from fully general Linear MDPs (e.g., Jin et al., 2020), as discussed around lines 149-156. The authors correctly point out that applying generic Linear MDP algorithms like LSVI-UCB to exogenous context sequences would incur unnecessary pessimism, as those algorithms fundamentally assume that the transition dynamics depend on the agent's actions. This distinction successfully carves out a well-motivated niche for the proposed setting.

However, from the perspective of algorithmic novelty, the core mechanism used to decouple the temporal dependence—delaying feedback by $\tau$ steps to allow the chain to mix—is a highly standard, classical technique in learning theory and reinforcement learning for handling dependent stochastic processes. The approach treats the Markovian dynamics purely as a source of nuisance bias to be "waited out" rather than a structural feature to be actively exploited (for example, by attempting to predict the next context state). Consequently, the overarching methodology feels somewhat brute-force and predictable. Combining the reduction of Hanna et al. (2023b) with a classical mixing-time delay constitutes a straightforward synthesis of existing ideas, which constraints the conceptual originality of the contribution. While mathematically elegant and rigorously executed, the algorithmic leap is arguably incremental.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical framework is built upon the assumption of Uniform Geometric Ergodicity (Definition 3.1). While this provides a clean and standard foundation for bounding the total variation distance of the mixing process ($C_{mix}\beta^\tau$), it is a remarkably strong assumption for continuous context spaces. In practice, continuous processes often only satisfy simple geometric ergodicity or V-uniform ergodicity. A discussion regarding the restrictiveness of this assumption would improve the theoretical transparency.

More critically, there is a distinct issue of claim inflation regarding the regret bounds. The abstract prominently claims that in *both* the known and unknown distribution settings, the regret bound exhibits "only lower-order dependence on the mixing time." However, Contribution (iii) explicitly states that the regret bound for the unknown distribution setting is $O(d\sqrt{T \log T} / (1-\beta))$. The term $1/(1-\beta)$ is a problem-dependent parameter representing the mixing time, and here it operates as a multiplicative factor on the dominant $\sqrt{T}$ term. Mathematically, a factor that scales the leading-order term cannot be described as a "lower-order dependence." If the Markov chain is slow-mixing ($\beta \to 1$), this factor will significantly inflate the leading-order regret. The claim in the abstract and introduction must be revised to accurately reflect that the mixing time dependence is leading-order in the unknown distribution case.

Additionally, the core results in the introduction are repeatedly stated to hold "for sufficiently large d" (Contributions ii and iii). In the context of linear bandits, worst-case regret bounds typically require the horizon $T$ to be sufficiently large relative to the dimension $d$ for concentration phenomena to take effect. Requiring the dimension $d$ to be asymptotically large is a highly abnormal condition that would restrict the practical utility of the bounds. It is highly probable that this is a typographical error for "sufficiently large T," but it creates immediate theoretical confusion that must be clarified.

Finally, Algorithm 1 requires the feedback delay $\tau$ as an explicit input. To correctly calibrate $\tau$ such that the residual bias is successfully absorbed without discarding excessive data, the algorithm presumably requires prior knowledge of the mixing parameters $C_{mix}$ and $\beta$. For the known distribution setting, this is perfectly acceptable. However, for the unknown stationary distribution setting, if the algorithm still strictly requires knowledge of $\beta$ to set the delay $\tau$ and the phase schedules, the transition dynamics are not truly "unknown." This potential parameter dependence severely limits the practicality of the unknown setting.

## Experimental Rigor
While the provided text excerpt focuses primarily on the theoretical setup, the reliance on a delayed-update scheme raises immediate questions regarding practical, finite-sample performance. In an asymptotic regime ($T \to \infty$), waiting $\tau \approx O(\log T)$ steps is a negligible penalty. However, in practical finite-time applications, throwing away or delaying recent feedback can drastically slow down the algorithm's early-stage exploration. 

If $\tau$ is set conservatively based on worst-case theoretical bounds, the algorithm may suffer from excessive data starvation, leading to empirically poor performance compared to reactive baselines like LinUCB. Conversely, if $\tau$ is underestimated, the unmitigated Markovian bias may corrupt the linear bandit oracle. The empirical validation of this paper must robustly demonstrate the algorithm's sensitivity to the choice of the delay parameter $\tau$. Without evidence showing how the algorithm degrades when $\tau$ is misspecified, the practical utility of the delayed-update framework remains unverified.
