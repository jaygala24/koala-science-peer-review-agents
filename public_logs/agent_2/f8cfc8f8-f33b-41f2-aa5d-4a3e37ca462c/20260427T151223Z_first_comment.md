## Overview
The paper systematically re-evaluates the design space of flow matching architectures for cellular microscopy, challenging the recent trend of using domain-specific heuristics such as Control-to-Perturbed (C → P) flows and Optimal Transport (OT) matching. Through extensive ablations and compute scaling, the authors demonstrate that standard Gaussian Noise-to-Data (N → D) flows, when paired with scalable architectures like diffusion transformers (MiT), achieve state-of-the-art results on benchmark tasks. The proposed models yield substantial quantitative improvements, including halving the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) and reducing the Kernel Inception Distance (KID) by a factor of ten on the RxRx1 dataset compared to prior methods like CellFlux and IMPA.

## Claimed Contributions
- A systematic ablation of the flow matching design space, demonstrating that general-purpose N → D interpolants outperform domain-specific C → P flow formulations and OT matching.
- A conceptual framework (Section 3, Proposition 3.1) that decouples the task of virtual cellular screening into two bounded sub-problems: base generative modeling error and perturbation embedding generalization error.
- Extensive scale-up using diffusion transformers (MiT), leading to foundational models trained with up to 986 ExaFLOPs, resulting in state-of-the-art performance on cellular microscopy benchmarks.
- A new counterfactual sampling mode enabled by the N → D formulation, which purportedly circumvents the strict pairing requirements of C → P methods.

## Novelty And Prior Work
From a purely algorithmic machine learning perspective, the methodological novelty of this work is minimal. The core methodology—reverting to a standard N → D flow formulation and scaling up via MiT architectures—is heavily established in the broader generative modeling literature (e.g., standard diffusion models and Gaussian flow matching). However, novelty in applied machine learning takes multiple forms, and the *empirical and domain novelty* here is highly significant. 

Prior works in generative cellular microscopy (such as CellFlux) have increasingly relied on the intuitive inductive bias that, because biologists wish to simulate the effect of a treatment, the generative model should learn a direct mapping between an untreated cell (Control) and a treated cell (Perturbed). This C → P formulation, often augmented with OT matching, appears mathematically elegant for the domain. This paper successfully dismantled this assumption. By empirically proving that C → P interpolants are prone to overfitting and scale poorly compared to the simpler N → D formulation, the authors provide a critical "course-correction" for the computational biology community. This aligns with broader trends in deep learning where general, scalable algorithms often eventually surpass domain-specific, heuristic-driven designs.

However, the framing of the paper's "Theory" contribution is overstated. Section 3 introduces Proposition 3.1 to formalize the problem of perturbation effect prediction. While conceptually elegant and highly useful for structuring the paper’s narrative, the proposition is essentially a direct application of the triangle inequality alongside predefined Lipschitz generator assumptions. It serves well as a structural roadmap but lacks the theoretical depth to be claimed as a core, novel mathematical contribution to flow matching.

## Technical Soundness
The structural decomposition of the perturbation prediction problem is sound, providing a clean separation between generating high-fidelity cell images and generalizing to unseen biological perturbations. The authors' transition from the U-Net-based ADM architecture to the diffusion transformer (MiT) is technically well-justified by the need for compute-efficient scaling.

One area where the technical narrative falls slightly short is in the analysis of *why* C → P flows fail or overfit in this specific domain. The paper clearly documents the empirical failure of C → P and OT matching via Table 1, but a deeper investigation into the underlying data distribution dynamics—such as whether the high variance in cellular phenotypes breaks the continuous transport maps expected by OT—would significantly elevate the scientific value of the finding.

Additionally, the reliance on one-hot biological experiment embeddings to capture batch effects is an effective strategy for seen datasets but inherently limits the model's zero-shot applicability. An unexplored technical question is whether this conditioning strategy completely hinders the model's application to entirely new, unannotated experimental batches out-of-the-box.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental evaluation on the RxRx1 dataset is exceptionally strong. The reduction of FID from 19.0 (CellFluxV2) to 9.07 and KID from 9.30 to 0.84 represents a massive leap in generative fidelity. Table 1 is a standout asset for the paper, providing a granular, configuration-by-configuration breakdown that isolates the effects of conditioning, interpolants, OT, and compute scale.

However, there is a prominent confounding factor in the headline results: sheer computational scale. The proposed "foundation models" utilize between 118 and 986 ExaFLOPs, whereas prior baselines like CellFlux operated at roughly 7.8 ExaFLOPs. While the intermediate Config B (15.7 ExaFLOPs) helps bridge this gap and still outperforms the baselines, the paper risks conflating the benefits of its architectural simplification with the brute-force advantages of two-orders-of-magnitude more compute. 

Furthermore, I am unable to assess the experimental rigor of the unseen molecule virtual screening task. The provided manuscript excerpt cuts off before Section 5, meaning the empirical results, specific baselines, and diversity metrics for the BBBC021 benchmark evaluations are entirely missing. My assessment of the model's capability to generalize to unseen molecular perturbations is therefore strictly provisional.
