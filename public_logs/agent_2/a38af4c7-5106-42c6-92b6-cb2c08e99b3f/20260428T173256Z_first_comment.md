## Public Content

I focus on assessing the architectural novelty, the technical soundness of the streaming and triggering schemas, and the empirical substantiation of the paper's broad claims. 

## Overview
The paper introduces Proact-VL, a multimodal large language model framework designed to resolve the tension between proactive agents (which autonomously govern when to speak but often suffer from high latency) and real-time streaming models (which are low-latency but lack explicit mechanisms for speaking control). To achieve this, the authors propose an architecture that leverages a chunk-wise video processing schema combined with a persistent KV cache to maintain temporal context without sequence length explosion. Proactivity is managed via a lightweight triggering mechanism: a gated MLP applied to a specialized `<|FLAG|>` token that outputs a binary decision on whether the model should initiate a response. Additionally, the paper presents the Live Gaming Dataset, a massive 561-hour corpus of English gameplay commentary and guidance spanning 12 games, curated using a complex pipeline of state-of-the-art vision and language models. The authors propose this system as a general framework for human-like environment perception and real-time interactive AI companionship.

## Claimed Contributions
- **The Live Gaming Dataset**: A 561-hour, domain-specific dataset with structured annotations for real-time multi-agent coordination, commentary, and user guidance, enriched with paralinguistic nuance labels.
- **The Proact-VL Architecture**: An integration of chunk-wise continuous processing and a persistent KV cache with an autonomous, low-latency `<|FLAG|>` token proactive triggering head.
- **A Multi-Tier Loss Function**: Claimed in the introduction as a novel objective to resolve training instabilities and balance the proactive timing head with standard language modeling objectives.
- **Superior Response Latency and Quality**: Claims of achieving state-of-the-art metrics (such as TimeDiff and F1) and human-like interactive responsiveness.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper correctly identifies a highly relevant gap in the literature between continuous streaming VideoLLMs and explicit proactive conversational agents. The related work effectively categorizes the current landscape, correctly crediting foundational real-time models like VideoLLM-online and LiveCC. 

However, the technical and algorithmic novelty of the proposed solution is marginal. The foundational chunk-wise input mapping with historical KV cache updating directly mimics the established mechanics of the aforementioned streaming VLMs. Furthermore, the proactive trigger—applying a minimal gated MLP with a sigmoid activation and a scalar threshold $\tau$ to a specialized `<|FLAG|>` token—is a standard binary classification technique applied to a specific sequence step. While combining these two paradigms provides a practical and computationally clean engineering solution, it lacks the fundamental machine learning or architectural innovation typically expected at ICML. 

Additionally, the paper operates in a rapidly moving subfield. As the authors themselves note in their citations of early 2025 preprints, concurrent literature (e.g., Dispider, MMDuet2, and StreamBridge) is rapidly converging on virtually identical proactive streaming architectures utilizing lightweight activation triggers. While concurrent work does not strictly invalidate a submission, the simplicity of Proact-VL's implementation dilutes the uniqueness of its contribution, making the manuscript read more as an applied system and dataset paper than a core algorithmic advance. 

## Technical Soundness
The system architecture presents a logical and efficient method for bounding memory usage during continuous streaming via fixed-duration discretization. However, several critical technical issues undermine the paper’s soundness.

First, the core methodology relies on discretizing the input stream into 1-second chunks. Inherently, this imposes a base latency constraint: an event occurring at the start of a chunk will wait a full second, plus model inference time, before a response can be triggered. Because natural human conversational turn-taking and reaction gaps typically sit around 200 to 300 milliseconds, claiming that this system achieves "human-like" or "low-latency" environment perception is an overstatement. A fixed 1-second interval creates a rigid temporal bottleneck for sudden, fast-paced gaming events. Furthermore, the reliance on a static threshold parameter $\tau$ for the proactive trigger raises questions about flexibility; a fixed threshold is unlikely to generalize seamlessly across highly diverse pacing styles (e.g., a fast-paced shooter versus a slow-paced role-playing game).

Second, the manuscript suffers from severe reproducibility issues concerning its model nomenclature. The dataset curation pipeline heavily relies on models labeled as "GPT-4.1", "Qwen3-Omni-Flash", "DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp", and "Qwen3-VL-Plus". These do not correspond to standard, publicly released models in the broader ML community. If these are typographical errors for existing models (e.g., GPT-4o, Qwen2.5-VL, DeepSeek-V3), they reflect poorly on the paper's clarity and precision. If they refer to unreleased, proprietary beta models or internal API endpoints, it introduces an insurmountable barrier to reproducibility. 

Third, the text exhibits significant formatting and structural clarity issues. Figure references are entirely out of sequential order: Figure 4 is referenced in the Introduction prior to Figures 2 and 3, and Figure 6 is mentioned in the methodology without any preceding reference to Figure 5. Finally, the "multi-tier loss function" that is prominently highlighted in the introduction as a core methodological contribution is completely absent from the subsequent methodology text, leaving the theoretical claims regarding training stability unsupported.

## Experimental Rigor
The most critical and fatal flaw in the provided artifact is the complete absence of experimental results. The text truncates abruptly at the conclusion of the methodology (the proactive response mechanism). Consequently, there is zero empirical evidence to substantiate the paper's claims of "superior response latency and quality." 

Assuming an evaluation section exists in the complete manuscript, the heavily LLM-reliant pipeline introduces a verified risk of benchmark saturation and circular evaluation. The pipeline utilizes powerful proprietary teacher models to polish vocabulary, inject paralinguistic nuances, and sanitize language. Evaluating a student model on a benchmark derived from such intense LLM synthesis incentivizes the model to mimic the stylistic linguistic priors and hallucinations of the teacher models, rather than demonstrating grounded, multimodal video reasoning. Automated metrics like F1 and TimeDiff on synthetic benchmarks rarely capture the actual human experience of interacting with a proactive agent. 

Furthermore, the abstract and introduction frame Proact-VL as a "general framework" for real-time AI companions. However, the entirety of the dataset curation, parsing pipeline, and intended application context are strictly confined to the gaming domain (specifically commentators and guides). There is no methodology provided to indicate how the framework generalizes to open-world, everyday egocentric video, or traditional dialogue systems, rendering the claims of generality severely inflated.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Problem Significance**: The paper excellently identifies the tension between explicit speaking control (proactivity) and low-latency continuous processing, targeting a highly topical challenge in multimodal LLMs.
- **Dataset Scale**: The creation of a 561-hour dataset across 12 blockbuster titles represents a substantial and valuable curation effort. The inclusion of structured persona profiles provides an interesting target for continuous multi-agent coordination.
- **Pragmatic Design**: Utilizing a fixed-duration chunk schema with persistent KV caching ensures that sequence lengths do not explode during continuous video inference, presenting a computationally sound baseline for streaming applications.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Total Lack of Empirical Evidence**: The omission of all experimental data, baseline comparisons, latency measurements, and ablation studies in the provided text makes it impossible to verify any of the paper's core claims.
- **Incremental Architectural Novelty**: The methodology relies entirely on assembling existing, standard techniques (chunked caching combined with a straightforward binary classification head on a special token), lacking the algorithmic depth expected of an ICML contribution.
- **Ethics and Privacy Risks**: The dataset pipeline extracts "structured commentator and guide personas" and speaker identities from real YouTube influencers and professional tournament broadcasts. Without explicit consent or robust anonymization, training an AI to replicate the identifiable stylistic patterns of specific human creators raises significant ethical, privacy, and copyright concerns.
- **Anonymity Policy Violation Risk**: The paper includes a GitHub Pages link (`proact-vl.github.io`). While not inherently a violation if perfectly scrubbed, such URLs often inadvertently leak author identities via commit histories, account ownership metadata, or linked assets. This is raised as a strict Area Chair verification risk for double-blind compliance.
- **Unverified Methodological Claims**: The multi-tier loss function is entirely missing from the formulations, and the reliance on non-standard or unreleased LLMs (e.g., "GPT-4.1") prevents dataset reproducibility.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- The entirety of the experimental evaluation, including end-to-end latency benchmarks against streaming baselines, TimeDiff/F1 scores, and qualitative response demonstrations.
- The mathematical formulation, implementation details, and ablation studies of the "multi-tier loss function."
- Human evaluations to validate whether the heavily LLM-polished dataset correlates with actual human preference and whether the model's proactivity feels natural in real-time interactions.
- Out-of-domain evaluation to justify the "general framework" claims.
- Clarification on the exact, publicly accessible versions of the models used in the dataset generation pipeline.

## What Would Change My Assessment
To fundamentally alter this assessment, the authors must provide the missing evaluation section, demonstrating with rigorous baseline comparisons that the `<|FLAG|>` token mechanism significantly outperforms standard active speaker detection or simpler streaming heuristics. I would need to see a detailed defense of why this combination fundamentally differs from concurrent proactive streaming VLMs. Furthermore, the authors must correct the non-standard model nomenclature to ensure absolute reproducibility. Finally, the inclusion of a dedicated ethics and broader impacts statement addressing the scraping of real YouTube personas and ensuring explicit compliance with platform Terms of Service is required, alongside a limitations section addressing the inherent temporal bounds of 1-second chunking.

Compounded by incremental algorithmic novelty, severe reproducibility issues regarding model nomenclature, and unaddressed ethical concerns regarding persona scraping, the manuscript in its current state falls well below the acceptance threshold.
