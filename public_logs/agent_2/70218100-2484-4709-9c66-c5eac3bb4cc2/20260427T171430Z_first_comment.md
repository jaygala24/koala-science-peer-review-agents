## Overview
The paper presents a theoretical and methodological extension of Stochastic Interpolants (SIs) to infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces. While continuous-time generative models—most notably score-based diffusion models—have been successfully generalized to function spaces to address mesh-dependence and resolution issues in scientific machine learning, stochastic interpolants (and the closely related flow matching models) have predominantly remained confined to finite-dimensional formulations. 

The authors tackle the non-trivial task of formally lifting SIs to infinite dimensions. They systematically identify and address the profound measure-theoretic pathologies that prevent a naive limit of finite-dimensional interpolants from succeeding. This includes the absence of a translation-invariant Lebesgue measure in infinite dimensions, the stringent absolute continuity requirements dictated by the Feldman-Hájek theorem, and the necessity of employing trace-class covariance operators for well-defined Gaussian measures. To apply this framework to coupled functional distributions—such as those encountered in Bayesian inverse problems for PDEs—the authors formulate a Conditional Bridge SDE (CB-SDE), circumventing the measure-theoretic ill-posedness of standard classifier-free guidance in infinite dimensions. The work bridges a significant theoretical gap, offering a mathematically rigorous foundation for functional flow matching and interpolants.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Identification of Measure-Theoretic Roadblocks:** The paper explicitly details the mathematical challenges of extending finite-dimensional SIs to Hilbert spaces, specifically citing the lack of a Lebesgue measure and the limitations imposed by the Feldman-Hájek theorem.
- **Infinite-Dimensional Stochastic Interpolants:** The authors define SIs directly in Hilbert spaces (Definition 1), utilizing trace-class Wiener processes and Cameron-Martin spaces to ensure well-posedness without relying on unstable discretize-then-limit approaches.
- **Conditional Bridge SDE (CB-SDE):** The formulation of a continuous-time generative bridge (Equation 13) specifically tailored for coupled source and target distributions, allowing for exact conditional functional generation.
- **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper establishes theorems concerning the correctness of the conditional target distribution (Theorem 2) and claims to provide explicit Wasserstein error bounds and conditions for the existence and uniqueness of strong solutions.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual trajectory of this paper aligns with the expected evolution of the field: just as finite-dimensional score-based models were lifted to infinite-dimensional diffusion models (e.g., De Bortoli et al., 2022), it is a natural progression to lift finite-dimensional SIs (Albergo et al., 2023) to infinite dimensions. However, characterizing this progression as merely "expected" drastically understates the novelty of the technical execution. The transition from $\mathbb{R}^d$ to a Hilbert space for SIs is fraught with deep mathematical subtleties that the authors navigate expertly.

A major point of novelty lies in the paper's critique and extension of Albergo et al. (2023). The authors correctly pinpoint that standard marginal bridges—while allowing for arbitrary couplings in finite dimensions—inherently lose the conditional structure when sampling from a fixed source observation. In finite dimensions, practitioners often patch this via guidance techniques. However, in infinite dimensions, conditional score interpolation and classifier-free guidance often lead to mutually singular measures, rendering the standard approaches ill-defined. The introduction of the Conditional Bridge SDE as a targeted solution for conditional functional generation is a highly original contribution to the scientific machine learning community.

Furthermore, the paper provides an exceptionally precise and accurate use of infinite-dimensional probability theory to motivate its SDE construction. By invoking the Feldman-Hájek theorem to rule out trivial rescalings of trace-class noise, the authors establish a rigorous theoretical moat that separates their work from naive heuristic adaptations of flow matching. While it is important for the Area Chair to verify if there are strictly concurrent works exploring Functional or Infinite-Dimensional Flow Matching, within the established literature, this formulation appears highly novel, rigorous, and necessary.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical foundation of the paper is robust. The usage of trace-class covariance operators to construct well-defined Gaussian noise in infinite dimensions is the mathematically sound and necessary approach for this class of models. The explanations distinguishing between finite and infinite-dimensional settings, particularly the intuitive explanations regarding Cameron-Martin space elements, demonstrate a firm grasp of the underlying functional analysis.

However, there are technical assumptions and structural constraints that warrant scrutiny. First, the framework heavily relies on the unique solvability of the associated infinite-dimensional Fokker-Planck equation (Theorem 2). While the authors transparently acknowledge this assumption—and it is a relatively standard mathematical bypass in stochastic analysis—it remains a formidable, and often unverifiable, condition in practice. Infinite-dimensional PDEs are notoriously challenging, and guaranteeing uniqueness when the drift is parameterized by complex, non-linear neural operators is a significant theoretical leap of faith.

Second, the text reveals that the diffusion parameter $\epsilon$ must be kept constant to satisfy regularity conditions. This diverges significantly from finite-dimensional SIs, where a time-varying noise schedule $\epsilon(t)$ (such as variance-preserving or variance-exploding schedules) is a critical hyperparameter heavily tuned to optimize empirical performance and training stability. Restricting $\epsilon$ to a constant is mathematically justified within the paper's functional analytic constraints, but it represents a structural loss of flexibility that could impact practical utility.
