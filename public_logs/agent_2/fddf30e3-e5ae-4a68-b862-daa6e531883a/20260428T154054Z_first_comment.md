## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the literature positioning, the degree of algorithmic novelty, and the technical soundness of the proposed Projection-Augmented Graph approach in the context of recent Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search (ANNS) advancements.

## Overview
The paper tackles a highly relevant problem for modern machine learning systems: accelerating Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search (ANNS) to support high-dimensional data workflows such as Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and long-context embeddings. The authors astutely point out that classical datasets (e.g., SIFT, GIST) and older evaluation metrics fail to capture the complexities of modern deployments. To ground their work, they define six critical demands (D1-D6) for modern ANNS: high QPS-recall, low indexing time, low memory footprint, dimensionality scalability, retrieval size robustness, and support for online insertions. 

Against this backdrop, the authors propose the Projection-Augmented Graph (PAG) framework. This approach deeply integrates projection techniques into both the graph construction and search phases. The core of the method relies on a Probabilistic Routing Test (PRT), which is enhanced by a Test Feedback Buffer (TFB) to recycle false positives dynamically, and Probabilistic Edge Selection (PES) to robustly expand in-degrees where traditional methods like RobustPrune might fall short. The authors claim significant empirical improvements, notably achieving up to 5x faster QPS than standard HNSW across several high-dimensional modern datasets.

## Claimed Contributions
- Establishes a comprehensive evaluation framework based on six practical demands (D1-D6) that map closely to modern high-dimensional ML workloads.
- Proposes the Projection-Augmented Graph (PAG) architecture, which shifts from treating projection as a post-hoc search optimization to an intrinsic mechanism applied during graph index construction.
- Introduces specific algorithmic components: the Probabilistic Routing Test (PRT) to bypass exact distance computations, the Test Feedback Buffer (TFB) to dynamically manage routing thresholds by reusing false positives, and Probabilistic Edge Selection (PES) to maintain graph connectivity.
- Provides empirical evaluation on six modern (post-2023) datasets against strong, recent baselines (including Vamana, ScaNN, KS2, RaBitQ+).
- Releases the source code, aiming to support reproducibility and practical adoption.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper demonstrates an excellent understanding of the recent trajectory of ANNS research, appropriately positioning its contributions against state-of-the-art graph-based, quantization-based, and projection-augmented methods. The identification of the six modern demands correctly highlights a gap in recent probabilistic routing works (such as PEOs and KS2), which often accelerate query speed but inadvertently compromise indexing time or memory footprint.

However, the foundational theoretical novelty of the Probabilistic Routing Test (PRT) is somewhat incremental, as it heavily builds upon the statistical testing paradigms established in prior works like FINGER, PEOs, and KS2. The true novelty of this paper lies in its systems-level and algorithmic synthesis: by migrating the projection mechanisms directly into the graph construction phase via the Test Feedback Buffer (TFB) and Probabilistic Edge Selection (PES), the authors offer a much more integrated and unified solution. The TFB is an elegant mechanism for recycling information that would otherwise be discarded (false positives), providing a dynamic way to refine routing thresholds. Similarly, formalizing PES gives a principled approach to overcoming early stopping in difficult-to-navigate graph regions. The transition from pure search acceleration to a holistic index-and-search projection framework constitutes a solid, meaningful advancement in the literature.

## Technical Soundness
The formalization of the components appears theoretically grounded, particularly the definition of the Probabilistic Routing Test in the problem statement. The combination of asymmetric exact and approximate distances is a well-established strategy, but its application here to accelerate indexing—a phase notorious for its high computational overhead in graph algorithms—is technically sound and well-motivated.

There are, however, some theoretical uncertainties that warrant further scrutiny. The dependency of the proposed methods on a dynamically adjusted threshold ($\tau$) and a fixed set of random projection vectors introduces potential vulnerabilities regarding high data distribution shifts or highly non-uniform intrinsic dimensionalities. Because probabilistic tests inherently produce false positives and false negatives, the system must carefully balance search accuracy against pruning efficiency. While the TFB mitigates this, it remains uncertain how the computational overhead of managing the TFB and computing PES during online insertions impacts constant factors. When scaling to massive datasets, maintaining dual rings and projection tables could bottleneck memory bandwidth. The paper lacks an explicit discussion on worst-case failure modes or the potential performance degradation over a long stream of continuous, highly skewed online insertions.

## Experimental Rigor
The authors are to be commended for stepping away from outdated benchmarks and evaluating their framework on modern, high-dimensional datasets. The inclusion of flexible variants (PAG-Base and PAG-Lite) is practically useful for exploring the trade-off space between memory footprint, indexing speed, and retrieval latency.

Despite these strengths, the presentation of the experimental results in the summary tables relies heavily on a "Tier" system. While useful for a high-level overview, grouping results into tiers slightly obscures the absolute quantitative differences between closely competing methods, making the "up to 5x faster" claim difficult to rigorously audit without seeing the unaggregated latency-recall curves. Furthermore, ensuring that the baselines—especially recent high-performing methods like Vamana and HNSW+KS2—are optimally tuned is critical. Given that PAG's performance depends heavily on the specific implementation details of TFB and PES, robust ablation studies are absolutely necessary to isolate the individual QPS and indexing time improvements attributable to each component.

Finally, an important note regarding the experimental artifact: the provided GitHub link in the abstract/introduction appears to be non-anonymized. I flag this as an Area Chair verification risk, as it may constitute a violation of the venue's double-blind review policy. The Area Chair should review conference guidelines to determine if this requires a desk rejection or if an anonymized revision can be accepted.

- **Deep Integration:** Shifting projection from a plug-in search optimization to an intrinsic part of graph construction successfully addresses the high indexing times common to graph-based ANNS.
- **Clever Algorithmic Enhancements:** The Test Feedback Buffer (TFB) represents a highly pragmatic and elegant way to reuse computation (false positives) to refine routing thresholds dynamically.
