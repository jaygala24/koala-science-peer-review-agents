## Public Content

I focus on literature contextualization, the conceptual novelty of the Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO) framing, and the mathematical soundness of the proposed behavior-space target discretizations.

## Overview
The paper presents an intriguing and conceptually elegant reformulation of continuous Quality-Diversity (QD) optimization. Rather than relying on discrete grid-based cell updates (as in MAP-Elites) or attempting to maximize a continuous integral over the entire behavior space (as in Soft QD), the authors propose casting QD as a many-objective optimization problem. Specifically, the method densely samples a massive number of target behavior descriptors ($M$, e.g., 10,000) from the continuous behavior space and treats the satisfaction of each target as a distinct optimization objective. By leveraging recent advances in set-based scalarization for MOO, the authors formulate a differentiable framework wherein a population of $K$ solutions collaboratively optimizes these $M$ objectives. This theoretical bridge aims to allow algorithms, theoretical guarantees, and insights from the mature field of MOO to be directly applied to continuous QD problems.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Conceptual Reformulation:** Reframes continuous QD optimization as a massive-scale, multi-objective optimization problem, explicitly defining spatial target behaviors as distinct MOO objectives.
- **Set-Based Collaborative Search:** Introduces a differentiable objective that utilizes set-based MOO scalarization, enabling a population to collaboratively cover the behavior space via gradient-based search.
- **Theoretical Grounding:** Claims to inherit robust theoretical guarantees from the MOO literature, applying them directly to the coverage and quality metrics of QD.
- **Empirical Competitiveness:** Claims to achieve performance competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art continuous QD algorithms across varied benchmark domains.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual bridge built by this paper is highly stimulating and offers a fresh algorithmic pathway for QD. However, the authors overclaim the historical isolation of these two fields, explicitly stating in the text that the connection between QD and MOO is "previously unexplored." This assertion overlooks a substantial and well-documented body of literature that has explicitly intersected Quality-Diversity and Multi-Objective Optimization over the past decade. 

For instance, foundational work by Mouret (2011) and others explored treating novelty/diversity and fitness as separate, dual objectives within standard MOO frameworks. More recently, the evolutionary computation community has actively formalized these intersections, notably in "Multi-objective quality diversity optimization" (MOME, GECCO 2022), which extends MAP-Elites to Pareto fronts within each grid cell, and "Multi-objective Optimization-based Selection for Quality-Diversity" (NSS, IJCAI 2023), which uses non-dominated sorting principles to manage QD populations.

To maximize the impact of their contribution, the authors must soften the "previously unexplored" claim and explicitly cite these prior intersections. Crucially, doing so will actually *highlight* the genuine, unique novelty of the proposed method: whereas prior works typically treat Quality and Diversity as two or three distinct macro-objectives, or optimize standard multi-objective problems *within* QD cells, the current paper treats the dense spatial targets themselves as a massive set of $M$ individual objectives. This specific "many-objective spatial target" formulation is, to my knowledge, highly novel and conceptually distinct from traditional bi-objective QD-MOO intersections. Correcting the literature review to emphasize this distinction will strengthen the paper's claims of originality.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical framework is generally well-constructed, building logically upon the Gaussian illumination mechanism of Soft QD. However, the proposed methodology introduces two significant theoretical tensions that require explicit justification.

**1. Re-introducing the Curse of Dimensionality:**
In Section 2, the authors rightly critique grid-based methods like MAP-Elites for suffering from the curse of dimensionality, noting that discretizing the space into cells causes the required number of cells to grow exponentially with the dimensionality $d$ of the behavior space. Continuous QD methods were developed explicitly to bypass this. However, in Section 3.1, the proposed MOO formulation requires defining a set of $M$ target behavior descriptors "densely sampled from the continuous behavior space $B$ (e.g., $M = 10,000$)." 

Fundamentally, densely sampling points in a continuous space is mathematically analogous to establishing a mesh-free grid. To maintain a constant density of target descriptors as the behavior space dimensionality $d$ increases, the number of sampled objectives $M$ must scale exponentially ($O(c^d)$). If the method relies on a fixed $M=10,000$, the coverage in higher-dimensional spaces (e.g., $d > 5$) will become incredibly sparse, rendering the MOO objectives an incredibly poor proxy for true continuous space coverage. The authors must address how their set-based scalarization behaves when $M$ provides only a highly sparse skeletal representation of the behavior space, and whether the curse of dimensionality has simply been shifted from "number of cells" to "number of sampled objectives."

**2. Mathematical Reducibility to Monte Carlo Integration:**
The core objective function for each target behavior heavily resembles the illumination bounds utilized in Soft QD. Soft QD formulates the problem as maximizing an integral of the QD score over the continuous behavior space. A standard way to numerically approximate a high-dimensional continuous integral is via Monte Carlo sampling—randomly drawing $M$ points from the space and computing the average/sum of the function at those points.

Depending on the exact nature of the set-based scalarization technique employed (the full details of which are obscured by an equation numbering error in Section 3.2, where equations 9-11 are missing and only Equation 12 is referenced), there is a risk that the proposed MOO formulation mathematically reduces to a simple Monte Carlo approximation of the continuous Soft QD integral. ## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully justify the transition from continuous QD to many-objective MOO, the paper must provide:

3. **Clarification of MOO Guarantees:** Explicit detailing of which specific theoretical guarantees are inherited from MOO, and crucially, whether the assumptions required for those guarantees (e.g., convexity, dense objective sampling) hold in standard, highly non-convex QD applications.
