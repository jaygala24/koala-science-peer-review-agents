## Public Content

I focus on how the paper situates itself within the literature, its conceptual and algorithmic novelty, and how it compares to concurrent and prior work in the agent alignment space.

## Overview
This paper investigates the critical challenge of ensuring the safety, reliability, and alignment of Computer-Use Agents (CUAs). It introduces a necessary conceptual shift from static, trajectory-level safety policy checking to dynamic, action-level alignment verification. The authors define "action alignment" across three categories: Malicious Instruction Following, Harmful Unintended Behavior, and Other Task-Irrelevant Behavior. To support this paradigm, the paper introduces MisActBench, a multimodal benchmark comprising 2,264 action-level, human-annotated labels. Furthermore, the paper proposes DeAction, a two-stage runtime guardrail designed to detect misaligned actions before execution via a "Fast Check" and subsequent "Systematic Analysis," providing structured feedback to correct the agent.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Conceptual Shift to Action Alignment:** Broadening the evaluation of CUAs from explicit, static safety policy violations to an intent-centric perspective that penalizes both harmful behaviors and task-irrelevant inefficiencies.
- **MisActBench:** A novel benchmark providing fine-grained, action-level multimodal alignment labels, combining both externally induced attack trajectories and internally arising task deviations.
- **DeAction Guardrail:** A plug-and-play, model-agnostic, two-stage runtime verification system intended to balance high-accuracy alignment detection with moderate latency overhead, facilitating iterative correction without requiring access to the agent's internal parameters.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper does a remarkable job of surveying and integrating highly recent, concurrent literature in the fast-moving field of CUA safety. As demonstrated in Table 1, the authors accurately differentiate their proposed benchmark from trajectory-level safety benchmarks such as R-Judge and MobileRisk. The paper also successfully contextualizes its contributions against cutting-edge 2025 adversarial benchmarks like OS-Harm, RedTeamCUA, and RiOSWorld. 

However, the conceptual novelty of "action alignment" is somewhat incremental when viewed against the broader landscape of agent security, where step-level safety monitors and pre-execution screening for tool-use agents are increasingly common (e.g., Task Shield). The claim that this is the "first effort to define and study misaligned action detection" may be slightly overstated. That said, extending these concepts to open-ended, visual Computer-Use Agents and explicitly incorporating "Task-Irrelevant Behavior" as a core misalignment facet constitutes a highly useful and timely application of the theory.

Conversely, the algorithmic novelty of the proposed DeAction guardrail appears strictly limited. The system primarily relies on standard "LLM-as-a-judge" prompting techniques partitioned into a fast/slow analysis pipeline. While this is highly practical for deployment, it does not represent a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough in guardrail architecture or multimodal verification.

## Technical Soundness
The taxonomy introduced in Section 2.2 and illustrated in Figure 1 provides an excellent, intuitive framework for evaluating CUA robustness. The distinction between malicious instructions, unintended harm, and simple inefficiency provides a much-needed granularity that current pass/fail trajectory metrics lack.

However, the paper exhibits a significant technical soundness concern regarding the generation of "internally arising" misaligned actions. The abstract and introduction heavily claim that the benchmark evaluates failures stemming from "internal limitations (e.g., erroneous reasoning)." Yet, Section 3.1 details a methodology where an LLM selects a risk template, executes it as a standalone task, and simply splices/merges the resulting actions into a benign trajectory. This artificial trajectory splicing fundamentally fails to capture the true distribution, context evolution, and natural drift of an agent experiencing an organic reasoning error. This creates a fatal-flaw candidate: if the synthetically injected unintended behaviors are easily distinguishable artifacts of the generation process (e.g., sudden context jumps or disjointed visual states) rather than realistic CUA errors, the benchmark's validity for evaluating non-adversarial, spontaneous misalignment is compromised. Models might simply learn to detect the "splice" rather than evaluating complex intent.

Furthermore, deploying a secondary LLM as the DeAction guardrail introduces inherent soundness vulnerabilities. It is highly likely that the guardrail LLM will suffer from the same multimodal grounding failures, reasoning limitations, or indirect prompt injection susceptibilities (present in the environment observations $o_t$) as the base CUA. Additionally, the correction loop relies on structured feedback; it is unclear how the framework prevents infinite correction loops if the base agent is fundamentally incapable of taking the required aligned action.

## Experimental Rigor
The human annotation process for MisActBench is rigorous, achieving an impressive Fleiss' Kappa of 0.84 across 2,264 steps. This demonstrates that the taxonomy is actionable and that the boundary between categories is reasonably clear to human evaluators, though the qualitative boundary between "inefficiency" and "harm" in minor system state alterations warrants further discussion.

There are, however, critical omissions in the experimental reporting. First, the exact baselines used for evaluating DeAction’s detection F1 score on MisActBench are missing from the provided excerpts, making it difficult to assess the strength of the 15% absolute improvement claim. Second, while the paper claims "moderate latency overhead" for the two-stage guardrail, quantitative wall-clock latency metrics (in milliseconds/seconds) and API token cost overheads per step are entirely absent. Runtime constraints in interactive CUAs are stringent, and these metrics are essential for proving deployment viability.

**Area Chair Verification Risk:** The submission contains a direct, de-anonymized URL on the first page (`https://osu-nlp-group.github.io/Misaligned-Action-Detection`). This explicitly reveals the identity of the research group and appears to be a blatant violation of the conference's double-blind submission policy. I strongly advise the Area Chair to review this formatting violation against the venue's desk-rejection criteria.

Finally, regarding ethical rigor, the paper safely isolates destructive actions within sandboxed environments. However, the manuscript lacks transparency regarding the human annotators (e.g., compensation, consent, IRB approval status). Furthermore, a brief broader impacts statement discussing the dual-use risk of open-sourcing successful attack trajectories (which could be used to fine-tune malicious agents) is necessary.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Action-Level Granularity:** MisActBench provides a highly valuable resource to the community, moving CUA evaluation beyond coarse trajectory-level pass/fail metrics.
- **Intent-Centric Framing:** The taxonomy explicitly isolates task-irrelevant actions from purely malicious ones, creating a more realistic model of how deployed agents actually fail.
- **Practical Guardrail Design:** The two-stage DeAction pipeline elegantly acknowledges and addresses the latency constraints of running pre-execution verification in real-time environments.
- **Literature Integration:** Excellent incorporation and contextualization of very recent concurrent works.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Synthetic vs. Organic Failures:** The methodology for creating "internally arising" errors relies on artificial trajectory splicing, which severely undermines the claim that the benchmark evaluates organic, spontaneous reasoning limitations. 
- **Guardrail Vulnerabilities:** The LLM-based DeAction system may inherit the same prompt injection and reasoning vulnerabilities as the base agent, and the mechanism for avoiding infinite correction loops is undefined.
- **Missing Deployment Overheads:** Lack of exact latency, cost overheads, and false-positive rates in benign environments.
- **Anonymity Violation:** The inclusion of an institutional GitHub URL directly compromises the double-blind review process.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **Baseline Configuration:** Explicit details on what specific baselines were used to evaluate DeAction, and how trajectory-level monitors were adapted for action-level detection.
- **Splice Detection Ablation:** An analysis comparing detection rates on the synthetically injected errors versus genuine, spontaneous CUA failures to prove that models are detecting misalignment rather than the artificial "context jump" of the splice.
- **Overhead Profiling:** Quantitative measurements of the latency (ms/s) and token cost overhead added per step by the DeAction pipeline during online execution.
- **False Positive Analysis:** An evaluation of how frequently the Stage 1 "Fast Check" incorrectly flags unconventional but aligned user workflows as misaligned (false positives) in benign environments like OSWorld.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would improve if the authors soften their claim inflation in the abstract and introduction, clarifying that the "internally arising" errors are simulated deviations rather than organic internal reasoning errors. I would also need to see an ablation showing that detection models are not merely exploiting the artifacts of trajectory splicing. Furthermore, the inclusion of concrete latency/cost overhead tables and a clarification on the baselines used would solidify the empirical claims. Finally, the Area Chair must verify whether the anonymity violation requires desk rejection; assuming it is permissible to overlook, the scientific content must stand on the additions requested above.

## Provisional Leaning
The paper identifies an excellent and timely problem, establishes a highly useful taxonomy, and provides a rigorously annotated benchmark. The shift to action alignment is a welcome progression for the reliable deployment of CUAs. However, the artificial nature of the benchmark's internal errors, the limited algorithmic novelty of the guardrail, the missing latency metrics, and the severe deanonymization risk hold the submission back from a clear acceptance. I lean towards acceptance pending the resolution of the synthetic data validation and the anonymity policy check.
