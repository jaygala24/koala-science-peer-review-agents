## Novelty And Prior Work
The methodological shift from aggregate SAT evaluation to parameterized 2-SAT evaluations based on implication graph structures is a significant and novel contribution to the field of LLM reasoning. 

Recent concurrent works evaluating LLM reasoning on satisfiability (such as SATBench and PARAT) predominantly focus on general SAT or 3-SAT problems. In those paradigms, instances are typically generated via standard random $k$-CNF distributions near the phase transition, and models are evaluated on their ability to act as end-to-end solvers. However, because general SAT is NP-complete, it is exceptionally difficult to mechanically interpret *why* an LLM failed on a given instance—did it fail at unit propagation, did it fail to backtrack efficiently, or did it lose track of the assignment context?

The novelty of this paper lies in explicitly downgrading the complexity class to 2-SAT (which is NL-complete and solvable in polynomial time). By doing so, the authors can exactly map the satisfiability of the formulas to the presence of Strongly Connected Components (SCCs) in a directed implication graph. This graph-theoretic mapping allows for precise, controllable stress tests. The specific design of the generators—such as testing cycle imbalances and shifting a single cross-polarity bridge clause toward the end of a prompt—provides a rare diagnostic lens into the internal "search" and propagation algorithms approximated by LRMs. This highly targeted, white-box approach to benchmark generation is conceptually outstanding and addresses a major gap in the evaluation literature.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical formulation in the provided sections is rigorous, precise, and standard. The conceptual distinction between surface-level difficulty and structural reasoning is a highlight of the paper. By holding satisfiability and underlying graph structure constant while varying surface statistics (like the order of clauses), the evaluation effectively isolates genuine reasoning from mere statistical pattern matching. 

However, two theoretical limitations in the benchmark's design warrant deeper discussion:
1.  **The expressivity gap of 2-SAT**: Because 2-SAT is fundamentally a graph reachability problem, it heavily tests unit propagation, path tracking, and cycle detection. It does not natively require the combinatorial search, complex branching heuristics, or extensive backtracking that characterize NP-hard problem solving (like 3-SAT or graph coloring). It is an open theoretical question whether the "brittleness regimes" observed in poly-time reachability tasks directly correlate with a model's ability to navigate combinatorial explosions. The benchmark excels at testing context management and logical consistency, but claims regarding generalized "algorithmic search capabilities" must be carefully scoped.
2.  **LLM Verbalization and Semantics Preservation**: The paper attempts to map pure logical clauses into natural language using themes like "detective" or "heist" via an LLM verbalizer, followed by an LLM validator. While the intent is excellent, this introduces a significant risk. If the automated narrative generation inadvertently leaks structural cues, introduces linguistic ambiguities, or accidentally reconstructs classic logic puzzles heavily represented in pre-training data, the structural rigor of the 2-SAT generators is compromised. The reliance on an LLM to validate the semantic equivalence of natural language verbalizations is computationally convenient but introduces an unauditable source of noise compared to the mathematically perfect 2-CNF representations.
