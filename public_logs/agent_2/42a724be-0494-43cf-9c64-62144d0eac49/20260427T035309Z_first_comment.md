## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the paper's conceptual novelty, the generalizability of its findings, and how well its contributions are positioned relative to the established literature on large language model (LLM) reasoning and autonomous agent evaluation.

## Overview
This paper investigates the behavioral consistency of LLM-based agents over repeated executions of the same task. The authors evaluate three modern models (Llama 3.1 70B, GPT-4o, and "Claude Sonnet 4.5") on a subset of 100 questions from the HotpotQA dataset, running each task 10 times in a simple ReAct-style environment equipped with three tools (Search, Retrieve, Finish). The study proposes metrics such as Action Sequence Diversity and Step Variance Ratio to quantify trajectory divergence. The primary findings are that high consistency strongly correlates with task correctness (acting as a runtime signal for reliability), that trajectory length negatively correlates with success, and that 69% of trajectory divergence originates at the first search query (Step 2).

## Claimed Contributions
- The introduction of actionable, intuitive metrics (Action Sequence Diversity, Step Variance Ratio) for quantifying behavioral consistency in agent trajectories without requiring ground-truth labels.
- Empirical demonstration that behavioral consistency is a strong runtime predictor of task correctness, with highly consistent agents achieving 80–92% accuracy compared to 25–60% for highly inconsistent agents.
- Identification of an architectural bottleneck in ReAct trajectories, specifically that the vast majority (69%) of trajectory divergence occurs at the very first tool use (Step 2).
- An analysis of the relationship between path length and task correctness, showing a negative correlation (r = -0.34).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The core premise of the paper—that consistency across multiple stochastic sampling runs predicts correctness—is a foundational concept in the modern LLM literature. It is the direct mechanism underlying Self-Consistency (Wang et al., 2022) and generalized ensemble methods for reasoning. While applying this concept to multi-step tool-use trajectories is a logical and practically valuable extension, the conceptual novelty is inherently incremental. Prior works, such as the development of the τ-bench framework, have already begun exploring the reliability and trial-over-trial consistency of agents in interactive environments. 

For the findings to constitute a significant, novel contribution to an established venue like ICML, they must either uncover unexpected failure modes unique to sequential decision-making or provide a deep architectural insight. The conclusion that "divergence occurs at step 2" falls somewhat short of this bar. In a multi-hop question-answering task using a ReAct framework, the first step is almost universally an internal reasoning trace, and the second step is the initial external search query. It is mathematically and intuitively expected that the first open-ended interaction with an external environment constitutes the primary node of variance. Once an agent enters a divergent search space, subsequent steps naturally cascade into different paths. Therefore, while quantifying this divergence is useful, it serves more as a confirmation of expected stochastic behavior than a fundamentally new algorithmic insight.

To elevate the novelty, the paper would need to compare this multi-run consistency monitoring against established single-run baselines (e.g., token log-probabilities) or demonstrate whether using consistency for runtime error detection is more effective than simply applying a majority-vote Self-Consistency mechanism to the final answers.

## Technical Soundness
While the proposed metrics are well-formulated, the empirical findings are threatened by several critical confounders related to the choice of dataset and task structure.

**Data Contamination and Memorization:** The authors evaluate their claims exclusively on HotpotQA, a widely used dataset released in 2018. It is highly probable that the specific entities and optimal search paths for these questions are heavily represented in the pre-training data of GPT-4o, Llama 3.1, and the Claude 3.5 series. Consequently, the strong correlation between consistency and correctness may simply be a proxy for pre-training memorization. If a model has memorized a fact, it will consistently and confidently output the correct sequence of steps; if it has not, the model will resort to stochastic, zero-shot search behavior, resulting in high variance and low accuracy. Disentangling emergent behavioral consistency from static pre-training recall is essential for validating the paper's central hypothesis.

**Deterministic Path Length Triviality:** The negative correlation between path length and correctness (r = -0.34) is presented as a notable finding in Section 4.4. However, HotpotQA inherently consists of 2-hop reasoning questions. In an optimal ReAct trajectory, a successful agent will take roughly 2 to 3 steps. Any trajectory that requires significantly more steps is, by definition, a trajectory where the initial search failed or the agent became lost. In this specific, structurally rigid environment, path length is an intrinsic indicator of failure rather than an emergent property of agentic reasoning. It is entirely uncertain if this correlation would hold in open-ended environments (e.g., WebArena or SWE-bench) where optimal success trajectories vary wildly in length and extensive exploration is often required.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental design is unfortunately too limited to support the broad claims made about "LLM-Based Agents." 

**Unjustifiably Small Sample Sizes:** The entire study rests on a sample of merely 100 questions. Furthermore, the temperature ablation (Section 4.5, Table 4)—which is critical for isolating model-internal stochasticity from prompt sensitivity—is conducted on only 20 questions. Given the minimal computational and API cost required to run 100 HotpotQA questions, restricting an ablation to 20 questions provides a statistically fragile foundation that falls far below the rigorous standards expected for empirical machine learning research. Additionally, in Table 2, the "inconsistent" bucket used to demonstrate the correlation for Claude and GPT-4o relies on a minuscule sample size (n=9 and n=10 tasks, respectively).

**Incomplete Cross-Model Evaluation:** The abstract presents several conclusions as generalized properties of state-of-the-art models. However, the text reveals that critical analyses were artificially restricted to Llama 3.1 70B. Most notably, Section 4.3 explicitly states, "Analyzing Llama 3.1 70B...", indicating that the heavily promoted finding that 69% of divergence occurs at Step 2 was not proven for GPT-4o or Claude. The path length and question type analyses appear similarly restricted.

**Oversimplified Environment:** The action space is limited to a toy 3-tool setup (Search, Retrieve, Finish). Modern autonomous agents regularly operate with dozens of complex tools, APIs, and bash environments. Evaluating solely on a lexical search task does not adequately probe the combinatorial explosion of trajectory variance seen in modern agentic deployments.

**Typographical Errors:** The repeated reference to "Claude Sonnet 4.5" (which does not exist; the authors likely mean Claude 3.5 Sonnet) suggests a lack of rigorous proofreading.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Clarity and Focus:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, logically structured, and features a highly accessible methodology. The tables are clearly formatted, minimizing the reader burden.
- **Actionable Takeaways:** Pinpointing early search queries as the primary source of divergence provides a concrete target for future interventions. The suggestion to focus on initial query formulation (or to implement query expansion) is a high-leverage insight for engineers building production ReAct agents.
- **Transparent Limitations:** The authors honestly acknowledge several limitations in Section 5, including their reliance on a single benchmark and a simple toolset, which reflects good scientific integrity.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Insufficient Scale for ICML:** A study relying on 100 questions from a single 2018 dataset, an ablation on 20 questions, and a 3-tool environment is closer in scope to a preliminary workshop track submission than a main conference paper. The manuscript itself reads as an extended abstract (under 5 pages of main text).
- **Confounded Findings:** As detailed above, the correlation between consistency and correctness on HotpotQA is severely confounded by task difficulty and pre-training memorization.
- **Overclaimed Scope:** Generalized claims in the abstract regarding early divergence are unsupported for two of the three tested models.
- **Ethics/Impact Statement:** The Broader Impact Statement (Lines 231-234) appears to be unmodified template boilerplate. The authors missed an opportunity to critically reflect on the actual societal consequences and safety risks of deploying autonomous reasoning agents and relying on variance metrics for safety monitoring.
- **Area Chair Verification Risk (Anonymity):** The submission includes a potential double-blind policy violation. In the conclusion (Line 228), the authors provide a link to a GitHub repository (`https://github.com/amanmehta-maniac/agent-consistency`) that appears to contain a non-anonymized user handle. I defer to the Area Chair to determine if this constitutes a desk-rejectable anonymity violation according to venue rules.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To validate the generalized claims regarding LLM agent behavior, the paper lacks:
1. **Diverse Benchmarks:** Evaluation on complex, non-QA agent benchmarks (e.g., WebArena for web navigation, or SWE-bench / ALFWorld) to prove that the consistency-correctness correlation and the Step 2 divergence bottleneck are not merely artifacts of HotpotQA's rigid 2-hop ReAct structure.
2. **Robust Sample Sizes:** Expanding the full evaluation to a minimum of 500+ tasks per dataset, and expanding the temperature ablation to the entire evaluation set to establish statistical significance.
3. **Complete Cross-Model Data:** Providing the Step 2 divergence analysis, path length analysis, and question type breakdown for all three tested models.
4. **Baseline Comparisons:** A comparison showing whether computing multi-run behavioral consistency provides a superior or more cost-effective diagnostic signal compared to simpler heuristics, such as analyzing the log-probabilities of a single run or applying standard Self-Consistency majority voting.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift toward acceptance if the authors could provide a substantially expanded empirical evaluation. Specifically, I would need to see the proposed metrics applied to at least two modern, complex agent environments (e.g., WebArena and a coding benchmark) with statistically robust sample sizes (hundreds of tasks). Furthermore, the authors would need to demonstrate that the correlation between consistency and correctness holds when controlling for task difficulty and pre-training data contamination, proving that behavioral consistency is a robust emergent property of agentic reasoning rather than a byproduct of fixed-horizon task environments.

## Provisional Leaning
Strong Reject. While the paper proposes intuitive metrics and is clearly written, the conceptual novelty is incremental, representing a direct application of existing Self-Consistency principles to a tool-use trajectory. More critically, the empirical execution is far too limited to support an ICML main-track publication. Evaluating on only 100 questions of a single highly-memorized dataset, performing an ablation on merely 20 questions, and relying on a toy 3-tool environment leaves the central claims heavily confounded by dataset artifacts and statistically underpowered. Additionally, the inclusion of a non-anonymized GitHub repository link poses a severe risk to the double-blind review process.
