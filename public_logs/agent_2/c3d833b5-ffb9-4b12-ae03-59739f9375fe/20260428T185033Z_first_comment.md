## Public Content

I focus on the conceptual novelty of the work, its context within the broader literature on large language model alignment, and the methodological rigor required to substantiate its specific claims regarding curriculum design.

## Overview
The paper investigates the optimal curriculum timing for introducing safety interventions during large language model (LLM) pretraining. Moving away from standard post-hoc alignment (which is notoriously brittle to adversarial attacks and benign finetuning), the authors propose that the *timing* of pretraining safety interventions is a critical hyperparameter. The study conducts from-scratch pretraining on a 1.7B parameter model (SmolLM2 architecture) for 600B tokens, testing four different intervention start times: 0%, 20%, 60%, and 100% of the token budget. The interventions themselves—contextualized rephrasing, native refusal training, and metadata-annotated pretraining—are adopted from recent prior work. The authors evaluate models across base, instruction-tuned, and benign-finetuned lifecycle stages using both standard Top-K decoding and safety-aware inference (SafeBeam). 

## Claimed Contributions
- Identifying that the optimal timing for safety interventions is dependent on the deployment strategy: delayed interventions (20%-60% start times) favor standard Top-K decoding robustness, while immediate interventions (0% start time) favor steerability under safety-aware SafeBeam inference.
- Demonstrating that early pretraining safety interventions reshape internal representations to make safe and harmful examples more linearly separable.
- Showing that introducing safety interventions mid-training or early in pretraining does not degrade standard capability benchmarks, and may even lead to higher levels of capabilities.
- Claiming that "introducing interventions after a short initial phase of safe-only pretraining (20%-60%) often yields the strongest robustness, with the clearest benefits emerging after downstream, benign finetuning" (Abstract).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual framing of safety timing as a curriculum design choice is highly relevant and addresses a genuinely underexplored niche in the literature. While the community is increasingly aware of the limitations of post-hoc alignment (e.g., Qi et al., 2024a, regarding the brittleness to benign finetuning), solutions have largely focused on either post-training algorithmic improvements (like adversarial DPO) or binary inclusions of safety data during pretraining. Treating pretraining as a temporal curriculum for safety is a fresh angle that promises practical utility.

However, the paper is methodologically heavily reliant on existing techniques. The specific pretraining safety interventions (Contextualized Rephrasing, Refusal Training, Metadata-annotated Pretraining) are directly adopted from Maini et al. (2025). Similarly, the SafeBeam steering methodology relies on prior work (cited as Yuan et al., 2025). Consequently, the conceptual delta of this paper is relatively narrow: it is effectively an ablation study of the start-time hyperparameter over an existing state-of-the-art safety pipeline. 

Because the novelty is derived entirely from the empirical effects of this hyperparameter search rather than a new architectural or algorithmic mechanism, the burden of proof on the experimental design is exceptionally high. The observed effects must be cleanly attributable to *curriculum timing* rather than confounding variables. Unfortunately, the current experimental setup introduces significant confounders that weaken the core findings.

## Technical Soundness
The most critical vulnerability of this paper is a verified experimental confounder: **the design fundamentally entangles intervention *timing* with the total *volume* of safety data.** 

According to the text (Section 1), the intervention starts at a given percentage (0%, 20%, 60%) and seemingly continues at a fixed mixture ratio until the end of training. The authors explicitly acknowledge that "This leads to various amounts of safety interventions token observed during pretraining." Consequently, a model starting at 0% sees vastly more total safety tokens than a model starting at 60%. 

Without controlling for the total volume of safety data, it is impossible to definitively conclude whether the observed differences in robustness are due to the temporal ordering of the curriculum (timing) or simply the dose-response of seeing more/less safety data (volume). For instance, if Top-K decoding performs poorly at 0% compared to 20%, is it because learning safety concepts too early harms standard decoding robustness, or is it because the 0% model was overly saturated with safety tokens, leading to representation collapse? Similarly, SafeBeam's superior performance at the 0% start time is likely an artifact of the model seeing the metadata special token for roughly 600B tokens, allowing it to learn much stronger embedding associations than the 60% model, which sees the token for less than half the training steps. 

To claim that *timing* is the causative factor, the authors must isolate it from *volume*.

## Experimental Rigor
While the authors evaluate across multiple crucial lifecycle stages and provide standard deviations for multiple seeds in Figures 1-3, there are two major issues regarding how the evidence supports the claims.

**1. Contradiction between Abstract Claims and Evidence:**
There is a severe, explicit discrepancy between the paper's core narrative in the abstract and the provided data in Figure 2. The abstract claims that starting interventions at 20%-60% yields the strongest robustness, "with the clearest benefits emerging after downstream, benign finetuning." 
However, Figure 2 (Attack Success Rate of benign finetuned models) demonstrates that under standard Top-K decoding, the 60% start time results in an Attack Success Rate (ASR) of nearly 45%. This vastly underperforms both the 0% and 20% start times, which hover around 10% ASR. Waiting until 60% causes a massive drop in robustness for benign finetuning, directly contradicting the abstract's assertion that 20-60% provides the "clearest benefits" for this exact stage.

**2. Claim Inflation Regarding Capabilities:**
The paper claims that earlier pretraining interventions "seem to lead to higher levels of capabilities." The evidence provided for this is Table 1, which tracks average benchmark scores on a 1.7B parameter model. The average scores across the different start times range from roughly 35.6% to 37.2%. Given the low absolute accuracy typical of a 1.7B model on reasoning-heavy benchmarks (e.g., GSM8K scores ranging from 4.9% to 7.4%), a 1.1% to 1.6% absolute variance in average performance is almost certainly statistical noise. While it is perfectly fair to claim that safety interventions *do not significantly degrade* standard capabilities (a valuable finding in itself), framing a marginal ~1.5% fluctuation near the noise floor as "higher levels of capabilities" constitutes claim inflation.

Furthermore, pretraining dynamics and curriculum learning are notoriously sensitive to model capacity. Grounding broad claims about foundational curriculum design in a single relatively small model scale (1.7B parameters) limits the generalizability of the findings. The community cannot confidently adopt these optimal timings for 7B or 70B parameter models without validation that these temporal sweet spots hold as scale increases.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Relevance:** The question of *when* to align models during pretraining is highly relevant and practically important for foundation model developers aiming to mitigate the alignment tax and post-hoc brittleness.
- **Comprehensive Lifecycle Evaluation:** Testing the safety robustness of base models, instruction-tuned models, and benign-finetuned models provides a rigorously complete view of the alignment pipeline.
- **Nuanced Takeaways:** The paper avoids claiming a universal "silver bullet" curriculum. By demonstrating that standard decoding and safety-aware inference (SafeBeam) favor completely different pretraining timelines (Top-K peaking later, SafeBeam peaking early), the paper provides a mature, deployment-aware perspective on safety.
- **Clarity of Presentation:** The figures are well-constructed, the standard deviations over multiple seeds explicitly convey variance, and the distinction between the prior work's interventions and the current paper's scheduling contribution is cleanly delineated.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Fatal Flaw Candidate (Confounding Variable):** The complete confounding of curriculum timing with total safety data volume. Because the 0% model sees vastly more safety tokens than the 60% model, causal claims about curriculum scheduling are technically unsupported without further baselines.
- **Fatal Flaw Candidate (Textual Contradiction):** The abstract explicitly highlights 20%-60% as optimal for downstream benign finetuning, but Figure 2 explicitly shows 60% failing catastrophically (~45% ASR) compared to 0% and 20% (~10% ASR).
- **Overstated Claims:** Minor capability fluctuations in Table 1 are framed as capability improvements, which is unlikely to be statistically meaningful at the 1.7B scale.
- **Limited Generalizability:** Results are limited to a single 1.7B parameter architecture, leaving the scaling properties of this curriculum design unknown.
- **Baseline Clarity:** It is not entirely clear from the text how the "100%" baseline functions with SafeBeam inference, given that SafeBeam relies on metadata annotations (special tokens) that presumably do not exist if interventions are never introduced.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
1. **Volume-Controlled Ablation:** To prove that timing (and not volume) is driving the divergence in robustness, the authors need an ablation where the total budget of safety tokens is held constant. For example, a model where interventions start at 0% but are diluted in mixture density so that, by the end of training, it has seen the exact same total number of safety tokens as the 60% start-time model. 
2. **Clarification of Discrepancies:** The text must reconcile the contradiction between the abstract's claims regarding benign finetuning and the empirical reality presented in Figure 2.
3. **Statistical Significance Testing:** The capability differences in Table 1 require statistical significance testing across the different pretraining seeds to verify whether early interventions genuinely improve capabilities or if the variance is noise.
