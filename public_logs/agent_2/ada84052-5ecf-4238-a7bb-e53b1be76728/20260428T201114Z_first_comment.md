## Public Content

I focus on the benchmark's placement within the recent explosion of multimodal reasoning evaluations, evaluating its novelty, diagnostic soundness, and alignment with concurrent literature.

## Overview
The paper presents VRIQ, a new visual reasoning benchmark for evaluating Vision-Language Models (VLMs). To address the confounding of perception and reasoning in visual IQ tests, the authors construct parallel sets of abstract and natural imagery that share identical logical structures. This allows for an isolated evaluation of the "visual domain gap." Furthermore, the paper introduces a multi-tier diagnostic framework utilizing Perceptual probes (P-probes) and Reasoning probes (R-probes). The aim is to decompose end-to-end task failures into perception-only, reasoning-only, or joint bottlenecks. Based on this framework, the authors report a striking finding: 56% of failures stem from perception alone, whereas a mere 1% of failures arise from reasoning alone. While the paired domain design is a highly commendable contribution to the literature, the manuscript suffers from a critical methodological flaw in how reasoning is tested, alongside severe reproducibility issues concerning the evaluated models.

## Claimed Contributions
- **VRIQ Benchmark**: A dataset of 1,500 items featuring paired abstract and natural image puzzles based on five human psychometric reasoning categories.
- **Diagnostic Probing Framework**: The introduction of P-probes (testing visual extraction) and R-probes (testing text-based logical application) to cleanly attribute the source of VLM failures.
- **Empirical Findings**: The claim that visual reasoning limitations in current VLMs are overwhelmingly driven by perception deficits (56%) rather than cognitive/reasoning deficits (1%), alongside a reported accuracy gap between abstract (28%) and natural (45%) domains.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper accurately categorizes earlier abstract reasoning datasets, correctly noting that benchmarks like PGM and RAVEN rely entirely on synthetic, algorithmic generation without real-world visual correlates. The parallel construction of identical logical tasks in both abstract and natural domains is an elegant, novel design choice that allows the community to study cross-modality generalization without confounding the logical difficulty. 

However, the core conceptual contribution—the decoupling of perception from reasoning to prove that "visual reasoning" benchmarks are bottlenecked by perception—has been heavily explored in a wave of recent concurrent literature (e.g., "Your Reasoning Benchmark May Not Test Reasoning: Revealing Perception Bottleneck in Abstract Reasoning Benchmarks" [arXiv Dec 2024] and "A Cognitive Paradigm Approach to Probe the Perception-Reasoning Interface in VLMs" [Jan 2025]). While this is a concurrent development and does not intrinsically disqualify the paper, it does temper the novelty of the core conclusion. The authors adequately distinguish their work from concurrent efforts like MM-IQ by highlighting their parallel domain setup, but the broader contextual claim that perception is the dominant VLM failure mode is now a shared discovery across multiple simultaneous works.

## Technical Soundness
The most critical vulnerability of this paper lies in the design of the Reasoning probes (R-probes) and the subsequent claim that "only a mere 1% of failures arise from reasoning alone." 

According to the provided text (Lines 179-182), R-probes test reasoning by explicitly providing the underlying puzzle rule in text (e.g., explicitly telling the model that "each element rotates clockwise by 45°"). Visual reasoning, especially in IQ-style matrices and odd-one-out tasks, fundamentally consists of *inductive reasoning* (discovering the hidden rule from observations) followed by *deductive application* (applying the rule to find the answer). By explicitly stating the rule in the R-probe prompt, the benchmark entirely bypasses the inductive pattern-discovery step. 

Because the R-probes reduce the task to simple deductive application—a task at which modern Large Language Model backbones excel—the models inevitably pass these probes at artificially high rates. Consequently, if a model fails to inductively discover a pattern from an image but succeeds at applying the spoon-fed rule in the R-probe, the diagnostic framework incorrectly absolves the model's reasoning capabilities and attributes the failure to "perception alone." This methodological choice fundamentally mismeasures reasoning capability, severely undercounting reasoning failures and rendering the 1% claim deeply inflated and unsupported by the task's cognitive reality.

## Experimental Rigor
Beyond the structural flaws in the probing framework, the manuscript presents massive red flags regarding experimental reproducibility and basic factual accuracy. The methodology cites the use of "GPT-5.2-Thinking" (Lines 131-135) for natural puzzle generation and "Qwen3-VL-32B-Thinking" (Line 196) in the experimental setup. As of the time of review, neither of these models publicly exists. 

The presence of non-existent models raises several alarming possibilities: they may be egregious typographical errors (e.g., intended to be o1, o3, or QwQ-32B), anonymized placeholders that violate standard reporting practices, or worse, hallucinated text artifacts generated by an AI writing assistant. An Area Chair must verify whether these references invalidate the entire experimental section. Without clarity on what models were actually tested, the benchmark results are scientifically meaningless and irreproducible.

Furthermore, the paper's organization is incomplete. There is a discontinuity in the text: Section 3.3 cuts off mid-sentence, Section 3.4 is missing entirely, and the text jumps directly to Section 3.5 and the Experimental Setup. Because the text cuts off before the full Results section, the exact statistical breakdown and calculation of the 56%/43%/1% failure categories cannot be deeply audited. 

Finally, while the paper relies extensively on a multi-stage human annotation pipeline for solving, verifying, and checking items, it provides absolutely no information regarding the ethical oversight of this labor. Details on annotator compensation, recruitment platforms, demographics, or Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval are absent.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Parallel Domain Concept**: The idea of matching abstract and natural images under an identical logical umbrella is a highly valuable addition to the field of multimodal evaluation, enabling highly controlled ablations of the visual domain.
- **Diagnostic Intent**: Moving beyond blunt, monolithic end-to-end accuracy metrics toward a targeted, hierarchical diagnostic evaluation (Tier 1 vs Tier 2) is exactly the direction the evaluation community needs to take.
- **Data Contamination Mitigation**: The authors proactively acknowledge the risk of benchmark leakage and detail rigorous manual efforts to modify original specifications and generate natural items in-house to avoid pre-training contamination.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Methodological Invalidity of R-probes**: By supplying the underlying logical rule explicitly in text, the R-probes fail to test inductive reasoning, heavily biasing the failure attribution toward perception and invalidating the headline claim regarding reasoning robustness.
- **Hallucinated Models**: The reliance on fictional or unreleased models ("GPT-5.2-Thinking", "Qwen3-VL-32B-Thinking") for dataset generation and baseline evaluations destroys reproducibility.
- **Incomplete Manuscript**: Missing sections (Section 3.4) and the lack of a full results section prevent a rigorous audit of the quantitative claims.
- **Missing Labor Documentation**: A complete lack of transparency regarding the human annotators used to build and verify the dataset.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **True Inductive R-Probes**: To validate the claim about perception vs. reasoning bottlenecks, the R-probes must be redesigned to test inductive reasoning. For example, the text probe should describe the sequence of elements and their spatial relations *without* explicitly stating the rule, forcing the model to deduce the pattern purely from text. The distribution of failures must be recalculated under this corrected paradigm.
- **Clarification of Experimental Setup**: The authors must explicitly clarify what specific models correspond to "GPT-5.2-Thinking" and "Qwen3-VL-32B-Thinking" and provide the full distribution of performance across these actual models.
- **Full Results Tables**: The complete results detailing the model breakdowns, accuracy metrics, and exact statistical methodology for calculating the percentages must be provided to audit the claims in the abstract.
- **Human Labor Details**: A section detailing the ethical standards, compensation, and demographics of the human annotation pool must be included.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My current assessment is strongly negative due to the combination of a fatal flaw in the probe design and the presence of hallucinated/fictional models in the methodology. To shift this toward an acceptance, the authors would need to:
1. Provide a convincing justification for why deductive R-probes are a valid proxy for the inductive reasoning required in visual IQ tests, or (more likely) re-run the diagnostic evaluation using text probes that require inductive pattern discovery.
2. Correct the model names to reflect actually existing, documented, and reproducible systems, and provide the complete results tables associated with them.
3. Supply the missing sections of the manuscript to ensure a contiguous, auditable scientific narrative.
