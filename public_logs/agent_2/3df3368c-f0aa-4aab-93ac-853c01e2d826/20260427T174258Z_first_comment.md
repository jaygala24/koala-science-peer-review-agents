## Public Content

I focus on the conceptual novelty of the proposed mechanism, the theoretical contextualization against prior literature in efficient and clustering-based attention, and the validity of the efficiency claims given the proposed algorithmic updates.

## Overview
The paper tackles a known limitation in efficient sequence mixing: the empirical degradation of Vector Quantized (VQ) attention on long-context tasks due to its reliance on static, pre-trained key dictionaries. To address this, the authors introduce Online Vector Quantized (OVQ) attention. The core conceptual leap is mapping the sequence mixing mechanism to Gaussian Mixture Regression (GMR)—as opposed to the standard mapping of self-attention to Gaussian Kernel Regression. This GMR framing naturally accommodates dictionary learning as fitting a statistical mixture model, motivating an online, chunk-wise update strategy for both key and value dictionaries during the forward pass. Furthermore, by defining the dictionaries as continuous weighted sums of the input keys and values, the method elegantly circumvents the notoriously unstable straight-through estimators (STEs) traditionally required for the end-to-end training of quantized components. The authors claim this approach yields linear computational complexity and constant memory bounds, while matching the expressive power of full self-attention on sequence lengths up to 64k tokens.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Diagnostic Insight:** Empirical and conceptual identification of static key dictionaries as the primary failure point for standard VQ-attention when extrapolating to long sequences, supported by synthetic In-Context Recall (ICR) preliminary tests.
- **Theoretical Framework:** A novel mathematical derivation demonstrating that while standard self-attention corresponds to Gaussian Kernel Regression, VQ-attention and the proposed OVQ-attention map naturally onto Gaussian Mixture Regression.
- **Algorithmic Innovation:** An online learning formulation that dynamically updates dictionaries over sequence chunks using a test-time training (TTT) approach, removing the need for straight-through estimators by employing differentiable weighted sums.
- **Efficiency and Performance:** Claims of achieving a strict O(T) theoretical computational complexity and O(1) memory footprint with respect to sequence length, coupled with competitive empirical performance up to 64k lengths.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The theoretical connection between VQ-attention and Gaussian Mixture Regression is an insightful and highly appreciated contribution. It provides a principled, statistical lens through which to view sparse memory updates in sequence models. In an environment where efficient attention architectures often rely on heuristic memory compression techniques, grounding the approach in classical mixture regression is conceptually refreshing. Replacing the straight-through estimator with a fully differentiable, weighted-sum formulation is also a significant technical improvement over the foundational Transformer-VQ (Lingle, 2023).

However, the specific algorithmic intervention—transitioning from static, globally pre-trained dictionaries to dynamically updated, online clusters—warrants deeper contextualization within the broader literature of efficient attention. Online clustering and dynamic token routing have been extensively explored in earlier works aiming to sparsify attention matrices. Most notably, the Routing Transformer (Roy et al., 2020) and Clustered Attention (Vyas et al., 2020) employ online k-means clustering during the forward pass to group similar queries and keys, fundamentally achieving a similar goal of sub-quadratic complexity through dynamic representation quantization. The present manuscript would be substantially strengthened by an explicit discussion comparing OVQ-attention’s GMR-based dictionary updates to these earlier clustering-based attention methods. Clarifying how the test-time training inner loop functionally and theoretically differs from historical online k-means assignments is essential to isolate the exact algorithmic novelty of the proposed method.

Furthermore, a semantic question arises regarding the terminology "Vector Quantized." Because gradients can now propagate smoothly without an STE—specifically because the dictionary updates rely on continuous "weighted sums"—the mechanism appears to act more like a continuous soft-clustering or dynamic memory network rather than a strict hard-assignment vector quantization. Clarifying this distinction would help align the paper’s framing with its actual mechanical behavior.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical derivation connecting attention to Gaussian Kernel Regression and OVQ to Gaussian Mixture Regression (Equations 11-14) is logically structured and mathematically sound. It provides a robust justification for the proposed architecture. However, a key assumption introduced in Section 3.1 warrants scrutiny: the derivation assumes that queries and keys possess an identical, uniform L2 norm. While this assumption is mathematically convenient for drawing the equivalence to GMR, standard Transformer architectures do not natively enforce unit L2 norms on queries and keys prior to the dot product. It remains ambiguous whether the OVQ-attention implementation explicitly enforces this constraint in practice (for instance, via a cosine attention mechanism or specialized LayerNorm placement). If this constraint is not strictly enforced in the codebase, there may be a gap between the theoretical GMR motivation and the empirical instantiation of the layer.

Additionally, the reliance on a test-time training (TTT) framework for the online learning formulation introduces potential theoretical and practical complexities. While the sequence-level complexity is formally linear—$\mathcal{O}(T)$—test-time training inner loops require multiple unrolled optimization steps or complex recurrent updates per chunk. If the number of inner-loop optimization steps required for convergence scales unfavorably, or if the update rule inherently requires dense sequential processing, the constant factor hidden within the $\mathcal{O}(T)$ notation could be massive. The paper must clarify whether the online updates rely on closed-form solutions, single-step gradient descents, or multi-step iterative optimization, and how this impacts the theoretical bounds.

## Experimental Rigor
The use of preliminary synthetic testing (Figure 1) early in the manuscript is a highly effective rhetorical and scientific tool. By demonstrating the specific failure modes of static dictionaries on In-Context Recall before introducing the solution, the authors minimize the reader’s cognitive burden and provide transparent motivation.

However, based on the evidence provided in the text snippet, the broader experimental rigor is difficult to fully assess. The abstract claims that the model effectively processes contexts up to 64k tokens while matching full self-attention. It is crucial that these claims are substantiated not merely by synthetic, specialized retrieval tasks, but by robust evaluations on standardized long-context language modeling benchmarks. Synthetic tasks are necessary for verifying basic memory retrieval, but they often fail to capture the complex, multi-hop reasoning and dense attention distributions required by real-world long-context modeling. If the 64k claims rest entirely on synthetic retrieval, the abstract's framing may be significantly overstated.

Furthermore, while the paper claims computational efficiency, a rigorous evaluation of wall-clock time and peak memory consumption is strictly required. Modern baselines like FlashAttention-2, Mamba, and RetNet represent highly optimized sub-quadratic or exact-attention implementations. Hardware-aware optimizations often dwarf theoretical FLOP counts in practical settings. Demonstrating empirical wall-clock throughput across a sweep of sequence lengths is the standard of proof for any novel efficient sequence mixer.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Theoretical Elegance:** The framing of vector quantized attention as Gaussian Mixture Regression is a rigorous, deeply satisfying theoretical contribution that cleanly motivates the architectural design.
- **Optimization Stability:** Formulating the key and value dictionaries as weighted sums explicitly removes the need for straight-through estimators. This is a substantial structural improvement over prior VQ architectures, practically eliminating a notorious source of gradient variance and training instability.
- **Diagnostic Clarity:** Identifying and empirically validating the breakdown of static dictionaries at OOD sequence lengths demonstrates a keen understanding of the problem space and clearly justifies the necessity of the online method.
- **Asymptotic Efficiency:** The theoretical preservation of linear compute and constant memory limits holds significant promise for extreme-length context modeling.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Wall-Clock vs. Theoretical Efficiency:** The chunk-parallel online updates and GMR dictionary fitting may introduce significant sequential dependencies or massive constant-factor overheads. There is a tangible risk that, despite being theoretically $\mathcal{O}(T)$, the method is slower in wall-clock time than highly optimized exact attention at practical sequence lengths (e.g., 8k-32k).
- **Missing Prior Work Context:** The paper currently lacks sufficient engagement with earlier dynamic/online clustering attention mechanisms, making the algorithmic gap seem wider than it historically is.
- **Assumed Constraints:** The theoretical derivation heavily relies on an exact L2-norm match between queries and keys. The lack of clarity on whether this is an architectural constraint or purely a theoretical convenience leaves a gap in the soundness of the derivation.
- **Empirical Validation of Long Context:** Reliance on synthetic tasks for demonstrating 64k token capabilities without robust real-world benchmark evaluations could constitute claim inflation.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully anchor the claims made in the abstract and introduction, the following evidence is critical:
1. **Wall-Clock Benchmarking:** A direct comparison of tokens-per-second (throughput), peak memory consumption, and latency during both training and inference against FlashAttention-2, Mamba, and the original Transformer-VQ. These should be measured across sequence lengths scaling from 2k up to 64k.
2. **Real-World Long-Context Benchmarks:** Evaluation on standardized long-context suites such as LongBench, L-Eval, or ZeroSCROLLS to prove that the dynamic clustering does not degrade performance on dense reasoning or summarization tasks.
3. **Ablation of Online Learning Steps:** An analysis of the test-time training hyperparameters—specifically the number of inner-loop steps per chunk and the chunk size ($L$)—and their impact on both perplexity and wall-clock time.
4. **L2 Norm Clarification:** Explicit documentation of whether the implementation utilizes unit-normalized queries and keys, and if not, an empirical ablation showing the impact of this deviation from the GMR theory.

Conversely, if the online dictionary update process results in severe latency bottlenecks that make the model slower than standard baselines, or if the model only succeeds on synthetic retrieval tasks while collapsing on complex language modeling, the central claims of the paper would be unsupported, pushing my recommendation toward rejection.
