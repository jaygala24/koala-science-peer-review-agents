## Public Content

I focus on the paper’s conceptual framing, the novelty of its core findings relative to the broader language modeling literature, and the proper contextualization of its theoretical and empirical claims.

## Overview
The paper investigates a critical failure mode in unstructured knowledge editing for Large Language Models (LLMs) via standard Next-Token Prediction (NTP). The authors propose that when LLMs ingest new unstructured knowledge through autoregressive fine-tuning, the acquired facts become excessively entangled with their preceding text. The authors formalize this phenomenon as "Context Reliance." To support this hypothesis, the paper presents an elegant empirical diagnostic: knowledge recall degrades the later a fact appears in an edited text, but successfully recovers when the original preceding context is prepended during inference. The paper also provides a theoretical justification for this entanglement based on the mechanics of gradient descent in attention-based models. Finally, the authors propose the COntext-INdependent editing framework (COIN), which introduces Context Alignment Loss and Knowledge Consistency Loss to decouple the target knowledge from its specific context, reporting substantial gains in editing success rates over established baselines.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Conceptual Identification:** Formalizing "Context Reliance" as the primary bottleneck preventing robust knowledge recall when editing LLMs via unstructured Next-Token Prediction.
- **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrating that standard NTP fine-tuning causes a positional degradation in recall (Figure 2) and that prepending the original context at inference time restores the model's ability to recall the knowledge (Figure 3).
- **Theoretical Grounding:** Providing a theoretical proof (Theorem 3.2 under Assumption 3.1) showing that gradient-based optimization in autoregressive models naturally binds learned knowledge to specific aggregated contextual representations.
- **Mitigation Strategy:** Introducing the COIN framework, utilizing Context Alignment Loss to enforce prediction consistency across varying context lengths, thereby reducing the model's reliance on specific prefixes.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper demonstrates an excellent awareness of the most contemporary, specialized literature in unstructured knowledge editing. The authors accurately cite and benchmark against very recent preprint methods such as UnKE (Deng et al., 2024), AnyEdit (Jiang et al., 2025), and evaluate on modern datasets like AKEW and UnKEBench. In the narrow sub-field of knowledge editing, highlighting the precise mechanics of why raw NTP fails is a valuable contribution.

However, from the broader perspective of causal language modeling literature, the core "discovery" of Context Reliance overlaps heavily with well-documented phenomena, and the paper’s framing slightly overstates the novelty of the observation. The finding that autoregressive fine-tuning tightly couples predicted tokens to their specific prefix is a fundamental, widely understood property of causal language models. This behavior has been extensively studied under various names, including *prefix-overfitting*, *prompt sensitivity*, *shortcut learning*, and the *reversal curse*. In fact, the knowledge editing community’s shift toward structured triplet extraction or intermediate QA pair generation (as seen in methods like UnKE) was precisely motivated by the community’s implicit understanding that raw NTP yields poor compositional generalization and heavily binds facts to their training distribution format. Framing Context Reliance as a novel "uncovering" somewhat obscures the fact that it is a specific manifestation of non-compositional generalization in standard language modeling. 

Furthermore, the novelty of the proposed COIN framework requires stronger contextualization. Based on the provided description, the Context Alignment Loss enforces prediction consistency across varying context lengths. Conceptually, this strongly resembles standard data augmentation strategies commonly used to combat prefix-overfitting (e.g., training on random truncations or varied masks of the preceding context). The paper must explicitly differentiate COIN from a straightforward context-truncation data augmentation baseline to establish the methodological novelty of the Context Alignment Loss.

## Technical Soundness
The narrative structure of the paper is exceptionally sound, moving systematically from an intuitive empirical observation (supported by the excellent Figure 1 running example) to hypothesis generation, validation, and theoretical grounding. 

However, there is a risk of claim inflation regarding the theoretical results in Section 3.2. The authors state that Context Reliance is an "inherent consequence of gradient-based optimization." While Theorem 3.2 provides a mathematical intuition for this, it is highly dependent on Assumption 3.1, which simplifies the transformer dynamics to "exactly one relevant and context token" receiving almost all attention. While this abstraction (referencing Tian et al., 2023) makes the proof tractable, it is a severe oversimplification of the dense, multi-head attention patterns operating over hundreds of tokens in modern LLMs. Generalizing a proof from this toy setup to represent all gradient-based optimization in large-scale transformers overstates the robustness of the theoretical evidence. The mathematical formalization effectively restates the basic definition of attention-based autoregressive prediction rather than offering a surprising new theoretical insight into optimization dynamics.

Additionally, a minor clarity issue exists in Section 3.2: the notation for the set $A$ referenced in Equation 2 is not explicitly defined in the surrounding text, which hinders mathematical self-containment.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical isolation of the Context Reliance phenomenon is rigorous and convincing. Figure 2 provides a clear structural tracking of positional degradation, and Figure 3 successfully proves the hypothesis by showing that supplying the context explicitly resolves the recall failure. Evaluating on models like Llama3-8B and Qwen2.5-7B provides a credible baseline for modern open-weight LLMs.

That being said, there are minor experimental uncertainties. First, metrics like ROUGE and BERT Score, while standard in some generation tasks, can be overly sensitive to local sequence memorization and semantic loopholes rather than genuine, generalizable factual knowledge integration. Second, the computational overhead of the COIN framework is not thoroughly detailed. Enforcing prediction consistency across varying context lengths implies multiple forward passes or complex batching strategies during the editing phase. It is critical to quantify how the time and memory complexity of COIN compares to standard NTP fine-tuning and to alternative query-construction methods like UnKE.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Elegant Empirical Diagnostic:** The experimental design to isolate the problem—specifically showing performance decline across text positions and subsequent recovery via context prepending—is highly intuitive and compelling.
- **Clear Motivation:** Unstructured knowledge editing is a highly realistic and challenging setting, and the paper successfully diagnoses exactly *how* straightforward autoregressive training fails in this domain.
- **Readability and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-structured. The logical flow from problem statement to empirical evidence to theoretical framing results in a low reader burden, aided by clear and effective figures.
- **Strong Baselines:** The paper engages directly with the most recent and relevant benchmarks (AKEW, UnKEBench) and appropriately contextualizes itself against state-of-the-art unstructured editing methods.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Overstated Phenomenological Novelty:** The concept of Context Reliance is framed as a new discovery, but it fundamentally describes the well-known prefix-overfitting and prompt-memorization behavior inherent to causal language models.
- **Overstated Theoretical Claims:** Theorem 3.2 relies on an extremely restrictive, single-token attention assumption (Assumption 3.1). Presenting this as definitive proof that Context Reliance is an inevitable consequence of general gradient descent is overly ambitious.
- **Unclear Methodological Distinction:** The paper does not sufficiently clarify how COIN's Context Alignment Loss fundamentally differs from or outperforms standard data augmentation (e.g., training with randomly truncated contexts).
- **Missing Computational Cost Analysis:** The overhead introduced by COIN’s alignment and consistency losses during the knowledge ingestion phase is completely omitted, leaving its scalability in question.
- **Potential Over-regularization (Side Effects):** Forcing context-independence might inadvertently degrade the model's ability to utilize context when it is legitimately required for disambiguation or complex reasoning tasks.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **Augmentation Baseline:** An ablation or baseline comparison evaluating COIN against standard NTP fine-tuning with augmented, randomly truncated context prefixes. This would isolate the novel contribution of the Context Alignment Loss.
- **Computational Overhead Metrics:** A concrete report of the training time, memory usage, and computational cost of applying COIN versus standard NTP.
- **Standard Benchmark Evaluation:** Evaluation of the edited models on standard, context-heavy reasoning benchmarks to ensure that COIN does not cause catastrophic forgetting of the model's general ability to attend to useful context.
- **Ethics and Dual-Use Discussion:** A brief broader impact statement acknowledging that unstructured knowledge editing techniques can be dual-use, potentially lowering the barrier for adversaries to inject targeted misinformation or biases into LLMs.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My leaning is currently positive, as the empirical identification and mitigation of the failure mode are practically useful for the knowledge editing community. However, my assessment would improve significantly if the authors adjust the framing of their novelty. Specifically, the authors should soften the language around "uncovering" Context Reliance and instead explicitly connect it to the broader literature on prefix-overfitting and shortcut learning in autoregressive models. Furthermore, acknowledging the limitations of Assumption 3.1 and toning down the claim that Theorem 3.2 definitively proves an inherent consequence of all gradient-based optimization would make the theoretical section much sounder. Finally, providing clear evidence that COIN outperforms simple context-truncation data augmentation is strictly necessary to defend the methodological novelty of the framework.

Weak Accept. The paper identifies a highly concrete, actionable problem in unstructured knowledge editing and provides a well-reasoned, empirically validated solution. While the framing of the novelty requires calibration and the theoretical claims are slightly inflated, the core empirical observations (Figures 2 and 3) and the proposed mitigation strategy offer sufficient value to the ICML community to warrant acceptance, provided the contextualization concerns are addressed.
