## Public Content

I focus on the conceptual originality, theoretical grounding, and placement of the proposed methodology within the broader landscape of feature distillation and representation alignment. I approach this submission by evaluating how the structural innovations—specifically the residual-dynamical perspective on multi-layer alignment and the adaptation of Matryoshka-style sparsity—advance the literature on Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models.

## Overview
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models pretrained on 2D web data heavily rely on post-hoc alignment to acquire 3D spatial awareness, a critical prerequisite for robotic manipulation. Existing approaches typically align a single, manually selected layer of the VLA backbone to a frozen 3D vision foundation model. Extending this to multiple layers is theoretically desirable to capture hierarchical spatial representations, but naïve implementations using independent projectors suffer from optimization instabilities and gradient interference. 

To address this, the authors propose ROCKET, a framework that reformulates multi-layer alignment as a residual dynamical system matching problem. The paper identifies that independent layer-wise projectors induce conflicting parameter updates. By employing a shared projector—capitalizing on the "cone effect" where hidden states converge into a similar feature space—ROCKET enforces gradient coherence across the residual stream. Furthermore, to prevent shallower layers from dominating the optimization process, the authors introduce a Matryoshka-style sparse activation scheme. The framework reportedly achieves state-of-the-art results on embodied benchmarks such as LIBERO and RoboTwin while consuming a fraction of the compute required by competing methods.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Identification of Gradient Interference**: Providing theoretical and empirical evidence (e.g., Figure 1) that multi-layer alignment with independent projectors causes gradient conflicts within the residual stream of modern transformers.
- **Shared Projector Formulation**: Proposing a layer-invariant shared projector to theoretically guarantee a signal-aligned lower bound for parameter updates, ensuring coherent feature distillation across depths.
- **Matryoshka-Style Sparse Activation**: Introducing a novel structural sparsity scheme that dynamically balances alignment losses across different depths to prevent early-layer dominance.
- **Empirical Efficiency and Performance**: Achieving a "98.5% state-of-the-art success rate" on the LIBERO benchmark while utilizing roughly 4% of the compute budget of prior state-of-the-art spatial alignment baselines.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper makes several substantial and elegant contributions to the literature on knowledge distillation and representation alignment. In classical feature distillation (e.g., FitNets and subsequent multi-layer distillation techniques in NLP and CV), it is standard practice to map intermediate student representations to teacher representations using independent linear or non-linear projectors. The authors successfully challenge this paradigm within the context of Pre-LN residual networks by viewing the forward pass as a dynamical system. 

By framing the multi-projector setup as a source of gradient interference—where the backward pass through depth conflicts due to uncoordinated projector mappings—the paper introduces a highly novel diagnostic lens. The transition from identifying this conflict to solving it via a shared projector is logically sound. Relying on the "cone effect" (the tendency for deep network representations to collapse into a narrower, directional feature space) to justify a single projector for multiple depths is an insightful synthesis of recent representation theory with applied VLA fine-tuning.

Additionally, the adaptation of Matryoshka representation learning is highly creative. While Matryoshka principles are typically applied to nested embedding dimensions to allow variable-cost retrieval, here they are adapted as a depth-wise sparse activation mechanism. Using predefined sparsity levels across layers to balance distillation capacity structurally solves the differential convergence rate problem without resorting to computationally expensive dynamic loss weighting. This is a fresh architectural contribution to multi-layer supervision literature.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical framework underpinning the shared projector is a strong point, though it relies on specific assumptions that warrant scrutiny. Section 3.2 effectively translates the intuition of residual updates into a clear formulation of gradient conflict. 

However, the theoretical justification relies heavily on the assumption that residual updates are sufficiently small, meaning that back-propagation through depth acts approximately as an identity function. While this is mathematically convenient to prove gradient coherence, this assumption often breaks down during the early stages of training or when using higher learning rates, where residual updates can be large and highly directional. The authors should clarify the boundaries of this assumption. Does the "cone effect" hold if one attempts to align a very early student layer with a very deep teacher layer? If the representational gap is too wide, forcing a shared projector might theoretically induce negative transfer.

There are also specific formulation errors that need correction. In Equation 11, the text refers to a signal-aligned lower bound, but the equation itself appears to be missing an inequality operator (e.g., `>=`), rendering it a fragmented expression rather than a formal mathematical bound. Additionally, in Equation 7, the assignment notation `v_l :=` is ambiguous depending on the exact span of the underbrace; while an expert reader can decipher it from context, formal precision should be tightened for an ICML audience.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical claims are exceptionally strong but are hindered by ambiguous phrasing and a lack of specific baseline transparency in the introductory and abstract text. 

The claim of "achieving 98.5% state-of-the-art success rate on LIBERO" is mathematically ambiguous. It is unclear whether this denotes an absolute task success rate of 98.5% across a specific suite (e.g., LIBERO-Object or LIBERO-Spatial), or if the model achieves 98.5% *of the performance* of the best SOTA model. If the baseline is 90% and ROCKET hits 88.65%, phrasing this as "98.5% SOTA success" risks being interpreted as claim inflation. This must be disambiguated in the text.

Similarly, claiming "only about 4% of the compute budget" is a massive reported reduction. Evaluating the rigor of this claim requires an explicit apples-to-apples definition. Is this 4% calculated in theoretical FLOPs, sample efficiency, hardware-specific GPU hours, or wall-clock time? Furthermore, does this compute budget account for the inference cost of the frozen 3D vision foundation model during the training phase? If the prior SOTA methods involve end-to-end pretraining while ROCKET relies purely on a lightweight distillation phase, the comparison may be slightly mismatched.

Finally, while the reliance on robust simulation benchmarks (LIBERO, LIBERO-Plus, RoboTwin) is standard, VLA models often suffer a significant sim-to-real gap due to sensor noise, heavy occlusions, and dynamic lighting in physical environments. The lack of real-world robotic evaluation slightly limits our understanding of how well the theoretical multi-layer alignment robustly transfers out-of-distribution.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Theoretical Elegance**: The formulation of multi-layer alignment as a residual dynamical matching problem provides a much-needed theoretical explanation for why naïve layer-matching fails. 
- **Methodological Innovation**: Combining a shared projector to resolve gradient conflicts (demonstrated effectively in Figure 1) with a Matryoshka-style sparse activation scheme is a compelling, structurally sound approach.
- **Efficiency Gains**: Even if the exact baselines require clarification, the architectural design fundamentally scales much better than independent-projector networks, rendering 3D spatial alignment significantly more accessible.
- **Clarity of Motivation**: Table 1 and the surrounding discussion lucidly establish the inconsistent layer-selection problem in prior single-layer works, strongly motivating the necessity of ROCKET's approach.

- **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The Matryoshka-style sparse activation relies on predefined sparsity levels for different layers. It is unclear how sensitive the training stability is to the tuning of these heuristics, or how robust the "training-free layer selection strategy" is when migrating to entirely new VLA backbones or 3D teacher models (e.g., swapping from VGGT to DINOv2 or DepthAnything).
- **Formatting Errors in Proofs**: The mathematical expression of the lower bound in Equation 11 is malformed.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To strengthen the paper and address the uncertainties, the authors should provide:
1. **Explicit Definitions of Main Claims**: A clear, absolute metric for the 98.5% success claim and a detailed breakdown of the 4% compute calculation (FLOPs/GPU hours) including the specific baseline model being compared.
2. **Ablation Studies on Matryoshka Sparsity**: An ablation isolating the performance of the shared projector *without* the Matryoshka sparse activation, to quantify exactly how much the depth-wise sparsity contributes to resolving the differential convergence rates.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis**: Documentation on how varying the predefined sparsity levels impacts both gradient coherence and final task success.
4. **Real-world Discussion**: If hardware experiments are out of scope, a dedicated limitation section discussing potential failure modes of this alignment strategy in noisy, real-world robotic deployments should be added.
