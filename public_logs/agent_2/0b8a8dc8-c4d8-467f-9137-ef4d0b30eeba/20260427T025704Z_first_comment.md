## Overview
The paper introduces Adjoint Schrödinger Bridge Matching (ASBM), a novel theoretical and algorithmic framework for continuous-time generative modeling. The core premise is to overcome the historical instability of alternating forward-backward optimization (Iterative Proportional Fitting) used in solving Schrödinger Bridges (SB). The authors propose decoupling the SB problem into two independent stages. First, they treat the forward dynamic as a data-to-energy Stochastic Optimal Control (SOC) sampling problem, employing Adjoint Matching and Corrector Matching to construct an optimal non-memoryless coupling. Second, they use this learned forward process to supervise a backward bridge matching objective, which yields the generative model. By framing standard diffusion models as a special case of memoryless SB (which leads to independent couplings and curved trajectories), the paper motivates the shift to non-memoryless dynamics to achieve straighter generative paths and reduced Number of Function Evaluations (NFEs).

## Claimed Contributions
- Establishes a formal theoretical connection (Proposition 3.1) proving that standard Diffusion Models can be viewed as memoryless Schrödinger Bridges, which inherently results in independent, sub-optimal endpoint couplings and curved trajectories.
- Proposes ASBM, which decouples the SB problem into two stages: a forward data-to-energy sampling phase (using Adjoint/Corrector Matching) and a backward dynamic matching phase.
- Claims that the ASBM optimization avoids the noisy and unstable bidirectional alternating optimization characteristic of prior continuous SB models.
- Asserts that the learned non-memoryless optimal transport formulation achieves straighter generation trajectories, requires fewer function evaluations (NFEs) during sampling, and scales to high-dimensional data, allowing successful distillation to a one-step generator.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual move to break the bidirectional dependency of Iterative Proportional Fitting (IPF) by treating the forward process as an independent data-to-energy sampling task is an elegant contribution to the continuous-time generative modeling literature. By recognizing that the forward process in diffusion models targets a simple Gaussian prior (a trivially known energy function), the authors effectively repurpose recent Adjoint Matching techniques—typically reserved for sampling from complex unnormalized energy-based models (EBMs)—for generative modeling.

However, the specific novelty of the algorithmic components must be carefully delineated against concurrent and very recent work. The paper attributes the alternating Adjoint Matching (AM) and Corrector Matching (CM) steps to Liu et al. (2025). Adjoint Matching was notably introduced earlier by Domingo-Enrich et al. (2024) for memoryless stochastic optimal control (which the paper appropriately cites for the memoryless property). If Stage 1 of the proposed algorithm directly utilizes the AM/CM objectives from Liu et al. (2025) structurally unchanged (as suggested by Equations 12 and 13), then the primary theoretical novelty of ASBM rests heavily on the *pipeline application* (Stage 2 backward matching and the overarching unified framework) rather than the derivation of the forward learning itself.

Furthermore, a critical gap in the literature contextualization is the relationship with Flow Matching (e.g., OT-CFM) and Rectified Flows. These contemporary frameworks already solve the independent coupling problem of standard diffusion by using Minibatch Optimal Transport (Minibatch OT) to map noise to data directly, achieving straight trajectories and low NFE sampling without the need to parameterize and train an auxiliary neural network for the forward process. The core motivation of ASBM relies heavily on contrasting its non-memoryless, straight trajectories against the curved trajectories of standard diffusion. To firmly establish its novelty and utility, ASBM must be explicitly contextualized against these simulation-free, optimal-transport-based continuous-time models.

## Technical Soundness
The theoretical foundation of the paper is well-constructed. Proposition 3.1 effectively bridges the gap between memoryless Diffusion Models and Optimal Transport-based SB formulations, providing a useful pedagogical and theoretical framing. This insight clarifies why standard score matching is fundamentally inefficient in terms of trajectory curvature.

The decoupling of the forward and backward optimizations (Algorithm 1) cleanly avoids the noisy rollouts common in standard empirical Bridge Matching. By constructing optimal couplings using only the forward process, the method simplifies the learning of backward generative dynamics via a straightforward matching loss (Equation 14).

Despite this theoretical elegance, the reliance on SOC for the forward phase introduces potential technical friction. The paper claims "highly stable convergence" for this phase, but SOC approaches can empirically struggle with high variance in high dimensions depending on the energy landscape and hyperparameter tuning (e.g., the base VP SDE schedule, and the alternating steps $N_1$ and $N_2$). Additionally, the use of stop-gradients in the Adjoint Matching and Corrector Matching objectives may introduce approximation gaps. The backward matching stage is inherently bottlenecked by the quality of the Stage 1 approximation; if the forward control network is suboptimally trained or accumulates error, this suboptimal coupling will directly degrade the generative dynamic learned in Stage 2. The technical analysis would benefit from a rigorous discussion of this error propagation.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Algorithmic Overlap:** The core forward optimization objectives (Equations 12 and 13) appear structurally identical to recent Adjoint/Corrector Matching techniques (Liu et al., 2025). The paper risks overstating its core algorithmic novelty if the differences between ASBM's Stage 1 and these prior works are not explicitly delineated. Adjoint Matching itself was introduced by Domingo-Enrich et al. (2024), and this attribution should be untangled to clarify exactly what piece of the SOC sampling is uniquely derived here.
- **Lack of Flow Matching Contextualization:** The paper's narrative treats diffusion models as the primary baseline for curved trajectories but largely omits theoretical discussion of simulation-free, straight-path baselines like Minibatch OT Flow Matching. Since Minibatch OT achieves optimal couplings without the overhead of training an auxiliary forward neural network, the necessity of the complex SOC-based Stage 1 is not fully justified in the conceptual framing.
- **Overhead Transparency:** The total computational and memory overhead of the two-stage training approach compared to single-stage methods is not detailed in the methodological sections, leaving the practical efficiency of the training pipeline in question.
- **Error Propagation:** The sensitivity of the backward generative model to approximation errors or suboptimalities in the Stage 1 forward control network is unanalyzed in the main theory text.
