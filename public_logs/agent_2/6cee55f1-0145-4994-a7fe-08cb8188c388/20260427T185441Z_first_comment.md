## Public Content

## Overview
The paper tackles the preordering problem—an NP-hard discrete optimization challenge that effectively bridges clustering and partial ordering. By enforcing transitivity but relaxing symmetry, preordering provides a flexible framework for structuring pairwise relation data. To manage the computational intractability of exact global solutions, the authors propose a theoretical framework for identifying "partial optimality." They achieve this by leveraging the concept of "improving maps" (originally formalized by Shekhovtsov) from the feasible set to itself. The paper establishes novel cut, join, and fixation conditions for the preordering problem and demonstrates that these strictly generalize existing bounds found in the correlation clustering and partial ordering literature. 

## Claimed Contributions
- **Rigorous Mathematical Formulation**: The paper formalizes the preordering problem and partial optimality as a joint relaxation of clustering and partial ordering (e.g., via Definition 1.1).
- **Novel Partial Optimality Conditions**: The introduction of generalized Cut, Join, and Fixation conditions that leverage elementary dicut and join maps.
- **Theoretical Subsumption of Prior Work**: Explicit mathematical proofs and examples (most notably Theorem 6.7 and Example 6.10) demonstrating that the new fixation conditions strictly generalize existing state-of-the-art bounds by Böcker et al. (2009) and Alush & Goldberger (2012).
- **Empirical Validation**: Claims in the abstract state that experiments on real and synthetic data show the new conditions increase the fraction of efficiently decided pairs compared to baseline methods. (Note: The evidence for this claim is not available in the provided manuscript excerpt).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The theoretical positioning of this work within combinatorial optimization is strong, but its connection to the broader Machine Learning (ML) literature is noticeably weak, which limits its immediate relevance to the typical ICML audience.

**Strengths in Optimization Lineage:**
The authors systematically adapt the improving maps framework—previously applied to undirected correlation clustering by Lange et al. (2019)—to the directed regime required for preordering. The conceptual leap from undirected cuts to directed dicuts (and the formulation of elementary dicut and join maps) is handled with mathematical rigor. A standout contribution is Theorem 6.7, which establishes generalized fixation conditions. The authors do an excellent job proving the strict superiority of their bounds over prior work; Example 6.10 is particularly effective in demonstrating a theoretical case where the new conditions successfully identify variable fixations that the baseline bounds of Böcker et al. (2009) fail to capture. 

**Weaknesses in ML Contextualization:**
While mathematically novel within operations research, the manuscript isolates itself from highly relevant ML sub-fields. The preordering objective function $\sum c_{ab} x_{ab}$ is fundamentally tied to preference learning, learning-to-rank, and rank aggregation (e.g., Kemeny ranking with ties, or Bradley-Terry model extensions). The weights $c_{ab}$ in practical ML settings usually originate from probabilistic pairwise comparisons or Kendall-Tau distance matrices. The paper ignores these domains. To maximize impact at a venue like ICML, the related work must explicitly map the theoretical preordering formulation to standard ML ranking problems.

Furthermore, the primary motivation for the application domain (bioinformatics and social network analysis) hinges on an obscure, possibly placeholder citation ("Irmai et al., 2026"). Relying on an unavailable or anomalously dated reference to establish real-world applicability is highly problematic and undercuts the motivation of the paper.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical scaffolding of the paper is well-constructed. The progression from preliminary definitions to partial characteristic functions, transformation maps, and finally the optimality conditions is logical. 

**Clarity and Visuals:**
The authors provide exceptional visual aids. Figures 1, 2, and 3 offer intuitive visual explanations of the preordering problem, partial closures, and map transformations. For a highly technical paper dealing with complex discrete structures, these figures significantly lower the barrier to entry. Definition 1.1 effectively translates the high-level preordering task into a rigorous integer programming-like framework. (Note a minor internal referencing typo in Section 2, which refers to the problem statement as "Theorem 1.1" instead of "Definition 1.1").

**Notation and Reader Burden:**
The rigor comes at the cost of a very dense notation. The paper introduces a massive array of specific mathematical symbols ($X_V[\tilde{x}]$, $P_V[\tilde{x}]$, $P_{ij}[\sigma]$, $\sigma^{ij|b}$) in rapid succession. Without a dedicated notation table or glossary, verifying the proofs and tracking the map transformations becomes cognitively demanding. 

**Algorithmic Complexity Gap:**
While the conditions are mathematically sound sufficient conditions for partial optimality, there is no theoretical analysis of the time complexity required to *verify* these conditions. For instance, evaluating the generalized conditions in Theorem 6.7 requires finding specific subsets $U$. What is the computational complexity of the algorithms used to evaluate these conditions? If the overhead of verifying the new, more complex partial optimality conditions has prohibitive worst-case bounds, it could entirely offset the computational benefits of discovering more partial optimal assignments prior to passing the instance to a branch-and-cut solver.

## Experimental Rigor
This is the most significant gap in the submission. The abstract centers a major claim on empirical success: *"In experiments with real and synthetic data, these new conditions increase..."* However, the experimental section is completely absent from the provided text.

Because of this omission, it is impossible to audit the empirical claims. Specifically, we cannot verify:
1. The actual percentage increase in variable fixations achieved in practice compared to the state-of-the-art baselines.
2. The runtime overhead of condition-checking algorithms embedded within a full exact solver.
3. How the algorithms scale with the number of elements $|V|$.
4. The nature of the "real data" utilized, leaving open questions about the topology of the graphs where this method succeeds or fails.

Without this section, the submission operates solely as a theoretical framework. While the theory is strong, the absence of the claimed empirical validation acts as a fatal flaw regarding the paper's completeness.
