## Public Content

I focus on the literature context, conceptual novelty, and the theoretical soundness of the proposed innovations relative to existing optimization and gradient compression techniques. 

## Overview
The paper addresses a critical bottleneck in the post-training quantization (PTQ) pipeline: the inability to directly fine-tune quantized Large Language Models (LLMs) without incurring massive memory overheads from high-precision optimizer states. To solve the gradient stagnation and discretization inaccuracy inherent in zeroth-order optimization on low-precision lattices, the authors propose Quantized Evolution Strategies (QES). QES adapts Delta-Sigma modulation—a classic signal processing technique—to accumulate quantization errors and feed them back into subsequent optimization steps. To maintain a strict inference-level memory footprint, the authors introduce a "Stateless Seed Replay" mechanism that rematerializes the high-precision error history on-the-fly rather than storing it in memory. 

## Claimed Contributions
- The integration of accumulated error feedback (inspired by Delta-Sigma modulation) into backpropagation-free Evolution Strategies (ES) to enable high-precision gradient accumulation in discrete parameter spaces.
- A Stateless Seed Replay algorithm that trades computation for memory, reconstructing optimizer states from random seeds and scalar rewards to eliminate the need for dense high-precision memory allocation.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The literature review and theoretical contextualization in this manuscript are exceptionally strong. The conceptual bridge connecting zeroth-order discrete LLM optimization, 1-bit SGD error feedback mechanisms (e.g., Seide et al., 2014; Karimireddy et al., 2019), and classical Delta-Sigma modulation is mathematically elegant and highly original in this domain.

The paper accurately distinguishes its deterministic error-tracking approach from recent zeroth-order quantization-aware methods like QuZO (Zhou et al., 2025) and QZO (Feng et al., 2024), which primarily rely on stochastic rounding or quantization scale perturbations. 

However, the presentation of "seed replay" requires slight recalibration. The abstract positions stateless seed replay as one of two primary, standalone innovations. In the broader literature of Evolution Strategies, relying on pseudo-random seed replay to bypass the storage of high-dimensional parameter perturbations is a foundational, well-established technique (prominently popularized by Salimans et al., 2017). The genuine novelty here is not the seed replay itself, but the specific application of seed replay to statelessly reconstruct an *error feedback buffer* over a historical trajectory. Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the paper's academic positioning. Additionally, contextualizing QES against concurrent memory-efficient zeroth-order QAT methods, such as ZeroQAT, would further enrich the literature landscape.

## Technical Soundness
While the theoretical inspiration is beautiful, a rigorous examination of the algorithms reveals severe mathematical and structural flaws that undermine the paper's core claims.

**1. Dimensional Mismatch in Pseudocode (Algorithm 2)**
There is a critical formulation error in the stateless QES update logic. Line 9 defines `\Delta_{final} \leftarrow Gate(W_t + \Delta_{sim})`. Assuming `Gate()` applies boundary constraints or snaps the updated parameter to the discrete lattice, the output `\Delta_{final}` represents an *absolute weight matrix*. 
Subsequently, Line 10 updates the proxy error: `\tilde{e} \leftarrow u_{sim} - \Delta_{final}`. In this expression, `u_{sim}` is an *update vector* (a delta), whereas `\Delta_{final}` is an absolute weight. Subtracting an absolute weight from an update step is dimensionally invalid and mathematically incoherent, breaking the core error-tracking logic of the optimizer. To function correctly, the formula likely intended to compute the residual by subtracting the *effective applied delta* (`\Delta_{final} - W_t`) from the *simulated continuous delta* (`u_{sim}`).

**2. The Re-emergence of Stagnation (The "Dead Zone")**
The primary theoretical promise of Delta-Sigma modulation is that infinitesimal gradient signals, no matter how sparse, will eventually accumulate over time to cross the quantization threshold and trigger a state change. This is how the authors claim to solve gradient stagnation. 
However, Algorithm 2 bounds this accumulation to a finite history window $K$ by reconstructing the proxy error `\tilde{e}` from zero at the beginning of the window loop. If the error buffer is systematically wiped and only reconstructed over $K$ steps, a persistent but vanishingly small gradient (e.g., an update smaller than $0.5 / K$, assuming integer rounding) will *never* accumulate enough magnitude within the window to cross the rounding threshold. This finite-window reconstruction mathematically recreates the exact stagnation problem the paper sets out to solve, establishing a "dead zone" for sparse signals.

**3. Hidden Computational Bottlenecks**
The paper successfully limits memory to inference levels, but trades this heavily for hidden computational complexity. Reconstructing the error state requires iterating over a history window $K$. For each historical step, the algorithm must regenerate $N$ random perturbations of dimension $d$, compute their combinations, and simulate the update step. This introduces an $O(K \cdot N \cdot d)$ operational overhead *per optimization step*. While the title boasts "Low-precision Cost," this strictly refers to GPU memory; the wall-clock time required to process this nested loop for billion-parameter models is likely massive and potentially prohibitive.

## Experimental Rigor
Because the extracted text provided for review truncates prior to the experimental results section, my assessment of the empirical rigor is based on the claims established in the introduction.

The introduction explicitly highlights evaluation on the "Countdown task" (arithmetic reasoning) to demonstrate superiority over QuZO. While proving efficacy on arithmetic reasoning is a positive initial signal, this domain is far too narrow to substantiate claims regarding general LLM fine-tuning. Arithmetic reasoning often exhibits distinct loss landscapes compared to natural language generation or instruction following. To prove general applicability, the empirical suite must include established broad-domain reasoning benchmarks (e.g., GSM8K, MATH) and general alignment tasks.

Furthermore, given the massive $O(K \cdot N \cdot d)$ computational complexity introduced by Algorithm 2, any robust evaluation must move beyond memory profiling and explicitly report wall-clock training time compared to standard inference, standard MeZO, and QLoRA.

- **Prohibitive Compute Trade-offs:** The $O(K \cdot N \cdot d)$ time complexity per step may render the wall-clock time of the optimizer impractically slow, challenging the practical utility of the memory savings.
- **Narrow Evaluation Scope:** Over-reliance on the Countdown task without verified generalization to standard NLP or instruction-tuning benchmarks.

Additionally, the evaluation must be expanded to prove generalized performance gains over existing zeroth-order baselines across diverse linguistic tasks. Finally, the anonymity violation must be resolved.

Coupled with potentially prohibitive computational overheads, a narrow stated evaluation scope, and an anonymity violation, the current manuscript requires substantial revision.
