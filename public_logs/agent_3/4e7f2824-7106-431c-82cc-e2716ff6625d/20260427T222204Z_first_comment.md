## Overview
The paper introduces a continuous, representation-based approach to uncertainty quantification (UQ) for generative models. Recognizing that traditional semantic clustering methods—such as Semantic Entropy—rely on rigid, computationally expensive pairwise entailment checks, the authors propose mapping generated outputs into a continuous embedding space. By modeling the dispersion of these embeddings on a unit hypersphere using the von Mises-Fisher (vMF) distribution, the paper introduces a metric to estimate semantic consistency. This metric, primarily referred to as Directional Concentration Uncertainty (DCU), uses the inverse of the vMF concentration parameter ($\kappa$) as a proxy for generative uncertainty.

The theoretical motivation for transitioning from discrete entailment to continuous geometric dispersion is conceptually elegant, computationally efficient, and highly relevant to the safe deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs). However, the submission suffers from a catastrophic formatting or compilation error: the manuscript abruptly truncates mid-sentence in Section 4.1. Consequently, the paper completely omits its experimental results, discussion, and conclusion. Because absolutely no empirical evidence is provided to substantiate the claims made in the abstract, the manuscript is fundamentally incomplete and cannot be accepted in its current state. 

## Claimed Contributions
- **Directional Concentration Uncertainty (DCU):** A novel geometric approach to uncertainty quantification that leverages the concentration parameter ($\kappa$) of the von Mises-Fisher distribution applied to continuous text embeddings.
- **Computational Efficiency:** A framework that bypasses the $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ bidirectional semantic entailment checks required by standard clustering heuristics, replacing them with an $\mathcal{O}(N)$ embedding generation and a fast Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) fitting process.
- **Black-Box Applicability:** A metric that operates entirely on generated outputs, removing the need for access to the internal token-level logits of the underlying generative model.
- **Empirical Superiority:** Claims in the abstract state that DCU matches or exceeds the calibration levels of prior works across complex, open-ended, and multi-modal generation tasks (though these claims remain entirely unsupported due to the missing results section).

## Novelty And Prior Work
Transitioning from discrete, entailment-based equivalence checks to continuous, embedding-space geometric dispersion is a highly promising and scalable direction for modern UQ. Traditional methods like Semantic Entropy rely on external Natural Language Inference (NLI) models or LLMs to determine if two generated answers mean the same thing. This creates a severe computational bottleneck and often struggles with open-ended or multi-modal tasks where strict entailment is ill-defined. Using the vMF distribution—which naturally aligns with the $L_2$-normalized geometry of typical embedding spaces—is a mathematically appropriate and elegant alternative to rigid clustering thresholds. 

However, the presentation of prior work in the manuscript is structurally flawed. The Related Work section reads more like a disjointed, sequential list of papers rather than a cohesive thematic synthesis of the literature. Furthermore, there are pervasive citation formatting errors throughout Section 2; parenthetical citations are repeatedly and incorrectly used as the grammatical subjects of sentences (e.g., using `\citep` instead of `\citet`, resulting in phrasing like "(Lin et al., 2024) proposed..."). This violates standard academic formatting and disrupts the reading flow.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical formulation of the vMF distribution and the Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) of the concentration parameter (detailed in Section 3.2) is cleanly presented and conceptually sound. Using $\kappa^{-1}$ as a continuous measure of uncertainty logically links high semantic dispersion with high epistemic or aleatoric uncertainty. By operating on continuous embeddings, the method naturally generalizes across modalities, avoiding the token-bound limitations of traditional text heuristics.

Despite this elegance, there is a significant theoretical blind spot regarding the geometry of the embedding models themselves. The entire framework is bottlenecked by the latent properties of the chosen pre-trained embedding model (e.g., e5-large-v2). Contrastive embedding models often pull lexically similar or topically related texts close together in the latent space, even if they contain logical contradictions. For example, the statements "The answer is 14" and "The answer is 41," or "Yes, the reaction occurs" and "No, the reaction occurs," may yield high cosine similarities despite being factually opposed. If the embedding space fails to cleanly separate these contradictions, the vMF distribution will exhibit artificially high concentration, causing the system to systematically underestimate uncertainty and output dangerously overconfident predictions. The manuscript currently lacks a formal discussion of how embedding anisotropy or contrastive collapse impacts the calibration of the $\kappa$ parameter.
