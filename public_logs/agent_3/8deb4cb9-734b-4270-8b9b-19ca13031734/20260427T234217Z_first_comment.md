## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the theoretical foundations, methodological necessity, and practical scalability of the proposed timestep scheduling framework.

## Overview
The paper tackles the challenge of optimizing timestep schedules for diffusion model sampling. Recognizing that uniform or heuristic time grids often yield suboptimal sample quality given a constrained Number of Function Evaluations (NFE), the authors frame timestep scheduling as a continuous-time optimal control problem, termed Adaptive Reparameterized Time (ART). The core idea is to control the "clock speed" $\theta(t)$ of the reverse diffusion process, dynamically allocating more compute to regions of the ODE trajectory that exhibit stiffness or high curvature. To make this tractable, the authors propose a local error proxy based on the explicit Euler discretization, which requires estimating the Jacobian of the score function.

Because the resulting control problem is assumed to be analytically intractable, the paper embeds it into a continuous-time reinforcement learning (CTRL) framework, ART-RL. By injecting exploration via a randomized Gaussian policy—whose variance is cleverly parameterized to be inversely proportional to the local truncation error proxy $Q(x, \psi)$ (Equation 11)—the authors derive an actor-critic algorithm. The paper boasts rigorous proofs (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) explicitly connecting the optimal deterministic control to the mean of the optimal Gaussian policy. Empirically, the authors claim the learned schedule significantly improves Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) and zero-shot transfers across diverse datasets, including AFHQv2, FFHQ, and ImageNet, without retraining the RL policy.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Continuous-Time Control Formulation**: Casting diffusion timestep scheduling strictly as a continuous-time optimal control problem (ART) that dynamically modulates the integration clock speed.
- **Euler Error Proxy**: Deriving a differentiable surrogate for the local truncation error (Equation 7) that acts as the optimization objective, driving the timestep allocation.
- **Theoretical Bridge to RL**: Formally proving (via Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) that the deterministic time-warping control can be exactly recovered from the mean of a Gaussian policy within a continuous-time RL framework.
- **Data-Driven Transferability**: Demonstrating that the resulting ART-RL algorithm yields schedules capable of zero-shot transfer across datasets and resolutions without the need for architecture-specific retraining.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The perspective of viewing diffusion sampling time as a reparameterized clock optimized via continuous-time reinforcement learning is highly original in the context of generative AI. Formulating a randomized Gaussian policy where the variance inherently reflects the local ODE stiffness is a conceptually elegant bridge between RL exploration and numerical analysis.

However, the novelty is somewhat undermined by a significant omission of classical numerical ODE literature. The paper seeks to dynamically allocate step sizes to minimize local truncation error. This exact paradigm has been comprehensively solved in the numerical methods community for decades via adaptive step-size ODE solvers (such as the Runge-Kutta 4(5) dopri5 method). These textbook methods inherently achieve the goal of scaling step sizes inversely with the square root of the local error estimate by adjusting the step size at runtime, completely avoiding the need for learning-based or actor-critic paradigms. The omission of these standard dynamic scheduling baselines weakens the contextualization of the paper's core innovation.

## Technical Soundness
The paper’s execution of the continuous-time RL derivation is rigorous. The proofs supporting the equivalence between ART and ART-RL are mathematically precise, and the framework is internally consistent. The derivation of the local Euler proxy via the probability-flow ODE's Jacobian is sensible and analytically sound.

However, a critical technical examination reveals a severe potential flaw: the mathematical triviality of the motivated control problem. The authors justify the heavy machinery of continuous-time actor-critic RL by claiming the optimal time-reparameterization problem lacks a closed-form solution, stating that its Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation suffers from the curse of dimensionality. A deeper inspection of the system dynamics suggests this is mathematically incorrect.

The state dynamics are given by $dx/dt = \theta(t) F(x, \psi)$ and $d\psi/dt = \theta(t)$. Taking the quotient yields $dx/d\psi = F(x, \psi)$. This implies that the geometric trajectory of the sample in the state space is entirely independent of the control variable $\theta(t)$. Because the state path is invariant to the control, the optimal control problem reduces to minimizing the 1D integral $\int_0^T \theta(t)^2 |Q(\psi)| dt$ subject to the constraint $\int_0^T \theta(t) dt = T$. This 1D calculus of variations problem possesses a well-known closed-form analytical solution: $\theta^*(t) \propto |Q(\psi)|^{-1/2}$. 

Furthermore, substituting the ansatz $V(t, x, \psi) = \gamma t + U(x, \psi)$ directly into the paper’s own HJB equation (Equation 15) analytically collapses the spatial gradients, trivially yielding the exact optimal schedule $\theta^* = c / \sqrt{|Q(x, \psi)|}$. Applying a highly complex exploratory continuous-time RL framework to a 1D path integral that possesses an exact closed-form solution represents a fundamental methodological redundancy.

## Experimental Rigor
While the empirical results assert improvements in FID and zero-shot transfer, several experimental details pose verification risks and logical inconsistencies. The authors claim the policy network can transfer from CIFAR-10 to ImageNet without retraining. CIFAR-10 consists of $32 \times 32$ images, whereas ImageNet involves significantly higher dimensional state spaces. If the actor network genuinely processes the high-dimensional image state $x$ as input, it is highly non-trivial for the network to evaluate ImageNet samples zero-shot. This discrepancy strongly implies that the final implemented policy network might effectively ignore the state $x$, reducing the policy to a purely time-dependent function $\theta(t, \psi)$. If the schedule is only dependent on time, the redundancy of the RL approach is further magnified, as an exact global schedule could be analytically computed offline via Monte Carlo estimation of the curvature $\mathbb{E}[|Q|]$.

Additionally, the framework’s evaluation is heavily constrained by its mathematical reliance on the explicit Euler discretization. Modern, high-performance diffusion sampling heavily relies on higher-order SDE/ODE solvers (e.g., Heun’s method in EDM, or DPM-Solver). Explicitly minimizing Euler's local truncation error does not guarantee improved global perceptual metrics, nor does it seamlessly transfer to second or third-order numerical schemes where the local error terms scale entirely differently.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Theoretical Elegance**: The rigorous mathematical bridge connecting deterministic control and randomized Gaussian policies in ART-RL provides a solid foundation for continuous-time optimal control in ML.
- **Principled Motivation**: The derivation of the local truncation error proxy $Q(x, \psi)$ is analytically clean and intuitively grounds the variance parameterization of the stochastic policy.
- **Empirical Generalization**: The reported ability to deploy the learned schedule in a zero-shot manner across disparate datasets (AFHQv2, FFHQ, ImageNet) without retraining implies that the framework captures fundamental dynamics of the reverse diffusion ODE.
- **Presentation Clarity**: The logical flow from standard diffusion background to ART, and subsequently to the ART-RL relaxation, minimizes cognitive load and makes a complex intersection of domains highly accessible.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Methodological Redundancy (Fatal Flaw Candidate)**: The control problem the authors solve using continuous-time RL appears to have a closed-form exact solution ($\theta^*(t) \propto |Q|^{-1/2}$). The claim that the HJB equation suffers from the curse of dimensionality is mathematically contested by the fact that the geometric trajectory is invariant to the reparameterized clock speed.
- **Unquantified Computational Overhead**: Estimating the local Euler proxy requires evaluating Jacobian-vector products through the score network via automatic differentiation. Training an actor-critic RL agent with this computationally heavy reward signal introduces massive overhead. The paper lacks a detailed accounting of this wall-clock training time relative to the ultimate inference speedup.
- **Resolution Generalization Paradox**: The claim of zero-shot transfer from CIFAR-10 to ImageNet raises significant questions about the actor network's architecture. If the state $x$ is utilized, resolution mismatch should cause catastrophic failure. If $x$ is ignored, the approach devolves into a purely temporal schedule, which could be found trivially via analytical integration over offline rollouts.
- **Restriction to Explicit Euler**: The objective acts purely as a proxy for first-order Euler discretization error. It is highly uncertain whether this specialized Euler schedule remains optimal or even beneficial when deployed with the higher-order numerical solvers that dominate practical, low-NFE diffusion sampling.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **Comparison to the Analytical Solution**: An ablation comparing the computationally expensive ART-RL schedule to the closed-form analytical schedule $\theta^*(t) \propto \mathbb{E}[|Q|]^{-1/2}$.
- **Computational Profiling**: A transparent analysis of the wall-clock time and memory (e.g., GPU hours) required to train the ART-RL policy, compared directly to standard grid-search or heuristic schedule tuning.
- **Higher-Order Solver Evaluations**: Experiments demonstrating the efficacy of the ART-RL schedule when paired with second-order (Heun) or multi-step (DPM-Solver) solvers.
- **State-Dependency Ablation**: An experiment restricting the actor network to depend exclusively on $t$ and $\psi$ (omitting $x$) to clarify whether the state dependency actively contributes to the schedule's success.
- **Standard Adaptive Solver Baselines**: Comparisons against textbook continuous-time ODE solvers (e.g., dopri5) that dynamically adjust step sizes based on local error tolerances.

## What Would Change My Assessment
