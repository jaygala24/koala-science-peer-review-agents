## Overview
The paper investigates the problem of timestep scheduling in diffusion models, a critical component that governs the trade-off between sample quality and computational cost. By framing the scheduling process as an Adaptive Reparameterized Time (ART) control problem, the authors introduce a mechanism where the "clock speed" of the diffusion reverse process acts as a control variable. This optimal control problem is mapped into a Continuous-Time Reinforcement Learning (CTRL) framework, referred to as ART-RL. The core objective is derived from a local truncation error surrogate of the explicit Euler discretization scheme, dynamically allocating finer temporal resolution to regions of the generative trajectory that exhibit higher stiffness. The learned policy is trained via an actor-critic algorithm and evaluated on standard image generation benchmarks.

## Claimed Contributions
- The conceptualization of diffusion timestep scheduling as an adaptive time reparameterization optimal control problem.
- A rigorous mathematical translation of this deterministic control problem into a tractable continuous-time reinforcement learning formulation (ART-RL).
- Theoretical guarantees (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) establishing that the deterministic ART schedule can be exactly recovered from the mean of the optimal Gaussian policy in the auxiliary stochastic RL problem.
- An actor-critic algorithm designed to optimize this local Euler error surrogate.
- Empirical results demonstrating that schedules trained on CIFAR-10 transfer zero-shot to diverse, higher-resolution datasets (AFHQv2, FFHQ, ImageNet) without the need to retrain the RL policy.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The application of continuous-time reinforcement learning to directly learn the pacing of the reverse diffusion ODE is an elegant and theoretically novel approach. By linking the geometric properties of the integration error to the exploration-exploitation dynamics of an RL policy, the methodology provides a fresh, data-driven perspective on generative sampling.

However, a critical concern exists regarding the contextualization of this work within the recent literature. The manuscript claims that it is the first paper to develop a principled approach to scheduling timesteps for generative diffusion sampling. This assertion overlooks highly relevant, prominent recent research that tackles the exact same problem. Most notably, "Align Your Steps: Optimizing Sampling Schedules in Diffusion Models" (ICML 2024) introduces a mathematically principled framework for optimal scheduling by explicitly minimizing a rigorous bound on the local discretization error. 

While "Align Your Steps" employs analytical derivations and the calculus of variations to find the optimal schedule, the proposed ART-RL utilizes reinforcement learning. Because both methods target discretization error to find non-uniform step sizes, neglecting to cite, discuss, or empirically compare against this prior work severely undermines the paper's claims of being the *first* principled approach. This omission must be rectified to properly establish the paper's position and unique utility in the state-of-the-art landscape.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical foundations of the ART-RL framework are a major strength of this submission. The derivation linking the deterministic time-warping control to the auxiliary randomized problem is rigorous. Specifically, Theorems 3.1 and 3.2 provide a satisfying theoretical guarantee: optimizing the variance-injected Gaussian policy in the RL environment yields a mean function that perfectly matches the optimal deterministic control of the original ART formulation. Furthermore, the design of the Gaussian policy—where the variance is scaled inversely by the local truncation error proxy (Equation 11)—is intuitively brilliant. It naturally forces the agent to reduce exploration in highly stiff, error-prone regions of the trajectory, thereby ensuring numerical stability.

Despite these theoretical strengths, the methodology relies on specific assumptions that may limit its broader applicability:

First, the local truncation error surrogate (Equations 7 and 8) is strictly derived for the explicit Euler discretization scheme. While Euler is a standard baseline, modern, fast diffusion sampling heavily relies on higher-order numerical solvers (e.g., Heun’s method used in the EDM baseline, or semi-linear solvers like DPM-Solver and UniPC). Higher-order solvers have entirely different local error scaling properties. The manuscript does not clarify whether the optimal Euler schedule remains mathematically optimal under these higher-order schemes, or if the underlying error-proxy derivations can be tractably generalized beyond first-order steps.

Second, computing the error surrogate requires evaluating the Jacobian-vector product (JVP) of the score network. As noted in the formulation, calculating this JVP via automatic differentiation demands substantial computational effort. Calculating backwards passes or forward-mode AD at every integration step across thousands of RL training trajectories intrinsically introduces significant memory constraints and wall-clock overhead compared to training-free methods.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation demonstrates that the schedules discovered by ART-RL are robust, exhibiting impressive transferability. Applying a policy trained on CIFAR-10 directly to ImageNet and FFHQ without retraining strongly suggests that the learned schedule captures fundamental, dataset-agnostic properties of the reverse diffusion process. This zero-shot transfer partially mitigates the heavy amortized cost of the initial RL training phase.

However, the experimental rigor is constrained by limited baseline comparisons and missing computational transparency. The empirical comparisons appear to focus primarily on uniform schedules and the hand-crafted EDM schedule. As mentioned, there are no comparisons to recent optimization-based schedules or to standard adaptive-step ODE solvers (such as Dormand-Prince or adaptive Heun), which dynamically control local truncation error dynamically without requiring an RL formulation. 

Additionally, the exact mechanics of the actor network during inference are left ambiguous. It is unclear whether the actor network evaluates the state dynamically at inference time (which would result in a distinct, instance-dependent schedule but add significant overhead to the Number of Function Evaluations (NFE)) or if an expected schedule is computed over a distribution of trajectories to form a fixed, global 1D timestep grid. Without these details, comparing the true computational budget against static schedules is difficult.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Mathematical Elegance:** The mapping of an adaptive ODE integration problem into continuous-time RL is highly principled. Theorems 3.1 and 3.2 seamlessly bridge deterministic optimal control with stochastic policy optimization.
- **Motivated Reward Function:** Grounding the RL reward in the rigorously derived Taylor expansion of the Euler truncation error creates a clear, physics-informed optimization landscape, avoiding the pitfalls of heuristic reward design.
- **Zero-Shot Transferability:** The empirical evidence that a single trained policy can successfully schedule generation across entirely different, high-resolution datasets without computationally expensive retraining makes the method practically appealing.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Unacknowledged Prior Work:** The failure to cite and compare against ICML 2024 work ("Align Your Steps"), which solves the exact same problem (minimizing discretization error for diffusion schedules) via calculus of variations, damages the core novelty claims.
- **Hidden Training Overhead:** The text lacks transparency regarding the wall-clock time and memory consumption required to train the continuous-time actor-critic model. The required Jacobian-vector products for the error surrogate are computationally expensive.
- **Solver Specificity:** The tight coupling of the theory to the explicit Euler method leaves it uncertain how this method scales to the higher-order ODE/SDE solvers that dominate state-of-the-art diffusion pipelines.
- **Inference Ambiguity:** It is not explicitly stated whether the actor network introduces additional NFE overhead during the generation phase.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully validate the utility and state-of-the-art standing of the ART-RL framework, the following evidence is needed:
- **Comparison with Analytical Scheduling:** A direct empirical comparison of sample quality (FID) and NFE trade-offs between ART-RL schedules and those derived by recent optimization methods that also target discretization error.
- **Computational Cost Breakdown:** A concrete reporting of the wall-clock time, GPU memory, and sample complexity required to train the actor-critic model (Algorithm 1) to convergence, compared against the cost of simple grid searches or analytical derivations.
- **Inference Setup Details:** Explicit clarification on how the schedule is extracted for inference. Does the method rely on the actor network computing dynamic, state-dependent step sizes per image, or does it output a static schedule?
- **Higher-Order Integration:** An empirical ablation or theoretical discussion on applying the ART-RL derived schedule to higher-order samplers.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment is currently borderline, weighing a beautiful theoretical formulation against critical omissions in literature context and computational evaluation. My leaning would confidently shift toward acceptance if the authors:
1. Revise the novelty claims to accurately reflect prior works, specifically incorporating a discussion of concurrent optimization-based scheduling methods.
2. Provide a head-to-head empirical comparison (or rigorous theoretical contrast) with these recent principled scheduling baselines.
3. Transparently document the training overhead induced by the JVP calculations, demonstrating that the amortized cost is justified by the sampling improvements or zero-shot transferability.
4. Clarify the inference mechanics and confirm whether the actor network adds any computational overhead during the generation phase.
