## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the conceptual soundness, mathematical validity, and methodological rigor of the proposed Context-Conditioned Delta Steering (CC-Delta) method. While the paper introduces an elegant statistical pipeline for feature selection and gracefully attempts to handle SAE reconstruction errors, a severe mathematical contradiction in the framing of the intervention and a critically confounded baseline undermine the core claims.

## Overview
The paper proposes utilizing off-the-shelf Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) to mitigate Large Language Model (LLM) jailbreaks without fine-tuning. The authors argue that steering in the dense residual stream (as done in prior work like Contrastive Activation Addition) suffers from superposition, leading to poor safety-utility trade-offs. To resolve this, they introduce Context-Conditioned Delta Steering (CC-Delta), a method that identifies jailbreak-specific SAE features by strictly matching the tokens of a harmful request with and without a jailbreak wrapper. Features are selected using paired statistical testing (Wilcoxon signed-rank test with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction). During generation, the method intervenes by shifting the activations of these specific sparse features. The authors evaluate this approach across four instruction-tuned models against twelve wrapper and re-writer jailbreak attacks.

## Claimed Contributions
- A novel application of SAEs for jailbreak mitigation, aiming to shift the intervention space from dense activations to disentangled sparse latent spaces.
- The introduction of CC-Delta, a rigorous statistical filtering pipeline that isolates context-dependent jailbreak features while discarding generic template noise.
- Comprehensive empirical evidence demonstrating superior safety-utility trade-offs and robust out-of-distribution generalization compared to dense steering and fine-tuning baselines.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The paper successfully bridges two rapidly evolving subfields: mechanistic interpretability (via SAEs) and practical AI safety alignment. By repurposing SAEs—tools primarily designed for understanding model internals—as active defense mechanisms, the authors present a compelling dual-use case that avoids the prohibitive computational costs of task-specific fine-tuning seen in methods like Latent Adversarial Training (LAT) or Circuit Breakers. 

The methodological novelty lies primarily in the feature selection pipeline. Unlike standard dense steering methods that compute simple mean differences between paired prompts, CC-Delta leverages strict token-level alignment to conduct non-parametric statistical testing. This is a principled advancement over prior activation steering literature, effectively factoring out the distributional noise introduced by complex jailbreak templates.

## Technical Soundness
While the feature selection methodology is statistically robust, the core conceptual premise of the paper—that the intervention operates in a "sparse feature space" rather than a dense one during generation—suffers from a fatal mathematical collapse. 

The paper describes the inference-time intervention in Equation 2, which involves encoding the residual stream $h$ into the SAE latent space, shifting the selected features by $\alpha(m \odot \Delta)$, decoding back to the residual stream, and adding back the reconstruction error $e$. Formally:
$h_{tilde} = SAE^{-1}(SAE(h) + \alpha(m \odot \Delta)) + e$
where the reconstruction error is defined as $e = h - SAE^{-1}(SAE(h))$.

Substituting $e$ into the intervention equation yields:
$h_{tilde} = SAE^{-1}(SAE(h) + \alpha(m \odot \Delta)) + h - SAE^{-1}(SAE(h))$

Crucially, standard SAE decoders are linear transformations of the form $SAE^{-1}(z) = W_{dec} z + b_{dec}$. Because the decoder is linear, the decoding of a sum separates algebraically:
$SAE^{-1}(z_1 + z_2) = SAE^{-1}(z_1) + W_{dec} z_2$

Applying this property to the shifted latent vector:
$SAE^{-1}(SAE(h) + \alpha(m \odot \Delta)) = SAE^{-1}(SAE(h)) + \alpha W_{dec}(m \odot \Delta)$

When we substitute this back into the full expression for $h_{tilde}$, the $SAE^{-1}(SAE(h))$ terms perfectly cancel out:
$h_{tilde} = SAE^{-1}(SAE(h)) + \alpha W_{dec}(m \odot \Delta) + h - SAE^{-1}(SAE(h))$
$h_{tilde} = h + \alpha W_{dec}(m \odot \Delta)$

This mathematical simplification has profound structural implications for the paper:
1. **The intervention is formally a dense steering method.** The calculation $\alpha W_{dec}(m \odot \Delta)$ resolves to a single, constant, dense vector of the same dimension as the residual stream. Adding this fixed vector to $h$ is mathematically indistinguishable from standard dense activation steering. The claim that the model generation benefits from steering in a "sparse feature space" is structurally false at inference time. The SAE is ultimately acting as an offline dictionary to construct a highly specific dense steering vector.
2. **Computational overhead is mathematically redundant.** The text suggests that the inference procedure requires an SAE encode, shift, and decode step at every intervened token. If implemented this way, it introduces massive latency and memory overhead for calculations that algebraically cancel out to zero. If the authors implemented the dense algebraic simplification in their code instead, then the framing of the method in the manuscript is highly misleading.

## Experimental Rigor
The mathematical collapse outlined above severely confounds the paper's empirical evaluation, particularly the comparison against dense baselines.

The paper claims superiority over dense activation steering methods like Contrastive Activation Addition (CAA). However, CAA, as standardly implemented, relies on simple mean differences over entire sequences or specific token positions without the sophisticated isolation techniques introduced in this work. CC-Delta introduces a highly refined data pipeline: context-conditioned verbatim token matching, paired samples, and the Wilcoxon signed-rank test with FDR correction. 

Because the final CC-Delta intervention is ultimately just a dense vector addition, any observed performance improvements over CAA cannot be confidently attributed to the disentangled properties of SAE representations. It is entirely plausible—and indeed probable—that the performance gap is driven by the vastly superior feature selection methodology. Without a controlled dense baseline that applies the exact same token-matching and statistical filtering to dense residual channels (or PCA components), the core hypothesis that SAE representations are strictly necessary for these safety-utility gains remains unsupported.

Despite these critical evaluation flaws, the paper demonstrates excellent experimental design in other areas. The authors explicitly acknowledge (Remark 3.1) that their verbatim token-matching requirement restricts training to wrapper attacks. By subsequently evaluating the defense against structurally distinct re-writer attacks, they subject their method to a rigorous, out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization test. Furthermore, the formalization of safety-utility trade-offs (Equation 3) across multiple metrics (MMLU, IFEval, Fluency) is a highly commendable practice that allows for principled, apples-to-apples comparisons between inference-time parameter tuning and fine-tuning defenses.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Statistical Rigor:** The use of non-parametric paired testing (Wilcoxon) coupled with FDR correction is a substantial methodological improvement over existing heuristic mean-shift approaches in the activation steering literature.
- **Elegant Error Handling:** The theoretical formulation of adding back the SAE reconstruction error ($e$) ensures that the intervention isolates the semantic shift without imposing the base model reconstruction penalties inherent to imperfect off-the-shelf SAEs. (Even though the terms cancel algebraically in the final intervention, the conceptual intention to preserve baseline utility is well-reasoned).
- **Evaluation Framework:** The strict OOD evaluation on re-writer attacks and the formalized safety-utility thresholding provide a strong empirical template for future jailbreak mitigation research.
- **Safety Orientation:** The work responsibly utilizes established benchmarks (StrongReject, AdvBench) without disseminating novel attack vectors, adhering to strong AI safety and ethical guidelines.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Fatal Conceptual Contradiction:** The core narrative that CC-Delta steers in a "sparse space" during inference is invalidated by the linear nature of the SAE decoder, which reduces the entire mathematical intervention to a static dense vector addition ($h_{tilde} = h + v_{dense}$). 
- **Confounded Dense Baselines:** The lack of a "Dense CC-Delta" baseline makes it impossible to disentangle the benefits of the novel statistical feature selection pipeline from the use of the SAE dictionary itself.
- **Missing Code and Implementation Details:** The lack of an anonymized code repository raises significant reproducibility concerns. Reviewers and practitioners must be able to verify whether the computationally expensive encode/decode steps were actually executed during the forward passes or if the dense algebraic simplification was utilized. 
- **Missing Results Verification:** Based on the manuscript excerpts available during this review cycle, the central empirical results and learning curves were truncated, meaning the claims of superiority in the abstract could only be assessed through the lens of the experimental setup and mathematical equations rather than final metric verification.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To validate the central claims made in the paper, the following evidence is critical:
1. **A Controlled Dense Baseline:** The exact Context-Conditioned feature selection pipeline (token matching + Wilcoxon + FDR) must be applied to standard dense residual stream channels or simple PCA projections. Comparing this "Dense CC-Delta" against the SAE-based CC-Delta is the only way to isolate the true value added by the sparse dictionary.
2. **Clarification on Inference Computation:** The authors must explicitly state whether the forward passes during generation compute the full SAE encoding/decoding, or if they simply add the pre-computed dense vector $\alpha W_{dec}(m \odot \Delta)$. If the full SAE passes are computed, empirical measurements of inference latency and memory overhead compared to standard dense steering must be provided to justify the inefficiency. 
3. **Open-Source Artifacts:** Code for the token alignment, statistical filtering, and inference intervention is required to verify the exact mathematical operations and enable reproducibility.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment is currently anchored on the mathematical contradiction and the confounded baselines. My leaning would shift toward a positive recommendation if the authors:
1. Revise the paper's conceptual framing to accurately reflect that the method uses SAEs as an offline dictionary to construct a highly specific *dense* steering vector, rather than claiming to steer in a sparse space during inference.
2. Provide the controlled "Dense CC-Delta" ablation. If the SAE-derived dense vector significantly outperforms a purely dense-derived vector using the exact same statistical filtering, the core utility of the SAE in this pipeline would be empirically vindicated.
3. Confirm the inference implementation details regarding computational overhead and correct the manuscript accordingly. 

## Provisional Leaning
Reject. While the Context-Conditioned statistical filtering is a genuinely valuable contribution to activation engineering, the paper's core theoretical framing is contradicted by its own equations. The intervention mathematically collapses into dense activation steering, heavily confounding the empirical comparisons against existing dense methods and structurally invalidating the central claim of "sparse steering" during generation. Until a controlled baseline disentangles the new feature selection pipeline from the SAE representations, the paper's primary hypothesis remains unproven.
