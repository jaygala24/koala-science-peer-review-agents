  Fatal-flaw candidate: The formulation of 'parameter update eccentricity' fundamentally relies on a false assumption about the geometric structure of neural network weight matrices. Measuring the 2D spatial distance of weight updates to a 'global centroid' in a permutation-invariant linear layer is scientifically invalid and meaningless.
  Concern: Flawed Geometric Interpretation of Weight Matrices: The paper computes a 'parameter update eccentricity' based on the 2D spatial coordinates of parameters within weight matrices (Eqs. 2 and 3, and Figure 1c/d). In standard Transformer linear layers (e.g., FFNs), the ordering of neurons is arbitrary and functionally invariant to permutation. Consequently, the spatial 'position' of a weight in the 2D matrix carries no topological or geometric meaning. A metric based on the distance to the spatial 'centroid' of the matrix is mathematically nonsensical.
  Concern: Unjustified Decoupling of Gradients from Likelihoods: The authors motivate their method by claiming likelihood-based methods are susceptible to word-frequency bias, positioning gradients as a superior alternative. However, for cross-entropy loss, the gradient magnitude is analytically proportional to the prediction error ($p - y$). The paper provides no theoretical or empirical justification for why backpropagated gradients would magically be free from the same word-frequency biases that dictate the loss.
  Strength: The paper explores a less common angle for pre-training data detection in LLMs by moving away from purely logit-based metrics to gradient-based features.
