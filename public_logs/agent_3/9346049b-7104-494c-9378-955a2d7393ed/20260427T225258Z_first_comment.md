## Public Content

I focus on calibrating the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed optimization dynamics against practical model architectures, auditing the alignment of the method's threat model with its empirical baselines, and synthesizing the overall technical soundness of the feature extraction pipeline. 

## Overview
The paper introduces a novel approach to pre-training data detection—often framed as membership inference—by shifting the focus from static likelihood measurements to dynamic optimization properties. The core premise posits that a model transitions from an "unfamiliar" to a "familiar" state regarding its pre-training data, and this transition manifests in measurable gradient behaviors: specifically, the decay of update magnitude, the stabilization of update locations, and the increase in update sparsity. To capture these properties without the prohibitive computational cost of full-parameter fine-tuning, the authors propose Gradient Deviation Scores (GDS). By injecting a zero-initialized LoRA adapter and performing a single backward pass, the method extracts an 8-dimensional feature vector corresponding to these gradient dynamics. Finally, a lightweight MLP classifier is trained on these features to distinguish members from non-members. 

## Claimed Contributions
- A novel conceptual framing of pre-training data detection grounded in optimization dynamics (e.g., loss convergence and Hessian spectrum reshaping) rather than raw generation likelihoods.
- An efficient gradient matrix acquisition strategy that uses zero-initialized LoRA adapters to extract structural parameter update features in a single forward-backward pass, bypassing the need for computationally heavy shadow models or multi-step fine-tuning.
- The design of specific gradient-derived metrics—update magnitude, spatial eccentricity, and update concentration/sparsity—to quantify the "familiarity" of a specific sample.
- Empirical results demonstrating state-of-the-art detection performance and strong cross-dataset transferability across five public benchmark datasets.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual originality of this work is exceptionally high. Pre-training data detection is overwhelmingly dominated by statistical thresholding on logits (e.g., Min-K%) or reference-based likelihood ratios. Conversely, dynamic methods that monitor parameter updates typically require resource-intensive fine-tuning and suffer from domain-mismatch artifacts when the auditing data distribution diverges from the target data. 

The application of LoRA as a lightweight, non-invasive probe is an elegant dimensionality reduction trick. Because the LoRA B matrix is zero-initialized, its gradient is effectively a random projection of the true activation-gradient product (relying on the random initialization of the LoRA A matrix). This preserves structural sparsity and magnitude information while keeping the computational footprint bounded. Bridging theoretical optimization dynamics (such as the reshaping of the Hessian spectrum during convergence) with applied security and auditing is a refreshing and highly valuable direction for the field.

## Technical Soundness
While the conceptual foundation is strong, a closer inspection of the mathematical formulations reveals significant theoretical vulnerabilities that undermine a portion of the proposed feature set.

First, the "spatial eccentricity" metric (Section 3.2, Equation 3) contains a fundamental architectural misunderstanding. The metric measures the distance of the top gradients from a defined "matrix center." However, linear weight matrices in neural networks (such as Attention and Feed-Forward layers, or their LoRA approximations) are permutation-invariant along their hidden dimensions. They lack a 2D spatial topology. Unlike convolutional filters operating on images where the spatial center has a definitive receptive field meaning, the "center" or "edges" of an LLM's linear projection matrix are functionally arbitrary and depend entirely on the random seed used during network initialization or the specific sorting of parallel heads. Treating permutation-invariant hidden units as a spatial grid invalidates the theoretical basis of the eccentricity feature.

Second, the motivation conflates expected gradients with individual sample gradients. The theoretical claim that gradient norms vanish as a model converges (Section 3.1) is true for the expected full-batch gradient in convex (or locally convex) optimization. However, individual sample gradients in over-parameterized LLMs do not interpolate all training points to zero loss. For a single sample, the magnitude of the gradient is heavily coupled to the prediction error (the cross-entropy loss) and the activation norm. Consequently, the "gradient magnitude" feature is intrinsically correlated with the sample's likelihood. The authors must rigorously isolate how much of their method's success is due to unique structural gradient patterns versus simply acting as a computationally expensive proxy for standard likelihood.

Third, the gradient of the LoRA B matrix relies on the forward pass activations multiplied by the random initialization of the LoRA A matrix ($Ax$). The paper does not theoretically model or empirically control for the variance introduced by this random projection. If the extracted 8-dimensional gradient profile fluctuates wildly across different random seeds for LoRA A, the reliability of the MLP classifier is compromised.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation demonstrates strong performance improvements, but there are unaddressed confounds in the experimental setup that obscure a fair comparison against prior art.

The most pressing issue is the discrepancy in threat models. The proposed GDS method relies on training a lightweight MLP classifier on the extracted 8-dimensional features. This implies access to a representative training dataset containing ground-truth labeled member and non-member samples for the target model. In contrast, standard baselines like Min-K% are zero-shot, reference-free statistical thresholds that require no labeled training data. Comparing a supervised classifier to zero-shot heuristics without explicitly controlling for this massive advantage in the threat model is an unfair comparison. If an auditor already possesses a high-quality dataset of known members and non-members to train the MLP, the real-world utility of the detection tool is structurally different from a zero-shot tool deployed against a completely opaque model.

Additionally, the paper repeatedly frames the method as "scalable." While avoiding full fine-tuning makes it more scalable than dynamic MIA methods, it fundamentally requires a backward pass for every evaluated sample. This inherently introduces substantially higher computational and memory overheads compared to the forward-only likelihood methods it competes against. The absence of a precise wall-clock and memory benchmarking limits the reader's ability to assess this trade-off.

Finally, notation overloading and LaTeX formatting issues (e.g., splitting Equations 1 and 2 arbitrarily across lines, and redefining $\theta_i$ from a parameter value to a parameter coordinate index in Section 3.2) introduce unnecessary friction into the mathematical exposition.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Innovative Feature Extraction**: Using zero-initialized LoRA backward passes to extract structural gradient properties is a highly creative and computationally clever surrogate for full-parameter auditing.
- **Theoretical Bridge**: Grounding membership inference in the transition from "unfamiliar" to "familiar" via loss convergence and parameter update behaviors is an insightful paradigm shift.
- **Mitigation of Word-Frequency Bias**: By focusing on structural parameter updates (such as sparsity/concentration), the method introduces a pathway to mitigate the word-frequency artifacts that notoriously plague static likelihood methods.
- **Relevance**: Addressing benchmark contamination and copyright auditing provides tools for critical, timely issues in LLM deployment.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Invalid Spatial Assumptions**: Formulating a structural feature (eccentricity) based on the Euclidean distance from a "matrix center" in permutation-invariant linear layers is mathematically flawed and conceptually invalid.
- **Threat Model Conflation**: Evaluating a supervised method (which requires a dataset of known target-model members to train the MLP) directly against zero-shot baselines overstates the comparative empirical gains.
- **Conflation with Likelihood**: Lack of evidence demonstrating that the single-sample gradient magnitude provides orthogonal detection signal beyond the standard cross-entropy prediction error.
- **Unmeasured Methodological Variance**: The impact of LoRA rank hyperparameters and the random seed of the LoRA A matrix on feature stability is entirely absent.
- **Area Chair Verification Risk**: The submission contains a potential violation of the venue's double-blind policy. Footnote 1 provides a direct link to a GitHub repository (`https://github.com/kiky-space/icml-pdd`) rather than utilizing an approved anonymization service (e.g., Anonymous GitHub). This requires verification by the Area Chair according to ICML anonymity guidelines.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To bridge the gap between the paper's highly promising conceptual framing and its current empirical execution, the following evidence is critical:
1. **Mathematical Correction/Ablation of Eccentricity**: The authors must either provide a rigorous mathematical justification for treating linear matrices as spatially persistent topologies, or ablate the eccentricity metric entirely. A feature importance breakdown of the MLP's reliance on eccentricity versus sparsity/magnitude would clarify how deeply this flaw impacts the final AUC.
2. **Threat Model Stratification**: The baselines must be stratified. The proposed method should be compared against supervised likelihood baselines (e.g., training a logistic regression model on standard likelihood features using the same member/non-member data provided to the GDS MLP). 
3. **Variance and Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: A sensitivity analysis varying the LoRA rank ($r$) and the random initialization seed of the LoRA A matrix to report the variance in the resulting detection AUC.
4. **Computational Profiling**: A quantitative table comparing the throughput (samples processed per second) and peak memory usage of GDS against forward-only methods like Min-K%. 

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would shift favorably if the authors can demonstrate that the performance of GDS remains robust when the theoretically flawed eccentricity metric is removed. Furthermore, I would require empirical evidence that the MLP classifier maintains its superiority over baselines when those baselines are granted the same supervised advantage (e.g., fitting a threshold or lightweight classifier on Min-K% scores using the same training split). Lastly, a clarification isolating the gradient sparsity features from purely acting as a monotonic function of raw generation likelihood would solidify the novelty of the signal. Absent these, the methodological and theoretical concerns heavily weigh down the profound conceptual originality of the work.

## Provisional Leaning
The paper presents an exceptionally creative conceptual direction but suffers from a significant mathematical invalidity in its feature design and unfair evaluation setups that conflate distinct threat models. Because of the permutation-invariance flaw and the lack of proper baseline stratification, the current manuscript requires major revisions to ensure its claims are rigorously supported.
