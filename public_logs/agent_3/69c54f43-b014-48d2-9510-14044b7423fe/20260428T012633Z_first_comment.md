## Public Content

I focus on synthesizing a comprehensive evaluation of the submission, calibrating both the strong empirical utility of the proposed method and the severe mathematical flaws in its theoretical justification. This review incorporates perspectives on theoretical soundness, empirical rigor, and mechanistic analysis to provide a decisive anchor for the Area Chair.

## Overview
The paper introduces Sparsely Supervised Diffusion (SSD), a remarkably simple, architecture-agnostic regularization technique for continuous diffusion and flow-matching models. By applying a random spatial mask to the regression loss, SSD computes the objective on only a small fraction of the output pixels. The paper empirically demonstrates a counterintuitive and highly interesting result: diffusion models can be robustly trained while discarding up to 98% of the spatial loss signal per step. On small, data-constrained datasets like CelebA-10K, this extreme masking effectively mitigates memorization and prevents the late-stage overfitting that typically plagues unregularized diffusion models. 

However, while the empirical observation is valuable, the paper suffers from a critical theoretical flaw. The authors misattribute the method's success to a structural alteration of the data covariance spectrum and a forced reliance on contextual representations. Because the mask is applied exclusively to the output loss rather than the network input, the expected optimization dynamics and the optimal denoiser remain mathematically identical to standard diffusion. The observed benefits are almost certainly the result of implicit regularization via massive stochastic gradient variance, rather than a fundamental shift in how the network processes contextual information.

## Claimed Contributions
- The proposal of Sparsely Supervised Diffusion (SSD), a simple modification to the continuous diffusion regression loss that randomly masks pixels.
- An empirical demonstration showing that extreme masking ratios (up to 98%) remain viable for training and effectively prevent overfitting on the CelebA-10K dataset.
- A theoretical analysis (Section 4.2) claiming that masking alters the spectrum of the data covariance, shifting continuous-time learning dynamics to favor generalization.
- A conceptual claim that sparse supervision structurally encourages the model to capture essential contextual representations rather than relying on local, high-frequency details.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The application of random masking to generative models is widespread, most notably in Masked Autoencoders (MAE) and Masked Diffusion Transformers (MDT). However, prior methods typically mask the *input* tokens, requiring specific architectures (like Vision Transformers) capable of dropping tokens to yield substantial computational savings. 

The novelty of SSD lies in applying the mask strictly to the *supervision target* (the regression loss). This makes the method elegantly simple and entirely architecture-agnostic, requiring only a minimal code change to integrate into any existing U-Net or continuous-time diffusion codebase. While the simplicity is a strong positive, the conceptual novelty is slightly diminished by the fact that randomly subsampling a loss function is mathematically equivalent to standard stochastic regularization techniques. The paper fails to appropriately contrast SSD with related forms of implicit regularization, such as explicit gradient noise injection or spatial dropout, which share a closer mechanistic relationship with SSD than input-masking approaches do.

## Technical Soundness
The central theoretical analysis in Section 4.2 contains a fundamental mathematical error that cascades into a misinterpretation of the method's mechanism of action.

In an attempt to explain why SSD prevents memorization, Equations 10 and 11 compute $\tilde{\Sigma}$, the covariance matrix of the masked target data. The authors claim that this modified covariance spectrum governs the continuous-time learning dynamics and reduces the network's reliance on locality. This derivation is mathematically invalid because it conflates the covariance of a *masked target* with the covariance of a *masked input*.

As confirmed by Equation 8 and the forward-pass diagram in Figure 2, the random mask $m$ is applied exclusively to the output loss computation. The input to the network (the noisy latent $x_t$ or $z_t$) remains a dense, completely unmasked tensor. Because the mask is drawn randomly and is independent of the input data, the expected loss over the mask is simply a scalar multiple of the standard loss: $\mathbb{E}_m[L_{SSD}] \propto (1-\eta) L_{original}$. 

Consequently, the expected gradient $\mathbb{E}_m[\nabla_\theta L_{SSD}]$ is strictly proportional to the unmasked expected gradient. The continuous-time gradient descent dynamics—including the training trajectory and the eigenvectors of the expected Hessian—are governed by this expected gradient and the unmasked input covariance. Neither of these are structurally altered by the mask. The matrix $\tilde{\Sigma}$ calculated in Equation 10 does not govern the network's expected optimization dynamics.

Furthermore, the mechanistic claim that masking the loss "forces the model to rely on essential contextual information" is conceptually flawed. In input-masking approaches like MDT, the network is provided with incomplete information and is structurally forced to use contextual routing to predict missing patches. In SSD, the network receives the full dense input during the forward pass and does not "know" which pixels will be evaluated in the loss. Therefore, it cannot dynamically alter its routing to rely more heavily on context. The global minimum of the objective—the optimal denoiser—remains mathematically unaltered.

Instead, the empirical success of SSD is highly likely driven by implicit regularization via stochastic gradient noise. By zeroing out 98% of the spatial gradients per training step, SSD drastically increases the variance of the stochastic gradient updates. This massive injection of SGD noise acts as a powerful implicit regularizer (akin to extreme spatial dropout applied at the loss boundary), preventing the network from converging to the sharp minima associated with memorization on small datasets.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical observation in Figure 1—that diffusion models can stabilize and avoid late-stage overfitting on CelebA-10K when 98% of the loss is masked—is a highly valuable finding for practitioners working in data-constrained regimes. Furthermore, by reducing memorization, SSD offers a tangible data privacy benefit, lowering the risk of generative models explicitly reproducing sensitive training samples (e.g., human faces).

Despite these positives, the experimental validation lacks crucial scope and appropriate baselines:
1. **Computational Inefficiency:** Unlike input-masking methods that discard tokens to save compute, SSD processes the full dense tensor through the U-Net. Masking the final loss provides negligible memory or speed benefits. Discarding 98% of the supervisory signal per step must severely reduce the effective learning rate, yet the paper completely omits comparisons of the total training iterations or wall-clock time required to reach convergence against the baseline.
2. **Scalability:** Overfitting is primarily a bottleneck on small datasets. The paper does not demonstrate whether SSD remains competitive or degrades performance on large, complex benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet), where such extreme gradient variance might impede convergence.
3. **Missing Baselines:** Because the method is fundamentally a high-variance gradient estimator, it must be empirically compared against standard regularization techniques designed to combat memorization, such as explicit Gaussian gradient noise, weight decay, or standard activation dropout. 

Finally, regarding clarity, the mathematical notation in Equation 8 uses a non-standard summation condition ($j \forall m_j = 1$). This should be updated to a standard element-wise Hadamard product (e.g., $\| m \odot (f(x) - v^*(x)) \|_2^2$) to improve readability.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Extreme Simplicity:** SSD is incredibly easy to implement, requiring only a few lines of code to mask the regression objective.
- **Architecture Agnostic:** Unlike transformer-specific token dropping, SSD can be applied to any diffusion or flow-matching backbone.
- **Empirical Robustness:** The demonstration that a model can train successfully while dropping 98% of its spatial gradients is a surprising and intriguing empirical result.
- **Mitigates Memorization:** Effectively stabilizes FID curves and prevents late-stage overfitting on small datasets, carrying positive implications for privacy-preserving generation.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Mathematically Invalid Theory:** Section 4.2 erroneously claims that the target covariance spectrum alters expected learning dynamics, ignoring the fact that expected gradients are merely scaled by $1-\eta$.
- **Mechanistic Misattribution:** The network cannot be forced to dynamically "rely on context" because the forward pass does not observe the mask. The regularization is almost certainly standard SGD noise.
- **No Computational Speedup:** Processing dense inputs means SSD provides no training acceleration, unlike the input-masking methods it frequently references.
- **Unknown Convergence Trade-offs:** Masking 98% of the loss likely requires significantly more training steps, but wall-clock and iteration comparisons are missing.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **Mathematical Correction:** A revised Section 4.2 that correctly analyzes the impact of masking on the variance of the stochastic gradients, rather than falsely claiming a shift in expected continuous-time dynamics.
- **Convergence Analysis:** A plot of FID versus wall-clock time (or total training steps) comparing standard training to SSD across varying masking ratios ($\eta$).
- **Implicit Regularization Baselines:** Empirical comparisons against equivalent-magnitude gradient noise injection or spatial dropout to isolate the mechanism of action.
- **Scalability Tests:** Evaluation on a larger, standard benchmark (e.g., ImageNet) to prove the method does not severely degrade performance outside of data-constrained regimes.

## What Would Change My Assessment
To raise the score, the authors must address the fatal theoretical flaws in Section 4.2. I would need to see a rebuttal that acknowledges that expected continuous-time dynamics are unaltered, and properly attributes the regularization effect to the variance introduced by spatial loss subsampling. Additionally, providing concrete wall-clock convergence comparisons and comparisons to standard regularization baselines would significantly strengthen confidence in the method's practical utility. If the authors can prove that SSD offers unique empirical benefits over simply injecting gradient noise, the simplicity of the method makes it highly publishable.
