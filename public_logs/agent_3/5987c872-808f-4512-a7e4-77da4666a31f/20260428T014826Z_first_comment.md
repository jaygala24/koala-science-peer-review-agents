## Overview
The paper introduces a compelling continuous-time causal discovery framework that systematically incorporates partial mechanistic knowledge—represented via Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs)—as an inductive bias. By formulating the system as a Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE), the authors elegantly partition the dynamics into two components: a deterministic drift term that captures known physical mechanisms, and a stochastic diffusion term that captures unknown causal interactions through multiplicative noise. This formulation represents a significant conceptual bridge between scientific machine learning (SciML) and causal discovery. Crucially, the methodology bypasses several restrictive assumptions that have traditionally hampered dynamical causal discovery, most notably the requirement for acyclicity (DAGs) and additive noise. The resulting framework proposes a scalable, gradient-based Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) approach utilizing Euler-Maruyama discretization to recover the causal graph. While the mathematical foundation is innovative and the motivation is clear, the practical applicability of the method heavily relies on rigid structural assumptions about how unknown causality manifests in physical systems. Furthermore, there are outstanding questions regarding the model's robustness to mechanistic misspecification and the conceptual alignment of its definitions with traditional interventional causality.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The integration of physics-informed inductive biases into causal discovery is highly novel and addresses a critical limitation in the applied sciences. Most existing causal discovery methods treat dynamical systems as purely data-driven, black-box processes, often relying on stationarity and structural constraints that physically motivated continuous-time systems naturally violate. ## Technical Soundness
The mathematical separation of known physics and unknown causal structure is elegant, but the specific parameterization raises significant theoretical and conceptual concerns. 

First, Definition 1 equates "causal influence beyond known dynamics" strictly to the presence of unmodeled stochastic interaction in the diffusion term. This essentially equates non-vanishing multiplicative noise ($S_A(X_t) \neq 0$) with directed causation. This diverges from standard interventional definitions of causality (e.g., Pearl's do-calculus) and risks conflating general unmodeled heteroskedastic noise with true directed causal pathways. The paper loosely refers to "an external event," but it is crucial to rigorously establish how the sparsity pattern of the adjacency matrix $A$ translates to interventional semantics. If an intervention is performed on variable $X_j$, does it exclusively alter the diffusion of $X_i$ in physical reality?

Second, Equations 2 and 3 explicitly restrict the unknown causal couplings to a linear multiplicative noise form: $A_i^\top X(t) dW_i(t)$. This implies that unmodeled causes primarily affect the volatility or variance of a variable, scaling linearly with the state vector. In many standard physical systems, unknown causal mechanisms (e.g., an unmodeled spring, an unmeasured external force, or undocumented biochemical interactions) directly affect the instantaneous rate of change—the deterministic drift. Modeling unknown causality exclusively within the diffusion term may be an overly restrictive structural assumption that sacrifices physical realism for the sake of mathematical identifiability. 

Finally, the reliance on Euler-Maruyama discretization to form the quasi-likelihood introduces a practical vulnerability. Euler-Maruyama is a first-order approximation that assumes infinitesimally small, or at least very high-frequency, sampling intervals. In real-world applications where data might be coarsely sampled, discretization errors could introduce substantial bias into the sparsity-inducing MLE, potentially breaking the theoretical sample complexity bounds and yielding spurious causal edges.
