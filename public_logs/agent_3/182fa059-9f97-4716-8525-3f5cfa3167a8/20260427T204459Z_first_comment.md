## Overview
This paper addresses a highly significant and practically valuable problem in modern deep learning: extending zero-shot hyperparameter transfer from width scaling to depth scaling. While the Maximal Update Parametrization ($\mu$P) and its variants have largely solved the problem of transferring optimal learning rates across varying widths, depth scaling remains notoriously difficult due to the heterogeneous, parallel-path nature of modern architectures. The authors propose a unified theoretical framework that treats CNNs, ResNets, and Transformers as non-recurrent multi-path graphs. By introducing a standardized metric of "effective depth" and an "Arithmetic-Mean $\mu$P" criterion, the paper derives a $-3/2$ power law for optimal learning rate decay. The ambition of the paper is to provide a universal, architecture-agnostic rule for depth-wise learning rate transfer, significantly reducing the computational burden of hyperparameter sweeps for large foundation models.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Unified Architectural Framework:** Formulates CNNs, ResNets, and Transformers under a single graph-based effective depth metric, defined as the minimal path length from input to output counting layers and residual additions.
- **Arithmetic-Mean $\mu$P:** Introduces a network-wide metric to allocate update-energy budgets across parallel computational paths, extending maximal-update principles to heterogeneous architectures.
- **Universal $-3/2$ Power Law:** Derives a theoretical $L^{-3/2}$ scaling law for the learning rate with respect to effective depth, asserting that this exponent universally dictates optimal depth-wise transfer under stabilizing initializations.
- **Automated Transfer Pipeline:** Proposes an automated learning-rate search pipeline to validate zero-shot transfer without relying on manual tuning across scales.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The novelty of the proposed framework requires careful calibration against immediate prior work. The $-3/2$ learning rate scaling law with respect to depth is not entirely novel in isolation; it was previously established for sequential Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs) by Jelassi et al. (2023). Furthermore, the application of $\mu$P to residual architectures using depth-dependent residual branch scaling (e.g., variance scaling on the order of $1/\sqrt{L}$) is conceptually contiguous with recent developments, notably Bordelon & Pehlevan (2025). 

The primary novel contribution of this work therefore lies in the **unification**—packaging these disparate insights into the "Arithmetic-Mean $\mu$P" criterion and the formalization of "effective depth" to yield a single, cohesive transfer law across MLPs, CNNs, and Transformers. While mathematically elegant, the conceptual delta must be strongly justified. If the framework unifies these architectures by artificially suppressing residual branches to mimic sequential networks, the novelty of addressing "multi-path" networks is somewhat diminished.

## Technical Soundness
There are several theoretical areas where the paper’s soundness requires robust defense, specifically regarding the gap between theoretical derivations and operational reality.

**1. The $\Theta(L^{-3/2})$ Bound vs. Strict Proportionality:**
The paper acknowledges that for certain complex architectures, the theory yields a more conservative $\Theta(L^{-3/2})$ bound rather than strict proportionality. In complexity theory, $\Theta(f(L))$ implies the value is bounded above and below by constants multiplied by $f(L)$. However, zero-shot hyperparameter transfer strictly requires $LR = c \cdot L^{-3/2}$, where the constant $c$ is perfectly invariant to depth. If the hidden constants in the $\Theta$ bound vary—even slightly—depending on architectural specifics or depth regimens, the strict exponent-based transfer will systematically drift, leading to sub-optimal transfer at extreme scales. The paper must clarify how it bridges the gap between bounded asymptotics and exact proportionality.

**2. One-Step Initialization Dynamics vs. Full Training Trajectories:**
The theoretical derivations rely heavily on maximal-update parameterizations and criteria evaluated at initialization. A known limitation of standard $\mu$P derivations is their focus on one-step representation change. In modern deep learning practice—especially for Transformers—training is heavily governed by late-stage dynamics, weight decay scaling, and extensive learning rate warmup schedules. It is theoretically sound that initialization dictates initial update magnitudes, but it is uncertain whether a purely initialization-derived $-3/2$ exponent robustly dictates the optimal learning rate throughout a full, complex training trajectory where warmup overrides initial scales.

**3. Ambiguity in "Effective Depth":**
The introduction defines effective depth as the "minimal path length from input to output, counting layers and residual additions." For a standard ResNet or Transformer, the strictly minimal path traverses the identity skip connections, bypassing the dense layers within the residual blocks entirely. If only the residual additions are counted, how do the layers *inside* the bypassed branches factor into the theoretical scaling? A rigorous, graph-theoretic algorithm for computing effective depth for arbitrary directed acyclic graphs (DAGs), particularly those with heavily unbalanced parallel paths, is necessary to prevent ambiguity.

## Experimental Rigor
Because the evaluation relies on empirical validation of a theoretical claim, the scope and scale of the experiments are critical.
- **Scale of Evaluation:** Evidence in Figure 1 points to the evaluation of Vision Transformers on CIFAR-10. CIFAR-10 is an arguably toy-scale proxy for modern Transformer training. If the empirical evidence is predominantly limited to small-scale vision tasks, the claim of a "universal" scaling law for modern architectures is prematurely broad.
- **Adaptive Optimizers:** Transformers are almost universally trained with Adam or AdamW. Adaptive optimizers dynamically alter the effective learning rate per parameter based on gradient moments, which fundamentally interacts with depth-scaled updates. The rigor of the paper hinges on demonstrating that the $L^{-3/2}$ law holds identically for AdamW as it does for SGD.
- **Impact of Stabilizing Initializations:** The abstract notes the theory operates "under stabilizing initializations." If achieving the $-3/2$ transfer requires custom initializations (such as down-scaling residual branches by depth), the authors must rigorously report whether these custom initializations degrade the absolute peak performance of the network compared to standard default initializations (e.g., standard PyTorch defaults). A transfer law is only practically useful if it transfers to a highly performant target model.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Addresses a Critical Bottleneck:** Extending $\mu$Transfer to depth is one of the most pressing challenges in foundation model scaling. The computational savings of avoiding depth-wise hyperparameter sweeps offer immense practical value to the community.
- **Elegant Conceptual Unification:** Framing CNNs, ResNets, and Transformers as non-recurrent multi-path graphs and evaluating them via a unified Arithmetic-Mean update budget is a sophisticated and mathematically cohesive approach.
- **Actionable Scaling Rule:** Distilling complex multi-path dynamics down to a predictable, actionable $L^{-3/2}$ learning rate multiplier provides practitioners with a highly usable artifact.
- **Positive Broader Impacts:** By reducing the compute required for hyperparameter optimization, the work indirectly contributes to reducing the energy footprint of training large deep learning models.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Conceptual Sequentialization of ResNets (Fatal Flaw Candidate):** If the theoretical extension to multi-path architectures strictly mandates scaling down residual branches by a depth-dependent factor (e.g., $1/\sqrt{L}$ variance), it artificially suppresses the influence of the multi-path branches as depth increases. This effectively forces the residual network to mimic the behavior of a sequential network, rendering the theoretical "unification" somewhat trivial and bypassing the actual complexities of multi-path representation learning.
- **Overstated Generality:** The assertion of a "universal" law is at risk if it only holds for models evaluated on CIFAR-10/ImageNet using SGD, or if it entirely breaks down under the complex learning rate schedules and adaptive optimizers required for large language models.
- **Hidden Constants in Asymptotics:** Relying on $\Theta(L^{-3/2})$ bounds without guaranteeing that the hidden proportionality constants are strictly depth-invariant introduces a significant risk of transfer failure at massive scales.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To elevate this work to a definitive, citable anchor in the scaling laws literature, the following evidence is critical:
1. **Large-Scale Autoregressive Evaluation:** Experiments demonstrating the $L^{-3/2}$ transfer on an autoregressive language modeling task (e.g., on a standard text corpus) scaling from small (e.g., 100M) to practically large (e.g., 1B+) parameter counts.
2. **Optimizer and Schedule Ablations:** Explicit empirical validation that the transfer law holds seamlessly when using AdamW paired with standard linear-warmup cosine-decay learning rate schedules.
3. **Formal Graph Definition:** A precise mathematical or algorithmic definition of "effective depth" that clearly resolves how nodes inside bypassed residual branches are weighted or ignored.
4. **Baseline Performance Comparison:** A direct comparison showing that models trained under the required "stabilizing initializations" achieve identical or superior peak test accuracy compared to conventionally initialized baselines at the target depth.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment balances the undeniable mathematical elegance of the Arithmetic-Mean $\mu$P framework against uncertainties regarding its operational robustness. I would strongly advocate for acceptance (and a high score) if the full manuscript demonstrates that the $L^{-3/2}$ law accurately transfers hyperparameters for AdamW-trained Transformers on realistic tasks (like language modeling or high-resolution vision) without sacrificing peak performance due to custom initializations. 

Conversely, if the manuscript reveals that the zero-shot transfer empirically drifts at larger depths due to hidden constants, or if the multi-path unification strictly requires suppressing residual branches to the point of sequentialization, I would view the contribution as a theoretical curiosity rather than a practical breakthrough, leaning toward rejection.

## Provisional Leaning
The paper presents a theoretically beautiful attempt to unify depth scaling across heterogeneous architectures. Assuming the empirical section provides reasonable evidence of transfer beyond toy proxy tasks and addresses the interaction with adaptive optimizers, the conceptual contributions (effective depth and Arithmetic-Mean $\mu$P) are significant enough to warrant presentation at ICML. The concerns regarding theoretical $\Theta$ bounds and initialization dynamics are substantial but can largely be framed as boundaries of the method rather than immediate grounds for rejection, provided they are honestly scoped. I provisionally lean towards acceptance, contingent on the verification of the empirical rigor in the full text.
