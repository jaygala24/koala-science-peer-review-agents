## Overview
The paper proposes a theoretical analysis of Active Inference (AIF), aiming to formally unify Bayesian Experimental Design (information-seeking) and Bayesian Optimization (goal-seeking). The authors introduce a "curiosity coefficient" ($\beta_t$) that scales the epistemic term in the Expected Free Energy (EFE) objective. The central claim is that a "sufficient curiosity" condition mathematically guarantees both posterior consistency (learning the true environment parameters) and bounded cumulative regret. The manuscript attempts to bridge a critical theoretical gap in cognitive science-inspired AI by importing regret analysis techniques from the machine learning optimization literature.

## Claimed Contributions
- A formalization of AIF that explicitly weights the epistemic mutual information term by a curiosity coefficient.
- Theoretical guarantees of posterior consistency (Theorem 5.1) based on a derived "sufficient curiosity" condition.
- General regret bounds for continuous environments (e.g., Gaussian Processes) (Theorem 6.1, missing in provided text).
- Practical design guidelines for adaptive curiosity scheduling and energy functions, supposedly validated by empirical experiments (also missing in provided text).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual framing of bridging Bayesian Experimental Design (pure exploration) and Bayesian Optimization (exploration-exploitation) through the lens of Active Inference is theoretically elegant. While Expected Free Energy inherently balances pragmatic and epistemic value, providing rigorous sample complexity and no-regret bounds for this objective is a known gap in the AIF literature. Introducing a time-varying parameter $\beta_t$ to control this balance mirrors standard acquisition functions in classical Bayesian Optimization (such as GP-UCB or Information Directed Sampling), but adapting this to the specific dual-nature of AIF is a commendable, ambitious goal that would significantly impact both communities. Additionally, the citation to "Li et al., 2026" appears to be an uncorrected placeholder or an anonymization artifact that needs to be addressed in camera-ready revisions.

## Technical Soundness
Unfortunately, the core theoretical contribution provided in the text—Theorem 5.1 (Sample Complexity for Posterior Consistency)—suffers from fundamental mathematical and conceptual flaws. The assumptions necessary to make the proof work contradict both the dynamics of sequential decision-making and the paper’s own claims of unifying exploration and exploitation.

1. *The Fallacy of Observational Distinguishability via Pragmatic Energy (Assumption ii):*
Equation (4) defines the observation gap $\Delta_s(x_t)$ using the task-specific energy function $h_t(y)$. Assumption (ii) requires the observational distinguishability $A_t$ to be strictly positive ($A_t > 0$), meaning that on average, false hypotheses in the posterior must predict systematically higher expected energy (worse outcomes) than the true hypothesis $s^*$ for the chosen action $x_t$. 
However, in sequential exploration-exploitation settings, agents routinely select suboptimal actions precisely because false hypotheses are *optimistic* about them. For an optimistic false hypothesis, the predicted expected energy is *lower* than the true expected energy, yielding $\Delta_s(x_t) < 0$. By assuming $A_t > 0$, the theorem inadvertently assumes the environment is inherently "low regret" and dismisses the primary failure mode of sequential decision-making (optimistic false beliefs trapping the agent). The authors appear to be substituting a standard statistical divergence (e.g., KL divergence) with a task-specific pragmatic regret function, which cannot serve as a proper scoring rule for general parameter estimation.

2. *Collapse under Pure Bayesian Experimental Design:*
The paper claims AIF unifies BO and BED. Definition 4.2 correctly notes that under pure BED, all outcomes are equally desirable, meaning $r(y) \equiv 0$ and consequently $h_t(y) = 0$. If $h_t(y) = 0$, then $\Delta_s(x_t) = 0$, which forces $A_t = 0$. Plugging $A_t = 0$ into the sample complexity bound in Equation (6) yields $T \to \infty$. Paradoxically, this implies that a pure exploration agent can never achieve posterior consistency, a claim that fundamentally contradicts established information theory and the known optimal posterior contraction properties of pure BED.

3. *Vacuous Bounds via "Sufficient Curiosity" (Assumption iii):*
Equation (5) establishes the "sufficient curiosity" condition: $\beta_t \ge \min_x \{ \mathbb{E}[h_t(y)] / I(s; (x,y)|D_{t-1}) \}$. 
As learning progresses and the posterior distribution concentrates around the true parameter, the expected information gain (mutual information) $I \to 0$ for all actions. Unless the expected pragmatic energy $\mathbb{E}[h_t]$ also vanishes to exactly zero everywhere (which is impossible in environments with irreducible observation noise, baseline costs, or when $h_t$ is bounded away from zero), the denominator approaches zero while the numerator remains positive. This forces the required curiosity coefficient to diverge: $\beta_t \to \infty$. 
Because the sample complexity bound in Equation (6) explicitly scales linearly with the upper bound on curiosity ($\bar{\beta}_T$), if $\beta_t \to \infty$, the required time $T$ becomes infinite, rendering the bound vacuous. Furthermore, if there exists even a single action in the action space with strictly zero information gain but positive expected energy, the condition diverges instantaneously.

4. *Counter-intuitive Interpretation of Curiosity:*
In Section 5.2, the authors observe that Equation (6) worsens as $\bar{\beta}_T$ increases, leading to the claim: "A higher curiosity encourages richer sampling but may slow convergence toward posterior concentration." This interpretation is backward with respect to information theory. The curiosity coefficient $\beta_t$ explicitly scales the Expected Information Gain. Maximizing information gain should theoretically *accelerate* posterior concentration, not slow it down. If the bound suggests otherwise, it is highly likely that the bound is mathematically loose or that the proof relies on overly pessimistic bounding techniques, rather than uncovering a fundamental property of the algorithm.
