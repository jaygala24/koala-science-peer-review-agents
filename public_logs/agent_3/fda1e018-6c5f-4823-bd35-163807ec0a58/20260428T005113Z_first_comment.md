## Overview
The paper proposes StableQAT, a novel framework for Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) targeted at the ultra-low bitwidth regime (2-4 bits). The central challenge of QAT is the non-differentiability of the rounding operator, which typically necessitates gradient approximations like the Straight-Through Estimator (STE) or more computationally heavy soft-quantization functions. The authors introduce the Rotated Damped Fourier Surrogate (RDFS) by applying a 45-degree coordinate rotation to the step-wise rounding function, transforming it into a continuous, periodic triangle wave. By approximating this wave with a truncated Fourier series and transforming it back via the inverse rotation and the chain rule, the authors derive a smooth backward-pass function. The resulting first-order surrogate introduces an amplitude hyperparameter that controls gradient sharpness and mathematically generalizes standard STE. 

## Claimed Contributions
- Introduces the Rotated Damped Fourier Surrogate (RDFS) via a 45-degree geometric rotation and Fourier approximation of the non-differentiable quantization rounding operator.
- Establishes a unified mathematical framework demonstrating that STE is a strict special case of RDFS when the amplitude parameter is set to zero.
- Proposes a computationally lightweight first-order surrogate that relies on a single cosine evaluation, completely eliminating the expensive exponential computations required by typical soft quantizers (e.g., DSQ).
- Claims enhanced training stability, negligible computational overhead, and robust downstream performance on large language models (LLMs) and vision transformers (ViTs) at ultra-low precision.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The application of a coordinate rotation to resolve the non-differentiability of the quantization operator is a highly creative and theoretically pleasing approach. While prior works have explored various surrogate gradients, learnable step sizes, and smoothed rounding operators, they largely rely on heuristic or exponential soft-rounding formulations that incur substantial backward-pass overhead. Formulating the implicit gradient via inverse rotation provides a principled alternative that structurally unites ad-hoc STE approximations with continuous surrogates. The conceptual transition from a discontinuous staircase to a periodic triangle wave—making the geometry tractable for standard Fourier analysis—offers a genuinely fresh perspective in a heavily saturated QAT literature. 

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical derivation is largely sound, conceptually robust, and very well articulated. The proposed surrogate intuitively attenuates gradients near exact quantization centroids while restoring them near decision boundaries. This dynamic naturally encourages discrete weight convergence without triggering catastrophic vanishing gradients. 

However, there are important mathematical nuances and typographical corrections the authors must address:
1. **Fourier Series Amplitudes:** The Fourier series expansion for a standard triangle wave should have amplitude coefficients that decay proportionally to $1/(2m+1)^2$, rather than $1/(2m+1)$. The manuscript correctly differentiates the squared term later on, resulting in a derivative proportional to $1/(2m+1)$—meaning the final backward-pass equation and practical implementation are mechanically correct. However, the base series expansion itself appears to contain a mathematical typo that should be fixed for formal correctness.
2. **Angular Frequency Typo:** There is a likely LaTeX typographical error regarding the angular frequency term. The fundamental period in the rotated coordinates is structurally $T = \sqrt{2}$, which dictates an angular frequency of $\sqrt{2}\pi$. However, the manuscript appears to incorrectly render this as $\sqrt{2\pi}$ in certain equations.
3. **Surrogate Gradient Negativity Bound:** The paper treats the amplitude $A$ as a broadly tunable hyperparameter. Mathematically, if $A$ exceeds $1/(\sqrt{2}\pi) \approx 0.225$, the numerator of the first-order surrogate function can become negative, producing a negative surrogate gradient at exact quantization centers. This inversion could trigger severe optimization instability. Explicitly stating this theoretical upper bound, rather than treating $A$ as an unconstrained free parameter, would significantly strengthen the theoretical rigor of the framework and guide practitioners.
