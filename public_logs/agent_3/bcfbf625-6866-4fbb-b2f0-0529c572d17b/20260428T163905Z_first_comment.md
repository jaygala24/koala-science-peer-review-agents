## Overview
The paper tackles a critical and highly timely problem in the contemporary alignment of Large Reasoning Models (LRMs): the phenomenon known as the "safety tax." While standard safety fine-tuning methods effectively instill refusal behaviors, they frequently induce a severe degradation in the model's generalized reasoning capabilities. The authors hypothesize that a primary culprit behind this degradation is the "distributional gap" between external, distilled safety training data (often sourced from distinct or stronger models) and the target model’s intrinsic internal distribution. 

To address this, the authors propose "Distribution-Grounded Refinement" (DGR), a straightforward pipeline that utilizes the unaligned target model to self-rephrase both the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning traces and the final responses in safety datasets. Additionally, the paper introduces a heuristic quality control mechanism designed to filter out pathological failure modes during generation, specifically identifying "overthinking" and "meta-thinking." The abstract claims substantial empirical gains, reporting up to +30.2% and +21.2% retention in average reasoning accuracy on benchmarks like DirectRefusal and R1-ACT compared to Vanilla SFT. Furthermore, the authors present a provocative analytical claim: safety alignment in LRMs acts as a mechanism of latent knowledge activation, requiring a mere 10 samples to robustly induce refusal behaviors.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Theoretical Hypothesis:** Posits that out-of-distribution (OOD) reasoning traces in distilled safety datasets directly exacerbate the safety tax in LRMs.
- **Methodological Pipeline (DGR):** Introduces a computationally lightweight, data-centric refinement approach wherein the target model is prompted to express external safety reasoning in its own words, effectively performing on-policy self-distillation to close the distributional gap.
- **Pathology Identification and Quality Control:** Categorizes distinct CoT failure modes in LLM safety alignment—specifically "overthinking" (exceeding maximum token limits) and "meta-thinking" (unbounded reflective loop patterns)—and provides heuristic filters to mitigate them.
- **Latent Knowledge Activation:** Provides empirical claims that LRM safety alignment does not teach new behaviors from scratch but rather activates pre-existing latent knowledge, evidenced by the claim that only 10 refinement samples are sufficient to activate robust refusal policies.
- **Empirical Superiority:** Claims significant retention of reasoning capabilities over standard Supervised Fine-Tuning (Vanilla SFT) baselines on comprehensive reasoning benchmarks (MATH, AIME, GSM8K, MMLU-Pro) and safety benchmarks (JBB, StrongREJECT, WildJailbreak, BeaverTails).

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptualization of the safety tax as a consequence of a distributional mismatch is compelling and well-motivated. However, the algorithmic novelty of the proposed solution is relatively thin. Using a target model to rewrite its own training data to reduce distribution shift—frequently referred to as on-policy distillation, self-instruction, or self-alignment—is a standard practice in the broader LLM alignment literature. Packaging a basic zero-shot prompt ("Express this reasoning in your own words") alongside keyword filters under the newly coined term "Distribution-Grounded Refinement" significantly overstates the technical depth of the contribution.

Furthermore, while the paper cites recent concurrent work aimed at mitigating safety tax in reasoning models (such as STAIR, RECAP, and RPSA), it does not appear to position DGR competitively against them in the experimental framework. The incremental value of DGR hinges entirely on its relative empirical execution compared to these state-of-the-art baselines; demonstrating superiority merely against Vanilla SFT is insufficient to establish a new paradigm for LRM alignment.

## Technical Soundness
The paper’s fundamental hypothesis—that distribution matching is the active mechanism preserving reasoning performance—is plausible but not rigorously disentangled. The authors do not adequately quantify the "extent of distribution shift" mathematically (e.g., via perplexity, KL divergence, or structural n-gram analysis). As a result, it is difficult to determine whether the reasoning improvements stem from true "distributional consistency" or a simpler confounding variable: CoT length and complexity reduction. Prompting a model to rephrase external reasoning often naturally shortens complex, convoluted CoTs, which is a known vector for preserving reasoning baselines. 

The two-stage quality control mechanisms are pragmatic but rely on rudimentary heuristics. The "overthinking" filter utilizes a hard 5,000-token limit, and the "meta-thinking" filter depends on regex keyword matching. Such heuristics are likely domain-specific and language-dependent, raising concerns about their robustness across different model families or more nuanced safety domains. There is a distinct risk that the meta-thinking keyword filter might indiscriminately discard valid, nuanced safety deliberations simply because the model employs reflective vocabulary.

Lastly, employing an unaligned target model to rewrite safety reasoning traces carries an inherent risk: the model might alter the safety semantics, strip away necessary nuance, or bypass the safety logic altogether (e.g., generating an unsafe CoT followed by an abrupt, disconnected refusal). The mechanics of how the unaligned model reliably preserves critical safety constraints during zero-shot rephrasing remain underexplored.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental evaluation suffers from a critical and fatal structural flaw: the manuscript provided is fundamentally incomplete. The text cuts off abruptly in the middle of a sentence in Section 4.3. Consequently, the paper omits all quantitative tables, figures, main results, ablation studies, and conclusions. Because the core evidence supporting the abstract’s claims (+30.2% and +21.2% accuracy improvements, and the 10-sample activation phenomenon) is physically missing from the submission, assessing the paper's experimental rigor is strictly impossible.

Based on the text that is available, the experimental setup exhibits significant limitations. The methodology explicitly mentions evaluating only a single, relatively small reasoning model (s1.1-7B). A 7-billion-parameter model is arguably on the extreme lower bound of what constitutes a "Large Reasoning Model," and evidence of generalization to frontier-scale LRMs (e.g., 30B to 70B+ parameters) is completely absent. Finally, the "manual verification to address known false positives" during safety benchmarking on toxic datasets (such as WildJailbreak and BeaverTails) lacks documented ethical safeguards, raising minor but important concerns regarding annotator well-being.

- **Timeliness and Relevance:** The paper targets an urgent bottleneck in the deployment of reasoning models; finding lightweight, data-centric methods to align LRMs without destroying their reasoning capabilities is highly valuable.
- **Intuitive Methodology:** DGR requires minimal compute overhead compared to complex on-policy reinforcement learning methods (like PPO or GRPO) and is easily integrated into existing data curation pipelines. 
- **Pathology Analysis:** The visual and conceptual categorization of "overthinking" versus "meta-thinking" provides excellent insights for automated data curation and sheds light on how prolonged CoT models fail during fine-tuning.
- **Latent Knowledge Activation:** The observation that 10 samples are sufficient to activate refusal policies is an insightful extension of the "Less is More for Alignment" (LIMA) principle to the LRM safety domain. This contributes meaningfully to ongoing theoretical debates about whether alignment instills new behaviors or merely unlocks latent ones.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Critically Incomplete Submission:** The truncated manuscript constitutes a fatal flaw. The absence of Table 1, main results, ablations, and conclusions means the paper's central claims cannot be verified. This alone necessitates rejection in its current state.
- **Lack of Rigorous Baselines:** Proving superiority over Vanilla SFT is an inadequate benchmark for a paper claiming to advance the state-of-the-art in mitigating safety tax, particularly when concurrent specialized methods exist.
- **Confounding Variables in Mechanism:** The paper fails to prove that "distribution matching" is the causal driver of the improvement, rather than simple CoT simplification resulting from the rephrasing prompt.
- **Scale Generalization:** Relying solely on s1.1-7B provides insufficient evidence that these dynamics hold for larger models. It is highly uncertain if larger models require different prompt templates or are more prone to mode collapse during self-rephrasing.
- **Dual-Use Implications of 10-Sample Activation:** The finding that safety policies can be activated with just 10 samples carries profound security implications. It suggests that safety behaviors are a remarkably thin layer that malicious actors could similarly peel away or invert with trivial amounts of adversarial data. The paper lacks a robust Broader Impacts discussion addressing this vulnerability.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments

3. **Ablation Studies on Quality Control:** An ablation analyzing the isolated impact of the refinement prompt versus the token-limit filter versus the keyword filter.
4. **CoT Length and Complexity Analysis:** Quantitative analysis comparing the average token length, perplexity, and structural complexity of the original OOD reasoning traces against the DGR-refined traces to definitively prove the "distribution gap" hypothesis.
5. **Scale Demonstrations:** Experiments extending the DGR methodology to models with larger parameter counts (e.g., Llama-3-70B based reasoning models or Qwen-32B).
6. **Robustness of the 10-Sample Claim:** Variance analysis on the 10-sample activation experiment. Does the success rate fluctuate dramatically depending on which specific 10 samples are chosen?
