## Overview
The paper tackles a fundamentally important problem in continuous optimization: finding the saddle point of a strongly convex-concave bilinearly coupled problem, formally defined as $\min_x \max_y f(x) + \langle Mx, y \rangle - \phi^*(y)$. This formulation broadly encompasses linearly constrained strongly convex optimization and various regularized primal-dual machine learning models. The authors set an ambitious and highly desirable goal: to achieve the optimal linear convergence rate of $O(\frac{s_{\max}}{s_{\min}}\sqrt{\frac{L}{\mu}} \log(1/\epsilon))$ using a single-loop "direct spectral acceleration" approach. If successful, this would eliminate the need for the cumbersome double-loop structures and Chebyshev inner-loop preconditioning required by prior state-of-the-art optimal methods (e.g., Salim et al., 2022a). 

While the motivation is excellent and the conceptual separation into objective-dominated and coupling-matrix-dominated regimes is theoretically insightful, a close inspection of the mathematical bounds provided in the main theorems reveals severe contradictions. The iteration complexities derived in the paper's core theorems mathematically evaluate to unaccelerated or suboptimal rates, directly falsifying the central claims of the paper. Consequently, despite the strong motivation, the paper currently contains fatal theoretical flaws that preclude acceptance in its present form.

## Claimed Contributions
1. **Direct Spectral Acceleration:** A single-loop primal-dual algorithm that matches optimal linear convergence rates without relying on Chebyshev inner loops.
2. **Regime-Specific Algorithmic Designs:** The development of two separate algorithms—x-side acceleration for the objective-dominated regime and y-side acceleration for the coupling-matrix-dominated regime.
3. **Proximable Dual Term Inclusion:** Generalizing beyond standard linear equality constraints by incorporating a proximable dual term $\phi^*$, accommodating inequality constraints and nonsmooth regularizers.
4. **Stochastic Block-Coordinate Extension:** Providing a stochastic variant of the method for separable objectives and deriving explicit finite-dimensional worst-case lower bounds that the proposed algorithms ostensibly match.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The attempt to remove inner-loop Chebyshev acceleration is a significant algorithmic pursuit. Double-loop algorithms are notoriously difficult to implement, tune, and debug in practice because the stopping criteria for the inner loop heavily impact the outer loop's theoretical guarantees and empirical stability. By proposing a direct single-loop momentum strategy (relying on carefully constructed Lyapunov functions), the authors are targeting a major pain point in primal-dual optimization. The conceptual lens of explicitly dividing the problem space into an objective-dominated regime and a coupling-matrix-dominated regime is a novel and elegant way to tailor the acceleration mechanisms. In principle, the paper’s approach represents a clear conceptual advance over the methods of Salim et al. (2022a) and similar double-loop precedents. 

## Technical Soundness
Unfortunately, the paper suffers from critical structural flaws in its mathematical claims. The central thesis is that the algorithms achieve the optimal accelerated rate of $O(\frac{s_{\max}}{s_{\min}}\sqrt{\frac{L}{\mu}} \log(1/\epsilon))$. However, evaluating the explicitly stated Lyapunov contraction factors ($\Pi$) in the core theorems yields complexities that are substantially worse. Because the iteration complexity directly scales as $O(\Pi \log(1/\epsilon))$, any suboptimal term in $\Pi$ defines the bottleneck of the algorithm.

**1. Contradiction in the Objective-Dominated Regime (Theorem 2.3):**
For Algorithm 1, Theorem 2.3 provides the mathematical formulation for $\Pi$. The expression explicitly contains the additive term $\frac{L}{\mu} + \frac{L}{\mu}\frac{1}{4\alpha}$. The paper enforces the parameter constraint $\alpha \le 1/5$. Therefore, the contraction factor strictly satisfies $\Pi \ge \Omega(L/\mu)$. 
In the objective-dominated regime, the true optimal convergence rate's dependence on the objective condition number is bounded by $O(\sqrt{L/\mu})$. Because Algorithm 1's complexity is bottlenecked by $\Omega(L/\mu)$, the method remains *unaccelerated* with respect to the strong convexity and smoothness of the primal objective. It falls drastically short of both the known optimal rate and the paper's own stated lower bounds. 

**2. Contradiction in the Coupling-Dominated Regime (Theorem 2.4):**
For Algorithm 2, Theorem 2.4 defines the parameter $\xi$ and the corresponding contraction factor $\Pi$. Based on the provided text, $\xi$ is chosen such that $\xi = O(\frac{s_{\max}}{s_{\min}}\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{L}})$. The expression for $\Pi$ contains the additive term $\xi \frac{s_{\max}^2}{s_{\min}^2}$. 
If we substitute the definition of $\xi$ into this term, it mathematically evaluates to $O(\frac{s_{\max}^3}{s_{\min}^3}\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{L}})$. In the specified coupling-matrix-dominated regime, we have $\xi \gg 1$, meaning the spectral ratio of the coupling matrix dominates the problem. In this regime, an iteration complexity scaling with $O(\frac{s_{\max}^3}{s_{\min}^3}\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{L}})$ is strictly and severely worse than the claimed optimal rate of $O(\frac{s_{\max}}{s_{\min}}\sqrt{\frac{L}{\mu}})$. Thus, the y-side acceleration also fails to achieve the claimed optimal linear convergence.

**3. Inheritance of Flaws in the Stochastic Extension (Theorem 3.2):**
The stochastic block-coordinate extension detailed in Theorem 3.2 inherits the same structural mathematical issues found in the deterministic case. Specifically, the bound for $\Pi$ in Theorem 3.2 explicitly retains the unaccelerated $O(L/\mu)$ term. Consequently, the claim that the stochastic algorithm establishes optimal linear rates matching the lower bounds is unverified and contradicted by the theorem's own equations.

Because all three primary upper bounds (Theorems 2.3, 2.4, and 3.2) evaluate to suboptimal complexities, the paper's overarching claim of "matching explicit worst-case lower bounds" is mathematically unsupported.
