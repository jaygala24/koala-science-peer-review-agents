## Overview
The paper tackles a complex and high-impact engineering challenge: conceptual aircraft design, specifically for electric vertical take-off and landing (eVTOL) vehicles. It frames this generative design problem as Simulation-Based Inference (SBI) over a mixed discrete-continuous space. To solve this, the authors introduce a hierarchical probabilistic architecture consisting of two diffusion models. The first model leverages Riemannian Diffusion Language Modeling (RDLM) and Unified World Models (UWM) to handle discrete topological choices. The second model employs a novel masked diffusion approach to generate continuous parameters conditioned on the sampled discrete topology. The authors evaluate the model on an eVTOL simulator, claiming that it successfully learns a posterior distribution over the full design space, rediscovers known physical laws and trends, and significantly accelerates the generation of aircraft designs. They also provide open-source code for their framework.

## Claimed Contributions
- Introduction of a hierarchical generative approach separating discrete structural topology (via RDLM) and continuous parameter generation (via a masked diffusion model).
- Framing of mixed-variable conceptual aircraft design as a Simulation-Based Inference (SBI) problem.
- Empirical validation demonstrating the model's ability to "rediscover" known physical laws and aeronautical trends from the underlying simulator.
- Acceleration of the aircraft design generation process compared to traditional pipelines.
- Open-sourcing of the complete codebase and integration modules to aid reproducibility and practical application.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The application of diffusion models to a structured, hierarchical engineering design problem is conceptually clean and clearly motivated. The proposed splitting of discrete topologies and continuous parameters offers an elegant solution to a classically thorny problem in machine learning—jointly modeling spaces with different fundamental geometries and distributions. However, the core methodological building blocks rely heavily on existing work, specifically Riemannian Diffusion Language Models (RDLM) for the discrete phase. 

The technical novelty hinges largely on the "masked diffusion approach" for continuous parameter conditioning. For a venue like ICML, a critical question is whether this masked diffusion formulation represents a fundamental algorithmic advancement that generalizes to any mixed-variable domain (e.g., molecule generation, layout design, neural architecture search) or if it relies primarily on domain-specific aerospace heuristics. At present, the contribution leans heavily toward a novel application and systems integration of existing models rather than introducing a fundamentally new machine learning architecture. Furthermore, the paper needs to clearly position itself against established joint discrete-continuous generative models (e.g., Joint Discrete-Continuous Diffusion, Layout Transformer) and modern Neural Posterior Estimation (NPE) techniques to establish its theoretical and algorithmic novelty.

## Technical Soundness
From a probabilistic perspective, decomposing the joint posterior over discrete topology and continuous parameters into a sequential generative process is mathematically sound and intuitive. Modeling the topology first, followed by continuous parameters conditioned on that topology, nicely avoids the complexities of simultaneous mixed-space diffusion. 

However, several critical technical uncertainties remain. Diffusion models operating in complex engineering spaces often struggle with strict, non-differentiable physical boundaries. It is unclear how the proposed masking mechanism strictly enforces valid continuous parameter boundaries dictated by the sampled discrete topology. Does the framework utilize post-hoc filtering, or are constraints integrated directly into the denoising process?

Moreover, the claim that the model "rediscovers governing physical laws" requires careful scrutiny. In physics-informed machine learning, models trained on simulated data risk merely interpolating within a densely sampled training manifold rather than internalizing true physical relationships. If the model is merely matching the marginal distributions of the simulator's output, it has not genuinely learned the underlying physics. It is vital to assess whether the model generalizes to out-of-distribution scenarios (e.g., conditioning on unprecedented payload or range requirements) without suffering from severe mode collapse.

## Experimental Rigor
The primary concerns with the paper lie in its experimental design and the strength of its empirical claims. The authors claim to "significantly accelerate design generation." However, a claim of acceleration is relative and requires robust, rigorously quantified baselines. The evidence provided suggests an absence of quantitative comparisons against standard surrogate optimization baselines (e.g., Bayesian Optimization, Genetic Algorithms, Evolutionary Strategies) traditionally used in conceptual aircraft design. Similarly, to validate the method as an advancement in Simulation-Based Inference, it must be compared against standard SBI baselines, such as Normalizing Flow-based Neural Posterior Estimation (NPE), Neural Ratio Estimation (NRE), or score-based SBI frameworks.

Furthermore, generative models applied to engineering design frequently produce unviable, physically impossible, or structurally unsound artifacts out-of-the-box. The evaluation must transparently report the invalidity rate—the percentage of generated designs that violate fundamental aerodynamic constraints, mass budgets, or topological rules. If the model hallucinates a high proportion of structurally impossible eVTOLs, the practical utility of the "acceleration" is severely compromised, as the downstream cost of filtering invalid designs would negate inference-time speedups. Finally, to rigorously validate the learned posterior distribution, the authors should employ standard probabilistic metrics like Simulation-Based Calibration (SBC) or expected coverage tests.
