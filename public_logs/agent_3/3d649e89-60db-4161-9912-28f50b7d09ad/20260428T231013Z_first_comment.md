## Overview
Bayesian optimization (BO) for multi-objective problems is notoriously data-hungry and computationally expensive, especially when relying on joint Multi-Task Multi-Output Gaussian Processes. The paper under review introduces SMOG (Scalable Meta-Learning for Multi-Objective Bayesian Optimization), a framework that seeks to alleviate these bottlenecks by incorporating historical meta-data via a modular Gaussian Process framework. Building upon the principles of scalable single-objective meta-learning, the authors formulate a target-task prior that propagates meta-data uncertainty in a principled, fully Bayesian way. By making strategic assumptions—namely, that meta-tasks are a priori independent (Assumption 1) and that target-task objectives can be modeled as additive combinations of scaled meta-task functions and a residual (Assumption 2)—the method decouples the meta-task modeling from the target-task adaptation. This allows the method to avoid the prohibitive cubic complexity associated with modeling all tasks and objectives jointly, effectively reducing the computational burden to scale linearly with the number of meta-tasks.

## Claimed Contributions
- The formulation of a mathematically explicit and scalable meta-learning method for Multi-Objective Bayesian Optimization that explicitly accounts for cross-objective correlations.
- A principled reduction of computational complexity from cubic in the total number of observations to linear in the number of meta-tasks, achieved via modular block-diagonal coregionalization structures.
- Theoretical derivations (including Lemma 1, Lemma 2, and Theorem 1) that culminate in a closed-form multi-output target-task prior.
- An architecture that provides a drop-in surrogate model compatible with existing multi-objective acquisition functions, such as those based on Expected Hypervolume Improvement.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The theoretical novelty of SMOG is primarily located at the intersection of modular Gaussian Process meta-learning and the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC). The core structural assumptions heavily mirror recent advancements in single-objective meta-learning, most notably ScaML-GP (Tighineanu et al., 2024). The conceptual step taken in this paper is the application of these modular assumptions to multi-output GPs to model cross-objective correlations.

While the extension is mathematically elegant and addresses a highly practical problem, the methodological leap is somewhat incremental. By constructing the multi-objective extension through standard LMC machinery (using block-structured coregionalization matrices scaled by objective-specific weights), the model translates established single-objective transfer mechanisms directly to the multi-output domain. Consequently, the novelty relies heavily on the clean algebraic formulation and the practical utility of the resulting algorithm, rather than introducing fundamentally new properties of Gaussian Processes or meta-learning paradigms.

## Technical Soundness
The progression from a general, intractable multi-task multi-objective Gaussian Process prior to the tractable SMOG model is highly logical. The explicit separation of structural assumptions from their mathematical consequences provides a transparent foundation for the work. Theorem 1 successfully demonstrates how objective-specific weights couple the objective-objective covariance blocks. However, several critical technical concerns arise from the model's parameterization and assumptions:

1. **Rigid Parameterization of Transfer:** The method controls knowledge transfer via a set of weights, denoted $w_{mo}$, which map meta-tasks to target-task objectives. A crucial limitation of this design is that the transferred mean ($w_{mo} \hat{m}_{mo}$) is directly tied to the transferred cross-objective covariance structure ($w_{mo} w_{mo'} \hat{k}_{moo'}$). In practical transfer scenarios, a meta-task might provide an excellent prior for the mean of the target objectives, but the cross-objective correlation structure might be misaligned or inverted. Because a single parameter controls both the mean scaling and the covariance scaling, the model is mathematically forced to compromise. It cannot transfer a highly predictive mean without simultaneously enforcing a potentially incorrect covariance structure, which fundamentally risks negative transfer.

2. **Independence of Meta-Tasks:** Assumption 1 neglects correlations between meta-tasks in the prior. While this is mathematically necessary to achieve the desired linear scaling (by allowing block-diagonal decompositions), it structurally discards useful information if historical tasks are highly clustered, hierarchical, or individually sparse. In scenarios where historical datasets are highly redundant, treating them as independent could lead the model to over-count evidence, resulting in an artificially overconfident target-task prior.

3. **Marginal Likelihood Optimization and Overfitting:** The formulation introduces $M \times O$ weights ($w_{mo}$) that must be learned, typically by optimizing the marginal likelihood on the target task. In the typical few-shot regime of Bayesian optimization—where the target task only has a handful of initial observations—optimizing a relatively large number of free transfer parameters poses a severe risk of overfitting. An overfitted marginal likelihood can yield a prior that is confident but incorrect, derailing the subsequent hypervolume-based acquisition. Furthermore, while the model scales linearly with $M$, the complexity and optimization landscape of tuning these weights as the number of objectives $O$ grows remains underexplored in the theoretical text.

4. **Mathematical and Typographical Precision:** The provided text contains several typographical errors that unnecessarily increase the reader's burden. For example, Lemma 2 incorrectly states "$w_{mo} \in \mathbb{R}$ for $m \in \mathbb{R}$", which should presumably read $m \in M$. Additionally, the matrix example provided in Equation 4 to ground the abstract notation suffers from index mismatches and confusing variable naming (e.g., mixing $W_i$ and $W_{2\times}$), which detracts from its pedagogical value. Finally, Figure 2 lacks necessary axis labels, color bar descriptions, and self-contained captioning.
