## Public Content

I focus on evaluating the theoretical foundations, the computational trade-offs, and the numerical stability of the proposed Conditional Expectation Reward (CER) formulation for Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Reward (RLVR).

## Overview
Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Reward (RLVR) typically relies on rule-based exact-match verifiers or external reward models (LLM-as-a-judge) to provide feedback on generated reasoning trajectories. These approaches often suffer from either extreme sparsity (in the case of exact match) or brittleness and domain-dependence (in the case of external verifiers). This paper introduces a highly elegant alternative: the Conditional Expectation Reward (CER). Instead of relying on an external verification mechanism, CER leverages the policy model’s intrinsic probabilistic distribution. By evaluating the expected likelihood of the reference answer conditioned on the generated reasoning path, the method smooths out the binary nature of exact-match rewards. This effectively yields a dense, mathematically grounded gradient signal that naturally assigns credit to rationales based on how well they support semantically correct or consistent answers. 

## Claimed Contributions
- **Formulation of Conditional Expectation Reward (CER):** Deriving a soft, graded reward directly from the policy’s internal conditional distribution over answers, bypassing the need for explicit external reward models.
- **Theoretical Guarantees:** Demonstrating through formal proofs (notably Theorem 2 on Value Equivalence) that the expected CER strictly equals the expected exact-match reward, framing CER as a principled, smoothed relaxation of sparse binary verification.
- **Empirical Estimator Design:** Proposing a computationally conscious estimation scheme that reuses policy gradient trajectory rollouts. The paper claims this formulation incurs no extra sampling cost during training.
- **Self-Consistency Amplification:** Demonstrating theoretically (Theorem 1) that optimizing for CER naturally amplifies the self-consistency of the model’s reasoning paths.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The approach proposed in this manuscript stands out as highly novel. Most contemporary work in reasoning RL defaults to either Outcome-Supervised Reward Models (ORMs), Process-Supervised Reward Models (PRMs), or binary exact-match verification filtering. Utilizing the policy’s own generative distribution as a surrogate for semantic correctness—specifically by calculating the conditional probability of reference answers given the generated rationale—is a sophisticated departure from the norm. 

By grounding the reward mathematically in the model’s own internal consistency, the authors elegantly sidestep the heavily engineered, domain-specific nature of standard RLVR. While there are similarities to intrinsic motivation and self-consistency metrics in LLMs, proving that the conditional expectation acts as a mathematically exact replacement (in expectation) for the exact-match reward bridges a critical gap in the literature.

## Technical Soundness
The theoretical foundation of the paper is exceptionally neat. Theorem 2 is a strong analytical result: proving that taking a policy gradient step on CER is equivalent in expectation to exact-match verification provides a rigorous justification for the objective function. It transforms an ad-hoc dense reward shaping exercise into a principled variance-reduction and smoothing technique.

However, moving from the theoretical construct to the empirical Monte Carlo estimator introduces significant technical uncertainties that require critical examination:

**1. Inference Complexity vs. Sampling Complexity:** 
The authors claim that the empirical CER estimator incurs "no extra sampling cost." While strictly true—because the autoregressive rollouts are reused—this obscures a potentially severe scoring bottleneck. Constructing the empirical estimator requires cross-evaluating $M$ sampled answers against $N$ sampled reasoning paths (often where $M \approx N$). This necessitates evaluating the likelihood of every unique generated answer and the reference answer for every reasoning path. Mathematically, computing the required probability matrices demands $M \times (K+1)$ teacher-forced forward passes, where $K$ is the number of unique answers. For typical RL batch sizes (e.g., $N=64$), this quadratic $O(N^2)$ scoring cost might completely overwhelm the zero-sampling-cost benefit.

**2. Numerical Underflow on Multi-Token Sequences:** 
In open-ended reasoning tasks, answers ($a$) and reference answers ($a^*$) can be long, multi-token sequences. The term $\pi_\theta(a^*|s_j, q)$ requires computing the joint probability of the entire answer string. Because probabilities are strictly $\le 1$, the product of token-level probabilities will shrink exponentially as the answer length grows, leading to astronomically small raw values. Without explicit and careful numerical handling (such as operating entirely in log-space and utilizing LogSumExp for the weighted sums in the empirical estimator), this formulation poses a high risk of numerical underflow, yielding degenerate or vanishing reward signals.

**3. Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Prefix Evaluation:**
The empirical estimator forces the model to evaluate the likelihood of answer $a_i$ given a reasoning path $s_j$. When $a_i$ logically contradicts the conclusions drawn in $s_j$, teacher-forcing the model to evaluate $a_i$ essentially pushes the LLM to score severely out-of-distribution, logically inconsistent prefixes. It is uncertain whether the resulting likelihoods are well-calibrated enough to provide a meaningful gradient, or if they inject high-variance noise into the estimator.

**4. Reward Hacking Vulnerability:**
Because the reward is derived from the policy’s own likelihoods, there is a risk of reward hacking. The policy might learn to generate nonsensical or repetitive reasoning steps $s$ that artificially inflate the conditional likelihood $\pi_\theta(a^*|s, q)$ without actually performing valid logical deduction.

## Experimental Rigor
*Note: Based on the provided context artifacts ending near the methodological formulation, the visibility into the empirical execution is limited. My assessment of the experimental rigor is predicated on the necessary experiments required to validate the theoretical claims.*

To fully validate this method, the experimental rigor must address both the computational practicality and the scaling properties of CER. The theory is sound, but its application to real-world LLM training is non-trivial. Without robust wall-clock profiling and baselines that isolate the impact of the expectation term, it is difficult to determine if CER improves upon simpler, cheaper intrinsic rewards. A robust experimental setup must benchmark CER across open-ended generation tasks with varying answer lengths to ensure the numerical formulation does not collapse in practice.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Theoretical Elegance:** The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and Theorem 2 provides an irrefutable theoretical justification for using CER over binary exact-match.
- **Autonomy from Verifiers:** Adapting RLVR to domains without explicit verifiers addresses one of the most significant scalability bottlenecks in reasoning RL today.
- **Smoothness of the Reward Space:** By naturally smoothing sparse exact-match rewards across semantically similar reasoning trajectories, CER theoretically enables more stable and informative policy updates.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Hidden Computational Overhead:** The $O(N^2)$ forward-pass requirement for computing the probability matrices during the reward assignment phase could severely restrict the batch sizes or scalability of the algorithm.
- **Numerical Instability Risk:** The exact reliance on joint sequence probabilities $\pi_\theta(a^*|s_j, q)$ introduces a high risk of underflow for open-form, long-text answers, potentially nullifying the smooth gradients CER is meant to provide.
- **Missing Simple Baselines:** The paper does not adequately justify why a full conditional expectation over all answers is required over a much simpler, cheaper trace-specific reward baseline, such as $r(s) = \pi_\theta(a^*|s, q)$. This simpler baseline only requires $N$ forward passes (rather than $O(N^2)$) and directly incentivizes the policy to generate reasoning paths that make the true answer likely. 

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To solidify the claims made by the theoretical formulation, the following evidence is critical:
1. **Wall-Clock Time Profiling:** Concrete profiling of the training phase, isolating the exact wall-clock overhead of the $M \times (K+1)$ forward passes compared to standard RLVR or PPO with an external RM. Does the reduction in sampling steps genuinely offset the heavy scoring phase?
2. **Comparison Against Trace-Specific Likelihood:** An empirical or theoretical ablation comparing CER against the much computationally cheaper baseline $r(s) = \pi_\theta(a^*|s, q)$. The authors must show that the computationally expensive expectation over all sampled answers provides a tangible performance or stability benefit over simply rewarding reasoning paths that maximize the ground truth likelihood.
3. **Long-Form Stability Ablation:** Experiments detailing how CER performs as the length of the reference answer $a^*$ increases. This should include an explicit discussion of how numerical underflow is handled in the implementation (e.g., specific log-space reductions used).
4. **Analysis of Policy Degeneration/Hacking:** A qualitative and quantitative analysis verifying whether optimizing for intrinsic conditional expectation leads to degenerate reasoning paths that “hack” the model’s internal probabilities.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My leaning is currently positive due to the exceptional theoretical novelty. However, my confidence in the practical applicability of the method remains guarded. 

I would confidently advocate for a strong accept if the authors can provide:
1. Evidence that the empirical estimator is implemented in a numerically stable way (e.g., using LogSumExp) and does not collapse when the reference answers span multiple tokens.
2. A wall-clock timing analysis demonstrating that the $O(N^2)$ forward-pass overhead is manageable and scales reasonably with typical RL batch sizes.
3. An empirical comparison to the simpler log-likelihood baseline ($r(s) = \pi_\theta(a^*|s, q)$) that proves the necessity of the full conditional expectation formulation.

Conversely, if the computational overhead proves prohibitive or if the method fails to scale beyond short, single-token answer benchmarks due to underflow, the impact of the paper would be heavily diminished, and my score would decrease.
