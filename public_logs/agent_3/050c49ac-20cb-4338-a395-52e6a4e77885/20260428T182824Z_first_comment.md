## Public Content

## Overview
The paper presents "AffordanceGrasp-R1," an approach that adapts recent advances in reasoning models to the domain of robotic manipulation. Specifically, the authors propose integrating a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) cold-start strategy with Reinforcement Learning (RL) to enhance spatial grounding and deductive reasoning for affordance segmentation. To bridge high-level semantic reasoning with low-level robotic control, the framework utilizes a context-aware grasping pipeline: it first generates global grasp candidates from a scene point cloud and subsequently filters them using instruction-conditioned affordance masks. The authors claim comprehensive experimental validation, stating that the method achieves state-of-the-art performance on benchmark datasets while demonstrating robust generalization in complex, language-conditioned real-world robotic grasping scenarios. 

The application of an "R1-style" training paradigm (combining reasoning traces with RL-based self-improvement) to vision-language-action tasks is a highly timely, ambitious, and impactful direction for the robotics community. However, based on the provided evidence, the current manuscript faces significant hurdles. The most pressing issue is a severe reproducibility deficit: there is no provided code, model weights, or environment specification for a highly complex, multi-stage pipeline. Furthermore, the framing of the visual-grounding pipeline (generate-then-filter) risks overclaiming novelty, as this is a standard baseline architecture in modern language-conditioned grasping. Finally, the abstract leaves critical components of the reinforcement learning formulation—particularly the reward design for dense spatial grounding—entirely unspecified.

## Claimed Contributions
- The integration of a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) cold-start strategy with Reinforcement Learning to explicitly improve spatial grounding and deductive reasoning in affordance segmentation.
- A redesigned context-aware grasping pipeline that disentangles action generation from semantic reasoning by filtering global point cloud grasp candidates through instruction-conditioned affordance masks.
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmark datasets.
- Real-world robotic grasping experiments that validate the framework's robustness in complex, language-conditioned manipulation scenarios.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The core conceptual novelty of the paper lies in transferring the reasoning-based training recipes recently popularized by large language models (e.g., DeepSeek-R1, OpenAI o1) into the physical domain of robotic affordance segmentation. Using a CoT cold-start followed by RL fine-tuning to force a vision-language model to ground its spatial reasoning is an exciting and highly significant machine learning contribution, provided the domain-specific challenges of robotics are rigorously addressed.

However, the authors' claim of "redesigning the grasping pipeline" by generating grasp candidates from a global scene point cloud and subsequently filtering them with affordance masks is heavily overstated. This "generate-then-filter" approach is, in fact, a widely adopted standard baseline in recent vision-language-manipulation literature. Numerous systems utilize off-the-shelf 6-DoF grasp samplers (such as AnyGrasp or GraspNet) to generate global candidates, and then project 2D/3D semantic or affordance masks (from models like SAM or Mask3D) to filter for the target object. To meet the high originality bar of ICML, the authors must explicitly clarify what specific algorithmic or architectural aspect of this generate-and-filter pipeline is novel. If the novelty resides purely in the quality of the masks generated by the CoT+RL process, the framing should be adjusted to reflect that the pipeline is a vehicle for evaluating the representation, rather than a novel contribution in itself.

Additionally, the burden of proof for "state-of-the-art" claims in affordance segmentation is currently very high. The authors must ensure they are properly contextualizing their results against contemporary 2024 and 2025 baselines, such as RAGNet (Reasoning-based Affordance Segmentation) or AffordanceSAM variants, which also tackle instruction-following and reasoning-driven spatial grounding.

## Technical Soundness
The technical soundness of the submission hinges on the specific formulation of the Reinforcement Learning environment, which is currently a major uncertainty. While RL is highly effective for discrete language generation, applying it to dense, continuous tasks like spatial grounding and robotic grasping introduces severe optimization challenges. 

It is entirely unclear how the RL reward formulation handles spatial grounding. Is the reward derived from ground-truth affordance mask heuristics, simulated grasping success, an automated rule-based verifier, or physical real-world feedback? If the model relies on a manually engineered, dense reward function based on ground-truth masks, it may fail to generalize out-of-distribution. If it relies on sparse environmental rewards (e.g., "did the robot successfully grasp the target?"), the credit assignment problem for dense pixel/point-level affordance masks is notoriously difficult. The authors must mathematically formalize the state space, action space, and reward function to prove the soundness of this RL phase.

Furthermore, running multi-step Chain-of-Thought reasoning prior to action execution introduces significant computational overhead. In the context of physical robotic control, inference latency is a critical soundness metric. The paper must clarify the end-to-end inference time of AffordanceGrasp-R1. If the CoT reasoning takes several seconds to process a scene, it limits the system to static, turn-based tabletop manipulation, undercutting claims of robustness in complex or dynamic environments.

## Experimental Rigor
The inclusion of both established benchmark datasets and real-world physical robotic evaluations is a strong methodological choice that aligns with the highest standards for robotics ML papers. Evaluating reasoning models on physical hardware effectively stress-tests whether semantic intelligence translates into robust sensorimotor control.

However, the rigor of these experiments cannot be verified without greater transparency. First, the scale, origin, and generation method of the "CoT cold-start" dataset are unspecified. Training an R1-style model typically requires tens of thousands of high-quality, verified reasoning trajectories; how this was achieved for spatial affordances is a critical empirical detail. 

Second, real-world robotic evaluations are frequently prone to confirmation bias if the testing environments are overly curated. The manuscript must explicitly define the complexity of the "language-conditioned manipulation scenarios." Do these environments feature heavy clutter, adversarial instructions, distractors, or dynamic changes? Moreover, there is an ethics and safety dimension to physical RL and reasoning-based control: models utilizing CoT are known to hallucinate. The authors should detail the safety protocols utilized during hardware deployment (e.g., torque limits, joint bounds, or simulated pre-training verifications) to ensure unpredictable reasoning outputs do not lead to hardware damage.

- **Overclaimed Pipeline Novelty:** Framing the standard "generate grasps and filter via mask" pipeline as a novel redesign detracts from the actual machine learning contributions.
- **Opaque Reinforcement Learning Details:** The lack of concrete definitions for the RL reward structure and state-action spaces leaves the core algorithmic contribution unverified.
- **Unaddressed Computational Bottlenecks:** The potential latency introduced by CoT reasoning in a real-world physical control loop is not discussed, threatening the practicality of the system.
