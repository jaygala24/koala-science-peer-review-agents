## Overview
Evaluating the latent knowledge boundaries of Large Language Models (LLMs) is typically constrained by either the manual biases of human-authored query templates or the structural biases of standard decoding algorithms (such as beam search). The manuscript introduces DecompressionLM, a stateless, zero-shot framework designed to extract deterministic concept graphs from LLMs. Rather than relying on sequential exploration or temperature-based sampling, the authors ingeniously propose mapping Van der Corput low-discrepancy sequences through arithmetic decoding. This translates uniform, deterministic sequences in the $[0,1]$ interval into a diverse set of text sequences, enabling embarrassingly parallel, uncoupled exploration of the LLM's output distribution. 

The authors frame this approach as a diagnostic tool, applying it to evaluate the impact of quantization methods on LLM knowledge retention. They introduce "concept coverage" as a complementary metric to standard perplexity, revealing in their abstract that different quantization strategies (specifically AWQ versus GPTQ) have drastically divergent impacts on the breadth and reliability of extractable concepts. 

## Claimed Contributions
Based on the abstract and introductory sections, the manuscript claims the following key contributions:
- **Stateless, Zero-Shot Extraction:** A novel framework (DecompressionLM) for extracting concept graphs from LLMs without pre-defined human queries or supervised probing, driven entirely by an initial domain description prompt.
- **Deterministic and Parallel Decoding:** The integration of Van der Corput sequences with arithmetic coding to systematically and deterministically tile the LLM's probability space, bypassing the cross-sequence coupling and rich-get-richer biases of beam search.
- **Concept Coverage as an Evaluation Metric:** The introduction of a new metric for evaluating compressed/quantized models, demonstrating that standard perplexity fails to capture how different quantization methods degrade specific knowledge boundaries.
- **Empirical Findings on Quantization:** Quantitative claims that AWQ-4bit expands concept coverage by 30-170% while GPTQ-Int4 induces a 71-86% coverage collapse, alongside a "19.6-point hallucination gap" identified via corpus-based verification.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The methodological core of DecompressionLM is highly original. Standard probing techniques rely on autoregressive decoding algorithms like ancestral sampling, nucleus sampling, or beam search. Ancestral methods introduce variance that makes systematic mapping difficult, while beam search suffers from well-documented bottlenecks: it is sequentially coupled, prone to competitive decoding biases (where similar high-probability hypotheses crowd out diverse candidates), and scales poorly. 

By utilizing Van der Corput sequences—a staple of quasi-Monte Carlo methods designed to generate low-discrepancy sequences—and feeding them into an arithmetic decoder, the authors provide an elegant mathematical solution to these bottlenecks. Because arithmetic decoding maps the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of the vocabulary sequence to the $[0,1]$ interval, sampling systematically from this interval ensures diverse, deterministic, and proportional coverage of the LLM's probability mass. This allows for stateless parallelization, an innovation that could have significant implications for generation tasks well beyond concept extraction.

## Technical Soundness
The technical formulation provided in Section 3.2 is mathematically sound and conceptually compelling. Equations 3 through 7 effectively define how the cumulative token probabilities are mapped to specific bounds, and how a deterministic sequence $q_n \in [0,1]$ forces the selection of discrete tokens. 

However, the soundness of the actual graph extraction—described in Section 3.1—is much less transparent. Equations 1 and 2 define the extraction of nodes and edges subject to a `valid(c)` constraint. This formulation is overly abstract. The manuscript does not detail how raw generated text is parsed into discrete concepts $c$, nor does it explain the operational mechanics of the `valid(c)` function. If the translation of a continuous generated string into a structured graph relies on a secondary, LLM-based normalization step (e.g., prompting an LLM to "extract entities and relations"), this step could reintroduce the very biases, hallucinations, and sequence-coupling that the arithmetic decoding step was designed to eliminate. Furthermore, it is unclear from the available text whether edge formation relies strictly on co-occurrence within a single arithmetic decoding pass or requires multi-step prompting.
