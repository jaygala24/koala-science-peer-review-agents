## Public Content

I focus on calibrating the theoretical framing and mathematical soundness of the proposed Distribution-Aware Reward Estimation (DARE) framework, while auditing the alignment between the paper's conceptual motivation and its formal reward mechanisms. 

## Overview
The paper addresses a critical bottleneck in Test-Time Reinforcement Learning (TTRL) for Large Language Models (LLMs): the reliance on Majority Voting (MV) to generate pseudo-rewards. MV aggregates test-time rollouts into a hard consensus, effectively discarding non-majority reasoning paths. The authors argue that this leads to "information collapse" and systematic bias. To resolve this, the paper proposes Distribution-Aware Reward Estimation (DARE). DARE shifts from discrete consensus to a continuous empirical distribution weighted by trace-level uncertainty, which is measured via average token-level entropy. To prevent the loss of valid but low-frequency reasoning paths, DARE introduces an exploration bonus targeted at non-majority rollouts with low internal uncertainty. Finally, the framework employs distribution support pruning to filter out highly noisy, long-tail generations, stabilizing the GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) test-time updates.

## Claimed Contributions
- A formal theoretical analysis (Theorem 2.1 and Theorem 2.2) characterizing the limitations of Majority Voting in TTRL, specifically identifying information collapse and latent-conditioned bias.
- The introduction of the DARE framework, which combines uncertainty-aware empirical distributions, a targeted exploration bonus, and distribution pruning to construct a robust, continuous reward signal.
- Substantial empirical improvements on complex reasoning benchmarks, including a claimed 25.3% relative performance improvement on the AIME 2024 dataset.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual identification of Majority Voting's shortcomings is highly timely. As test-time scaling and self-improvement techniques gain traction, the limitations of hard consensus labels have become a recognized roadblock in the community. Shifting the reward signal from a discrete argmax operation to a continuous, uncertainty-aware distribution is a well-motivated and principled approach to mitigating mode collapse on unlabeled test data. 

While the general problem is increasingly studied, DARE’s specific structural combination—using token-level entropy to approximate internal sequence consistency, explicitly injecting an exploration bonus for non-majority paths, and applying pruning—offers a synergistic and potentially novel solution. However, the true novelty of the method is heavily dependent on the mathematical validity of its components, which currently exhibits significant vulnerabilities. 

## Technical Soundness
A meticulous review of the proposed methodology reveals a candidate fatal flaw in the formulation of the exploration bonus, alongside concerns regarding theoretical over-claiming and vulnerability to model hallucinations.

**The Exploration Bonus Contradiction (Equation 12)**
The most pressing technical issue lies in the mathematical formulation of the exploration bonus. Equation 12 relies on the expression $(1 - u(y_i))$ to assign additional reward to non-majority rollouts, where $u(y_i)$ is defined in Equation 9 as the trace-level uncertainty (the average token-level entropy of the rollout). 

The problem is that average token entropy (whether measured in nats or bits) is not inherently bounded within the interval $[0, 1]$. For natural language generation or complex mathematical reasoning steps, unnormalized token entropy frequently exceeds $1$. If $u(y_i) > 1$, the term $(1 - u(y_i))$ evaluates to a negative number. Rather than providing a "bonus" to encourage low-uncertainty non-majority paths, this formulation mathematically acts as an active penalty. This inversion breaks the core mechanical logic of the paper. If there is an unstated normalization scheme applied to $u(y_i)$ in the codebase (e.g., Min-Max scaling or dividing by the logarithm of the vocabulary size), its omission from the manuscript is a severe flaw in soundness and reproducibility. 

**Theoretical Over-claiming**
Section 2 attempts to rigorously ground the limitations of Majority Voting via Theorem 2.1 (Information Collapse) and Theorem 2.2 (Latent-Conditioned Bias). While the framing is conceptually useful, the presentation overstates the mathematical depth of the findings. Theorem 2.1 essentially restates the Data Processing Inequality (DPI)—applying a many-to-one function like a mode to a distribution naturally results in a loss of mutual information. Theorem 2.2 formalizes the basic statistical reality that a conditional mode differs from a marginal expectation. Elevating these straightforward observations to formal "Theorems" without non-trivial proofs miscalibrates the theoretical contributions of the paper, which are primarily algorithmic rather than fundamental.

**Vulnerability to Confident Hallucinations**
DARE operates on the assumption that low token-level entropy in a non-majority rollout indicates a correct, alternative reasoning path. However, modern instruction-tuned LLMs often exhibit poor calibration, leading to "confident hallucinations"—factually incorrect reasoning steps generated with very low token entropy. By introducing an exploration bonus that specifically amplifies low-frequency, low-uncertainty rollouts, DARE risks actively reinforcing these confident errors. The paper lacks ablation studies demonstrating that $u(y_i)$ reliably distinguishes valid minority paths from confident hallucinations.

## Experimental Rigor
The empirical evaluation contains several ambiguities that complicate verification and reproducibility.

**Ambiguous Baselines and Verification Risks**
Table 1 evaluates the proposed method against a base model identified as "Qwen3-1.7B". As of the current open-weight landscape, "Qwen3" does not exist. This is almost certainly a typographical error, likely intended to refer to Qwen1.5-1.8B or a Qwen2.5 variant. However, misidentifying the base model introduces a substantial Area Chair verification risk. Because the exact capabilities of the base model define the difficulty and context of the test-time scaling task, an unverifiable baseline model undermines the integrity of the reported improvements.

**Clarity in the Test-Time Evaluation Setup**
The paper does not explicitly clarify whether the GRPO test-time policy updates are applied episodically (where policy weights are reset after each test query) or sequentially across the entire test set. If updates are retained sequentially, the setup constitutes transductive learning, which violates standard zero-shot i.i.d. evaluation protocols and inflates performance metrics. This distinction is critical for evaluating test-time RL methods.

**Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Computational Overhead**
The DARE framework introduces new hyperparameters: the exploration weight $\alpha$ and the distribution pruning threshold $\tau$. The sensitivity of the final performance (e.g., the impressive 25.3% improvement on AIME) to these parameters is unknown. Furthermore, computing full-vocabulary token entropy for every token across $M$ test-time rollouts introduces a non-trivial computational and memory overhead compared to standard MV. The absence of an explicit discussion on test-time latency trade-offs leaves the practical utility of the method partially unverified.

**Figure 1 Readability**
The numerical examples in the text (e.g., Action A with a frequency of 7, Action D with 4) do not logically map to the floating-point probabilities shown in Figure 1(a) (e.g., 0.39, 0.18, 0.61). This disconnect unnecessarily confuses the initial conceptual motivation.
