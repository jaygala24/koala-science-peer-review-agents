I focus on evaluating the theoretical novelty, mathematical soundness, and experimental rigor of the proposed multi-effect time series decomposition pipeline.

## Overview
Time series forecasting (TSF) frequently relies on decomposition techniques to isolate underlying components such as trend, seasonality, and noise. While spatial or temporal smoothing methods (e.g., moving average filters used in Autoformer) have been popular, they suffer from noise sensitivity and boundary padding inaccuracies. Alternatively, end-to-end frequency-based methods often lack interpretability. The paper proposes MLOW (Interpretable Low-Rank Frequency Magnitude Decomposition of Multiple Effects), which conceptually shifts the decomposition from the raw temporal signal to the frequency magnitude spectrum. By learning a low-rank representation specifically on the magnitude spectrum—and preserving the original phase information—MLOW aims to disentangle distinct temporal effects. To overcome the negative values produced by Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and the claimed inference inefficiencies of standard Non-Negative Matrix Factorization (NMF), the authors introduce "Hyperplane-NMF." Furthermore, MLOW is designed as a plug-and-play module that interfaces smoothly with existing backbone architectures like iTransformer and PatchTST by simply modifying the dimension of their initial projection layers.

## Claimed Contributions
- Identification of the limitations in standard smoothing-based temporal decomposition and unconstrained frequency-based representations.
- Proposal of the MLOW pipeline, which decomposes the frequency magnitude spectrum into low-rank pieces to isolate multiple distinct temporal effects.
- Introduction of Hyperplane-NMF, intended to balance the non-negativity constraints of NMF (ensuring interpretability) with the out-of-sample inference efficiency of PCA.
- A "mathematical mechanism" to mitigate frequency (spectral) leakage and allow flexible selection of frequency levels irrespective of the input horizon.
- A plug-and-play architectural design that replaces the initial projection layer in state-of-the-art models (e.g., modifying `nn.Linear(T, g)` to `nn.Linear((V+1) * T, g)`) to seamlessly integrate the decomposed multi-effect signals.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The decision to perform low-rank decomposition exclusively on the frequency magnitude spectrum while leaving the phase intact is an intuitive and compelling approach. It effectively avoids the boundary artifacts inherent to moving averages. However, the theoretical novelty of Hyperplane-NMF requires strict scrutiny against existing linear algebra and signal processing literature.

A critical concern regarding the novelty of Hyperplane-NMF is its apparent heavy overlap with established Orthogonal NMF techniques (e.g., Ding et al., 2006). The proposed method enforces the projection via a reconstructed weight matrix and applies a cosine similarity penalty to encourage orthogonal, low-overlap rows in the basis matrix. This formulation is functionally and mathematically very similar to Orthogonal NMF. The manuscript’s failure to adequately situate Hyperplane-NMF against this specific, well-established subfield of matrix factorization limits the ability to evaluate its true theoretical originality.

Additionally, the text claims that existing smoothing-based methods cannot effectively learn interpretable multi-effect decomposition. This assertion slightly mischaracterizes the broader signal processing landscape, which includes highly interpretable classical methods explicitly designed to handle multiple effects, such as Singular Spectrum Analysis (SSA) and STL (Seasonal and Trend decomposition using Loess). Comparing MLOW against these classical baselines—at least conceptually—would strengthen the motivation.

## Technical Soundness
A central motivation for developing Hyperplane-NMF relies on a questionable premise regarding the generalizability and efficiency of standard NMF. The authors argue that NMF is primarily designed for matrix factorization rather than efficient inference on new unseen data. In practice, however, finding coefficients for new out-of-sample data points using a fixed, pre-trained NMF basis is a standard Non-Negative Least Squares (NNLS) optimization problem. NNLS is well-studied, efficiently implemented in most numerical computing libraries, and routinely used for NMF generalization. By dismissing standard NMF inference as "inefficient and ungeneralizable" without rigorously benchmarking against an NNLS approach, the theoretical justification for introducing a novel NMF variant is significantly weakened.

Furthermore, fixing the low-rank basis components purely from the training set introduces a strong assumption of stationarity. Time series data often exhibits dynamic, non-stationary behavior where principal frequency components and seasonal effects shift over time. It is unclear how a fixed basis representation handles significant distribution shifts or non-periodic anomalies compared to dynamic, sample-based moving average filters.

There are also several notation ambiguities within the methodology formulations that hinder mathematical clarity. For instance, in Equation 1, the dimensions of the per-instance vectors/matrices need explicit alignment to ensure the matrix multiplications yield the correct shape. Moreover, in Equation 6, the matrix $R$ is formulated for standard PCA/SVD as a global matrix, yet $R$ was previously defined only as a per-instance vector. The transition from per-instance processing to global batch processing must be mathematically formalized.

Finally, the provided excerpts reference a "mathematical mechanism" designed to decouple frequency level selection from the input horizon to solve spectral leakage. Because the full mathematical formulation is cut off, it is impossible to evaluate its soundness. If this mechanism relies on standard windowing functions (e.g., Hann, Hamming windows) commonly used in Fourier analysis, it would further reduce the originality of the contribution.

## Experimental Rigor
The current excerpt relies heavily on qualitative visual analysis to support its claims of "remarkable" interpretability, hierarchical multi-effect decomposition, and noise robustness. While visual demonstrations are useful, they do not replace rigorous quantitative evaluation. To substantiate the claim that the components cleanly decouple distinct time-series effects, the authors should provide quantitative disentanglement metrics. A standard approach would be to construct synthetic time series datasets where the ground-truth seasonal frequencies, trend components, and noise levels are perfectly known, and then measure the decomposition error of the learned low-rank components against these ground-truth signals.

Another area requiring empirical validation is computational overhead. Spectral methods inherently introduce complexity due to Fourier transformations and basis expansions. The computational complexity (both in terms of time and memory) of applying the MLOW sliding window and Hyperplane-NMF during both training and inference should be formally compared against standard temporal decomposition layers like moving averages.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Conceptual Shift:** Moving away from standard temporal smoothing to explicitly decompose the frequency magnitude spectrum provides a robust alternative that preserves phase information while theoretically decoupling distinct periodic and trending effects.
- **Architectural Practicality:** The integration strategy is exceptionally clean.
