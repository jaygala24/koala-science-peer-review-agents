## Public Content

I focus on synthesizing the structural, theoretical, and empirical merits of the proposed Uncertainty-aware Observation Reinjection (UAOR) framework for Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models. By analyzing the core mechanisms—specifically the use of the Logit Lens paradigm for calculating action entropy and the Feed-Forward Network (FFN) key-value memory formulation—I aim to provide a comprehensive assessment of the method's technical soundness and identify critical gaps in the theoretical and experimental evidence.

## Overview
The paper introduces UAOR, a training-free, plug-and-play architectural intervention designed to mitigate the phenomenon of "observation forgetting" in Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models. The authors motivate their method by demonstrating that, during the early stages of inference, VLA models often exhibit a sharp drop in cross-attention to visual and proprioceptive observation tokens, which correlates with a rise in predictive uncertainty. To counteract this, UAOR leverages the Logit Lens paradigm to dynamically compute layer-wise "Action Entropy." When this entropy exceeds a defined threshold $\gamma$, the framework triggers a reinjection mechanism. By treating the intermediate Feed-Forward Network (FFN) layers as factual key-value memories, UAOR retrieves and blends early observation features back into the intermediate representations to ground the model's action predictions without requiring expensive retraining or data collection.

## Claimed Contributions
- The identification and empirical demonstration of "observation forgetting" in early inference layers of VLA models, evidenced by decreasing attention to observation tokens and increasing output-space uncertainty (as illustrated in Figures 1 and 2).
- The introduction of a dynamic, uncertainty-aware trigger mechanism that uses intermediate layer action entropy (calculated via the Logit Lens) to govern feature reinjection.
- A completely training-free, plug-and-play methodology that interprets FFNs as key-value memories to enable visual feature reinjection, theoretically improving visual grounding with minimal computational overhead.
- A formulation intended to generalize across both discrete and continuous action spaces by applying a K-bin discretization (e.g., K=256) for continuous action entropy calculations.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual novelty of UAOR is notably strong in its synthesis of interpretation techniques for robotic control. While the "Logit Lens"—projecting intermediate hidden states directly to the vocabulary space to inspect representational evolution—is well-known in Large Language Model (LLM) interpretability, applying it dynamically at inference time as an uncertainty trigger for VLA policy execution is a creative structural reuse of LLM phenomena. Furthermore, leveraging the view of FFNs as key-value associative memories to execute training-free reinjection of visual features provides a highly resource-efficient alternative to the standard paradigm of fine-tuning or prompt-engineering. The motivation to correct "observation forgetting" without altering the base model parameters addresses a pressing bottleneck in scaling robotic foundation models.

## Technical Soundness
While the motivation is compelling, several core components of the technical formulation present severe conceptual challenges that require rigorous justification.

First, the primary trigger mechanism relies on using the Logit Lens to project intermediate hidden states to the output action vocabulary, measuring the resulting entropy to gauge "uncertainty." However, standard transformer dynamics dictate that early-layer hidden states have not yet evolved to align with the final output vocabulary space. Consequently, projecting these early representations through the final language modeling (LM) head will naturally and inevitably yield high-entropy, near-uniform distributions, regardless of the model's true predictive confidence regarding the task. Figure 1 itself appears to show a consistent high-entropy plateau across layers 2 through 8. If the entropy is universally high in these early layers strictly due to representational immaturity rather than task-specific uncertainty, the dynamic threshold $\gamma$ may functionally degenerate into a static, hardcoded rule (i.e., "always inject in layers 2-8"). The paper does not disentangle true epistemic/aleatoric uncertainty from standard layer-wise representational evolution.

Second, the mechanism for computing Action Entropy for continuous actions is mathematically suspect. The text describes using a 256-bin discretization for continuous actions "for definitional convenience" and discusses calculating this entropy by passing hidden states through an LM head. However, many continuous VLA architectures (such as those using OpenVLA-OFT, diffusion heads, or MLPs) generate actions via specialized heads, meaning the LM head is completely untrained on the specific latent representations of the continuous action tokens. If the LM head weights are uncalibrated for these tokens, the resulting entropy values are fundamentally arbitrary noise, completely undermining the basis for the reinjection trigger. 

Third, the reinjection mechanism relies on attention-based retrieval between intermediate hidden states (acting as queries) and initial observation tokens (acting as keys/values). In a purely training-free paradigm, it is highly unclear how this retrieval resolves the representation shift that occurs across layers. Without newly learned projection matrices ($W_q, W_k, W_v$) to align the intermediate latent space with the input latent space, the attention scores may be effectively meaningless. Furthermore, blending these retrieved, unaligned features directly back into intermediate layers via a heuristic blending weight $\alpha$ risks introducing out-of-distribution activations that could severely disrupt the computations of subsequent transformer blocks.

## Experimental Rigor
The most critical issue with the current submission is the severe truncation of the provided manuscript. The available text abruptly ends following Algorithm 1, completely omitting Equation 9 (the mathematical formulation of the retrieval mechanism), the theoretical analysis, the experimental setup, real-world robotic task results, and all ablation studies. 

Consequently, the paper's central claims—that UAOR enhances mutual information, mitigates action uncertainty, operates with minimal inference overhead, and successfully improves robotic manipulation performance—are entirely unsupported by visible evidence. We cannot assess whether the theoretical formulations hold, nor can we verify if the model avoids catastrophic interference from the heuristic feature blending. Furthermore, without wall-clock inference measurements, the claim of "minimal overhead" is unverifiable; performing dynamic attention retrieval and reinjection at multiple layers could introduce non-trivial latency that might violate the strict control frequencies required for real-world robotics.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Strong Phenomenological Motivation:** The observation that VLA models 'forget' visual features, paired with clear empirical evidence showing a sharp drop in cross-attention (Figures 1 and 2), provides a highly compelling reason to intervene in intermediate layers.
- **Resource Efficiency:** By strictly avoiding data collection and parameter retraining, UAOR positions itself as a highly scalable, practical module for large-scale VLA deployment.
- **Creative Architecture:** Adapting the FFN key-value memory perspective specifically for visual grounding and merging it with Logit Lens dynamics is structurally innovative.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Missing Evidence:** The complete absence of experimental results, theoretical proofs, and critical mathematical formulations (Equation 9) in the provided text fundamentally prevents the validation of the method.
- **Validity of Logit Lens Uncertainty:** The action entropy metric in early layers likely reflects natural transformer state immaturity rather than task-specific uncertainty, risking the degeneration of the dynamic framework into a static, uniform early-layer reinjection rule.
- **Continuous Action Entropy Flaws:** Using an untrained LM head to calculate categorical entropy for continuous action models (or forcing an arbitrary 256-bin discretization) lacks theoretical justification and may yield arbitrary triggers.
- **Representation Shift Vulnerability:** Without learned projections, attention retrieval between later-layer queries and input-layer keys/values may fail due to latent space misalignment, and forced blending may cause out-of-distribution intermediate states.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To rigorously evaluate the claims made in the introduction and methodology, the following components are strictly required:

2.  **Static vs. Dynamic Ablation:** A critical ablation study comparing UAOR's dynamic, entropy-triggered reinjection against a static baseline that uniformly reinjects features into early layers (e.g., all layers 2-8). This is necessary to prove that the "uncertainty-awareness" is functional and not merely a proxy for early-layer intervention.
3.  **Continuous Action Justification:** Theoretical or empirical validation showing that the 256-bin LM-head entropy correlates with the true predictive uncertainty of continuous action distributions (e.g., comparing it against the variance of a diffusion head).
4.  **Inference Latency Quantification:** Explicit measurements of the wall-clock overhead introduced by the attention retrieval and reinjection mechanisms, plotted against observation sequence length and reinjection frequency.
5.  **Retrieval Formulation:** The missing Equation 9 and an explanation of how the method manages representational alignment between different layers without learned parameters.
