## Overview
The paper investigates the theoretical and empirical disparities between Wasserstein Variational Inference (WVI) and Black-Box Variational Inference (BBVI), specifically when restricted to the Gaussian variational family. Recent literature in the variational inference community has increasingly suggested that the Bures-Wasserstein geometry inherently provides superior iteration complexity and optimization dynamics compared to standard Euclidean parameter-space optimization. The authors compellingly argue that this apparent geometric advantage is largely an artifact of an unbalanced comparison of stochastic gradient estimators. Specifically, WVI implementations have typically employed the second-order Bonnet-Price gradient estimator, whereas BBVI has relied predominantly on the first-order reparameterization gradient. By equipping both Stochastic Proximal Bures-Wasserstein Gradient Descent (SPBWGD) and Stochastic Projected Gradient Descent (SPGD) with the Bonnet-Price gradient estimator, the authors derive matched, state-of-the-art non-asymptotic discrete-time iteration complexity bounds. Through this alignment, the paper seeks to unify the parameter-space and measure-space optimization perspectives, effectively disentangling the geometry of the optimization space from the gradient estimation strategy.

## Claimed Contributions
- **Identification of the Estimator Gap:** Demonstrates that the observed iteration complexity gap between WVI and BBVI for the Gaussian family is fundamentally driven by the choice of gradient estimator rather than inherent advantages of the Bures-Wasserstein geometry.
- **Matched Theoretical Bounds:** Derives identical, state-of-the-art non-asymptotic discrete-time iteration complexity bounds (scaling as $\tilde{O}(d\kappa/\epsilon)$) for both BBVI (via SPGD) and WVI (via SPBWGD) when utilizing the Bonnet-Price gradient estimator.
- **Cross-Pollination of Estimators:** Proposes theoretically evaluating BBVI equipped with Price's second-order estimator, while also modifying WVI to accommodate the first-order reparameterization gradient, expanding its applicability to settings where Hessian computations are prohibitive.
- **Pedagogical Unification:** Offers a clear and concise theoretical summary (featured prominently in Table 1) that disentangles the effects of the optimization space from the gradient estimation strategies, serving as a useful reference for the community.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The conceptual unification offered by the paper is valuable, providing a highly necessary corrective to recent literature. However, the theoretical groundwork builds heavily on established equivalences. The tight relationship between Bures-Wasserstein and Euclidean parameterizations for Gaussian Variational Inference is well-documented in prior work (e.g., Yi & Liu, 2023; Hoffman & Ma, 2020). Consequently, the primary novelty does not lie in discovering this geometric equivalence, but rather in explicitly formalizing how mismatched optimization oracles created a misleading theoretical gap. 

From an algorithmic perspective, demonstrating that a second-order oracle (which leverages the Hessian via Price's theorem) achieves superior iteration complexity compared to an algorithm relying strictly on first-order information (the reparameterization gradient) is a standard outcome in optimization theory. Because the underlying geometry between the parameter space and the measure space for Gaussians is already known to be tightly linked, translating a Hessian-based estimator from WVI to the parameter space is a relatively straightforward extension of existing SPGD bounds. Thus, while the paper succeeds brilliantly in "correcting the record," the core conceptual novelty is somewhat incremental.

## Technical Soundness
The mathematical derivation of identical iteration complexities for SPGD and SPBWGD using Price's estimator appears sound and provides a mathematically satisfying resolution to a prominent open debate. The authors clearly outline the equivalence mappings for Price's gradient estimators between the two spaces.

However, significant foundational concerns arise regarding the geometric validity of the proposed modifications to the WVI algorithm when adapting it to use first-order gradients. To make WVI compatible with the first-order reparameterization gradient, the authors alter the covariance update rule (e.g., by adding a transpose to $M_{t+1}$ in the update for $\Sigma_{t+1/2}$) to handle non-symmetric stochastic gradients. According to Brenier's theorem, optimal transport maps must possess symmetric Jacobians. Introducing a non-symmetric update means that the resulting pushforward is no longer an optimal coupling. This explicitly violates the foundational optimal transport justification for Bures-Wasserstein gradient flows. If the update is not an optimal transport map, the algorithm is no longer performing steepest descent in the Wasserstein metric. It is currently unclear how this breakdown in the geometry affects the underlying theory, and whether the modified algorithm degrades into a heuristically justified parameter update that has severed its connection to the Bures-Wasserstein space.

Furthermore, the theoretical proofs heavily rely on the assumption that the target potential $U$ is both $\mu$-strongly convex and $L$-smooth (Assumption 3.1). While standard in theoretical optimization, this assumption is highly restrictive in the context of variational inference, where target posteriors in complex machine learning models are frequently non-log-concave, multimodal, and lack strong convexity.

## Experimental Rigor
*Note: As the full experimental section was not completely visible during the internal review phase, the assessment of empirical rigor is based on claims made in the abstract and introductory sections, rendering it an Area Chair verification risk.*

The paper states that empirical evaluations validate the theoretical claims, explicitly noting that the use of Price's gradient is the "major source of performance improvement." A rigorous validation of this claim requires an explicit distinction between iteration complexity and wall-clock time. Price's gradient requires evaluating or approximating the Hessian ($\nabla^2 U$), carrying a per-iteration computational cost that generally scales at $O(d^2)$ or $O(d^3)$. In contrast, the reparameterization gradient involves Jacobian-vector products that scale at $O(d)$. 

If the empirical results strictly compare performance based on iteration count without penalizing the massive computational overhead of the second-order oracle, the evaluation is fundamentally an "apples-to-oranges" comparison in practical terms. A rigorous empirical study must demonstrate whether the iteration complexity improvements of Price's gradient translate to actual wall-clock time benefits across varying dimensionalities, or at least transparently acknowledge when they do not.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Crucial Clarification of the Literature:** The paper provides an essential corrective to the VI community. By pinpointing that the perceived superiority of WVI over BBVI bounds was an artifact of comparing second-order estimators against first-order estimators, the authors prevent future research from chasing phantom geometric advantages.
- **Rigorous and Matched Bounds:** The derivation of identical, state-of-the-art non-asymptotic discrete-time bounds for both SPGD and SPBWGD using Bonnet-Price estimators is a rigorous technical achievement. Table 1 effectively distills these contributions, offering a clear comparative landscape that will be highly useful to practitioners and theorists alike.
- **Bridging Subfields:** Disentangling the underlying optimization geometry (measure space versus parameter space) from the choice of stochastic gradient estimator provides significant conceptual clarity, creating a useful bridge between researchers focusing on optimal transport and those working on standard parameter-space VI.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Obscured Practical Trade-offs:** Highlighting improvements in iteration complexity without heavily emphasizing the prohibitive computational cost of Hessian evaluations risks obscuring fundamental practical trade-offs. Comparing a second-order method to a first-order method strictly on iteration count is unsurprising and often practically misleading for high-dimensional machine learning tasks.
- **Violation of Optimal Transport Geometry:** The modification of the WVI update to accommodate non-symmetric stochastic estimators (required for the reparameterization trick) fundamentally breaks the Bures-Wasserstein optimal transport formulation. By abandoning symmetric Jacobians, the method violates Brenier's theorem, casting significant doubt on whether the geometric interpretations and theoretical guarantees of WVI are preserved.
- **Restrictive Analytical Scope:** The theoretical reliance on the Gaussian variational family, combined with the assumption of a strongly convex and smooth target potential, significantly narrows the scope of the findings. The insights may not generalize to more expressive posterior approximations, such as normalizing flows or particle-based WVI methods, nor to target distributions lacking strong convexity.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
- **Wall-Clock Time and High-Dimensional Evaluations:** The empirical evaluation must prominently feature wall-clock time comparisons between Price's gradient and the reparameterization gradient across varying dimensionalities ($d$). This is necessary to accurately assess the practical utility of the estimators and to determine at what dimensionality the Hessian computation overhead outweighs the iteration complexity gains.
- **Hessian Approximation Analysis:** If Hessian-vector products (e.g., Hutchinson's estimator) are utilized in the experiments to mitigate computational costs, the variance injected by these approximations must be accounted for. It is unclear if the theoretical iteration complexity bounds explicitly incorporate the noise introduced by such approximations.
- **Geometric Validity Proofs:** The paper requires an explicit theoretical justification or proof demonstrating that the modified WVI update (which handles non-symmetric estimators) still corresponds to a valid proximal step in the Wasserstein-2 metric space, despite no longer representing an optimal transport map.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would become more confident and positive if the authors can:
1. Provide robust empirical evidence showing that, when accounting for wall-clock time, the unification of these bounds leads to practical algorithmic benefits, or explicitly frame the paper's contribution strictly as a theoretical corrective rather than a practical performance enhancement.
2. Formally address the breakdown of the optimal transport geometry when the update matrices are not symmetric. Clarifying whether the modified SPBWGD algorithm retains any meaningful connection to the Bures-Wasserstein space, or analyzing how this violation impacts the theoretical convergence guarantees, is critical.
3. Explicitly discuss how the theoretical bounds hold up when Hessian approximations are employed in high-dimensional settings.

## Provisional Leaning
Leaning Accept. The paper provides a highly valuable theoretical clarification that corrects a misleading narrative in the recent variational inference literature. While the conceptual leap is somewhat incremental given prior knowledge of these geometric equivalences, and the practical applicability of second-order estimators is heavily constrained by computational costs, the formal derivation of matched non-asymptotic bounds is rigorously executed. The identification of the gradient estimator as the primary driver of complexity differences, rather than the optimization geometry, is an important, sobering message for the community. As long as the practical trade-offs and geometric compromises are transparently addressed, this work serves as an excellent foundational reference.
