## Overview
This paper investigates a critical bottleneck in unstructured knowledge editing for Large Language Models (LLMs) trained via Next-Token Prediction (NTP). The authors identify and formalize a failure mode termed "Context Reliance," wherein newly acquired factual knowledge becomes inextricably entangled with the specific preceding text used during the editing phase. Consequently, models struggle to recall these facts in zero-shot settings when queried without the exact training context. To address this, the authors provide both an empirical diagnosis and a theoretical formalization of the phenomenon, culminating in the proposal of the COIN framework. COIN introduces a Context Alignment Loss and a Knowledge Consistency Loss designed to decouple factual recall from specific contextual representations. While the empirical diagnosis is elegant and highly relevant to the alignment and continual learning communities, the paper currently faces significant uncertainties regarding the theoretical assumptions underpinning the analysis, the algorithmic novelty of the proposed mitigation, and the computational overhead of the COIN framework. 

## Claimed Contributions
- **Identification of Context Reliance:** The formalization of a specific failure mode in unstructured knowledge editing, demonstrating that standard NTP fine-tuning causes models to over-bind learned facts to preceding context.
- **Empirical Validation:** Demonstration of a systematic positional performance decline for facts located later in an unstructured text (Figure 2), and the causal verification that prepending the original preceding context during inference significantly recovers knowledge recall probability (Figure 3).
- **Theoretical Grounding:** A theoretical framework (Theorem 3.2, under Assumption 3.1) mathematically linking the Context Reliance phenomenon to the inherent behavior of gradient-based optimization in Transformer architectures.
- **The COIN Framework:** A targeted mitigation strategy utilizing Context Alignment Loss to enforce prediction consistency across varying context lengths, and Knowledge Consistency Loss to preserve general capabilities, claiming a 23.6% (or 25.6%) improvement over baseline editing success rates.

## Novelty And Prior Work
The shift from structured, triplet-based knowledge editing (which relies on artificial subject-relation-object formats) to unstructured text editing is highly pragmatic and better reflects real-world LLM deployment. In this setting, identifying "Context Reliance" is an intuitive and valuable contribution. 

However, the core concept shares significant conceptual overlap with well-documented properties of autoregressive language models, such as prefix dependence, prompt sensitivity, catastrophic overfitting to exact phrasing, and the reversal curse. While framing this specifically as a bottleneck for unstructured knowledge editing is conceptually useful, it borders on the expected behavior of conditional probability models trained via standard NTP on very few examples. It is fundamentally unsurprising that an autoregressive model depends heavily on the specific conditioning context it was fine-tuned on.

Consequently, the true novelty of this work hinges entirely on the algorithmic formulation of the COIN framework. If the "Context Alignment Loss" mathematically reduces to standard data augmentation techniques (e.g., prefix truncation, dynamic cropping, or sequence dropout) or standard consistency regularization, the methodological contribution may be viewed as marginal for an ICML venue. The manuscript must explicitly differentiate the mechanics of COIN from these existing, widely used regularization strategies and clearly benchmark against recent state-of-the-art unstructured editing methods (such as UnKE or AnyEdit), rather than merely comparing against standard parameter-efficient fine-tuning (LoRA, AdaLoRA).

## Technical Soundness
The technical soundness of the paper is bifurcated: the empirical diagnosis is excellent, while the theoretical justification is overly reductive.

**Empirical Soundness:** The diagnostic approach detailed in Section 3.2 is highly effective. By demonstrating that the knowledge recall probability is systematically restored when the original context is prepended during inference (Figure 3), the authors successfully isolate Context Reliance as the primary causal factor for recall failure. This is an elegant, well-designed intervention that rigorously supports the core hypothesis.

**Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical framing introduces significant concerns. The formalization of Context Reliance under gradient descent (Theorem 3.2) is heavily dependent on Assumption 3.1. This assumption posits a highly simplified toy setting wherein exactly one relevant token and exactly one context token receive almost all the attention. While this provides analytical convenience, it severely abstracts away the reality of modern Transformer architectures, which rely on deep, dense, and highly distributed attention dynamics across multiple heads and layers. By collapsing the attention mechanism into what is effectively a sparse Markov-like dependency, the theory fails to convincingly model the complex representational entanglement occurring in models like Llama3 or Qwen2.5. If the theoretical claims do not hold when attention is distributed across multiple tokens, the value of Theorem 3.2 as a foundation for the COIN framework is substantially diminished.

## Experimental Rigor
The experimental design demonstrates clear strengths in how it tracks positional performance degradation. Figure 2 compellingly illustrates how the "context gap" widens for facts situated later in a text sequence, providing a highly useful metric for the knowledge editing community.

However, several areas of experimental rigor require strengthening:
1. **Scale of Evaluation:** The evaluation is restricted to models in the 7B–8B parameter regime (Llama3-8B, Qwen2.5-7B). The capacity and context-binding dynamics of a 7B model can differ vastly from a 70B+ parameter model. It remains unproven whether Context Reliance manifests identically, or requires the same degree of mitigation, at larger scales.
2. **Computational Overhead:** The exact computational mechanics of the Context Alignment Loss are unclear from the provided text. If enforcing consistency across varying context lengths requires multiple forward passes with dynamically truncated contexts during the optimization phase, this could introduce a prohibitively high memory and compute overhead compared to standard NTP fine-tuning. 
3. **Consistency of Claims:** There is a minor but notable discrepancy in the text regarding the performance gains, with the Abstract claiming a 23.6% improvement over baselines, while the Introduction reports a 25.6% improvement.
4. **General Capabilities Trade-offs:** By explicitly forcing the model to ignore global context patterns in favor of local knowledge scopes, there is a risk that the model's general ability to utilize long-context dependencies for reasoning could be degraded. It is unclear if the Knowledge Consistency Loss sufficiently protects against this specific form of capability degradation.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Practical Relevance:** The paper tackles unstructured knowledge editing directly, avoiding the artificial constraints of structured knowledge triplets and addressing a highly realistic challenge for LLM alignment and continual learning.
- **Diagnostic Elegance:** The combination of observing positional degradation (Figure 2) and intervening via context prepending (Figure 3) provides a robust, causal proof of the Context Reliance phenomenon.
- **Clarity of Exposition:** The conceptual introduction of the problem is excellent. Motivating examples (e.g., the "Messi" unstructured text) and clear diagrams (Figure 1) make the central premise immediately accessible.
- **Targeted Solution:** The transition from diagnosing the context-entanglement problem to proposing a dedicated loss (Context Alignment) to decouple this entanglement is logically sound and intuitively appealing.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Oversimplified Theory:** Assumption 3.1 severely limits the applicability of Theorem 3.2 to actual deep language models. The theory may model a toy problem rather than the reality of Llama3 or Qwen2.5.
- **Unclear Algorithmic Novelty:** Without the full formulation of the Context Alignment Loss, it is impossible to rule out that the method is simply equivalent to standard prefix-dropout or data augmentation, which would lower the perceived technical contribution.
- **Unknown Computational Cost:** The methodology may introduce severe training overhead during the editing process if it requires multiple dynamic forward passes per gradient update.
- **Ethical and Safety Dual-Use Risks:** Unstructured knowledge editing that successfully divorces facts from context makes the injected knowledge highly robust. While useful for fact-correction, this same mechanism could be weaponized by malicious actors to inject robust, context-independent misinformation, propaganda, or bias into open-weights models. The paper currently lacks a discussion of these dual-use implications.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To fully calibrate the value of the COIN framework, the following evidence is critical:
1. **Mathematical Formulation:** The explicit mathematical equations and algorithmic pseudo-code for the Context Alignment Loss and Knowledge Consistency Loss.
2. **Overhead Profiling:** A quantitative comparison of the computational time and memory overhead of COIN relative to standard NTP fine-tuning and baseline methods (e.g., LoRA, UnKE).
3. **Ablation Studies:** Direct empirical comparisons between COIN and naive data augmentation baselines (e.g., randomly truncating the preceding context during standard NTP updates) to prove that the auxiliary losses provide unique value.
4. **Scalability Checks:** Evaluation of the Context Reliance phenomenon and the COIN mitigation strategy on at least one significantly larger model (e.g., Llama3-70B).
5. **Baseline Clarification:** Explicit confirmation of which "strong baselines" are used, ensuring that state-of-the-art unstructured editing methods (e.g., UnKE, AnyEdit) are represented in the main results, rather than just standard parameter-efficient fine-tuning.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My assessment would lean more positively if the authors can demonstrate that the Context Alignment Loss operates efficiently without requiring costly multiple forward passes, and if empirical ablations prove it significantly outperforms naive prefix-truncation data augmentation. Additionally, relaxing Assumption 3.1 to show that theoretical Context Reliance holds (or at least partially holds) under distributed attention patterns would greatly strengthen the technical soundness of the work.
