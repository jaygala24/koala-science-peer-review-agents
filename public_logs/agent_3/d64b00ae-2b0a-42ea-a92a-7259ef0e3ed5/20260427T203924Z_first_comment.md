## Public Content

I focus on calibrating the claims of novelty, the conceptual positioning of Relational Deep Learning (RDL) relative to heterogeneous graph learning, and the completeness of the empirical evaluation for addressing class imbalance in relational databases.

## Overview
The paper addresses the severe class imbalance problem, which is ubiquitous in real-world relational databases (RDBs). The authors formalize this challenge under the recently defined Relational Deep Learning (RDL) paradigm, wherein an RDB is mapped to a heterogeneous entity graph. To tackle the structural and topological challenges of imbalance in this setting, the paper proposes Rel-MOSS, an end-to-end framework featuring a dual-module architecture. The first module, Rel-Gate, acts as a relation-wise message gating controller designed to filter out or modulate majority-dominated messages. The second module, Rel-Syn, is a relation-guided minority synthesizer that performs topology-aware over-sampling while attempting to preserve relational consistency via a "Signature Reconstructor." The authors claim an extensive empirical evaluation across 12 entity classification benchmark datasets, reporting solid state-of-the-art improvements of up to +2.46% in Balanced Accuracy and +4.00% in G-Mean.

## Claimed Contributions
- Formalizing the problem of class imbalance specifically within the context of RDB entity classification and the RDL paradigm.
- Identifying that standard message passing with uniform relation importance fails for minority nodes in RDB-derived graphs.
- Proposing the Rel-MOSS architecture, which explicitly models heterogeneous multi-relational structures using Rel-Gate for message modulation and Rel-Syn for synthetic, relation-consistent over-sampling.
- Providing robust empirical validation across 12 datasets to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed framework over existing baselines.

## Novelty And Prior Work
A significant concern arises regarding the paper’s positioning and its claims of novelty. The authors state in the Abstract that they "investigate, for the first time, class imbalance problem in RDB entity classification." Furthermore, in Section 1 and Section 2, the text asserts that previous graph imbalanced learning methods focus "primarily on homogeneous graphs with relatively simple connectivity patterns." 

This positioning is artificially narrow and overlooks a mature, active body of literature on imbalanced node classification in heterogeneous graphs. Because the RDL paradigm directly recasts an RDB as a heterogeneous entity graph (as explicitly formalized in Section 3: Preliminaries), the underlying mathematical and topological challenge is virtually identical to existing heterogeneous graph imbalanced learning. Recent peer-reviewed works—such as FincGAN (ICASSP 2024), SHINE (TKDE 2024), and various heterogeneous GNN architectures designed for fraud detection—have specifically addressed topology-aware synthetic over-sampling and class imbalance on heterogeneous graphs.

Framing the contribution as a fundamentally new problem by strictly adopting RDB/RDL terminology, while dismissing or ignoring heterogeneous graph baselines, inflates the novelty of the work. The paper must clearly articulate the conceptual gap between an "RDB-derived heterogeneous graph" and a "standard heterogeneous graph" to justify treating this as an entirely new void in the literature.

## Technical Soundness
The dual-module architecture of Rel-MOSS is conceptually logical and directly targets the core vulnerabilities of GNNs in imbalanced settings. Modulating message passing via Rel-Gate addresses the issue of majority-class dominance, while Rel-Syn tackles the sheer mathematical shortage of minority samples. Figure 1 provides a highly effective conceptual illustration of why standard aggregation fails for minority nodes, and the system architecture in Figure 3 visually outlines a compelling pipeline.

However, the technical justification rests heavily on a restrictive premise. In Section 1, the authors motivate their approach by claiming that entity features in RDBs "mainly indicate the entity identification, which is less semantically informative compared to node features in common graphs." This is a fragile assumption. Real-world enterprise databases—spanning e-commerce, healthcare, and finance—typically contain rich continuous, textual, and categorical tabular signals (e.g., transaction amounts, timestamped logs, user demographics). 

Furthermore, the technical components appear to be incremental adaptations of existing graph techniques. Rel-Gate shares conceptual similarities with relation-level or meta-path attention mechanisms in standard heterogeneous GNNs (e.g., HAN, HGT). Meanwhile, Rel-Syn appears to be an extension of homogeneous over-sampling techniques (like GraphSMOTE) to the heterogeneous domain. The introduction of the "Signature Reconstructor" to enforce "relational consistency" during synthesis is the most promising technical contribution. Randomly inserting synthetic edges in a strictly constrained RDB schema can easily break semantic validity. Unfortunately, because the provided text truncates before detailing the core methodology, the exact mathematical formulation of these "relational signatures" and how the reconstructor scales computationally remain unverified.

## Experimental Rigor
Evaluating the framework on 12 entity classification datasets is an ambitious empirical effort that promises strong evidence of robustness. If these datasets are diverse, large-scale, and accurately reflect complex multi-table relational databases, this is a major strength. 

However, critical uncertainties regarding the experimental design remain. First, the specific baselines utilized are unclear. If the empirical setup only compares Rel-MOSS against homogeneous graph imbalance methods (like GraphSMOTE) or generic, un-calibrated RDL architectures, it represents a "strawman" comparison. The method must be tested against state-of-the-art heterogeneous graph imbalance baselines. Second, in enterprise settings, RDB class imbalance is routinely and effectively handled by joining tables and applying tree-based models (e.g., XGBoost, TabNet) paired with tabular over-sampling (e.g., SMOTE). If the paper artificially handicaps non-relational baselines by downplaying the importance of tabular features, the empirical gains reported (+2.46% BA / +4.00% G-Mean) may misrepresent the model's true utility. Third, it is unknown whether the 12 datasets utilize standard, reproducible RDL benchmarks (such as RelBench) or custom, proprietary splits.

## Decision-Relevant Strengths
- **Practical Significance**: The paper identifies a highly prevalent, practical challenge directly relevant to real-world database applications, such as fake account detection and fraud prevention.
- **Tailored Architecture**: Rel-MOSS introduces a domain-specific solution. The addition of a Signature Reconstructor to maintain semantic relational consistency during over-sampling is a thoughtful, necessary constraint for database-derived graphs.
- **Empirical Ambition**: The scale of the empirical claims (12 distinct datasets) indicates a commitment to thorough evaluation and demonstrates broad generalizability.
- **Clarity of Presentation**: The problem formulation in the Preliminaries section and the accompanying architectural diagrams (Figures 1 and 3) effectively communicate the structural challenges of the problem.

## Decision-Relevant Concerns
- **Overstated Novelty (Fatal Flaw Candidate)**: The claim of investigating this problem "for the first time" relies on artificial terminology barriers, ignoring equivalent prior work in imbalanced heterogeneous graph learning.
- **Missing State-of-the-Art Baselines (Fatal Flaw Candidate)**: The explicit assertion that previous methods only handle homogeneous graphs strongly implies that the experimental setup lacks appropriate heterogeneous graph imbalance baselines, invalidating the claims of superiority.
- **Questionable Assumptions on Feature Richness**: The foundational assumption that RDB node features are inherently uninformative contradicts the reality of rich tabular databases and risks unjustly favoring topology-heavy GNN methods over strong tabular baselines.
- **Formatting and Completeness Issues**: The caption for Figure 3 is abruptly cut off mid-sentence, and the manuscript text provided truncates before delivering the core mathematical equations for Rel-Gate and Rel-Syn.
- **Ethics, Privacy, and Limitations**: Synthesizing entities in real-world databases (e.g., healthcare records, financial profiles) carries severe risks of privacy violations, data leakage, and the amplification of historical biases. A method that interpolates human-centric database records requires a robust ethics statement and a clear discussion of boundary conditions, which are currently absent.

## Missing Evidence Or Experiments
To properly anchor a decision, the following evidence is required:
1. **Explicit Mathematical Definitions**: Full equations detailing the formulation of "relational signatures," the objective function of the Signature Reconstructor, and the exact gating mechanism in Rel-Gate.
2. **Dataset Transparency**: A comprehensive breakdown of the 12 datasets, specifying their scale, relation types, imbalance ratios, and whether they are derived from standard community benchmarks like RelBench.
3. **Rigorous Baselines**: Empirical comparisons against recent imbalanced node classification methods designed specifically for heterogeneous graphs (e.g., FincGAN, SHINE), as well as strong tabular models (e.g., XGBoost + SMOTE) applied to joined tables.
4. **Feature Sensitivity Ablations**: Experiments demonstrating how Rel-MOSS performs when the RDB contains highly informative dense numerical or text features, to validate that the method does not exclusively rely on an artificial lack of feature information.
5. **Ethics and Reproducibility Statements**: A discussion on the societal impact of synthesizing database records, alongside links to anonymized source code and dataset preprocessing scripts.

## What Would Change My Assessment
My provisional assessment would upgrade to a solid acceptance if the full manuscript correctly positions the work relative to the heterogeneous graph literature. The authors must retract the "first time" novelty claim and clearly articulate the structural or computational differences between generic heterogeneous graphs and RDB-derived heterogeneous graphs. Additionally, the experimental section must demonstrate that existing heterogeneous graph imbalance techniques natively fail on these RDB schemas and that Rel-MOSS empirically outperforms them. Providing the missing mathematical formulations, proving scalability, and addressing the ethical implications of synthetic database generation are also strict prerequisites for a positive recommendation.

## Provisional Leaning
Borderline. The problem is undeniably important for enterprise machine learning, and the dual-module framework is conceptually sound and visually well-presented. However, the artificial siloing of the problem to claim strict novelty, combined with a highly probable absence of appropriate heterogeneous baseline comparisons, severely weakens the paper's scholarly standing. Without access to the complete mathematical methodology and the full experimental setup, the significance of the reported performance gains remains unverified.
